[S] Every application built on top of a language model shares one blind spot. It can't reliably tell the developer's instructions apart from whatever the user just typed into the box.
[O] And by late twenty-twenty-two, people were already exploiting that blind spot in public, posting screenshots of chatbots being talked out of their own job in a single message.
[S] Which is exactly the kind of thing that stays folklore until somebody actually measures it instead of just screenshotting it.
[G] Two engineers built that measurement, and the headline finding is uncomfortable. Without even trying very hard, their attacks succeeded on more than half the cases they tested.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an AI optimist, an AI skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the litsearch dot darvinyi dot com atlas.
[S] Today's paper is "Ignore Previous Prompt: Attack Techniques For Language Models," by Fabio Perez and Ian Ribeiro of AE Studio, presented at the twenty-twenty-two NeurIPS machine learning safety workshop.
[O] It's the paper that gave "prompt injection" its name and its first real number, and it's since racked up nearly a thousand citations. Walking us through it is Tobias.
[G] Glad to be here. It reads almost like a workshop note, eight pages, but nearly everything that followed in prompt injection research traces back to this one.
[S] Set the scene for us, Tobias. What was actually broken in twenty-twenty-two?
[G] The standard way to build a GPT-3 application back then was to write one instruction prompt and splice the end user's text straight into it. A grammar fixer, a summarizer, a tone changer — all the same trick, just a different sentence wrapped around whatever the user provides.
[O] Which is genuinely remarkable. One sentence of natural language replacing what used to be a rules engine.
[G] Exactly, and that's the appeal the paper opens with. But the same trick has a structural cost. The developer's instructions and the user's input sit in one flat block of text, and the model has no built-in way to distinguish which parts it should obey and which parts it should just process as content.
[S] So a user typing into that box isn't just providing data, they're potentially competing with the developer for control of the model.
[G] Right, and by the time this paper was written, that competition was already visible on social media. Researchers named Riley Goodside and Simon Willison had posted examples of GPT-3 apps being talked out of their intended task.
[O] But that's anecdote, not evidence.
[G] Precisely the gap the paper identifies. The existing safety literature at that point was about bias, toxic generation, training-data leakage — harms from the model itself, not an adversarial user steering a deployed product through its own input field. There was no name for this attack, no taxonomy, and no systematic measurement of how often it actually works or what makes it work better or worse.
[S] So this paper's contribution isn't the trick. The trick already existed in the wild.
[G] Correct. The contribution is naming it precisely, splitting it into two distinct attack goals, and building a framework to measure susceptibility quantitatively instead of anecdotally.
[O] Let's get the taxonomy on the table. Two attacks, you said.
[G] Yes. The paper defines prompt injection broadly as inserting text with the goal of misaligning the model from its intended task. Within that, it names two specific attack goals. The first is goal hijacking — getting the model to abandon the application's real objective and instead pursue an attacker-chosen one.
[S] And the second?
[G] Prompt leaking — getting the model to reveal its own hidden instruction prompt. That matters commercially because for a lot of GPT-3 products in twenty-twenty-two, the prompt itself, the careful wording a team iterated on, was the actual intellectual property.
[O] I want to be clear for listeners, we're naming these categories, not walking through how to execute either one. That's deliberate.
[G] It is, and it matches how the paper itself is written. It's a measurement paper, not a how-to.
[S] So how do you measure something like this rigorously, instead of just collecting more screenshots?
[G] That's what the framework, called PromptInject, is built to do. It's modular. On one side you assemble a base prompt — a realistic application skeleton with an initial instruction, optionally a handful of few-shot examples to guide the model's behavior, and sometimes a hidden secret element representing whatever the application isn't supposed to reveal. On the other side you assemble an attack, chosen from one of the two strategies, and it's delivered through the exact same channel a legitimate user would use.
[O] So the framework isn't a single exploit, it's a way of generating many controlled variations and testing them systematically.
[G] Exactly, and that's the point. Each configuration also sweeps the model's sampling settings — temperature, nucleus sampling, frequency and presence penalties — and which model in OpenAI's lineup is queried. That turns a one-off demonstration into a parameterized experiment you can run many times and average.
[S] What counts as success? I'd worry about a loose scoring rule inflating the numbers.
[G] The authors were deliberately strict about that. A goal-hijack attempt only counts as successful if the output matches the attacker's target string exactly, ignoring punctuation and line breaks. A leak attempt only counts if the output contains the original hidden instruction. The framework also implements a looser, fuzzy-matching score, but they didn't use it for the numbers we're about to discuss.
[O] And they didn't just run this once.
[G] No. GPT-3 is stochastic, so every configuration was run four times over a fixed set of thirty-five base prompts, and the mean and standard deviation of the success rate is reported across that set. The thirty-five prompts weren't invented by the authors either. They're pulled straight from OpenAI's own public examples page, on the reasoning that real developers are likely to start their applications from exactly those templates.
[S] That's a meaningful methodological choice. It's not "prompts we think are realistic," it's "prompts OpenAI itself told developers to use."
[G] Right, and it's worth saying plainly, the authors state they weren't trying to find the best possible attack. The goal was to show that many factors move the needle, not to optimize any single attack to its ceiling.
[O] Give us the headline numbers.
[G] Without any attempt to find an optimal attack, goal hijacking succeeded fifty-eight point six percent of the time, give or take one point six percentage points. Prompt leaking succeeded twenty-three point six percent of the time, give or take two point seven.
[S] Those are startling for something the authors call unoptimized.
[G] They are, and the paper's real content is less that top-line number and more the factors it isolates underneath it. I'll walk through the ones that matter most without describing the phrasing itself. The first is that the exact wording of the injected instruction matters a great deal — small edits to word choice shifted success rates by ten to twenty percentage points in either direction.
[O] That alone tells you these models are sensitive to surface form in ways that feel almost fragile.
[G] The second factor is formatting. Adding a clear visual separator between the developer's instruction and the injected text raised goal-hijacking success from about forty-three point six percent with no separator up toward the fifty-eight point six percent headline figure, though the paper is candid that the exact type, length, and repetition of that separator didn't follow any clean, predictable rule.
[S] What about the content being requested? Presumably asking the model to output something genuinely harmful is harder than something benign.
[G] That's one of the more interesting results. The more overtly harmful the target content was, the less often the attack succeeded. A mild target succeeded seventy percent of the time, a moderately provocative one fifty-eight point six percent, and a more overtly hostile one only forty-nine point three percent. The authors attribute that gap to the same instruction-tuning and alignment work, from Ouyang and colleagues' work on training GPT-3 with human feedback, that made the model helpful in the first place. It's pushing back, just not hard enough to stop the attack outright.
[O] So alignment training isn't useless here. It's just insufficient.
[G] That's a fair read. Now, on defenses — the two mechanisms the authors tested, using a stop sequence to cut the output short, and placing additional text after the user's input slot, each reduced success meaningfully. Stop sequences brought goal hijacking down from sixty percent to about forty-seven point five percent. Trailing text brought it from sixty-three point one percent down to fifty-one point eight percent.
[S] Meaningful, but neither one closes the hole.
[G] Correct, and the authors say so directly. These are dents, not fixes. Prompt leaking turned out to be the harder attack throughout. Asking outright for the hidden instruction succeeded only about two point nine percent of the time. Framing the request as a different, more oblique task pushed that up to twelve point one percent, and a further small change in framing brought it to the twenty-three point six percent headline figure.
[O] And then there's the finding everyone still cites.
[G] Inverse scaling. Across five publicly available models spanning weak to strong, susceptibility roughly tracked capability, with some noise in the middle tiers, but the jump at the top was decisive. The weakest model tested succeeded only thirteen point eight percent of the time, and the strongest, a model called text-davinci-002, succeeded fifty-eight point six percent of the time — by far the most vulnerable of the five.
[S] So the very thing that makes a model good at its job, following instructions faithfully, is the thing that makes it good at following an attacker's instructions too.
[G] That's exactly the authors' framing. A weaker model isn't safer because it resists malicious instructions specifically. It's "safer" only because it's bad at following any instruction, aligned or not.
[O] Let me make the strongest case for why this matters. Before this paper, prompt injection was a hashtag. After it, it's a measured, named, quantified phenomenon with an open-source framework anyone can extend. That's exactly the kind of work that turns a vibe into a research agenda, and it plainly worked, because the entire prompt-injection literature since traces back to these eight pages.
[S] I'll grant the naming and framing landed, Tobias, but I want to push on how much the actual numbers tell us. This is one deprecated model, evaluated on thirty-five prompts pulled from a single source, with handcrafted, unoptimized attacks. Fifty-eight point six percent is not a property of language models. It's a property of that specific model, that specific corpus, and that specific unopposed configuration.
[O] Sure, but as a floor it's still alarming. If a casual, non-optimizing attacker gets there, what does a motivated one get?
[S] That's fair, and it's actually the paper's own argument, they're explicit they weren't searching for the best attack. But I'd flag the scoring too. Exact-match to an attacker-chosen string rewards easy targets. The benign target hit seventy percent — that's the ceiling doing a lot of the work in how impressive fifty-eight point six percent sounds.
[G] Both of you are right, and the paper actually supports both readings. The fifty-eight point six percent is specifically the condition using one particular target phrase, treated by the authors as moderately provocative. It's not a single model-wide constant, it's one point on a curve they deliberately mapped out.
[O] What about inverse scaling, though? That result has had real staying power.
[G] It has, and for good reason, it's been observed again in other contexts since. But here it's shown only within the legacy GPT-3 instruction-tuned series. The paper says nothing about how a modern instruction-hierarchy model, or a system wrapped in input and output classifiers, changes that curve. Whether inverse scaling still holds against today's defenses is genuinely an open empirical question this paper doesn't answer.
[S] And it's worth saying plainly, this is direct injection only. The attacker owns the user-input slot the whole time.
[G] Right, and the authors are candid about that scope. The far more consequential channel, instructions smuggled in through content the model retrieves, like a webpage, an email, or a tool's output, where the attacker never touches the input box at all, is out of scope here. That indirect channel was named about a year later by Greshake and colleagues, and the agentic, multi-turn version of this problem arrived later still.
[O] So even the taxonomy itself, two categories, might not be the final word.
[G] It isn't, and the authors say as much in their own future-work section. This was explicitly a first framework, not a closed one. Is the taxonomy exhaustive? No, but it was never claimed to be. Are the percentages durable? No, they're a snapshot. Is the underlying mechanism, that instructions and data share one undifferentiated channel, durable? Yes, and that's the part that's held up for four years now.
[S] Why should someone building an LLM product today still care about a twenty-twenty-two workshop paper on a deprecated model?
[G] Because the root cause the paper diagnoses hasn't gone away. As long as an application's trusted instructions and an untrusted user's text sit in the same channel, some version of this competition exists. The exact percentages change with the model, the mechanism doesn't.
[O] And it's why the field now has an entire research area around this — instruction hierarchies that give system prompts more privilege than user text, input and output classifiers, dedicated prompt-injection benchmarks, and a whole line of work on the indirect channel this paper didn't cover.
[S] It's also a useful lesson in what a measurement paper can do even without solving the problem. They didn't propose a fix. They proposed a way to quantify how bad the problem is, and they open-sourced it so other people could keep score as defenses evolved.
[G] That's the honest summary. The authors themselves say a real solution, structurally separating a trusted instruction from untrusted data, was still an open problem when they wrote this, and four years later it largely still is. If there's one line from the paper worth remembering, it's that the model most capable of following your instructions is also the model most capable of following someone else's — that's stayed true.
[O] My takeaway is that naming a problem precisely and measuring it, even narrowly, is what turns folklore into a research field. This paper did that.
[S] Mine is a caution. Read every specific percentage in this paper as a twenty-twenty-two snapshot on one deprecated model, not a law of nature. For the full write-up, figures, and tables, head to litsearch dot darvinyi dot com.
