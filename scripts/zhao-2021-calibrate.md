[O] Understood. So set the scene. Why does this paper need to exist?
[G] Because a year earlier, Brown and colleagues had shown with GPT-3 that a large autoregressive language model can do a task from nothing but a natural language prompt, a description plus a handful of labeled examples, no gradient updates at all. That's an attractive story operationally. You get rapid prototyping, a natural language interface even non-experts can use, and one model serving every task instead of a separate fine-tuned copy for each. But the prompt itself has three independent knobs, which examples you choose, what order you put them in, and what template wraps them, and nobody had systematically measured whether that prompt was secretly a confound.
[S] So the field had been treating the prompt as a fixed, minor hyperparameter.
[G] Essentially, yes. Prior in-context learning papers reported one score per task without probing that axis. And it's a strange thing to skip, because in standard machine learning, the order you feed training examples in during training is an afterthought. Here it isn't, because these are left-to-right autoregressive models. The hidden state updates causally, token by token, so where an example sits in the prompt is architecturally load-bearing at inference time, not incidental.
[O] Naomi, give me the headline instability number.
[G] Four-shot SST-2, sentiment classification, GPT-3 at two point seven billion parameters. Varying only the permutation of the same four training examples moves accuracy from fifty-four point three percent to ninety-three point four percent. Their appendix has an even starker two-example case, one ordering of two training examples reaches eighty-eight point five percent test accuracy, and simply reversing those same two examples drops it to fifty-one point three percent, right at chance. And crucially, this doesn't wash out as you scale up. It's still visible at sixteen shots, and it's still visible at the full one hundred seventy-five billion parameter model.
[S] So bigger doesn't fix it.
[G] Bigger does not fix it. That's one of the paper's more uncomfortable findings.
[S] Okay, so why does order matter this much? What's actually going on inside the model?
[G] They identify three separate biases, and each one is a systematic shift in the model's output distribution, not random noise. The first is majority label bias, the model gets pulled toward whichever answer appears most often among the training examples in the prompt, even on datasets the authors deliberately keep label-balanced. This explains a repeated oddity in their baseline numbers, accuracy often drops going from zero-shot to one-shot, because adding a single training example just teaches the model to repeat that example's label. On four-shot LAMA with the two point seven billion parameter model, fifty point two percent of predictions are a verbatim repeat of one of the four training answers, against a twenty-four point seven percent base rate at which that repeat would actually be correct.
[O] So a single, correctly labeled example can actively hurt you, because the model treats it as something to copy rather than evidence to learn from.
[G] Exactly that. The second bias is recency, the model over-weights training examples sitting near the end of the prompt, a direct consequence of that left-to-right processing. And it can outright overpower majority. A prompt with three Positive training examples and one Negative, ending in that lone Negative, still pushes the model to predict Negative on nearly ninety percent of test inputs, despite three quarters of the training examples being Positive. On LAMA, the training answer sitting in prompt position one through four gets over-predicted by eight point five percent, eight point three percent, fourteen point three percent, and sixteen point one percent respectively, a roughly monotonic climb toward the end of the prompt.
[S] That's a genuinely surprising result, recency beating majority outright.
[G] It is, and it's the cleanest evidence that this isn't just "unbalanced labels confuse the model." The third bias is independent of the prompt's contents entirely, common token bias, GPT-3 favors answer strings that are common in its pretraining corpus. On a balanced fourteen-way topic classification task, DBPedia, it predicts the "book" class eleven times more often than "artist," and the frequency of a label name in Google's Ngram corpus correlates with GPT-3's rate of predicting that class at point six seven. For fact retrieval, the same bias shows up as the model guessing common entities, something like "America," over the correct but rarer ground truth entity.
[O] Do all three point the same direction, or do they fight each other?
[G] The net effect, across a sentiment prompt they visualize directly, is a simple shift in the whole output distribution toward one class. If you could set the classification threshold optimally for that shifted distribution, point six eight instead of the default point five, you'd get ninety-four percent accuracy on the same prompt. So the information for a much better classifier is already in there. The problem is where you draw the line.
[S] So the fix is finding that better threshold without any labeled data to find it with.
[G] Right, and that's the elegant part. They borrow the recipe from Platt scaling and from Guo and colleagues' work on calibrating neural networks, an affine transform, q hat equals softmax of W times p hat plus b, where W and b get applied to the model's raw output probabilities. They constrain W to a diagonal matrix, called vector scaling, specifically so the parameter count stays linear rather than quadratic in an output space of roughly fifty thousand tokens. The obstacle is that in the zero- or few-shot setting there's no labeled validation data to fit W and b against. Their trick is to feed the model the exact same training examples, permutation, and format, but swap the real test input for a content-free placeholder, the string "N slash A," the token "mask," or the empty string, and read off whatever prediction comes back. Since a content-free input carries no signal about the true label, any skew away from a uniform prediction is exactly the bias you need to remove. In their running sentiment example, feeding "N slash A" gets scored sixty-one point eight percent Positive, not fifty-fifty, and that sixty-one point eight is exactly the correction they apply. They set W to the inverse of that content-free prediction and leave b at zero, which forces the content-free input to a uniform score, then apply that same correction to real test inputs.
[O] And the cost of doing this?
[G] Essentially free. No extra training data, negligible added compute, and in their own words, a few lines of code on top of a forward pass you were already running. In practice they average the probe across all three content-free strings, N slash A, mask, and empty string, since they found the ensemble strongest.
[O] Give us the headline results.
[G] They test this across three GPT-3 sizes, two point seven billion, thirteen billion, and one hundred seventy-five billion parameters, plus GPT-2 at one point five billion, across text classification, fact retrieval, and information extraction tasks. That's a genuinely wide sweep, dozens of model-size-by-dataset-by-shot-count cells. They cap most of the main sweep at eight training examples, past that querying the OpenAI API for every permutation gets prohibitively expensive, though a handful of their size curves push out to sixteen shots specifically to check that the instability doesn't just wash out with more context. Across nearly every cell of that grid, calibration improves both mean and worst-case accuracy, by up to thirty points absolute. And it's consistent enough that the two point seven billion parameter model, calibrated, sometimes beats the uncalibrated one hundred seventy-five billion parameter baseline by up to nineteen point three points, despite being more than fifty times smaller.
