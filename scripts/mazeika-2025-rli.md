[S] Now take those same models and hand them two hundred forty real freelance jobs — jobs where an actual person got paid actual money to deliver actual work — and watch what happens.
[O] The best agent in this study, a system called Manus, completes two point five percent of them to a standard a real client would accept.
[S] Two point five percent. Not a two and a half percent improvement — two point five percent of the jobs, full stop. Everything you've heard about agents eating white-collar work has to argue against that number now.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an AI optimist and an AI skeptic pull apart one paper with a guest who knows it cold. Today's paper is Remote Labor Index: Measuring AI Automation of Remote Work, out of the Center for AI Safety and Scale AI, led by Mantas Mazeika, Alice Gatti, and Cristina Menghini, with Summer Yue, Alexandr Wang, and Dan Hendrycks as senior authors.
[S] I'm the skeptic. I think this is the most honest number we've gotten on economic automation all year, and I also think there are three or four ways people are going to misquote it.
[O] And I'm the optimist, here to argue the floor is exactly where you'd expect a brand-new, brutally hard benchmark to sit on day one. Joining us is Maya, who's spent real time inside this paper. Maya, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. The way I'd frame it: this is the first benchmark that tries to price AI automation in actual dollars and actual hours, rather than in quiz questions. That framing alone is why it's worth forty minutes of anyone's commute.
[S] So set up the gap, Maya. Why did the field need this, when there's already a pile of agent benchmarks out there?
[G] Because almost all of them measure a narrow slice. SWE-bench and SWE-Lancer are software engineering. OSWorld, WebArena, and GAIA are basic computer and web use. The paper is explicit that each one is a useful signal in isolation, but none of them captures how broad the actual remote-labor economy is — the design work, the audio and video production, the CAD and architecture and operations work people are genuinely paid to do.
[O] What about GDPval? That one already claimed near-human performance on real professional tasks.
[G] The paper addresses that directly. GDPval, from Patwardhan and colleagues, shows AI models near human parity on tasks shared across professions — writing, web search, administrative work. But those are shared subtasks, not whole projects. This paper's argument is that being good at the slice of work every profession has in common tells you almost nothing about whether an agent can finish an entire commissioned project end to end.
[S] Which is a much harder thing to build a benchmark around, because now you need real projects with real prices attached, not synthetic tasks.
[G] Exactly, and that's most of the paper's actual labor. It isn't a clever new eval trick. It's two hundred forty real, paid freelance projects, and building that dataset honestly is most of the contribution.
[O] Walk us through what one of these projects actually looks like.
[G] Every project has three pieces. A brief — the text describing the work. Input files — everything needed to do the job. And a human deliverable — the gold-standard artifact a paid professional actually produced. The examples in the paper give you the range: an interactive dashboard for exploring World Happiness Report data, a set of 3D animations showcasing a new earbuds design, a brewing-themed merge game, architectural plans for a container home, and a paper formatted to IEEE conference standards.
[S] And because the brief and the human deliverable both come from the same person who did the paid work, you know the brief actually contains enough information to finish the job. That's not always true of synthetic benchmarks.
[G] Right, that's the design insight. The person who wrote the brief is the same person who satisfied it, so completeness is guaranteed by construction rather than by a benchmark author's guess.
[O] Where did two hundred forty projects even come from? You can't just scrape this off the internet.
[G] They went bottom-up. They scoped the collection using Upwork's job taxonomy — sixty-four categories total — and filtered out anything that needed physical labor, client interaction, teamwork, or back-end work that can't be graded on the spot. That left forty-three eligible categories. Then they recruited three hundred fifty-eight verified Upwork freelancers, each with real track records, and paid them between fifteen and two hundred dollars, averaging forty-one dollars, to sell samples of work they'd already completed for actual clients.
[O] Forty-one dollars to buy a sample of work that a client originally paid hundreds or thousands of dollars for. That's a strange asymmetry — the freelancer did the hard part for someone else already, and this is basically a licensing fee for using it as a benchmark.
[G] That's a fair way to put it. The freelancers aren't being paid freelancer rates to redo the work — they're being paid a smaller fee to license work they'd already delivered and gotten paid for once. The paper doesn't dwell on the ethics of that, but it's worth naming.
[S] How do you get from raw submissions down to a clean benchmark?
[G] Five hundred fifty raw projects came in from that freelancer pool, plus a long-tail pass of custom commissions and permissioned digital work found online. After multiple rounds of review, cleaning, anonymization, and standardizing everything to a common brief format, two hundred forty projects survived, spanning twenty-three of Upwork's categories. Video leads at thirteen percent, then CAD at twelve, graphic design at eleven, game development and audio both at ten.
[O] And these aren't toy-sized tasks.
[G] Not remotely. Human professionals took a mean of twenty-eight point nine hours per project, median eleven and a half, with some running past four hundred hours. Costs averaged about six hundred thirty-three dollars, median two hundred, with the most expensive project at twenty-two thousand five hundred dollars. Altogether the benchmark represents over six thousand hours of real work, worth just under a hundred forty-four thousand dollars if an agent completed every single project.
[S] That completion-time number is the one that actually convinced me this is different from prior agent benchmarks. Twenty-eight point nine hours is more than double what comparable benchmarks like HCAST and GDPval report, and it matches the real distribution of job lengths on Upwork itself.
[G] Right, and that's not a coincidence — they checked it against a random sample of real Upwork jobs specifically to make that claim. It's also why roughly eighty percent of RLI is work outside software and writing, the categories prior benchmarks lean on, while those two only make up a small share here.
[O] What about contamination? A benchmark built from real freelance briefs sounds like exactly the kind of thing that leaks onto the internet.
[G] They took that seriously. Only ten of the two hundred forty projects are public, released alongside the open-sourced evaluation platform. The other two hundred thirty are a private test set. None of the briefs are searchable, and there's a domain blocklist covering the long-tail items whose human deliverables exist somewhere online, so a model can't just retrieve the answer.
[S] Okay, now the part I actually want to argue about — how do you grade a delivered 3D model or a merge game or an architectural plan? You can't just diff it against a reference string.
[G] You can't, and the authors tried the obvious fix first — granular per-project rubrics — and found it didn't work. A deliverable could tick every rubric box and still plainly fail professional standards, especially on design work. So they switched to a holistic call: would a reasonable client accept this as the commissioned work? Evaluators rate it on a three-point scale — worse than the human, as good and acceptable, or better than the human — with a written justification, and every judgment is a majority vote across three independent evaluators.
[O] And that has to be done by hand, because judging a rendered 3D file or a video file is itself beyond what an LLM judge can currently do reliably.
[G] That's the paper's own reasoning, yes. Evaluating these deliverables is a genuinely agentic task — rendering dozens of file formats, doing multimodal comparison — so they argue LLM-as-judge isn't feasible yet and grade everything with trained human workers and subject experts. Inter-annotator agreement on the automation-rate call comes in at ninety-four point four percent, and they also sampled fifty human-versus-model pairs for a manual audit and found zero false negatives, bounding the false-negative rate under about six percent.
[S] Four metrics come out of this setup, right? Not just pass or fail.
[G] Four. Automation rate is the binary pass rate against the human bar. Elo captures relative quality from pairwise comparisons, canonicalized so the human baseline sits at one thousand, and a four-hundred-point gap means ten-to-one odds. Dollars earned sums the price of every project an agent actually completed. And the signature one, autoflation, treats the whole two-hundred-forty-project bundle like a price index — it asks how much cheaper that bundle gets once you're always allowed to use whichever is cheaper, the human or the AI, and a failed AI attempt counts as infinitely expensive, so it never drags the price down.
[O] Give me the headline table.
[G] Across six frontier agents — ChatGPT agent, GPT-5, Claude Sonnet 4.5, Grok 4, Gemini 2.5 Pro, and Manus — Manus leads at two point five percent automation rate. Grok 4 and Sonnet 4.5 tie at two point one percent. GPT-5 on the command-line scaffold gets one point seven. ChatGPT agent is at one point three. Gemini 2.5 Pro trails at zero point eight.
[S] Put dollars on that, because percentages hide how small this is.
[G] Manus earns seventeen hundred twenty dollars of the roughly hundred forty-four thousand on offer. Gemini 2.5 Pro earns two hundred ten dollars. That's the entire quantitative story of current agentic automation of remote freelance work, in one number each.
[O] Is there anything moving under that floor, or is it just flat?
[G] It's not flat. Elo is the tool built to catch that. Manus leads there too at five hundred nine point nine, well below the human baseline of one thousand, but the ordering across all six models tracks release recency fairly closely. So even while the pass-fail bar stays near zero, the relative-quality signal shows steady movement.
[S] Why do they fail? Is this a reasoning gap or something more mundane?
[G] Mostly mundane. They clustered the written justifications across roughly four hundred evaluations. Poor quality shows up in forty-five point six percent of failures, incomplete or malformed deliverables in thirty-five point seven, corrupted or unusable files in seventeen point six, and internal inconsistencies in fourteen point eight — these aren't mutually exclusive, so one deliverable can rack up several. Concretely: videos that run eight seconds instead of the requested eight minutes, robotic voiceovers, floor plans that don't match the supplied sketches, a rendered house whose appearance changes across different 3D views.
[O] So they're not failing because they can't understand the brief. They're failing because they can't check their own work.
[G] That's the pattern the authors point to. A recurring theme is that agents can't verify and fix their own output when it requires interactive audiovisual judgment — watching the video back, comparing renders across angles, listening for a bad splice.
[S] Where do they actually win, then?
[G] Audio editing, mixing, and production. Image generation for ads and logos. Report writing. And code for interactive data visualization — Sonnet 4.5 successfully built a working, self-hosted World Happiness Report dashboard, one of the example projects from earlier. Those are exactly the domains where today's generative tools are already strong and self-contained.
[O] And there's a scaffold result buried in here that I think matters as much as the model comparisons.
[G] The GPT-5 one. Same model, two harnesses. On a command-line agent scaffold it gets one point seven percent automation and an Elo of four hundred thirty-six point seven. On a computer-use scaffold — mouse, keyboard, screenshots — the exact same model drops to zero point eight percent and an Elo of four hundred thirty-one point six. The authors conclude current models can't yet fully exploit computer-use environments.
[O] Let me make the optimist case, because I think the floor is misleading people. The average cost to generate one of these AI deliverables was two dollars and thirty-four cents — every model capped itself under thirty dollars of API spend. Against a human freelancer's average bill of over six hundred dollars, that's an economic reality nobody's pricing in yet. And unlike a calculator automating one task, these are general systems — the paper's own framing is that an agent that eventually clears this benchmark without overfitting would carry the same broad cognitive skills into whatever new remote job appears next, not just the two hundred forty already on file.
[S] I'll take the second half of that later, Maya, but the first half undersells the confound sitting right in the middle of the leaderboard. You're comparing a June agent to October models on one leaderboard.
[G] That's accurate, and it's a real gap in the paper. It's disclosed in an appendix, not flagged in the main results table, and it means the number-one row isn't quite contemporaneous with the rest.
[O] Fair, dock Manus specifically. But Elo still shows an honest recency ordering across the other five, and that's the metric designed to survive exactly this kind of leaderboard mixing.
[S] Here's my second objection, and it's about the automation-rate bar itself. It's binary. A deliverable that's ninety percent of the way to acceptable scores identically to one that's completely empty. That's a defensible bar for could this replace the freelancer, but it erases a lot of real, partial economic value.
[G] The data actually supports your worry more than mine. Ternary inter-annotator agreement on the Elo comparisons is fifty-six point nine percent — well above the thirty-three percent you'd get from random guessing, but nowhere near the ninety-four point four percent agreement on the binary automation call. So the partial-credit signal that's supposed to rescue nuance from the floored headline is measurably noisier than the floor itself.
[S] And a third thing, Maya — scope. By the authors' own design, RLI excludes anything needing client interaction, teamwork, back-end development, or waiting to evaluate. They say outright that a hundred percent score on RLI wouldn't mean full automation of remote labor.
[O] I'd actually flip that one in the optimist's favor. Those exclusions were made for measurability, not because that work is somehow safe from automation — if anything, RLI is undercounting the addressable economy, not overselling coverage of it.
[G] I think that's the right read. On the confound question, the skeptic's case is strong — model and scaffold and calendar time are genuinely tangled in this table. On whether there's a real underlying trend, the Elo data earns the optimist's case, even if it's noisier than the top-line number suggests.
[S] Last one, and it's about reproducibility, not the science itself. Ten public projects out of two hundred forty means almost nobody outside CAIS and Scale AI can actually re-run this leaderboard.
[G] That's a real trade-off the paper accepts rather than resolves. It's also honest about where that leads — the authors predict inter-annotator agreement will fall as AI deliverables get better, because acceptable or not becomes a genuinely close call instead of an obvious one. That's their own forecast about their own method degrading, which is more candor than most benchmark papers offer.
[O] So what actually changes if this floor starts moving?
[S] For me it's a template for evaluation practice generally, not just labor economics. If judging outputs is itself too hard for an automated grader — which is exactly why they went fully manual here — that's a live warning for every field trying to lean on LLM-as-judge for open-ended agentic work, not just this one.
[G] And the paper frames autoflation specifically as something to watch quarter over quarter, the way you'd watch a price index, rather than a one-time pass or fail grade. That's a deliberate choice — the headline number is designed to start near zero and be tracked, not to be the whole finding.

[G] RLI is the first benchmark that grounds AI automation in actual paid transactions rather than quiz scores, and the honest finding is that end-to-end automation of real remote work sits near zero today, even as knowledge benchmarks saturate.
