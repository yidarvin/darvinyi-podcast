[S] Here's an uncomfortable question for anyone who believes reinforcement learning taught DeepSeek R-one and its cousins to actually reason: what if all that training did was make the model more likely to say something it could already say?
[O] That sounds almost like a rhetorical trick, but this paper turns it into a real experiment. Sample enough times from the base model, before any reinforcement learning, and it starts catching up to, and in a lot of cases beating, its own R-L-trained descendant.
[S] Which, if it holds, undercuts the entire "reinforcement learning discovers new strategies" story that's been driving reasoning-model hype since R-one shipped.
[O] Or it's a measurement artifact from cranking the sample count up absurdly high. I want to know which before we're done.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the frontier of machine learning research. Today's paper is "Does Reinforcement Learning Really Incentivize Reasoning Capacity in LLMs Beyond the Base Model," by Yang Yue and colleagues at Tsinghua's LeapLab and Shanghai Jiao Tong University, first posted to arXiv in April twenty twenty-five.
[S] It's on the docket because it's become the reference challenge to the "R-L-V-R teaches reasoning" narrative, the paper practitioners cite when they want to argue that reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards is sharpening a model's existing distribution rather than expanding it.
[O] Joining us is Julian, who's spent real time inside this paper's pass-at-k curves and its appendix. Julian, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. This is one of those papers where the headline sounds like a hot take, but the actual experimental design is unusually careful, so I'm happy to walk through both.
[S] Start with the term, for anyone who hasn't been living inside this literature. What is R-L-V-R?
[G] Reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards. You start from a pretrained base model, sometimes one already fine-tuned on long chains of thought, and you optimize it against a reward that's automatic and binary: did the final answer match the key, did the code pass its unit tests. No human labeling in the loop, which is exactly why it scales so cheaply.
[O] And the story people tell about why that works is borrowed straight from classical reinforcement learning. Agents playing Atari or Go start from nothing and discover strategies no human taught them. DeepSeek R-one's own paper leans into that framing pretty hard, self-improvement, emergent reflection, the model teaching itself to reason.
[G] Right, and that's the implicit claim this paper interrogates directly. Does R-L-V-R actually let a model acquire reasoning it didn't have, the way an Atari agent discovers a strategy nobody programmed in? Or is it using patterns that were already sitting in the base model, just surfacing them more often?
[S] Why couldn't anyone answer that already? R-L-V-R models obviously score higher on math and code benchmarks.
[G] Because almost every benchmark number you've seen is an average-case metric, greedy decoding or the mean over a few samples, which is just pass at one. That can't tell you whether a model gained a new ability or just got more reliable at an old one. A model that solves a hard problem once in a thousand tries, and one that can never solve it, look identical under pass at one sampled once.
[O] So you need a metric that asks not "what does the model usually do" but "what is the model capable of, given enough tries."
[S] That's pass at k, presumably.
[G] Exactly. Sample k outputs for a problem; pass at k is one if any single one is correct, zero otherwise. Average over a dataset and you get the fraction of problems solvable within k attempts. As k grows large, you stop measuring average behavior and start tracing the model's reasoning boundary, its potential, not its luck on one draw. They use the standard unbiased pass-at-k estimator from the original Codex paper, so the numbers aren't noisy empirical averages.
[O] And the test is clean: if R-L-V-R genuinely adds capability, the R-L model's pass-at-k curve should sit above the base model's curve at every single k, not just the small ones.
[G] That's the whole logic of the paper in one sentence.
[S] I'll push on this immediately, because it's the obvious objection. At large k, aren't you just letting the model get lucky? Guess enough times and you'll stumble onto the right answer for the wrong reasons.
[G] Three guardrails. First, not best-of-N or majority voting: pass at k credits the model the moment a single correct sample exists among the k, so it can't discard a correct answer it actually produced. Second, base and R-L models get the identical zero-shot prompt, no few-shot crutch for the base model. Base models do produce unformatted junk early on, but with enough samples they format and solve fine.
[O] And the guessing worry specifically?
[G] Third guardrail. For code, unit tests make a lucky pass essentially impossible. For math, where a wrong chain can still stumble onto the right number, the authors manually inspected the chains behind correct answers on the hardest problems in G-S-M-eight-K and A-I-M-E twenty twenty-four. Exact numbers when we get to results, but the short version is, it isn't guessing.
[S] What's the actual experimental footprint here? One model family, or something broader?
[G] Deliberately broad, which is part of why this landed as hard as it did. Three domains, math, code, visual reasoning. Multiple families: Qwen two point five at seven, fourteen, thirty-two billion, a math-specialized Qwen variant, LLaMA three point one at eight billion, and a vision-language Qwen model. They use released checkpoints from public projects, and, so no single group's recipe is doing something idiosyncratic, they also re-implement six algorithms on one shared framework: P-P-O, G-R-P-O, Reinforce plus plus, R-L-O-O, Re-Max, and D-A-P-O.
[O] That re-implementation step matters. It's the difference between "this one released checkpoint happens to behave this way" and "this is a property of the training paradigm itself."
[G] Right, and it lets them define something they call the sampling efficiency gap: treat the base model's pass at two hundred fifty-six as a rough ceiling on what's reachable, and measure how far an R-L model's pass at one falls short of that ceiling. Smaller is better, and it turns out to barely move across all six algorithms.
[S] One more thing before results. How do they check whether an R-L model's outputs are actually novel, versus just things the base model already assigned high probability to?
[G] Perplexity. Take a response the R-L model produced, and score how surprising it would have been under the base model's own probability distribution. If R-L is discovering genuinely new reasoning, those responses should look unusual, high perplexity, to the base model. If R-L is just reweighting what the base model already does, they should look ordinary, low perplexity.
[O] Alright, let's get to the actual curves.
[G] The headline pattern repeats on every math benchmark and family they tried: at small k, the R-L model wins clearly, the familiar "reinforcement learning improves performance" story. But as k grows, the base model's curve is steeper, catches up, crosses over, and wins outright. On Minerva with a thirty-two billion parameter model, base ends up ahead by roughly nine percent at k equals one hundred twenty-eight, meaning it solves about nine percent more of the validation set.
[S] Nine percent doesn't sound huge.
[G] It's not the most dramatic example. On A-I-M-E twenty twenty-four, the strongest released R-L models, called Oat-Zero and D-A-P-O, start out nearly thirty percentage points above the base model at low k. And they still get overtaken once k is large enough. That's the crossover holding even from a massive early lead.
[O] And this isn't just a math quirk, you said code and vision show the same thing?
[G] Same crossover on Human-Eval-plus and M-B-P-P-plus for code, and on Math Vista and Math Vision for visual reasoning. Code is actually the cleanest evidence against the guessing objection, since passing a full unit test suite by luck is close to impossible, and the pattern still shows up there too.
[S] Now give me the manual chain-of-reasoning numbers, because that's the part I actually trust most here.
[G] On the hardest G-S-M-eight-K problems, solved less than five percent of the time, the base model answered twenty-five, twenty-four with a genuinely valid reasoning chain. The R-L model also answered twenty-five, twenty-three valid. On the hardest A-I-M-E twenty twenty-four problems, it's five of six valid for base, four of six for R-L. Not lucky guessing on either side, both models reason their way there, base just reaches slightly more.
[S] That's a genuinely good control. I'll concede the guessing objection is handled.
[G] There's a table I think is the single most persuasive result. On A-I-M-E twenty twenty-four at k equals one thousand twenty-four, every problem gets bucketed: both solve it, only base, only R-L, or neither. Both-solve is sixty-three point three percent, base-only thirteen point three percent, R-L-only zero point zero percent. The R-L-solvable set is nearly a strict subset of the base-solvable set.
[O] Zero percent R-L-only is a striking number.
[G] It's the number that makes the perplexity result make sense. Score R-L-generated responses under the base model's own distribution and they land in the low-perplexity tail, exactly what the base was already likely to say. OpenAI's o-one outputs, scored the same way, land far higher, genuinely surprising to the base. And the longer training runs, the more this compresses: pass at one climbs from twenty-six point one to forty-two point five, while pass at two hundred fifty-six, the coverage number, steadily falls.
[S] So more training is actively making the ceiling worse, even as the headline number people usually report keeps going up.
[G] That's the uncomfortable version of the finding, yes. And it isn't sensitive to which of the six algorithms you use. The sampling efficiency gap sits at forty-three point nine for G-R-P-O and forty-two point six for R-L-O-O, and stays above forty points for every algorithm they tested. Whatever's causing this, it isn't a quirk of one optimizer.
[O] What did move the needle, if anything did?
[G] Distillation. A model like DeepSeek R-one Distill Qwen, seven billion, trained by imitating a stronger teacher's outputs rather than reinforcement learning against a verifier, stays above its base model at every k. It's importing genuinely new reasoning patterns from outside, which reinforcement learning, working only with a binary signal on its own samples, structurally cannot do.
[O] Let me make the strongest optimist case, because I don't think this paper is as damning as the title sounds. R-L-V-R reliably lifts pass at one, and that's what actually ships, nobody's product samples a thousand times per query. The authors themselves say this is a finding about current R-L-V-R, binary rewards, single-turn, modest compute, not a claim that reinforcement learning can never expand reasoning in principle.
[S] I'll take the other side, and I don't think it's close. The whole reason R-L-V-R got compared to AlphaGo and Atari was the promise of self-improvement without a ceiling, a model bootstrapping past what any human or teacher gave it. This paper shows that promise isn't happening at any scale they tested, on any of six algorithms. That's the central claim these systems were sold on.
[G] Both are fair readings, and the paper adjudicates part of this itself. Their explanation is architectural. Classical reinforcement learning, AlphaGo, Atari, starts from nothing in an environment small enough to explore exhaustively. R-L-V-R starts from a pretrained model inside an astronomically larger action space, language itself, and that prior is what makes exploration tractable, wander too far and you get nonsense with zero reward. But the same prior boxes the policy in: policy gradient pushes up already-likely, already-correct responses and down already-unlikely ones, leaving little room to sample outside what the prior already covers.
[O] So the prior is a ladder that helps you climb, but it's also the only ladder you're allowed to use.
[G] That's a fair way to put it, and it's the authors' own account of why distillation behaves differently. A teacher model injects a different prior from outside, rather than reweighting the one you started with.
[S] I want to push on generalization, though, Julian. The model zoo is heavily Qwen-family. LLaMA three point one at eight billion is the only real outsider, and the weakest model in the set. If Qwen base models are just unusually well primed for these benchmark formats already, some of this crossover could be a property of that family, not of R-L-V-R as a paradigm.
[G] That's a legitimate limitation, and the first thing I'd stress-test when extending this work. It doesn't undermine the within-model comparisons, base versus its own R-L checkpoint controls for a lot, but "current R-L-V-R is bounded by the base model" is best read as strongly supported for this family, and still open for others.
[O] What about scale? Everything here tops out around thirty-two billion parameters and a fairly small number of rollouts per step. Maybe this whole pattern washes out once you're training a frontier-scale model with a real compute budget behind the R-L stage.
[G] They tried, and were honest about how limited it is. OpenAI's o-one has no public base model to compare against, Qwen three mixes R-L-V-R with chain-of-thought fine-tuning so its contribution can't be isolated, and DeepSeek R-one Zero was too slow to self-host at large k. So as a stand-in they used an API for Magistral Medium, a near-frontier model trained with pure reinforcement learning on Mistral Medium three. Same pattern: at k equals one it solves roughly seven more A-I-M-E twenty twenty-four problems and eight more A-I-M-E twenty twenty-five problems than its base, but the gap narrows as k grows.
[S] So the frontier evidence points the same direction, it's just thinner.
[G] Thinner, and the authors say so themselves. Whether this holds once genuinely pretraining-scale compute goes into the R-L stage is, in their words, a critical open question they can't answer with the resources available to an academic lab.
[O] One more thing in the paper's favor. They tried to explain the drop away and couldn't. They raised the R-L model's sampling temperature until its output entropy matched the base model's, in case reduced diversity alone explained the narrowing. Pass at k improved slightly, but the R-L model still underperformed the base across the curve. So low entropy is a contributor, not the whole story.
[S] Which I actually find persuasive, they went looking for the boring explanation and it didn't fully hold up.
[O] Step back, if this holds, what changes for how people build these systems?
[G] It reframes what reinforcement learning for reasoning is currently doing. Not discovery, redistribution. If you want a model that can do something its base couldn't, the evidence points toward distillation from a stronger teacher, or the directions the authors flag as future work: exploration above the token level, deliberate curriculum data, process-level rewards instead of one binary signal, and multi-turn agentic training where a model acts and gets feedback rather than emitting one shot.
[S] For anyone doing evaluation for a living, there's a sharper lesson underneath that. Pass at one is a claim about reliability, not capability. Report only greedy decoding and you cannot tell a model that got smarter from one that just got luckier more often, and this paper rigorously demonstrates how much that distinction can hide.
[O] Which connects straight back to benchmark contamination debates too, if a benchmark score is really an average-case number, it's vulnerable to exactly the kind of overclaiming this paper is pushing back on.
[G] If I leave you with the paper's own sentence, it's this: current R-L-V-R methods have not fully realized the potential of reinforcement learning to elicit genuinely novel reasoning in language models, and the authors think that's fixable, not fundamental.
[O] Mine is that pass at one going up is still real progress worth shipping, this paper argues about what's underneath the number, not that the number is fake.
[S] And mine is, trust the crossover, question the Qwen-heavy model zoo, and go read the full write-up on the Litsearch site for the exact curves and the table we walked through.
