[O] Here's a strange fact about the smartest AI models money can buy. Feed them a moral dilemma, and the biggest, most capable version of the model is often not the one that reasons about it best.
[S] Not a tie. A loss. In several model families, the smallest, cheapest version outscores its own flagship sibling on the exact same test.
[O] And it gets stranger. Score these same models on math, on code, on the leaderboard everyone stares at, Chatbot Arena, and none of it predicts how well they reason through an ethical dilemma. The correlation is basically zero.
[S] Which either means moral reasoning is a hidden capability nobody's been measuring, or the measurement itself is shakier than the headline chart makes it look. Today's paper argues the first. I want to stress-test that.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where we take one paper and argue about what it actually proves. I'm the optimist.
[S] I'm the skeptic. Today's paper is MoReBench: Evaluating Procedural and Pluralistic Moral Reasoning in Language Models, More than Outcomes, out of a multi-institution team spanning the University of Washington, NYU, Scale AI, Harvard, MIT, and Oxford, accepted at ICLR twenty twenty-six.
[O] To help us get inside it, we've brought in Claire, who's spent real time with this benchmark and the moral-AI literature it builds on. Claire, welcome.
[G] Happy to be here. This is a paper that tries to answer a question most benchmarks dodge, not "what did the model decide," but "how did it get there."
[S] Claire, set up the gap for us. Why do we need a benchmark like this at all?
[G] Because almost every existing eval for AI values grades the outcome, not the process. Benchmarks like ETHICS, or Delphi, or the Moral Machine, present a scenario and check whether the model's final answer matches some target judgment. That works fine when there's a verifiable answer. Math has a number. Code has a passing test. Moral dilemmas don't.
[O] Right, because a well-reasoned answer can land on either side. Should you report a colleague's deception if the deception is protecting sick children? Reasonable people disagree.
[G] Exactly, and the framing here is clever, because it turns that ambiguity from a bug into the whole point. If the conclusion is inherently contestable, you're forced to grade the reasoning that got you there instead of the verdict. Prior process-focused attempts existed, hand-built taxonomies for self-driving-car rationales, philosopher-versus-model comparisons, narrow classifiers trained to spot deductive versus abductive reasoning, but the authors argue those don't scale past a handful of cases.
[S] And the enabling technology is new too, presumably.
[G] Right, reasoning models. When a model emits a visible thinking trace before its final answer, you suddenly have something to grade beyond the conclusion. The bet here is that you can score that trace at scale, if experts write down in advance what a good trace has to contain.
[O] So walk us through what they actually built.
[G] The core artifact is one thousand contextualized moral scenarios, each paired with a hand-written rubric. Altogether that's just over twenty-three thousand criteria, an average of twenty-three per scenario, ranging from twenty up to forty-nine. Scenarios split into two roles the authors expect AI to play in the real world. Fifty-nine percent are "Moral Advisor," where the AI guides a human through a personal dilemma, sourced from an existing dataset called DailyDilemmas. The other forty-one percent are "Moral Agent," where the AI itself has to act autonomously under safety-flavored constraints, sourced from a companion dataset called AIRiskDilemmas. There's also a slice of expert-written cases pulled from ethics literature, debate competitions, and applied-ethics news.
[S] Who's actually writing these twenty-three thousand criteria?
[G] Fifty-three moral philosophy experts, about two-thirds with a doctorate, average age around thirty-nine. Each criterion has to be objective, specific to that exact scenario, and atomic, meaning it grades exactly one thing. Every rubric then gets reviewed by a second expert, so each scenario reflects at least two philosophers' judgment of what good reasoning has to contain. Criteria sort into five dimensions: identifying the relevant considerations, reasoning clearly, reasoning logically by weighing trade-offs, landing on a helpful outcome, and avoiding a harmful one. Each criterion also carries a signed weight from minus three up to plus three, skipping zero, so a criterion can actively penalize a bad move, not just reward a good one.
[O] So scoring a response is basically tallying which weighted boxes it checks.
[G] That's the mechanism. Sum the signed weight of every criterion the trace satisfies, divide by the total possible weight if it had nailed everything. A perfect trace scores one hundred, the exact inverse scores zero. But there's a wrinkle. A chattier model gets more sentences, which means more chances to accidentally tick a box, the same length bias that's dogged a health benchmark called HealthBench. So they report two versions. The plain average is MoReBench-Regular. There's also a length-corrected version, MoReBench-Hard, that rescales the score against a reference length of one thousand characters, so a model gets credit for being efficient, not just exhaustive.
[S] Who grades all this? You can't pay fifty-three philosophers to read every model's output forever.
[G] An LLM judge, and to their credit they meta-evaluate it before trusting it. Two human experts independently labeled criterion-fulfillment on about seven thousand response-criterion pairs, with strong agreement between them, a Cohen's kappa of point seven five. Then they tested a dozen candidate judge models, scoring each one by its worst performance across five slices, three source models crossed with the two roles, deliberately picking the floor instead of the average so no judge could hide bias behind a good overall number. GPT-5 on high reasoning effort came out on top at seventy-seven and a half percent. But it cost over one hundred fifty dollars to run the meta-eval. An open-weight model, GPT-oss at one hundred twenty billion parameters, scored seventy-six percent, nearly as good, for under two dollars, roughly eighty times cheaper. So that's the judge they used for everything else.
[O] That's a big call. A one-point F1 gap standing in for the entire evaluation pipeline.
[G] It is, and we should come back to that. Two more checks matter for whether you should trust the rubrics at all. Discriminatory power: they had experts deliberately write low, medium, and high quality reasoning traces for thirty cases, and the rubric scored them apart in the expected order, a clean statistically significant separation. Robustness: a separate group of experts wrote high-quality traces arguing the opposite conclusion on those same cases, and scored statistically identically to the original high-quality group. So the rubric rewards reasoning quality, not a preferred answer, which is exactly what you want from a process-based grader. And to guard against contamination, only half the one thousand scenarios are public. The rest are held back as a private test set.
[S] And the theory dataset you mentioned earlier?
[G] Right, there's a sibling set, MoReBench-Theory, one hundred fifty scenarios where the rubric grades reasoning strictly inside one of five normative frameworks: Kantian deontology, Benthamite act utilitarianism, Aristotelian virtue ethics, Scanlonian contractualism, and Gauthierian contractarianism. The question there isn't "did the model reason well," it's "can the model reason the way a specific philosophical tradition demands, when you explicitly tell it to."
[O] Okay, results. Give me the headline.
[G] They graded the thinking traces of fourteen frontier reasoning models, spanning GPT-5, Claude, Gemini, DeepSeek, GPT-oss, and Qwen3. The single biggest finding is negative: none of the signals we normally use to rank models predict this.
[S] Start with the scaling story, since that's the one that should unsettle people.
[G] On the regular, uncorrected score, the biggest model in a family is usually not the best. In the GPT-5 and Gemini-2.5 families, the mid-size version wins. In Claude 4, GPT-oss, and Qwen3, the smallest version wins. The authors read this as inverse scaling, bigger models can reason more implicitly inside their weights, so they emit terser visible traces with fewer gradeable steps. Smaller models have to spell more out, which hands them more opportunities to hit a rubric criterion.
[O] And that gets support from the length-corrected version, right? Because if it's really about verbosity—
[G] Exactly, that's the tell. Once you correct for length, the trend partly reverses. The largest models in the GPT-5, Claude, and Qwen3 families move back to the top. Gemini is the one family where that doesn't happen.
[S] What about the cross-benchmark comparison? Does anything predict this?
[G] Nothing. They plot MoReBench against Chatbot Arena, which measures user preference, Humanity's Last Exam, a general reasoning test, AIME, math competition problems, and LiveCodeBench, competitive coding. Pearson's r across all of those comparisons ranges from about minus point two five to plus point two two. Essentially flat. Moral reasoning, as measured here, doesn't track user preference, general reasoning, math, or code.
[O] That's the sentence that should reorganize how people think about eval suites. You cannot infer this capability from any benchmark currently in wide use.
[S] If it's real. Hold that thought. What about where models actually break down?
[G] There's a clean dimension-level table. Averaged across all fourteen models, harmlessness is the one dimension everyone's dependable at, eighty-one percent of criteria satisfied on average, best model at eighty-five and a half. That's a clear fingerprint of safety training. Everything else is weaker and much more spread out. Logical process, actually weighing competing considerations into a justified trade-off, is the worst dimension, forty-eight percent on average. Gemini-2.5-Pro satisfies just under twenty-seven percent of logical-process criteria. The best model on that dimension, a Qwen3 variant, hits sixty-five.
[S] Give me the Gemini example, because "worst at logical reasoning" from the model that wins math and code olympiads is a striking claim.
[G] There's a case in the paper where the scenario involves a student using an AI chess tutor. Gemini-2.5-Pro misses a fairly commonsense causal link, that leaning on the tutor could blunt the student's own thinking and hurt their near-term tournament performance. GPT-5-mini catches exactly that connection. So a model with genuine competition math and competition code credentials doesn't automatically transfer that reasoning skill into a moral context.
[O] There's a personality difference buried in here too, isn't there, not just a raw skill gap?
[G] There is. On the "helpful outcome" dimension, Claude Opus and Sonnet score low, thirty-two and thirty-nine percent, because their traces tend to land on what the authors call a "balanced analysis" or a "nuanced compromise" rather than a concrete recommendation. Qwen3 and DeepSeek, by contrast, hand over actual options and their consequences. The same underlying reasoning quality could, in principle, produce either style, but only one of them satisfies a rubric asking for actionable guidance.
[S] And the theory dataset results?
[G] Models are noticeably better at deontology and utilitarianism, both around sixty-five percent, than at virtue ethics and contractarianism, both under fifty-nine, and the gap holds up statistically once you control for baseline differences between models. The authors' read is that it's a training-paradigm fingerprint, deontological and utilitarian framing shows up a lot in the philosophy literature and, plausibly, in how human feedback for reinforcement learning gets structured and rationalized. Virtue ethics and contractarianism are just less represented in that pipeline.
[O] Alright, let me make the strongest case for why this matters. This is careful benchmark engineering, not a paper with a viral chart and nothing behind it. Inter-annotator agreement at point seven five. A deliberate lowest-of-five-slices metric for judge selection that punishes hidden bias instead of averaging it away. An actual discriminatory-power test and an actual robustness test on the rubrics themselves, both of which came back clean. Half the data held private specifically to blunt contamination. And the finding underneath all that rigor is genuinely new, moral reasoning is not a byproduct of general reasoning capability. You cannot buy it by scaling math and code performance. That should change how the field thinks about what "smart" means.
[S] I'll grant the rigor is unusually high for this kind of paper. My deflationary case has three parts, and they compound. First, the judge. Every score in this paper comes from GPT-oss at one hundred twenty billion parameters, chosen for cost, whose own worst-case F1 against human labels was seventy-six percent. On its hardest slice it disagrees with a human expert roughly one time in four. Spread that across twenty-three criteria per scenario, and a three or four point gap between two models is well inside the noise the judge itself introduces.
[G] That's fair, and it compounds with which dimension is hardest to grade. Logical process is exactly the dimension the paper says models are weakest at, and it's plausibly also the dimension hardest for an LLM judge to score reliably, since it requires understanding whether a trade-off argument actually holds together. Some of "models are bad at logical reasoning" is probably entangled with "the judge is worse at scoring logical reasoning."
[O] Okay, that lands. What's your second point?
[S] The cross-vendor comparison isn't apples to apples, and the paper admits it in a footnote that doesn't carry through to the headline chart. For open models like Qwen3 and DeepSeek, the "thinking trace" being graded is the actual raw chain of thought. For closed models like GPT-5, Claude, and Gemini, it's a provider-generated summary of a hidden trace. A summary is shorter and pre-digested by construction. That interacts directly with both the length correction and the sheer count of gradeable intermediate steps. So when the paper says a model like Qwen3 leads the pack, some meaningful fraction of that could be "raw trace beats compressed summary," not "open model reasons better."
[G] I think that's the single most important caveat in the paper, honestly, and it undercuts the cleanest-sounding result, the scaling story, more than the framing lets on. To their credit, they flag it explicitly rather than hiding it.
[S] Third point, smaller but real. The orthogonality claim, the headline "moral reasoning doesn't correlate with anything else," rests on correlating scores across roughly fourteen models. A Pearson correlation with that few data points has a wide confidence interval. Near-zero at fourteen points is consistent with the true relationship being anywhere from a modest negative correlation to a modest positive one. "No relationship detectable at this sample size" is the honest phrasing. "Orthogonal axis" is the marketing phrasing.
[O] Claire, adjudicate. Score it.
[G] On the rigor of the rubric itself, the agreement number, the discriminatory-power test, the robustness test, I'd score that clearly to the optimist. That part of the paper earns its confidence. On "the specific model rankings are trustworthy down to a few points," the skeptic wins outright, both the judge-noise problem and the summary-versus-raw-trace confound are real and unresolved as published. On the big claim, that moral reasoning is a separate capability axis from scaling and from STEM benchmarks, I'd call it directionally correct but overstated in confidence. Fourteen models is enough to say "we found no evidence these are linked." It's not enough to say "these are orthogonal."
[S] That's the responsible read. I'll take it.
[O] So what would move each of you?
[S] For me, grade closed models on raw chain of thought instead of provider summaries, or drop the cross-vendor claims until someone can. Report judge agreement broken out by dimension, especially logical process. And widen the model set before asserting orthogonality as a settled fact rather than a preliminary finding.
[G] I'd add one thing, going a step beyond what the paper itself argues, some kind of human-performance ceiling per dimension, so a reader can tell whether forty-eight percent on logical process means there's real room to improve, or that even a thoughtful human philosopher wouldn't clear eighty here. Without that anchor the absolute numbers are hard to interpret.
[O] This connects to something bigger than one paper, though. As more evals move toward rubric-based grading for anything that isn't cleanly verifiable, this is basically a case study in what that costs you. You inherit whatever bias and noise your judge model has, and you inherit whatever asymmetry exists in how different vendors expose their reasoning. Anyone building an agentic eval on hidden reasoning traces is going to hit exactly this wall.
[S] Worth saying plainly. This isn't a flaw unique to MoReBench. It's the standard cost of rubric-based, judge-scored evaluation wherever a benchmark can't fall back on a unit test or a ground-truth number. Contamination, judge reliability, it all recurs.
[O] Alright, takeaways. Claire, give us the paper's.
[G] Grading the reasoning instead of the answer turns moral dilemmas from an unscoreable mess into a rich testbed, and once you do that, the results are genuinely surprising. The biggest model in a family is often not the best at it, and performance here doesn't track math, code, or user-preference benchmarks at all. Models are dependable about avoiding harm and noticeably weaker at actually integrating competing considerations into a real trade-off.
