Today it's Large Language Monkeys, Scaling Inference Compute with Repeated Sampling, out of Stanford, Oxford, and Google DeepMind — Bradley Brown, Jordan Juravsky, Ryan Ehrlich, Ronald Clark, Quoc Le, Christopher Ré, and Azalia Mirhoseini, posted in twenty twenty-four.
[S] Joining us is Simone, a researcher who has gone deep on this paper. Simone, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. This is a paper about a very old idea — try more than once — done at a scale and with a rigor nobody had really applied to language models before.
[O] Simone, set the table for us. Why does anyone need a paper to tell us that trying more than once helps?
[G] Because almost nobody had systematically measured it. The last several years of progress came almost entirely from training compute — bigger models, more data, longer runs — a relationship well charted by training scaling laws. Inference got comparatively little attention. Larger models cost more per token, chain-of-thought spends more tokens, but in practice a model usually gets exactly one attempt before someone reads off its answer.
[S] There was some precedent, though, right? This isn't the first time anyone tried sampling more than once.
[G] Right, and the authors are upfront about that. AlphaCode, the competitive-programming system, kept improving all the way up to a million samples per problem. Similar hints had shown up in math and puzzle benchmarks. But nobody had asked the systematic question: how far does this actually extend, across model sizes, families, and post-training, and when does it stop paying off?
[O] So what's the actual recipe here? It sounds almost too simple.
[G] It is simple, deliberately so. For a given problem, sample the model many times independently at a positive temperature, so you get a pile of different candidate solutions. Then apply a verifier — something domain-specific, like unit tests or a proof checker — to pick a final answer out of the pile. That's it. The paper's whole framing is that this decomposes into two separate problems, and conflating them is where people go wrong.
[S] Walk us through that decomposition.
[G] The first is coverage: as you generate more samples, what fraction of problems does at least one sample solve? The second is precision: given the pile, how reliably can you actually pick out a correct one? With unlimited sampling and a model that assigns any nonzero probability to a correct answer, coverage eventually goes to one. That's almost trivial. What's hard, and what the paper spends half its pages on, is precision — finding the needle once it's actually in the haystack.
[O] And coverage, when you do have a way to check automatically, is exactly pass at k. The same metric from the Codex paper.
[G] Right, they borrow the unbiased pass at k estimator directly from Chen and colleagues. Generate a large pool of samples once, count how many are correct, then compute what a smaller draw of size k would have looked like combinatorially, rather than actually re-sampling every time. It's a variance-reduction trick, nothing more, but it's what makes the coverage curves trustworthy.
[S] What did they actually test this on?
[G] Five tasks, chosen specifically to span the verification spectrum. GSM8K and MATH are math word problems, one hundred twenty-eight random test problems each, graded by an oracle that checks the final answer — no way for a model to verify itself at inference time. MiniF2F-MATH is a hundred thirty of those same MATH-style problems formalized into Lean four, checked by an actual proof checker. CodeContests is competitive programming, checked against hidden test cases. And SWE-bench Lite is real GitHub issues, checked by the repository's own unit test suite.
[O] So three of those five have an automatic verifier baked in, and two don't.
[G] Exactly, and that split is the whole design of the paper. MiniF2F, CodeContests, and SWE-bench have verifiers, so coverage there converts directly into a usable success rate. GSM8K and MATH don't, so they become the stress test for whether you can find the correct answer without a referee.
[S] What models are we talking about?
[G] A wide sweep, deliberately. Llama-3 at eight and seventy billion parameters, Gemma at two and seven billion, and the entire Pythia family, eight models from seventy million up to twelve billion. For SWE-bench they use DeepSeek-Coder-V2-Instruct inside an open-source coding agent called Moatless Tools, since that task needs more context than Llama-3 supports. Up to ten thousand samples per problem on math and code; SWE-bench is capped at two hundred fifty attempts per issue, since each one is an expensive multi-turn agent trajectory.
[O] Let's get to the actual numbers, because this is where the paper earns its title.
[G] Across all five tasks, coverage rises smoothly as you add samples, over four orders of magnitude. On a single attempt, GPT-4o beats every open model on every task. But as the sample budget grows, all three weaker models cross over GPT-4o's single-shot number. The most dramatic case is CodeContests with Gemma-2B: coverage goes from a pass at one of zero point zero two percent to a pass at ten thousand of seven point one percent. That's over three hundred times higher.
[S] And on math specifically?
[G] Pythia-160M goes from zero point two seven percent coverage at one sample to fifty-seven percent at ten thousand. That's a model with a hundred sixty million parameters, tiny by current standards, finding a correct answer more than half the time once you let it try enough. There's one clean failure worth naming, though: every single Pythia model scores exactly zero coverage on CodeContests, even at ten thousand samples. The authors' read is that Pythia simply didn't see enough code during pretraining. You cannot sample your way to a capability the base model never acquired.
[O] Now give us the SWE-bench headline properly, because that's the one people are going to remember.
[G] DeepSeek-Coder-V2-Instruct inside Moatless Tools: fifteen point nine percent of issues solved with one attempt, fifty-six percent with two hundred fifty attempts, using the benchmark's own hidden test suite as the verifier. The prior single-attempt state of the art was forty-three percent, from a system called CodeStory Aide that mixes GPT-4o and Claude 3.5 Sonnet. So repeated sampling with a weaker open model clears that by thirteen points. For reference, GPT-4o given a single attempt in that same agent framework only manages twenty-four point six seven percent.
[S] That's expensive, though, isn't it? Two hundred fifty independent multi-turn agent runs per issue.
[G] Not cheap in absolute terms, but the relative cost story is the more interesting finding. Priced at real API rates: five samples from DeepSeek-Coder-V2 solves twenty-nine point six two percent of issues for ten dollars eighty cents total. One sample from GPT-4o solves twenty-four percent for thirty-nine dollars. One sample from Claude 3.5 Sonnet solves twenty-six point seven percent for fifty-one dollars. Sampling the cheap model five times outperforms both, at under a third of the cost.
[O] That's the part that gets me. It's not just "more samples help." It's a genuinely new lever — you can trade a fixed dollar or FLOP budget between model strength and sample count, and depending on the task, the cheap-and-many side of that trade sometimes wins outright.
[S] Does that hold everywhere, though, or just on this one benchmark?
[G] It's task-dependent. Holding total inference FLOPs fixed, the smaller Llama-3-8B-Instruct beats the seventy billion model on MiniF2F, GSM8K, and MATH — more coverage per FLOP from many cheap samples than one expensive one. On CodeContests it flips: the bigger model is almost always the better use of the same budget. No universal answer, it depends on the task.
[O] And the curves aren't just going up, they're predictable, right? That's the scaling-law claim.
[G] That's the second contribution. They model the log of coverage as a power law in the sample count, then exponentiate to predict coverage directly — the same function class the GPT-4 technical report used for training compute. For most model and task pairs this fits tightly; Llama-3-8B-Instruct on MATH fits with a mean error near zero point three percent. Within a model family, the coverage curves on a log-sample axis trace S-curves with a shared slope but different horizontal offsets — a bigger model mostly buys a leftward shift, the same coverage for fewer samples. It's not perfect everywhere: Llama-3-8B-Instruct on MiniF2F-MATH is the paper's worst fit, with nearly ten times the error.
[S] Okay, now the part I actually care about most. What happens when you take away the referee?
[G] This is section four, and it's the paper's most important result, more than the SWE-bench headline. On GSM8K and MATH, the only tasks without an automatic verifier, they test three selection methods: majority vote, a reward model picking the single best sample, and a reward-weighted majority vote. With Llama-3-8B-Instruct on MATH, oracle coverage climbs from eighty-two point nine percent at a hundred samples to ninety-eight point four four percent at ten thousand. All three real selectors plateau around a hundred samples. The best of them goes from forty point five percent to forty-one point four one percent over that same hundred-fold increase.
[O] So the model is generating the right answer far more often as you sample more, it's just that nothing downstream can tell which one it is.
[G] Precisely, and they checked whether that's because the correct answers are actually accidental, lucky guesses with garbage reasoning behind them. They manually graded a hundred five chains of thought from correct GSM8K samples, including ones from problems the model only solves ten percent of the time or less. Over ninety percent were faithful, valid reasoning, not noise. So the signal genuinely exists in there. Majority voting and an eight-billion-parameter reward model called ArmoRM just can't extract it once the correct answer only shows up in a small minority of samples.
[S] Even the verified tasks aren't as clean as the headline numbers make them sound, though.
[G] No, and the paper says so itself. On SWE-bench Lite, eleven point three percent of problems have flaky test suites — tests that don't give a consistent pass or fail on the same candidate solution, sometimes because they depend on unordered Python sets, and some even fail the dataset's own gold solutions. On CodeContests, of the hundred twenty-two problems with Python3 reference solutions, thirty-five of those references fail their own test suite, because the tests are stricter than the problem statement allows. The verifiers themselves aren't perfect referees, in either direction.
[O] Alright, let's argue this properly. My case: this is a genuinely new degree of freedom for getting more out of a fixed model or a fixed budget. You don't need a bigger model to beat a bigger model's single shot, you need a verifier and a sampling budget, and on anything with executable correctness — code, proofs — that's a real, durable, and cheap win. Thirteen points over the prior state of the art on a benchmark of real GitHub issues, at a third of the cost, is not a rounding error.
[S] I'll grant the coverage result is real. What I won't grant is that "exceeds state of the art by thirteen percent" is the honest headline — that's coverage at two hundred fifty attempts against a single attempt from a different system, a cost comparison wearing a capability comparison's clothes. More fundamentally, this only converts into real accuracy when a verifier already exists. Take that away, on the two tasks without one, and a hundred-fold increase in samples buys less than one point of usable performance. That's most of the difficulty of real-world reasoning, where you usually don't have unit tests waiting.
[G] You're both circling the same fact from opposite sides. The paper actually states two separate claims, not one: inference scaling works cleanly wherever verification is already solved — code execution, formal proofs — and it doesn't yet work where it isn't, which today is most open-ended reasoning. One thing neither of you raised: contamination. GSM8K, MATH, and CodeContests plausibly overlap with pretraining data, and at ten thousand samples a rare "correct" generation could be a memorized answer surfacing rather than genuine reasoning. The faithful chain-of-thought audit helps, but the paper runs no dedicated contamination check.
[S] That's exactly the kind of thing that should make the ninety-eight percent coverage number feel less clean than it sounds on first read.
[G] Second, the plateau result is about specific, cheap selectors — plain majority vote and one eight-billion-parameter reward model. The paper shows those two don't scale with sample budget. It does not show that verification in principle can't scale. A stronger or process-level verifier might behave completely differently, and that's precisely the gap later work went after.
[O] Which is a fair caveat, but it cuts my way too — it means the ceiling on the reasoning side might not actually be as low as the plateau makes it look, it just hasn't been solved yet by the tools tested here.
[S] Or it means the paper correctly identified the real bottleneck and left it as an open problem, which is the responsible thing to do rather than overclaiming a fix.
[G] I'd score it that way too. The coverage and cost results are demonstrated cleanly and reproduce across model families. The claim that inference scaling is a general solution to reasoning is not demonstrated, and the authors don't actually make that claim — it's mostly readers of the headline who do.
[O] So where does this land for how people build evals and think about compute today?
[G] It's become foundational. The exact gap this paper diagnosed — coverage racing ahead of what cheap selection can extract — is what the next wave, the reasoning-model line including o1, DeepSeek's R1, and reinforcement learning from verifiable rewards, set out to close, by training models and verifiers together instead of treating sampling and selection as fixed, separate pieces. For evaluation practice, it's a caution: a benchmark number built on an oracle or best-of-N selection is a coverage upper bound, not deployable accuracy, and those numbers can be worlds apart, exactly like here.
[S] And practically, it argues for spending real engineering effort on verifiers before spending it on ever-larger models, at least for tasks where nobody's built a good verifier yet.
[O] Alright, closing thoughts. Simone, what's the one thing to remember from this paper?
[G] That inference compute behaves like a genuine second scaling axis, with its own roughly log-linear law — but the entire payoff is gated by whether you can verify an answer once you have it. That's the paper's real contribution, more than any single percentage.
