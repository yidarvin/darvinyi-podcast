[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a guest scholar take one paper apart at a time. Today's paper is Insights Generator: Systematic Corpus-Level Trace Diagnostics for LLM Agents, out of Scale AI, led by Akshay Manglik and a large coauthor team, posted in 2026. Joining us to dig in is Maya.
[S] It's a paper about the unglamorous but very real problem of debugging AI agents once you have thousands of execution traces sitting in a folder and no clear place to start reading.
[G] Thanks for having me. I've spent a lot of time with this paper, and I think it's genuinely useful infrastructure thinking dressed up as a research paper, which is rarer than it should be.
[S] Maya, set up the problem for us. Why is this hard in a way that isn't just read more logs?
[G] Because reading more logs doesn't scale, and it doesn't catch the failures that matter most. The paper's framing is that evaluation dashboards only show you pass or fail on the final outcome. They don't reveal intermediate behavior, and they never surface a pattern that's spread thin across many traces but is completely real. A failure buried in some narrow slice of inputs never moves the top-line number, so nobody goes looking for it.
[O] And then there's the failures that don't even look like failures.
[G] Right, the paper calls those silent failures. The code runs, nothing crashes, the agent even sounds confident, but underneath it did the wrong thing. Their running example is a trace where an agent solves a problem with the wrong mathematical model entirely. The code executes cleanly, and a cross-checking step meant to catch errors doesn't catch it, because cross-checking only verifies you're internally consistent within your chosen approach. It never asks whether the approach itself is right. The paper finds that pattern in ninety four of one hundred twelve incorrect Python-using traces, which is thirty seven point six percent of the entire corpus.
[S] That's a specific enough number that I believe they actually found it, rather than gesturing at the idea.
[G] That's the whole point of the paper, honestly. It wants every claim like that to come with receipts. They name the target object a trace insight: a grounded, natural-language claim about a pattern across a group of traces, with trace-level evidence attached, not just a vibe.
[O] So what's out there already? Agent debugging isn't a totally unstudied problem.
[G] There's real prior work, but each piece only covers a slice. Some systems replay a single trajectory and ask what would have happened with a different decision at one branch point. Others classify failures against a fixed taxonomy someone wrote in advance, so anything outside that taxonomy is invisible by construction. And some treat the traces as secondary to optimizing the scaffold's code. The paper's claim, which I think holds up, is that nobody was doing iterative, open-ended pattern discovery across an entire corpus at once.
[O] Okay, walk us through how it actually works, Maya. Multi-agent systems for this kind of thing usually mean a swarm of agents doing vaguely the same job. What's different here?
[G] The core design choice is splitting hypothesis discovery from hypothesis validation, and giving each job to a different agent type. There's an Orchestrator, the only agent the user ever sees. It reads a schema of the corpus, decides what to dispatch next, and writes the final report, but it deliberately does none of the heavy analysis itself, so its context stays free for coordinating rather than getting buried in trace text.
[S] And the actual digging happens where?
[G] Two other roles. A Scout agent is optimized for breadth: it samples somewhere between fifty and two hundred traces, summarizes and searches over them, and proposes a batch of testable hypotheses, including hypotheses about omissions, meaning steps the agent should have taken and didn't. Scouts don't validate anything, they just propose. An Investigator agent then takes one hypothesis and validates it against the full corpus, computing statistics and running two or three cohort comparisons on different samples, returning a verdict of confirmed, refuted, or inconclusive, backed by eight to ten or more pieces of evidence, each one a trace id, a verbatim quote, and an explanation of the mechanism visible in that specific trace.
[O] That eight-to-ten evidence minimum is a nice detail. It's basically forcing the agent to show its work at a volume a human reviewer can spot-check.
[G] Exactly, and there's a second design choice underneath it all that I think matters more than the role split: no agent ever loads raw trace content directly into its own context window. Every interaction with the traces goes through callable tools, things like search, feature extraction, and cohort comparison, that run inside a stateful Python layer and hand back only aggregated results. That's what lets this operate over a corpus larger than any model's context window, because the model is never trying to hold the whole thing in its head.
[S] How do they keep the bookkeeping from falling apart across all these parallel dispatches? If the Scout finds something and the Investigator can't find the same trace again, the whole thing is useless.
[G] They link everything through a short trace identifier, a plain string, rather than a UUID, partly for token cost but mostly because models hallucinate UUID-style references far more than short readable ids. There's also a schema cache, per-column statistics, null rates, value distributions, that gets recomputed automatically and injected into every subagent's context the moment it's dispatched, so nobody starts cold.
[O] And underneath all that, what's actually doing the reasoning? Is this one big model doing everything?
[G] No, and this is a cost-engineering detail worth naming. The heavier reasoning roles, Orchestrator, Scout, and Investigator, all run on Claude Opus four point six in the production configuration. But the cheap, high-volume work, per-trace extraction and summarization, runs on Claude Haiku four point five at fifty-way concurrency. That split is presumably a big part of why this is even affordable to run at all.
[S] So the loop itself, at the top level, is just the Orchestrator repeatedly choosing between three moves.
[G] Right. Dispatch a batch of Scouts, dispatch a batch of Investigators on the best current hypotheses, or submit the report. Scout rounds and Investigator rounds can interleave in any order, for as many rounds as the Orchestrator thinks coverage requires, and only when it decides no credible lead is left undispatched does it write the final report.
[S] Let's get to whether it actually works. How do you even score something as fuzzy as whether an insight is good?
[G] That's one of the paper's real contributions, not just a footnote. They cross two questions to get four evaluation settings: who's grading, a calibrated LLM judge or a human expert, and what's being graded, the report as an artifact, or the downstream lift when someone actually acts on it. They ran this on two very different benchmarks: SpreadsheetBench, real spreadsheet manipulation tasks graded by exact cell match, and HLE, Humanity's Last Exam, expert-level academic questions.
[O] And they compared against real competing systems, not straw men.
[G] Four comparison systems, chosen to isolate different variables: a single-agent Claude Code baseline with no special trace tooling, a version of Claude Code using its own subagent dispatch to mimic the same scout-investigator split, a Trace2Skill-style flat fleet of analyst agents, and a Recursive Language Model implementation. On the LLM-judge side, using Claude Opus four point six as judge, coverage of the underlying findings was actually close across the strong systems, eighty seven to ninety eight percent, and this system didn't even lead there. It came in at ninety one percent average, behind the Claude Code subagent version at ninety five and the flat-fleet system at ninety seven.
[S] So on the metric closest to just did you find the things, it's not the best.
[G] Correct, and the paper doesn't hide that. Where it pulls ahead is pairwise quality. Judges shown two reports side by side, positions swapped to control for bias, picked this system's report as more useful seventy seven point nine percent of the time on average, against sixty two point four percent for the Claude Code subagent version and fifty two point six for the flat fleet. The recursive model and the plain single-agent baseline both scored under fifteen percent. On the specific rubric dimensions, it leads on mechanism explanation and on specificity, while being basically tied on raw detection.
[O] So the story so far is everyone finds roughly the same things, but this system explains them better and backs them with sharper evidence.
[G] That's the paper's read, and the ablations support it. Turning off subagent dispatch entirely and doing everything in the Orchestrator drops the win rate from seventy seven point nine percent all the way down to thirty two, a collapse of roughly forty six percentage points. Keeping the parallel dispatch but replacing the typed Scout and Investigator roles with one generic untyped subagent only drops it to sixty seven point five. So most of the value comes from just dispatching parallel subagents at all, and the typed role split adds a smaller, but real, increment on top of that.
[S] Okay, but LLM judges grading LLM outputs makes me nervous. What's the actual downstream number, the one where a human has to act on the report?
[G] This is the headline result, and I think it's the strongest evidence in the paper. Professional software engineers, working on live agents for a living, were randomly handed either this system's report or the Claude Code subagent report, blinded to which, and asked to modify a scaffold to improve its score on two hundred fifty held-out SpreadsheetBench tasks, same tools, same time budget. The unmodified baseline scored twenty seven percent. Engineers given the Claude Code report lifted it to forty three point two percent, a gain of sixteen point two percentage points. Engineers given this system's report lifted it to fifty seven point four percent, a gain of thirty point four points, almost double.
[O] And that's not a fluke of one lucky engineer, right? They stress-tested it.
[G] They did. It's statistically significant at a p value of roughly point zero one six under a Welch's t-test, it holds up under an exact permutation test, and it survives leaving any single one of the twelve participants out of the analysis one at a time. Interestingly, the engineers using this system's report also touched slightly more files on average, four versus three point two, but made smaller net edits, ninety four lines at the median versus one hundred forty nine, which reads like more targeted, less shotgun changes.
[S] Six people per arm is still six people per arm, statistically significant or not.
[G] Fair, and the authors say so themselves. They're upfront that it's one benchmark and a small sample, chosen because the task requires real domain expertise, not because six was the plan.
[O] What about the fully autonomous version, where nothing human is in the loop?
[G] That's a separate experiment, an iterative patcher loop. A coding agent repeatedly patches the scaffold based on a regenerated report each round, run out to five rounds or until it saturates. All three report-equipped systems, this one, the recursive model, and the Claude Code subagent version, converge to a held-out pass rate between eighty one and eighty four percent, up from forty one percent unmodified. The interesting contrast is the control condition with no report at all, which peaks at eighty percent after round one and then reverses to fifty eight by round three.
[S] Why does it reverse?
[G] Because without a report telling it what actually matters, the patcher starts inventing edits from the source code alone. At round two it added an instruction telling the agent to guess a value rather than leave a cell blank. But the benchmark grades by exact match, and plenty of correct answers are supposed to be blank. That one bad instruction accounts for twelve of the thirteen regressions in that round. It's a nice demonstration that the regenerated analysis, not just the patching itself, is what keeps improvement from backsliding.
[O] What happens when you cut out the LLM judge and the intervention study entirely, and just ask human experts to rate report quality directly?
[G] That's where it gets genuinely messy, and it's worth sitting with. Twelve human evaluators scored twenty three insights, split between the two systems, on a one-to-five rubric across correctness, depth, evidence, and actionability. The two systems came out statistically indistinguishable, four point two five versus four point two two overall. This system led on depth and evidence quality, the Claude Code version actually led on correctness, and actionability was a wash.
[S] So the same two reports get graded a landslide by one judge and a coin flip by another.
[G] Exactly. The authors' own explanation is that humans just aren't well equipped to evaluate insights spanning large sets of traces and evidence within a fixed time budget. That may be right. It's also the explanation that's most convenient for the paper's thesis, so I'd hold it a little loosely.
[S] Maya, isn't the humans just weren't good enough judges a pretty convenient thing for the paper to conclude right when the human result disagrees with them?
[G] It's convenient, and I said as much a second ago. But it's not baseless. The human study only gave each evaluator a fixed time budget to read insights citing eight to ten trace quotes apiece, and there's a real argument that thoroughly checking that kind of evidence takes longer than any rating study budgets for. I just don't think the paper gets to have it both ways. Either humans are the right judge of quality, in which case the intervention study is gold, or they're not, in which case you can't wave away the one place they disagree with the LLM judge without also questioning how much weight the intervention study puts on human judgment elsewhere.
[O] Okay, my optimist case. This is the first system that treats trace debugging as a first-class corpus-scale search problem instead of manual spot-checking, and it has the one result that's hardest to fake: blinded engineers, real held-out tasks, thirty point four points of lift, nearly double the next tool. If I'm running an agent team, that number alone is worth trying this.
[S] My skeptic case is that almost every other number in this paper is graded by a judge from the same model family as the system being judged. Claude Opus four point six clusters the gold reference set, Claude Opus four point six scores the pairwise comparisons, and Claude Opus four point six is the model running this system's own Orchestrator, Scout, and Investigator. That's not neutral grading, that's a model that may reward output which looks like its own writing style. And the one time you swap in actual humans to grade quality directly, the big gap disappears.
[G] You're both right about different halves of the evidence, and I think that's the honest read. The intervention study is close to as clean as agent-evaluation research gets right now: real practitioners, blinded, held-out tasks, a result that survives a permutation test and leaving any one participant out. That part of the optimist case holds. But the skeptic's point about judge entanglement is exactly the flaw the paper doesn't fully reconcile. The union-gold reference set that coverage and quality are scored against is built by clustering with the same judge model, and that same model powers this system's own agents. A different-family or human-anchored gold standard would answer this cleanly, and the paper doesn't have one yet.
[O] Okay, but the ablations are graded the same way, and they still show a mechanistic story that doesn't depend on trusting the judge's taste. Turning off subagent dispatch tanks the score by roughly forty six percentage points, no matter whose writing style the judge prefers. That's architecture doing work, not just prose winning a beauty contest.
[S] Sure, the relative ordering between this system's own variants is probably real. What I don't trust is the absolute claim that this system beats the other independent systems, when the referee shares a bloodline with one of the contestants. There's a second crack worth naming too. Under the fully autonomous patcher loop, with no human reading the report, this system ties the recursive-model baseline at roughly the same eighty one to eighty four percent pass rate, while costing around seventy six dollars and forty eight minutes per run against twenty three dollars and twenty minutes for the cheaper system. If a human isn't in the loop to appreciate the extra depth, you're paying more than three times as much for a tie.
[G] That's a real and, I think, underappreciated finding in the paper. The authors frame their contribution as human-in-the-loop utility, and to their credit they say so explicitly rather than claiming the autonomous result as a win. But it does mean the headline thirty point four point number is specifically a claim about pairing this system with a skilled human, not a claim that it's the best system, full stop, for every use case.
[O] Which is a narrower but still real claim. Most engineering orgs debugging an agent today have a human in that loop anyway.

That's a real, statistically significant, blinded result, and I respect that they ran the robustness checks. It's still a thin base to generalize from to harder domains like long-horizon software engineering or open-ended research tasks.
[G] Also fair, and the authors agree with you there. Generalization to harder benchmarks is explicitly left as future work, along with a larger, better-powered replication. So where I land: the architecture is sound, and the ablations give a real, evidenced mechanism for why it helps. The downstream human result is the strongest single piece of evidence in the paper, and I'd trust it. But the size of this system's overall lead over its closest multi-agent rival is probably somewhat inflated by judge-model kinship, and its case for value without a human reading the report is genuinely unresolved.
[O] Stepping back, what does this mean beyond one company's internal tooling?
[G] I think the most transferable idea isn't the specific architecture, it's the definition of a trace insight as a first-class, citable object, evidence attached, confirmed or refuted like a real claim. If that framing catches on, it's directly relevant to anyone doing agent evaluation, because it's a template for turning the eval score went down into an actual explanation you can act on, rather than a shrug.
[S] It's also a pretty pointed case study in judge reliability generally. If a same-family LLM judge and a panel of domain experts can disagree this much on the same twenty-three artifacts, that's a caution flag for a lot of other papers that lean on a single LLM judge and call it done.
[G] Agreed, and the paper is honestly better than average about naming its own limitation there, even if it doesn't fully resolve it.
