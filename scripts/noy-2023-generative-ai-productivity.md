[O] And the stakes of getting this right are unusually high, because writing is exactly the kind of task earlier automation waves left alone.
[G] That's the framing the authors use. The classic automation story, the polarization work from Autor and Dorn, the industrial robots evidence from Acemoglu and Restrepo, is about routine, codifiable tasks getting displaced, hitting middle-skill jobs hardest. Drafting a press release or a delicate client email is about as far from routine and codifiable as knowledge work gets. So the open question was whether a generative model substitutes for that writing, complements it, or something in between, and whether it helps everyone in a job equally or reshapes who's good at it.
[S] Which is a very different question from "can it write a decent paragraph." That's an economics question wearing a language-model costume.
[G] Exactly, and that's why they built a field-style experiment with real incentives instead of just grading outputs against a rubric in isolation.
[O] Walk us through the design, Maya. Who's actually in this study?
[G] Four hundred forty-four experienced, college-educated professionals, recruited through an online panel: marketers, grant writers, consultants, data analysts, HR professionals, managers. Each completed two occupation-specific, incentivized writing tasks — press releases, short reports, analysis plans, delicate workplace emails — designed as twenty to thirty minute assignments meant to resemble real work. Most had done similar tasks before and rated them as realistic.
[S] And the randomization happens between the two tasks.
[G] Right. Everyone does a baseline first task with no AI access. Then participants are randomly assigned: the treatment group gets ChatGPT for the second task, the control group doesn't. Because that assignment is random, any difference in outcomes can be read causally, which is the paper's real advantage over the surveys and observational adoption studies that dominated the public conversation at the time.
[O] How is quality actually measured? That's usually where a study like this gets soft.
[G] Each submission is graded blind by paid evaluators on a one to seven scale, across overall quality, writing quality, content quality, and originality. A subset of tasks used a convex payment scheme, an extra three dollars for landing a grade of six or seven, sharpening the incentive to produce excellent work rather than just finish and collect a flat fee. A supplementary arm let thirty percent of the treatment group revise or replace their first task output with ChatGPT's help, and a two-week follow-up survey checked whether use persisted after the study ended.
[S] Was any of this preregistered, or are we trusting after-the-fact framing?
[G] It was preregistered. Task design, primary outcomes, and the inequality analysis were all specified in advance, which rules out the cherry-picking risk where a researcher tries twenty comparisons and reports the one that worked.
[O] Give us the headline number, Maya.
[G] Take-up was high: ninety-two percent of treated participants signed up for ChatGPT, eighty-one percent actually used it on the second task, and they rated it four point four out of five for usefulness. The productivity result is the one everyone quotes. Average time on the second task dropped by about ten minutes, a thirty-seven percent reduction relative to the control group's twenty-seven minute average, a treatment effect of roughly point eight three standard deviations. Average evaluator grades rose by about point four five standard deviations, with gains spread fairly evenly across overall, writing, content, and originality sub-scores. The peer-reviewed Science version, on a slightly larger sample of four hundred fifty-three, summarizes it more simply: forty percent faster and eighteen percent higher quality.
[S] Before we move on, that take-up number cuts both ways. [G] It does, and the authors are explicit about that. There was still at least a sixty percentage point usage gap between the two arms, but any control-group contamination biases the estimate downward, not upward. So if anything, this is a conservative, lower-bound estimate of ChatGPT's true effect, not an inflated one.
[O] That's an important correction to the instinct that a messy real-world sample must be overstating things.
[S] Fair, I'll take that point. Though I'd still want to know how much of that ten-minute time savings and the point four five standard deviation grade bump is a first-contact novelty effect versus something that holds up on the tenth try. We'll come back to that.
[O] The second finding is the one that really breaks from the usual automation story, though. Tell us about who benefits, Maya.
[G] In the control group, first-task and second-task grades are strongly correlated, a slope of point four nine one — professionals who write well once tend to write well again, a persistent skill gap. Under treatment, that slope falls to point two four eight, a change of about point two four three, with a confidence interval that stays negative throughout. In plain terms, ChatGPT compresses the productivity distribution by lifting initially low-scoring writers more than high-scoring ones. High performers mostly convert access into speed while holding quality flat; lower performers gain more on quality itself.
[S] Is that compression about actual skill, though, or just regression to the mean dressed up as an effect?
[G] Worth separating two things. The paper tested a second heterogeneity axis, participants' self-rated writing skill relative to their other skills, and found no clear pattern there. So the compression tracks initial task performance specifically, not a self-assessed sense of who's a strong writer. That's a useful caveat, but it doesn't fully answer your regression-to-the-mean worry, because initial task performance still conflates skill with effort, task familiarity, and ordinary variance in a single graded sample.
[O] Which is a fair hedge, but the direction of the effect is still the striking part. Most automation technologies widen the gap between strong and weak performers. This one narrows it.
[G] That's the honest headline, yes, with the caveat just noted attached to it.
[S] Let's get into how the work actually changed, because I think this next part is underrated.
[G] ChatGPT restructured how the work got done. Before treatment, participants split their time roughly twenty-five percent brainstorming, fifty percent rough drafting, twenty-five percent editing. After treatment, the share spent rough drafting fell by more than half, while the share spent editing more than doubled — consistent with ChatGPT substituting for the drafting stage specifically, not just speeding up every stage proportionally.
[O] So people aren't using it to think faster, they're using it to skip the blank page.
[G] That's a fair read. And here's a detail that deserves more attention than it usually gets: the paper finds no evidence human editing improved on raw ChatGPT output. Respondents who edited or replaced their response with ChatGPT's output scored no higher than the unedited ChatGPT text graded on its own, even in the convex-incentive group, with real money on the table for improving it.
[S] That's genuinely uncomfortable. Either ChatGPT's output was already near whatever ceiling a one to seven rubric can detect, or professionals aren't adding real editorial value on top of AI drafts, even when paid to try.
[G] Both readings are consistent with the data, and the paper doesn't adjudicate between them. It's worth sitting with, because a rubric graded by a paid evaluator wouldn't necessarily catch a factual error or a client-inappropriate line that human editing is normally supposed to catch.
[O] There's a subjective side to this too, and it moved in an interesting direction. What happened to how people felt about the work, Maya?

More strikingly, concern and excitement about automation both rose at the same time: treated participants were more worried about being replaced, about point two six four standard deviations, and simultaneously more excited about their own capabilities being enhanced, about point three eight nine standard deviations, with overall optimism up around point two.
