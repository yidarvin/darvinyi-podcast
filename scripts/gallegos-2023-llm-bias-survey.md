[O] Sure, but flip that around. Before this paper, nobody could even tell you there were a dozen metrics to check in the first place. Half the field was reinventing the same test under a different name.
[S] That's my whole question for today. Does organizing a mess into three neat taxonomies actually fix the mess, or does it just give the mess a table of contents?
[O] Let's find out.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where we take one paper from the research frontier and argue about what it actually proves. Today it's "Bias and Fairness in Large Language Models: A Survey," by Isabel Gallegos and colleagues, published in Computational Linguistics in twenty twenty-four, and it's already pulled in just under twelve hundred citations.
[S] Which, for a survey paper, is basically a sign the field needed exactly this and didn't have it yet.
[O] Joining us to walk through it is Priya, a researcher who's spent a lot of time in this literature. Priya, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is one of those papers people cite less for a single result and more because it gave a very scattered field a shared map.
[S] So set up the actual gap here, Priya. Algorithmic fairness already has decades of theory behind it. Why doesn't it just apply to a language model?
[G] Because that theory was built for classifiers. Equalized odds, from Hardt, Price, and Srebro, or individual fairness from Dwork and colleagues, all of it assumes your model outputs a label, so you can compute a true positive rate or a false positive rate per group. An LLM doesn't do that. It emits a paragraph. "Biased" might mean the paragraph is toxic, or it stereotypes a group, or it just answers a woman's question differently than a man's, or it's less accurate for one dialect than another. None of the classical machinery has a native way to talk about any of that.
[O] So a separate literature grew up to fill the gap.
[G] It did, and it grew up messily. It traces back to Caliskan, Bryson, and Narayanan's WEAT test, the Word Embedding Association Test, which itself borrowed from a psychologist's Implicit Association Test. From there it fanned out into dozens of embedding tests, masked-token tests, generated-text classifiers, lexicon scans, each introduced on its own, with its own notation, its own tiny benchmark, and often no stated relationship to any of the others.
[S] And the existing surveys just didn't cover it.
[G] Not really. Chang and colleagues had a broad survey of LLM evaluation but say outright it doesn't focus on fairness and bias. Mehrabi and colleagues covered bias and fairness in machine learning back in twenty nineteen, but that's the classical, mostly-classification setting, pre-LLM. Nobody had unified the LLM-specific metrics, the datasets they run on, and the mitigation techniques meant to fix what they find.
[O] Which is a real gap. If two papers both claim to have reduced bias, using different metrics on different datasets, you genuinely can't tell if either one worked, or if they're even measuring the same thing.
[S] Okay, so how do they actually organize it? What's the unifying idea?
[G] The clever move is that they don't organize by which bias a technique targets, gender, race, religion, and so on. That's what the field was already doing, and it's part of why it fragmented. Instead they organize everything by what level of access a practitioner actually has to the model, because that determines which techniques you can even use.
[O] Walk us through it.
[G] There are three taxonomies. The first is metrics, and it splits into three families by what data structure they operate on. Embedding-based metrics need access to the model's internal vector representations, that's the WEAT lineage and its descendants, SEAT and CEAT, which adapt the same idea to sentence embeddings. Probability-based metrics need access to token probabilities but not necessarily embeddings, things like masked-token tests, where you compare the model's fill-in-the-blank probability for "the man was known for" versus "the woman was known for." And generated-text-based metrics are the only family that works against a pure black-box API that just returns text: distribution comparisons, auxiliary classifiers, or scanning the output against a lexicon.
[S] So the taxonomy is really answering, "what can I even measure, given how I'm allowed to talk to this model."
[G] Exactly, and that's why it generalizes past any one bias axis. The second taxonomy does the same thing for datasets, twenty-three of them, split into counterfactual inputs, where you compare sentence pairs with a social group swapped, and prompts, where the model produces free text. The sizes span an enormous range, from just over a hundred examples up to a dataset called Bias NLI, with five point seven million sentence pairs.
[O] What does the third taxonomy cover?
[G] Mitigation, organized by pipeline stage, because the stage determines what access you need. Pre-processing changes the training data or the prompt before anything happens, data augmentation, filtering, instruction tuning. In-training needs gradient access, so you can modify the loss function or the architecture itself. Intra-processing works on an already-trained model without retraining it, modifying the decoding strategy, for instance. And post-processing only touches the output text after generation, which is your only option if all you have is a closed API.
[S] That last point matters more than it sounds like. If you're a practitioner calling a model through an API, three of those four categories are simply not available to you.
[G] Right, and the paper makes that explicit. The taxonomy isn't just descriptive, it's telling you which shelf of the toolbox you're even allowed to open.
[O] I like that it also forces precision about what "bias" means in the first place, instead of just waving at the word. They define nine specific harms.
[G] That's Table one. Representational harms, like stereotyping, derogatory language, erasure, exclusionary norms, misrepresentation, disparate system performance, and toxicity, and allocational harms, direct and indirect discrimination. The example they give for disparate system performance is African-American English getting misclassified as not English far more often than Standard American English. That's not a vibe, that's a specific, nameable failure mode.
[O] Since this is a survey, the "results" section is really just the shape of what they found. What's actually in these tables?
[G] Beyond the metric and dataset catalogs, there are five new fairness desiderata the authors propose specifically for open-ended generation, because classical definitions like equalized odds only work cleanly for classification. Fairness through unawareness just means the model doesn't explicitly use the social group. Invariance means swapping the social group in a prompt shouldn't change the output. Equal social group associations and equal neutral associations are about probabilities balancing in both directions. And replicated distributions means the demographic term frequencies in what the model generates should match a reference distribution, not just avoid any single flagged word.
[S] Can you make invariance concrete, Priya? That's the one I keep hearing cited.
[G] There's a clean example in the paper. Ask, "if I am a male data engineer, what should I focus on," and then ask the identical question with "female" substituted. If the model's advice changes based on that one word, invariance is violated. Compare that to a coreference completion, where swapping a pronoun in a sentence doesn't change the model's answer, that one's compliant. Same substitution mechanic, opposite outcomes.
[O] And the mitigation catalog, how big is it really?
[G] Thirteen named subtypes across the four stages, plus a separate table that unifies all the loss functions and regularization terms different in-training papers proposed, so a distance-based embedding regularizer and a mutual-information-based one get read as two instances of the same design space instead of two unrelated tricks. That's genuinely useful, the kind of thing that saves a new researcher months of reading disconnected papers.
[S] I'll grant that's a real contribution. My question is whether sorting the furniture is the same thing as knowing which chair is comfortable. Does the taxonomy tell you which metric, or which mitigation, actually works?
[G] No, and it doesn't claim to. That's explicitly not what this paper is for. It's a map of what exists, formalized with one shared notation, not a meta-analysis ranking effectiveness.
[O] Okay, that's my opening for the optimist case, then, give me the floor. This paper does something genuinely rare for a survey. it doesn't treat LLM bias as a green field. It explicitly imports group fairness from Hardt, Price, and Srebro, individual fairness from Dwork and colleagues, and then carefully explains where that machinery stops working for free text. That's the right move, inherit the theory where it still applies, extend it where it doesn't. And WEAT is the load-bearing bridge for the whole embedding family, it traces straight back through the Implicit Association Test. This isn't a survey that invented a vocabulary out of nothing, it's genuinely lineage-aware.
[S] I'll give you that the lineage work is real scholarship, not a citation dump. But here's my deflationary case, and it comes straight from the paper's own section six, not from me being cynical. The authors admit, in their own words, that "several metrics are inconsistent with one another." They admit that mitigation papers "often claim to successfully debias a model if any metric demonstrates a decrease in bias," that's a cherry-picking risk baked into the literature the taxonomy just organized, and organizing it doesn't make it go away. And they admit that most of this work leverages Perspective API for detecting toxicity "despite the known flaws." So my question is: if the underlying measurements don't agree with each other, what did filing them into a clean three-way taxonomy buy us, beyond a better index?
[G] Both points are fair, and I'd adjudicate it this way. The unification is real at the level of vocabulary and access pattern, you can now look at any new metric and immediately know which family it belongs to and what it requires. That's not nothing, comparing incommensurable numbers across incompatible setups was exactly the problem before this paper. But the paper is honest that it stops there. It doesn't resolve, and doesn't claim to resolve, whether a positive score on, say, a masked-token metric tells you anything about real-world harm. That's a genuinely open validity question, and the authors flag it themselves in section six rather than papering over it.
[O] So score that one a draw with an asterisk, the map is real, the territory underneath is still contested.
[S] Second thing I want to press on: the whole catalog is timestamped. Every flagship dataset they lean on, StereoSet, CrowS-Pairs, BOLD, BBQ, is built around base models or single-turn completions. None of that was designed to catch bias that hides behind a refusal, or shows up as sycophancy, or gets shaped by a persona in a multi-turn chat assistant. That's exactly the deployment surface that matters most right now.
[G] That's accurate, and it isn't really the authors' fault, the field hadn't caught up yet when this was written. But it does mean a reader today should treat this specifically as a catalog of the pre-chat-assistant era of bias evaluation, not a live picture of what would catch bias in a modern, heavily fine-tuned chat model.
[O] One more point for the optimist side, though, and it's in section six, not something I'm inventing. the authors themselves write that framing bias as something you measure objectively "ignores the assumptions made in the operationalization of the measurement tool," and that a benchmark can "foster complacency when the benchmark is satisfied but the underlying power imbalance remains unaddressed." That's not a paper being naive about its own limits, that's about as self-aware as a survey gets.
[S] Agreed, and that's actually the strongest part of the whole paper to me, not the taxonomy itself, but section six admitting the taxonomy's limits. A weaker paper would have stopped at the tables.
[G] There's one more piece worth putting on the record, because it's a specific and slightly surprising omission. Kleinberg, Mullainathan, and Raghavan's impossibility result, that you generally can't satisfy calibration and both balance conditions at once, never shows up in the reference list, even though Hardt's fairness definitions do. Given how central that result is to knowing that fairness criteria can trade off against each other rather than simply being unmet, its absence is a real gap, not just a stylistic one.
[O] Zooming out, does any of this connect to evaluation practice more broadly, beyond bias specifically?
[G] Very directly. The metric-versus-real-world-harm gap they're describing is the same construct-validity problem that shows up anywhere a proxy metric stands in for something you actually care about, a benchmark score standing in for reasoning ability, a judge model's rating standing in for response quality. The lesson from this paper is that sorting your metrics into a clean taxonomy tells you what each one requires and assumes, but it doesn't tell you whether any of them track the thing you actually want to reduce. That gap doesn't close just because the tables got tidier.
[S] Which is a good practical takeaway even outside bias work. know exactly what your metric's access requirements are, and separately, whether anyone's shown it correlates with the thing you actually care about. Those are two different questions, and a lot of eval papers only answer the first one.
[G] My takeaway from the paper itself: it gives a fragmented field one shared vocabulary, organized by what a practitioner can actually access, but its own section six is honest that the underlying metrics still disagree with each other more than the tidy structure suggests.
[O] Mine is that this is exactly the kind of unglamorous work a maturing field needs. you can't fix what you can't even compare, and now you can compare.
