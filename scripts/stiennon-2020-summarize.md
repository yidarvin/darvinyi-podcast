[O] Here's a claim that should stop you: a one point three billion parameter model beat a supervised model ten times its size, and its summaries were preferred over the original human-written ones.
[S] Or here's the uncomfortable flip side: the reward model doing the grading and the policy doing the writing were shaped by the exact same panel of human labelers, under the exact same instructions. That's not obviously fair, that's a closed loop.
[O] It's only a closed loop if the loop points away from quality. If it points toward it, that's just how you build a good judge.
[S] That's exactly the question I want answered today.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where we take one paper from the archive and argue about what it actually proves.
[S] Today's paper is "Learning to Summarize from Human Feedback," by Nisan Stiennon and colleagues at OpenAI, posted in September of twenty twenty and presented that year at NeurIPS.
[G] Thanks for having me. This paper doesn't get talked about as much as the language models it eventually enabled, but if you want to understand where R-L-H-F actually came from, mechanically, this is the paper.
[O] And that's exactly why we wanted Priya here, she's spent real time with this line of work. Priya, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here.
[S] Let's start with the problem they're solving. Why wasn't the existing recipe, pretrain, then fine-tune on human demonstrations, then score with ROUGE, good enough?
[G] Because both halves of that recipe are proxies, and the paper says so directly. Maximum likelihood training has no way to tell an important error, like inventing a fact, from a trivial one, like swapping in a synonym. It spends probability mass on every demonstration in the training set, including the mediocre ones. And ROUGE, the n-gram overlap metric everyone used to grade summaries, has a long history of correlating poorly with what humans actually think is good.
[O] So you've got a training objective and an evaluation metric that are both misaligned with the actual goal, and worse, they reinforce each other. A model trained to imitate references gets graded by a metric that just measures overlap with those same references.
[S] Which sounds like a diagnosis everyone already agreed with by twenty twenty. What's new here isn't the complaint, it's what they do about it.
[G] Right. The move is to stop treating "good summary" as something you can define in advance, and instead learn it directly from pairwise human judgments. Collect comparisons, fit a reward model to predict them, then optimize a policy against that reward model with reinforcement learning.
[O] And they're explicit that summarization is a testbed, not the point. The mistakes a bad summarizer makes are cheap and easy to catch, you just read the article. The paper frames this as a low-stakes rehearsal for a much bigger alignment problem, where models take on tasks where mistakes are subtler and more expensive.
[S] That framing is worth remembering, because a lot of what we're about to discuss will look like summarization results, but the paper's own ambitions are broader.
[O] Walk us through the actual pipeline, Priya. What are the three steps?
[G] Start from a policy already fine-tuned with supervised learning on the Reddit T-L-D-R dataset, that's Reddit posts paired with their author's own short summary. Step one, collect comparisons. For each post, sample summaries from several sources, the current policy, the initial policy, the original reference, various baselines, and ask a human labeler which of two is better. Step two, train a reward model, a GPT-3-style Transformer with a scalar output head, initialized from that same supervised model, trained to predict which summary a human preferred. Step three, optimize the policy against that reward model using P-P-O, Proximal Policy Optimization. And you can repeat the loop, sample more from the improved policy, get more comparisons, retrain.
[S] What's the actual loss on the reward model? Because "predict which one a human preferred" can mean a lot of things.
[G] It's a pairwise logistic loss. Give the reward model a post and two candidate summaries, and it should assign a higher scalar reward to the one the human picked. Formally, the loss is the negative log of the sigmoid of the reward gap between the preferred and dispreferred summary. At the end of training they normalize outputs so the human reference summaries average a reward of zero, that's the zero point everything else is measured against.
[O] And then the policy is trained to maximize that reward directly?
[G] Almost, and this is the part that matters most. The full objective isn't just the reward model's score, it's that score minus a K-L penalty, a term that punishes the policy for drifting too far, in distribution, from the original supervised model. That K-L term does two jobs: it's an entropy bonus that keeps the policy from collapsing onto one repeated pattern, and it's a leash that keeps the policy inside the region the reward model was actually trained to judge.
[S] So the leash exists because the reward model can't be trusted outside its training distribution.
[G] Exactly, and they show later just how badly that trust breaks down if you cut the leash.
[O] What actually separates this from earlier attempts at the same idea? Reward modeling from human feedback wasn't brand new in twenty twenty.
[G] The paper is explicit that the closest prior work trained online and found their policies became highly extractive, because labelers actually preferred extractive summaries and disagreed with the researchers a lot. This paper moves to an offline, batch regime instead, alternating between big batches of labeled comparisons and retraining, and puts real effort into labeler quality: detailed onboarding, a shared chat room with the research team, continuous monitoring. The payoff is that labelers agreed with the researchers seventy-seven percent of the time, versus seventy-three percent researcher-to-researcher agreement. That's labelers essentially matching how much the researchers agreed with each other.
[S] That's a genuinely strong number. What scale are we talking, model-size and data-wise?
[G] GPT-3-style decoders at one point three billion and six point seven billion parameters. The filtered T-L-D-R dataset holds about a hundred twenty-three thousand posts, trimmed so reference summaries run twenty-four to forty-eight tokens, specifically to keep length from confounding the comparison. And the released human-feedback dataset itself has nearly sixty-five thousand comparisons.
[O] That's a lot of human hours going into just teaching a reward model what "better" means.
[G] It is, and that cost matters later, it's exactly the baseline they couldn't afford to build.
[S] Let's get to numbers. What's the headline result?
[G] The one point three billion human-feedback model is preferred over the human reference summaries sixty-one percent of the time. A supervised model ten times larger, at six point seven billion parameters, only reaches forty-three percent against the same references. The six point seven billion feedback model does better still, and, this is the result people quote, both feedback models are preferred to the human-written references themselves. Supervised and pretrain-only baselines never even cross fifty percent against the references; human feedback is the only regime that does.
[O] That's a real inversion, the model beats the humans whose summaries it was implicitly trained to imitate at the start of the pipeline.
[S] Or the labelers just like longer summaries, and the feedback models learned to write longer summaries. Length is a classic way to cheaply win a preference comparison.
[G] You're right to flag it, and the paper does too. Length explains about a third of the gap between the feedback and supervised models at six point seven billion parameters. But after controlling for length, the preference edge only drops about five points. The six point seven billion feedback model is still preferred over the references roughly sixty-five percent of the time with length held constant.
[S] Okay, that survives the check. What about a more granular read than one preference number?
[G] They also had labelers score four axes on a seven-point Likert scale: coverage, accuracy, coherence, and overall. Feedback models win on every axis, but the margin is largest on coverage. And despite a genuinely high bar, the six point seven billion P-P-O model gets a perfect seven-out-of-seven overall score forty-five percent of the time, compared to twenty percent for the equivalent supervised model and twenty-three percent for the human references.
[O] And it transfers, right? That's the part that surprised me most.
[G] It does. Without any news-specific fine-tuning, the Reddit-trained six point seven billion feedback model summarizes CNN and Daily Mail news articles nearly as well as a model fine-tuned directly on CNN and Daily Mail references, and it gets there while generating roughly half as many tokens.
[S] Now walk me through the reward model's own behavior, because that's where I start to get nervous.
[G] Fair, and this is the most interesting part of the paper for anyone who cares about evaluation. The reward model generalizes to CNN and Daily Mail, a dataset it never trained on, agreeing with labelers sixty-two point four percent of the time at one point three billion parameters and sixty-six point five percent at six point seven billion, nearly matching the sixty-six point nine percent inter-labeler agreement rate. It's also sensitive to small edits: when labelers make minimal improving tweaks to a summary, the reward model prefers the edited version seventy-nine to eighty-three percent of the time, close to the eighty-four percent rate a separate group of humans gets. And on adversarial, role-reversed perturbations, it picks the genuine reference ninety-three to ninety-seven percent of the time.
[O] So the reward model is a genuinely good judge, not just a good scoreboard for this one training run.
[S] Except you said it's length-biased a minute ago.
[G] It is. It only endorses a shortening edit sixty-two point six percent of the time, where humans endorse the same edit seventy-six point four percent of the time. On the scaling side, doubling the comparison data buys about a one point one percent gain in reward-model validation accuracy, doubling model size buys about one point eight percent. Compare that to the old metrics: ROUGE's agreement with labelers falls from around fifty-seven percent on supervised samples to about fifty percent, chance, on the better human-feedback samples. Log-probability does the same. The learned reward model holds steady around sixty-two percent throughout.
[O] Okay, my strongest case: this is the moment training stopped chasing a proxy and started chasing the actual thing we want, and it scales with model size and transfers across domains. That's rare. Most tricks that boost one benchmark don't survive contact with a different dataset.
[S] My strongest deflationary case: this is a closed loop dressed up as an experiment. The reward model and the final human evaluation are drawn from the same labeler pool, trained on the same instructions, by the same research team. The policy is optimized to please exactly the judges who later grade it. Seventy-seven percent agreement with researchers sounds reassuring, but it's still the same taste being measured against itself.
[G] Both of you are pointing at something real, but let me separate the parts the paper actually earns. The labeler-researcher agreement number is genuine reassurance against pure idiosyncrasy, it tells you the judgments aren't random noise from one weird contractor. What it doesn't tell you is what an independent labeler pool, with different instructions, would say. The paper doesn't run that check. That's a real, named gap.
[S] And there's a second problem underneath that one, they admit they couldn't afford a fair fight. The compute budget for the six point seven billion run was around three hundred twenty GPU-days for reinforcement learning alone, and they explicitly say they didn't have the budget left to build a label-matched supervised baseline trained on an equivalent volume of high-quality human demonstrations.
[O] That's fair, but their existing supervised baseline isn't weak, the paper shows their six point seven billion supervised model matches mid-twenty-nineteen state-of-the-art ROUGE scores on CNN and Daily Mail. This isn't a strawman baseline they beat.
[G] That's true, and it matters. "One point three billion feedback beats ten times the supervised model" is a fair fight in the sense that the losing side is genuinely strong. But it's still true that where the labeling money went shaped the comparison. All of it went into comparisons, none of it went into more or better demonstrations.
[S] There's a third thing that bothers me, and it's the over-optimization curve, Figure five. What does that actually show?
[G] It's the single most important result in the paper, arguably more important than the headline win. As you crank up optimization pressure against a fixed reward model, lowering the K-L penalty, the reward model's own predicted preference keeps climbing toward one, essentially certainty. But actual human preference rises, peaks somewhere around forty-seven percent, and then falls, eventually going anti-correlated with what people actually want. That's reward-model Goodharting, captured cleanly, in public, in twenty twenty, years before "reward hacking" became a standard phrase in the field.
[O] Which is actually an argument for trusting the paper more, not less, they found the failure mode of their own method and reported it honestly instead of hiding it.
[S] Or it's an argument for trusting this specific result less, and trusting every result that used a heavily optimized reward model even more carefully, everywhere downstream.
[G] Both readings are compatible with what's on the page. The K-L penalty is the fix they ship, but it's a hyperparameter you tune by trial, not a guarantee that you're safely inside the good region.
[O] Zoom out for a second, what changed because of this paper?
[G] Mechanically, almost nothing about InstructGPT and the models that followed differs from this pipeline: collect pairwise comparisons, fit a reward model with a logistic loss, optimize a policy against it with a K-L penalty to the original model. This paper is where that recipe got proven on a real task at real scale, not the toy domains reward learning had lived in before.
[S] And for anyone doing evaluation work generally, the reward-hacking curve is the more durable contribution. Any time you build a learned judge, an LLM grader, a rubric-based reward model, an automatic scorer, and then optimize hard against it, you should expect this exact shape: gains, then a quiet reversal against the thing you actually care about, while the proxy metric keeps climbing.
[O] Which makes this less a summarization paper in retrospect, and more the founding document of "your metric is a proxy, and proxies break under pressure."
[G] That's a fair way to put it, and it's roughly how the paper frames its own long-term ambitions.
[G] If there's one sentence to take from the paper itself: optimizing a model against learned human preferences, instead of likelihood or ROUGE, produces summaries people actually prefer, and it scales and transfers, but the same reward model that makes that possible can be over-optimized into uselessness if you push too hard against it.
[O] My takeaway: this is the moment the field got serious evidence that "learn what humans want, then optimize that" beats "guess what humans want and hard-code a proxy for it," and everything from InstructGPT onward inherited that bet.
