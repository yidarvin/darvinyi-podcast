[O] On a brand new benchmark of real, unscripted, multi-turn conversations, the best model on earth -- Gemini three Pro Preview, in its thinking mode -- clears just fifty four point six five percent of the tasks.
[S] And here's the number that worries me more. That fifty four point six five percent is graded from a text transcript of what the model said, not from the audio itself. we might be measuring reading comprehension wearing a microphone costume.
[O] Which is exactly why I wanted to bring in someone who has actually read this thing cover to cover.
[S] Welcome to Litsearch Audio. Today's paper is Audio MultiChallenge, a multi-turn evaluation of spoken dialogue systems on natural human interaction, out of Scale AI -- Gosai, Vuong, and colleagues. Joining us is Eleanor, who has spent real time inside this dataset.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is one of those papers where the headline number is almost the least interesting part -- the generation pipeline and the failure analysis are where the real substance is.
[O] Let's set the stage. Eleanor, what gap is this paper actually going after?
[G] The industry is moving from cascaded voice pipelines -- speech recognition into a text model into speech synthesis -- toward end-to-end systems that map audio straight to audio, or audio straight to a response, over shared latents. That should cut latency and stop errors from compounding across three separate stages. But the benchmarks certifying these systems haven't kept pace. VoiceBench, AudioBench, Big Bench Audio, MMAU-Pro, MMAR -- they're almost all single-turn, and mostly built on synthetic or carefully read speech.
[S] Which is a strange thing to certify a conversational assistant on, since nobody talks to Siri in one clean, scripted sentence.
[G] Exactly the authors' point. There's a prior long-form audio line -- BLAB, AudioMarathon -- that stretches the clip length, but even those treat one recording as a single comprehension question. And there's exactly one prior multi-turn speech effort, MTalk-Bench, and it tops out at two to three scripted turns.
[O] So the actual behavior people use a voice assistant for -- an extended, spontaneous back and forth -- is basically untested.
[G] Right. And the paper leans on a text-only finding to motivate this: strong single-turn performance on text LLMs doesn't predict robust multi-turn behavior. The authors argue that gap should be worse in audio, because speech is a continuous, high-frame-rate stream that eats context fast, which makes long-horizon state tracking harder than it is in text.
[S] Okay, but "probably worse in audio" is a hypothesis until you build the benchmark and show it.
[G] Which is what they did. They extend an existing text framework called MultiChallenge -- Inference Memory, Instruction Retention, Self Coherence -- into audio, and they add a genuinely new fourth axis called Voice Editing. And critically, they didn't just take that text dataset and run it through text-to-speech. They went and recorded real humans.
[O] Why does that distinction matter so much? Text-to-speech audio is still audio.
[G] Because TTS audio is clean. It doesn't have the disfluencies, the backchannels, the cross-talk, the accents, the "let's make it four yuccas, hmm, no, six" mid-sentence correction that real speech has. If you synthesize your test set from a script, you've engineered away the exact behavior you're trying to measure.
[S] That's a real methodological point in their favor. Let's get into the four axes, because "Voice Editing" is the one I hadn't seen before.
[G] Inference Memory is the classic one -- recall a detail from three turns back and use it, like remembering a stated allergy when the user later asks for a recipe. The audio-native twist is what they call Audio-Cue Gated memory, where the fact you need isn't in the words at all -- it's in the acoustic environment. Their example is heavy rain audible in turn one, silently constraining an outfit recommendation two turns later.
[O] So the model has to actually listen, not just transcribe and reason.
[G] Right, and that's the whole point of testing it end-to-end rather than through a transcript. Instruction Retention is the second axis -- following a constraint across the whole conversation, but here they push it into stacked and conditional rules, like "only switch to bullet points after I say a specific phrase." Self Coherence is the third -- does the model hold a correct earlier answer instead of caving the moment a user pushes back on it. And Voice Editing, the new one, is about spoken self-correction. In text you backspace before you hit send. In speech, the correction is audible, and the model has to filter out what was retracted and figure out the user's actual final intent.
[S] How do you even generate adversarial data for that at scale? You can't just prompt an LLM to "act confused."
[G] That's the clever part, and it's a two-stage hybrid pipeline. Stage one is fully synthetic: a Planner agent -- they use OpenAI's o3 -- picks an axis, a topic, and a persona, then devises a strategy to break a target audio model. A Tester agent, GPT-4o, writes the user's turns as text, those get converted to speech with gpt-4o-mini-tts, and fed to a target model, either GPT-4o Audio Preview or Gemini 2.5 Pro. If the target survives, the Planner escalates the strategy and tries again, until it finds a failure. Then it distills the winning trick into what they call a human-friendly blueprint.
[O] So the agents do the reconnaissance, and find the actual weak points.
[G] Exactly, and about sixty five percent of the final conversations trace back to one of these blueprints. But stage two is where the real data comes from -- a human contributor takes that blueprint only as loose guidance, not a script. They rewrite it in their own words, improvise, and record real audio against a live audio model until they personally induce a failure, within three to eight turns.
[S] So the blueprint tells them where the fault line probably is, but the actual conversation is unscripted.
[G] Right, and once they land the failure, the contributor writes the rubric themselves -- a set of atomic, binary, instance-specific criteria that decompose the ideal response into checkable pieces, instead of one vague reference answer. Then a three-stage quality process audits both the rubrics -- are they mutually exclusive, self-contained, accurate, atomic -- and the conversation itself, for audio quality, whether it's actually testing the assigned axis, and whether it feels contrived.
[O] What did that process actually produce, in numbers?
[G] Four hundred fifty two multi-turn conversations, three to eight turns each, from forty seven distinct speakers, just under fifteen hours of raw user audio at 48 kilohertz, with no post-processing. That's one thousand seven hundred twelve rubrics total, split across the axes -- Inference Memory one hundred thirty two conversations, Instruction Retention one hundred twenty, Self Coherence one hundred seventeen, and Voice Editing eighty three.
[S] And how do you actually score a model against that, given it's not a single right answer?
[G] Fixed-context evaluation. Every model gets the identical pre-seeded conversation history -- user turns as base64 audio, prior assistant turns as text -- and is scored only on its response to the final turn. They deliberately avoid the alternative, where a model responds turn by turn and later pre-recorded turns depend on how it answered earlier, because one slightly-off early response can invalidate everything downstream. Then an LLM judge -- o4-mini, chosen after comparing several candidates -- reads the response transcript and checks it against the human-written rubrics.
[O] And that gives you two numbers.
[G] Average Pass Rate, the strict one -- a task only counts as a pass if every single rubric for it is satisfied -- and Average Rubric Score, the softer one, the mean fraction of rubrics satisfied per task. APR is the headline metric.
[S] Before we get to results, how good is that judge, actually? An LLM grading an LLM off a rubric is exactly the kind of thing that can quietly drift.
[G] They measured it directly. Against human graders on the full set of rubric judgments, o4-mini gets a Cohen's kappa of point eight seven three and a macro F one of point nine three seven -- they report that as about ninety three percent agreement. And notably, they excluded any of the audio models under evaluation from acting as judges, specifically to avoid self-enhancement bias.
[O] That's a legitimately careful validation step. Let's get to the actual scores, because I want to know if fifty four percent is impressive or embarrassing.
[G] Both, depending on what you compare it to. Gemini 3 Pro Preview, thinking mode, leads at fifty four point six five percent APR, eighty two point five four percent ARS. Gemini 2.5 Pro thinking is next at forty six point nine percent, then 2.5 Flash thinking at forty point zero four percent. After that there's a real cliff. The best non-Gemini systems are Voxtral Small, at twenty six point three three percent, GPT-4o Audio Preview in text mode at twenty five point four four percent, Qwen 3 Omni at twenty four point three four percent, and GPT Realtime in text mode at twenty three point four five percent.
[S] And the open-source speech-to-speech models?
[G] Rough. LFM2 Audio, at one and a half billion parameters, bottoms out at nine point two nine percent. Per axis, Voice Editing is the hardest for everyone, averaging seventeen point nine nine percent, with Inference Memory close behind at twenty one point five five percent.
[O] Those are the two audio-native additions, right? The ones that didn't exist in the original text benchmark.
[G] Exactly, and that's the paper's central empirical claim -- the axes they built specifically to require genuine audio understanding are the ones nothing can do well.
[S] Walk me through the audio-cue result specifically, because that's the one I find most interesting.
[G] On the forty two tasks where the fact to recall lives only in the acoustic signal -- tone, background sound, never the words -- rubric compliance falls thirty six point five percent relative compared to plain semantic memory, averaged across models. And qualitatively, the paper says a lot of that failure is perceptual, not memory-related -- models often can't reliably hear the cue in the first place, before retention even becomes the issue.
[O] So it's not that they forget the cat meowing three turns back. It's that they never really registered it.
[G] That's their read, yes. Self Coherence tells a related but distinct story. Turn count barely predicts anything -- these conversations are short relative to a modern context window. But cumulative audio duration does predict it: Self Coherence APR drops from thirty three point three percent under a minute of audio, to twenty percent at three to five minutes, to thirteen point three percent beyond five minutes -- though that last bin only has three samples, so treat it as suggestive.
[S] And Voice Editing?
[G] Collapses even faster with duration. Seventeen point five percent in the three-to-five-minute bin, down to one point two percent and then zero percent beyond that -- though again, those bins are tiny, four samples and two samples.
[O] There's also a modality result I want you to explain, because it surprised me. Models get worse when you actually make them talk?
[G] Yes -- what they call the modality gap. For models that support both text and audio output, the same systems average nineteen point three six percent APR when outputting text, and sixteen point seven six percent when outputting audio. Emitting speech instead of a transcript costs real accuracy, on top of everything else.
[S] Which already tells you something about how "solved" audio output is, compared to audio input.
[G] Right, and they cite that as consistent with other recent work -- the post-training for speech output still lags speech understanding.
[O] What about the two ablations -- text-to-speech versus real human audio, and cascaded versus end-to-end?
[G] Those are the two cleanest experiments in the paper. First: they synthesize a TTS version of the same human transcripts, using Whisper to transcribe and gpt-4o-mini-tts to re-speak them, on a filtered subset that drops audio-cue and interruption tasks. Models configured for text output score seven point five percent relatively better on the clean TTS audio than on the real human audio. Models configured for audio output actually do about two point five percent worse on TTS.
[S] So the real, messy human speech is measurably harder than a synthesized read-through of the same words -- which is exactly the argument for why you'd bother collecting real audio in the first place.
[G] Precisely, and Voice Editing shows the largest swing of any axis in that ablation, because ASR-then-TTS smooths out exactly the hesitations and half-retracted phrases that axis is designed to test.
[O] And the cascaded comparison?
[G] They also ran frontier text LLMs as a cascade -- Whisper transcription feeding straight into the LLM -- against the same filtered subset. GPT-5 cascaded hits fifty one point one seven percent, Claude Opus four point five gets thirty nine point two two percent, both competitive with, though not beating, the top Gemini end-to-end systems. But on the audio-cue and interruption subset specifically -- sixty seven tasks where you genuinely need to hear something, not just read it -- Gemini 3 Pro's cascaded score drops thirty point three percent relative to its own end-to-end score, and Gemini 2.5 Pro drops twenty four point one percent. Meanwhile a fully deaf cascade -- Whisper into GPT-5, no audio model in the loop at all -- still scores thirty five point eight two percent on that exact subset. Even a GPT-4o cascade gets twenty three point eight eight percent.
[S] Hold on. That's the number I actually want to sit with. A system that never hears the audio still clears over a third of the tasks the paper is calling "audio-cue."
[G] That's a fair read of the number, yes.
[O] Okay, I think we've got enough on the table now to actually argue about what this means. Let me make the strongest case for taking this seriously first. This is a genuinely hard, genuinely well-built benchmark, and it's exposing something real: every frontier model, including the best one available, is bad at extended natural conversation. That's not a benchmark artifact, that's a capability gap the field needs to know about. The rubric methodology is careful, the judge is validated against humans at ninety three percent agreement, and the ablations are honest -- they show their own real-audio data is harder than the synthetic shortcut, which is the opposite of what you'd expect from a paper trying to inflate its own difficulty.
[S] I'll grant all of that. Here's my deflationary case. The paper's own limitations section says it plainly: evaluation doesn't operate on raw audio outputs. The judge reads a transcript. The headline fifty four point six five percent is a text-output configuration, not an audio-output one -- and we just established audio output costs another few points on top. Then there's the cascade result: a system with zero access to the audio scores thirty five point eight two percent on the subset specifically designed to require hearing something. That's not a small contamination of the signal, Eleanor, that's a meaningful fraction of the "audio-native" credit being reachable without ever touching the audio.
[G] Both points land, and I don't think they cancel out. On the transcript-only grading -- the authors are upfront about it, they name it as the key limitation, and it's genuinely the right criticism: you cannot certify a spoken assistant's naturalness, prosody, latency, or turn-taking from a text judge, full stop. On the deaf-cascade number, their own explanation is that strong reasoners can partially infer paralinguistic context from what survives in a transcript -- Whisper does transcribe disfluencies, hesitations, and interruption markers, so some of that signal isn't purely acoustic to begin with. I think that's plausible, but it doesn't fully answer Michael's point. It means the cleanest version of this axis would need tasks provably unsolvable from any transcript, and this paper doesn't isolate that slice.
[O] So score that one mostly to Michael.
[G] Mostly, yes. Where I'd push back on Michael is the selection question underneath all of it. Conversations only enter the dataset if a human contributor actually broke GPT-4o Audio Preview or Gemini 2.5 Pro during collection -- that's the two models used as the adversarial targets. So the dataset is, by construction, adversarially tuned against those two systems specifically, which arguably flatters other Gemini models at evaluation time, including Gemini 3 Pro, more than it flatters, say, an open-source model that was never in the loop during collection.
[S] That's a real point, and it cuts toward caution on the leaderboard, not just the grading method.
[O] There's one more thing worth putting on the record, since we're being skeptical: the response-length correlation. Rubric score tracks response length almost linearly, even with exclusion rubrics meant to penalize padding.
[G] Right, and their only check on that is a manual review of the Gemini family's responses specifically, concluding those aren't verbosity-driven. That's a single-family spot check, not a control applied across all twenty systems in the table. I'd treat the cross-model ranking as somewhat less trustworthy the more two models differ mainly in response length rather than in what they actually got right.
[S] So where does that leave us. Real gap, weak proof that the score is measuring what it claims to measure.
[G] I'd put it as: real gap, correctly identified axes, and a score that's an upper bound on multi-turn audio understanding under a generous, text-mediated grading setup -- not a measurement of spoken conversational quality itself. The paper is honest about that ceiling. It just means the number on the leaderboard is doing less work than the framing around it suggests.
[O] Let's close with why this matters beyond one leaderboard. If E2E voice assistants are actually this fragile past a few minutes of real conversation, that's a product problem today, not a research curiosity for later.
[S] And for anyone doing eval work more broadly, this is a clean case study in a general problem: a benchmark can be well-constructed and adversarially generated and still only measure a proxy for the capability it's named after. The fix here isn't more data, it's grading the actual audio output, which the authors say themselves is the obvious next step.
[G] Agreed, and I'd add the practical note for anyone building on this: the deaf-cascade and TTS ablations are the most useful diagnostic tool in the paper for other researchers, more than the leaderboard is.
