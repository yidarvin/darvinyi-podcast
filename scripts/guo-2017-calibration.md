[S] Here's a number that should bother anyone building on top of a modern neural network: divide every one of its output scores by a single tuned constant, and the network's error rate does not move at all, yet its stated confidence becomes roughly five to ten times closer to the truth.
[O] And that's the beautiful part. One number, no retraining, no touching the weights, and you go from a network that's constantly overstating how sure it is to one you can actually trust.
[S] "Overstating" is generous. I'd call it an emergent side effect of chasing accuracy with zero penalty for overconfidence. The fix works, but it also means the field shipped miscalibrated models for years before anyone checked.
[O] Which is exactly why this paper is still the default citation for calibration, nearly a decade after ICML twenty seventeen.

[S] And I'm here to keep that optimism honest. Today's paper is "On Calibration of Modern Neural Networks," by Chuan Guo, Geoff Pleiss, Yu Sun, and Kilian Weinberger at Cornell, published at ICML in twenty seventeen.
[O] Joining us is Duncan, who has spent a lot of time with this paper and the calibration literature it kicked off. Duncan, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. And worth saying up front, I'm not one of the authors, so anytime I say "the authors" or "they," that's exactly what I mean.
[S] Good, because we're going to hold them to a high standard today. Duncan, set the scene. What problem were they actually staring at in twenty seventeen?
[G] The starting point is a result from two thousand five. Niculescu-Mizil and Caruana had shown that the neural networks of that era were reasonably well calibrated on binary classification. That finding sat unchallenged for a decade, mostly because nobody had a strong reason to re-check it.
[O] Because architectures back then weren't changing that fast.
[G] Right, and then they changed enormously. Between LeNet in nineteen ninety eight and the ResNets, Wide ResNets, and DenseNets of twenty sixteen, classification accuracy improved dramatically. What the authors point out is that nobody had gone back to ask whether confidence kept pace with accuracy through that whole run.
[S] And the motivating cases in the introduction aren't abstract. A self-driving car deciding whether to trust its own obstacle detector or lean on other sensors. A diagnosis model deciding when to defer to a human doctor. Those are real deployment decisions that depend on the confidence number meaning what it claims to mean.
[G] Exactly, and that framing matters, because it sets a bar the paper's own evaluation doesn't fully clear. I'll come back to that.
[O] Tease accepted. Show us the headline picture first.
[G] It's the paper's own Figure one. A five layer LeNet and a hundred and ten layer ResNet, both trained on CIFAR-100. LeNet is far less accurate, forty four point nine percent error against the ResNet's thirty point six percent. But bucket LeNet's predictions by confidence and check how often they're actually right, and the bars land almost exactly on the diagonal. Do the same for the ResNet, and its confidence sits well above the diagonal at nearly every bin. It's almost always "sure," and wrong far more often than that certainty implies.
[S] So the more accurate model is the less trustworthy one, if you're reading its confidence at face value.
[G] That's the whole hook of the paper, yes.
[O] Walk us through how you actually turn "the bars don't line up" into something you can optimize.
[G] Two tools. First, the reliability diagram, exactly what I just described: bucket predictions into equal-width confidence bins, plot each bin's average confidence against its actual accuracy, and a perfectly calibrated model traces the diagonal. Second, and this is the number the whole paper hangs on, Expected Calibration Error, or ECE. Take those same bins, compute the gap between confidence and accuracy in each one, weight each bin's gap by how many samples fall in it, and sum. The paper uses fifteen bins for every number it reports.
[S] Fifteen bins, chosen how?
[G] The paper doesn't run a sensitivity analysis on that choice in its main text. Worth flagging now, because it comes back later.
[O] Noted. So Section three is where they go hunting for what's actually causing the gap.
[G] Right, and this is the part I find genuinely elegant. They vary one factor at a time on a ResNet trained on CIFAR-100: depth, width, whether Batch Normalization is used, and how much weight decay is applied. Three of those four push ECE up even when they hold accuracy flat or improve it. More depth, more width, and using Batch Normalization all worsen calibration. Less weight decay worsens it too.
[S] What's the actual mechanism, though? Correlation between "modern architecture" and "miscalibrated" isn't an explanation.
[G] The authors' mechanism is about what happens once a network can already classify nearly all of its training examples correctly. At that point it can keep lowering its training loss, the negative log likelihood, purely by pushing predicted probabilities closer to zero or one. That's confidence rising with zero benefit to classification accuracy. They show it directly on a hundred and ten layer stochastic-depth ResNet: past a certain point in training, test negative log likelihood starts rising, meaning it's overfitting, while test classification error keeps falling, from twenty nine percent down to twenty seven percent, in that exact overfitting region.
[O] So the network is trading calibrated probabilities for a couple points of extra accuracy, and if you're only watching the error curve, you'd never see the trade happening.
[G] That's the finding in one sentence, yes.
[S] I'll grant that's a clean diagnosis. What's the actual fix, mechanically?
[G] This is where temperature scaling comes in, and it's almost aggressively simple. Take the network's raw output scores before the softmax, the logits, and divide every one of them by a single learned positive number, call it T, the temperature. Then apply softmax as normal. Fit T by minimizing loss on a held-out validation set, which takes about ten iterations of a standard optimizer, a fraction of a second.
[O] And because you're dividing every class's logit by the same number, you can't change which class has the highest score.
[G] Correct, which is the elegant part. The predicted class, and therefore the model's accuracy, is completely unchanged. All temperature scaling does is reshape how confident the model is allowed to claim to be. Set T above one and you soften the distribution toward uniform. Set it below one and you sharpen it. At exactly one you recover the original, uncalibrated output.
[S] That's suspiciously cheap for a fix. What did they actually compare it against?
[G] A full field. Histogram binning and isotonic regression, both non-parametric methods that map raw confidence into corrected per-bucket scores. Bayesian Binning into Quantiles, which averages over many different binning schemes instead of committing to one. And Platt scaling, the classic binary calibration method that temperature scaling directly generalizes. They also built two richer multiclass extensions themselves: vector scaling, a separate scale and bias per class, and matrix scaling, a full linear transform of the logits.
[O] So they didn't just propose the cheap trick and declare victory. They built more expressive versions and let those compete too.
[G] They did, and that's where the paper's most interesting negative result shows up.
[S] Give us the actual numbers before we get to that.
[G] Table one is the core of the paper: ECE across six vision datasets and four document-classification datasets, with a range of architectures, before and after each calibration method. Before calibration, miscalibration is everywhere. Most dataset and model pairs sit somewhere between roughly four and seventeen percent ECE. CIFAR-100 with a hundred and ten layer ResNet is sixteen point five three percent uncalibrated. Birds with a ResNet-50 is nine point one nine percent.
[O] And after temperature scaling?
[G] That CIFAR-100 number drops from sixteen point five three percent to one point two six percent. The stochastic-depth variant goes from twelve point six seven percent down to zero point nine six percent. On ImageNet, ResNet-152 goes from five point four eight percent to one point eight six percent, and DenseNet-161 from six point two eight percent to one point nine nine percent.
[S] Those are big drops from turning one knob.
[G] And here's the negative result I mentioned: temperature scaling beats vector scaling and matrix scaling on almost every vision benchmark, despite being strictly less expressive. When you look at what vector scaling actually learns, its per-class parameters end up nearly constant across classes, meaning it converges to essentially the same solution as temperature scaling anyway. Matrix scaling is worse than doing nothing on some datasets: twenty one point one three percent ECE on Birds, twenty five point four nine percent on CIFAR-100, both worse than the uncalibrated baseline, and it fails to even converge on thousand-class ImageNet, because its parameter count grows with the square of the number of classes.
[O] So more parameters actively hurt here, because a validation set that's plenty big for fitting one number is nowhere near big enough to fit a full matrix without overfitting.
[G] Exactly, and that's the authors' own reading: calibration error in these networks is, in their words, intrinsically low dimensional. One number captures almost all of the correctable miscalibration.
[S] Is there any dataset where temperature scaling actually loses?
[G] One: Reuters. It's already calibrated below one percent ECE before doing anything, so there's very little room to improve, and on that dataset temperature scaling and several of the other methods roughly wash out or worsen it slightly. The authors are upfront that this may also reflect a quirk of how that particular test set is split.
[O] And cost. You mentioned fitting T takes a fraction of a second.
[G] Right, and the paper quantifies the gap. On CIFAR-100, with a hundred classes, getting a near-optimal vector scaling solution takes at least two orders of magnitude more compute than temperature scaling. Histogram binning and isotonic regression take roughly an order of magnitude more. Bayesian Binning into Quantiles takes roughly three orders of magnitude more, and it's also the hardest of all of them to implement, since it requires averaging over a whole space of binning schemes.
[S] So the ranking is: cheapest method, best or tied-best accuracy on almost everything that matters, and the hardest methods buy you nothing extra, and sometimes actively hurt.
[G] That is genuinely the shape of the result table, yes.
[O] Alright, let's argue about what this means. My case: this is one of those rare papers where the simplest possible idea turns out to be close to optimal, and it stayed that way. Temperature scaling is still the calibration baseline everyone reaches for nearly a decade later.
[G] Worth grounding that a little further, since it's a factual claim rather than a hunch: temperature scaling is the exact affine transform that later prompting-calibration work, including "Calibrate Before Use," builds directly on top of. That's an unusually durable track record for a one-parameter trick.
[O] That's my case in one sentence, then. It's the rare simplest-idea-wins result, and it's still winning.
[S] I don't dispute the durability. My case is that the evaluation supporting it has gaps the paper never stress-tests, and three of them matter a lot for the exact high-stakes settings the introduction uses to motivate the whole thing. First: ECE's binning choice. Fifteen bins, fixed, for every number in that table, with no check that the ranking between methods survives a different bin count or a different binning scheme.
[G] That one's fair, and it's not hypothetical. Later work, Nixon and colleagues in "Measuring Calibration in Deep Learning," and separately Kumar and colleagues in "Verified Uncertainty Calibration," both showed the standard ECE estimator is biased and can shift depending on exactly that choice. A model can look better or worse calibrated purely from how you draw the bins, independent of any real change in its behavior.
[O] Okay, point conceded on that one specifically. But that's a measurement refinement, not evidence the underlying finding is wrong.
[S] Agreed, it doesn't overturn the diagnosis. It does mean the precise numbers in Table one shouldn't be read as more solid than they are. Second crack: this only measures top-label calibration, whether the single predicted class's confidence is trustworthy. It says nothing about whether the rest of the probability distribution, the relative confidence among all the classes that weren't predicted, is calibrated at all.
[G] Also fair, and also not addressed in the paper. That gap is exactly why later work, Kull and colleagues on Dirichlet calibration for instance, built class-wise calibration metrics specifically because top-label calibration alone can hide a lot.
[O] That one I'll actually take some of. A downstream system that wants to ensemble models, or make an uncertainty-aware decision, does need more than "is the top guess trustworthy."
[S] Third, and the one I care about most: the paper states its own assumption outright, that training, validation, and test sets are drawn from the same distribution. Every number in Table one is fit and measured under that assumption. But the two scenarios the introduction opens with, a self-driving car's obstacle detector and a diagnosis model deciding when to defer to a doctor, are exactly the situations where the real world drifts away from whatever distribution the model was tuned on.
[G] That's the strongest of your three, and it was directly tested two years later. Ovadia and colleagues, in "Can You Trust Your Model's Uncertainty," found that calibration quality, including from temperature scaling specifically, degrades substantially once you move to inputs under distribution shift. This paper simply never puts that scenario in front of its own method.
[O] So the fix is real, and the motivating story for why you'd want it is exactly the story the paper's own evaluation doesn't test.
[S] That's my case in one sentence.
[G] I'd score it this way. The core diagnosis, capacity and reduced regularization eroding calibration as accuracy climbs, holds up completely; nobody has overturned that in nine years. The fix, temperature scaling, is also genuinely durable as a first thing to try. Where the paper is weaker is precisely in not stress-testing its own headline metric, and in not testing the deployment conditions it uses to justify the whole project in its own introduction.
[O] I can live with that as the final tally.
[S] Same.
[O] Zoom out for a second. Why should someone who evaluates models for a living, rather than someone training them, care about a nine year old calibration paper?
[G] Because calibration is itself an evaluation axis, and it's a different axis than accuracy. A model can top a leaderboard on accuracy while being systematically overconfident, and if anything downstream, a routing decision, a human-in-the-loop threshold, an ensemble weight, consumes that confidence number, accuracy alone won't tell you it's broken.
[S] And the paper's own honesty about ECE's limits is a useful template for eval design generally: report the metric, but also report what the metric structurally can't see, the binning sensitivity, the top-label-only scope, the i.i.d. assumption. Most of the paper's own blind spots are things it flagged in passing and simply didn't chase.
[O] My own hunch, going beyond anything the paper itself claims, is that today's much larger models likely have their own version of this story, confidence drifting away from correctness as capability and post-training pipelines get more aggressive, and that the honest way to check is the same reliability-diagram-and-ECE toolkit this paper popularized, just pointed at a new generation of models.
[G] That's a reasonable extrapolation, but it is an extrapolation. The paper itself only speaks to classification networks of its era, and it's explicit that it doesn't test structured prediction or regression at all.
[G] If there's one line to leave people with, it's the authors' own framing: probabilistic error and miscalibration can quietly get worse even while classification error keeps improving, and a large chunk of that damage turns out to be fixable with one number.
