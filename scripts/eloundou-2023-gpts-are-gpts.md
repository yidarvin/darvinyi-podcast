[O] Picture this: eighty percent of the American workforce waking up to find that at least a tenth of their job could be done twice as fast by a language model.
[S] Now picture the fine print. A big chunk of that number leans on software that, when this paper was written, did not exist yet. "Could be" is doing an enormous amount of work in that sentence.
[O] Fair, but even the strict version of the claim, direct access to the model and nothing fancier, still touches around fifteen percent of every task in the American economy. That is not nothing.
[S] It's not nothing. It's also not the number everyone quoted in the news in twenty twenty-three, which was closer to half the economy. I want to know exactly how much daylight sits between those two claims, and who's supposed to notice it.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a guest scholar dig into one paper from the litsearch dot darvinyi dot com map.
[S] Today's paper carries maybe the best pun in the field: "GPTs are GPTs," meaning Generative Pre-trained Transformers are General-Purpose Technologies. Eloundou, Manning, Mishkin, and Rock, out of OpenAI, OpenResearch, and the University of Pennsylvania, posted this in March of twenty twenty-three.
[O] To help us take it apart, we've got Eleanor with us, who's read this one closely. Eleanor, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. This is one of those papers that tried to turn a very fuzzy question, "will AI take my job," into something you could actually put a number on. Whether that number means what people think it means is basically our whole episode.
[S] Eleanor, set the scene for us. People had been trying to measure automation exposure for years before this paper existed. What was already on the shelf?
[G] Quite a bit, actually. Frey and Osborne, back in twenty seventeen, had economists guess which occupations were automatable and trained a classifier on those guesses. Brynjolfsson, Mitchell, and Rock built a rubric called Suitability for Machine Learning. Felten and colleagues, and separately Webb, mapped AI patents and capabilities onto worker tasks. All of it was careful, published work.
[O] So what's missing? Why does a paper specifically about GPTs need to exist at all?
[G] Every one of those tools was built around a different technology: robotics, classifiers, computer vision, patent language. None of them were designed around what a large language model is actually good at, drafting, summarizing, translating, writing and debugging code, answering open-ended questions. Those strengths cut across white-collar work in a pattern the older indices simply weren't built to catch.
[S] And the trigger, presumably, was watching GPT-4 land.
[G] Exactly that. The authors say plainly that after the jump in capability from GPT-3.5 to GPT-4, the live policy question got concrete fast: across the whole economy, which tasks can a language model, or software built on top of one, actually make faster, and who does that land on.
[O] Which is a much more answerable question than "will robots take our jobs," because at least you can go task by task and ask it directly instead of guessing at the occupation level.
[S] So how do you go task by task across the entire U.S. economy? That sounds like an enormous data problem before you even get to the AI question.
[G] It is, and the paper leans entirely on an existing government resource, the O-NET database, the Department of Labor's catalog of what almost every U.S. job actually involves. The version they use covers just over a thousand occupations, broken into nearly twenty thousand individual tasks, plus a finer layer of about two thousand what they call Detailed Work Activities, smaller atomic actions like "study scripts to determine project requirements."
[O] So the plan is: score every one of those tasks for exposure, then roll the scores back up to the occupation.
[G] Right, and the scoring instrument itself is deliberately simple. For a given task, could access to the model, or software built on the model, cut the time it takes to finish that task by at least half, while a third party wouldn't notice or care that a machine helped? If no, that's E-zero, no exposure. If the model alone, through something like ChatGPT, clears that bar, that's E-one, direct exposure. If the model alone can't do it but purpose-built software layered on top of it plausibly could, that's E-two.
[S] Fifty percent is an oddly specific threshold. Where does that number come from?
[G] The authors are upfront that it's somewhat arbitrary. They picked it for ease of interpretation by annotators, and because they expected real-world time savings to land below whatever threshold they chose, so they deliberately set the bar high rather than low.
[O] And then you combine E-one and E-two into the three headline measures.
[G] Alpha is the share of a job's tasks rated E-one, direct access, no extra tooling, the conservative lower bound. Zeta is E-one plus all of E-two, the maximal, "imagine the software gets built" upper bound. And beta, their preferred measure, is E-one plus half of E-two, discounting the more speculative software case because building it actually takes real co-invention effort.
[S] Who's doing the rating? This is where I start to get nervous.
[G] Two independent tracks. Human annotators, the authors themselves for the physically demanding tasks, plus experienced contractors who'd previously done alignment labeling work at OpenAI, rated a large sample directly. Separately, GPT-4 itself was handed a tuned version of the same rubric as a prompt and ran it over every task and occupation pair. Crucially, the authors are explicit that neither track counts as ground truth. Human ratings are the primary result; GPT-4 is treated as a scalable second opinion.
[O] And do the two agree with each other?
[G] Reasonably well at the occupation level, which is the paper's whole methodological linchpin. For their preferred beta measure, the Pearson correlation between human and GPT-4 ratings comes out to point five nine one, rising to point six five four for the upper-bound zeta measure, with categorical agreement running from around two-thirds up past eighty percent depending on which measure you look at.
[O] Okay, let's get to the numbers everyone actually quotes.
[G] Under beta, around eighty percent of U.S. workers are in an occupation where at least ten percent of tasks are exposed. About nineteen percent are in an occupation where more than half the tasks are exposed. Averaged across every task in the economy, direct exposure, the alpha measure, lands around fifteen percent. Once you add in the hypothetical LLM-powered software, that climbs to somewhere between forty-seven and fifty-six percent, depending on whether you trust the human or the GPT-4 upper bound.
[S] So fifteen percent is the number backed by something that existed in March of twenty twenty-three. The forty-seven to fifty-six percent range is a bet on tooling nobody had actually built yet.
[G] That's a fair way to put it, and it's the paper's own framing too. The gap between those two numbers is essentially the thesis. The economic reach doesn't come from the model in isolation, it comes from the applications people build on top of it.
[O] Now here's the part I find genuinely striking: exposure goes up with wages.
[G] It does, and that inverts the pattern from earlier automation research, where lower-wage, more routine work was consistently the more exposed category. When you regress exposure on how important different skills are to a job, science and critical-thinking skills are strongly negatively associated with exposure. Writing and programming skills are strongly positively associated, writing alone carries a coefficient of point four six seven on the beta measure, programming point six two three.
[S] What about employment size? Is this concentrated in a few huge job categories?
[G] Basically no relationship there. Exposure isn't concentrated in a handful of large occupations, it's spread thin across the whole wage distribution.
[O] Does more schooling mean more exposure too, or does it level off?
[G] It rises and then flattens, and that's one of the sharper results. O-NET groups jobs into five "Job Zones," from roles needing a few months of preparation up to roles needing four-plus years of education. Exposure climbs steadily from Job Zone one through Job Zone four, then plateaus or dips slightly at Job Zone five. The share of workers in highly exposed jobs runs from zero percent in Zone one up to roughly thirty-five percent in Zone four, even as median income climbs the whole way, from around thirty thousand dollars a year up past eighty thousand.
[S] Any occupations that come out fully exposed?
[G] By the human raters' most generous measure, fifteen occupations hit one hundred percent: mathematicians, tax preparers, financial quantitative analysts, writers and authors, web and digital interface designers. GPT-4's ratings are more generous still, it flags eighty-six occupations as fully exposed.
[O] Alright, time for the actual argument. My case: this is the first exposure measure built for the right technology, cross-checked by two independent raters who mostly agree, and it explains genuinely new variance. Prior indices, plus wage controls, only account for sixty to seventy-two percent of what this measure captures. Twenty-eight to forty percent of the signal here is new.
[S] My case against taking the headline at face value has three parts. First, the raters are grading their own kind, GPT-4 assessing what GPT-class models can do, on a rubric the authors themselves tuned specifically to match a small set of human labels. Second, "exposure" measures potential, not outcome, it says nothing about adoption, regulation, or whether anyone actually deploys the tooling. Third, the whole pipeline assumes a job is just a bag of independent tasks, which the authors admit might not hold.
[G] All three are real concerns, and to the paper's credit, it's honest about all three, that's one of its more admirable qualities. On the self-grading point, look specifically at the alpha correlation, the strict "does the model alone clear the bar" measure. Human and GPT-4 agreement there is only point two two three on Pearson's correlation. It's the beta and zeta measures, the ones leaning on the more speculative "software could be built for this" judgment, where agreement jumps to point five nine one and point six five four.
[S] So the strongest agreement between raters is riding on the most speculative part of the rubric. That's close to backwards from what you'd want if you were trying to reassure someone like me.
[O] Counterpoint: the paper never hides that gap, it reports both correlations side by side in the same table. The forty-seven to fifty-six percent figure is explicitly framed as an upper bound, and the authors say outright they are not predicting adoption timelines. If a reader mistakes an upper bound for a forecast, some of that is on the reader.
[G] That's a defensible distinction on paper, but I'd push back gently. The abstract leads with the eighty percent and nineteen percent figures, and those are the ones that traveled into headlines. The caveats are all there in the limitations section, but a limitations section doesn't caption a news article.
[S] Which brings me to the task-bag problem. The authors write, almost in passing, that if the task-based breakdown isn't a valid way to represent how a job actually gets done, quote, "our exposure analysis would largely be invalidated." That is not a small hedge to bury in a footnote.
[G] It isn't, and there's a related wrinkle worth flagging. The human annotators labeling individual Detailed Work Activities often didn't know which occupation that activity belonged to. So an activity like "process financial transactions" gets the same exposure label whether it's a gambling cage worker doing it in person or an online merchant doing it by email, even though those two situations have almost nothing in common physically. The paper actually shows this exact mismatch in one of its own tables.
[O] Okay, that one lands for me. I'll concede the aggregation logic is shakier than the headline numbers make it look.
[G] My honest read, going a little beyond what the paper states directly, is that this work is best used as a map of technical reach rather than a forecast. It tells you where the frontier of "could plausibly help" currently sits. It doesn't tell you where adoption will actually land, and I think the authors, who are careful writers throughout, would agree with that framing.
[S] There's a broader evaluation lesson buried in here too, isn't there. Strip away the labor-economics framing and this is basically an LLM-as-judge study wearing an economics costume.
[G] That's a sharp way to put it. You have a rubric, two independent raters, one human and one model, and a real validity question about whether the model grading its own family introduces circularity. That is precisely the construct-validity problem showing up across benchmark and reward-model design right now: when the judge and the thing being judged share an architecture, how much of the agreement is genuine signal and how much is shared blind spots?
[O] And to its credit, this paper reports that tension fairly transparently, breaking agreement out by alpha, beta, and zeta separately rather than hiding behind one blended correlation number.
[G] It does, and that kind of disaggregation is exactly what more benchmark papers should be doing whenever a model is asked to grade something close to its own capabilities. One more detail worth a smile: in the appendix, the authors literally have GPT-4 write its own one-paragraph conclusion, then hand-edit it into an alliterative version stuffed with G and P and T words. Even the paper's own ending can't resist proving the pun.
[S] That's a nice note to close the technical discussion on. Let's leave listeners with one line each.
[G] If you remember one thing, remember that the paper's real contribution is the rubric and the task-level method, not the specific eighty percent or fifty percent figures, those numbers will drift as the software layer catches up to the model.
[O] My takeaway: even the strict, no-extra-software, fifteen-percent number is a bigger and more broadly distributed shock than any prior automation wave, and for the first time it's landing hardest on white-collar, high-wage work.
[S] Mine: treat the forty-seven-to-fifty-six-percent range as a hypothesis about tooling that mostly didn't exist yet, not a measurement, and go check the litsearch writeup for the full breakdown by job zone and skill.
