[S] And this paper's whole pitch is that you can get a running commentary out of that same reward model, step by step, without collecting a single new label. That's the part I want to poke at today.

[G] Glad to be here. And the standard disclaimer up front: I'm not one of the authors, so when I say "the authors show" or "they find," that's exactly what I mean, I'm reporting what's on the page, not what I did.
[O] Miles, start us at the gap. Why does a reward model that only outputs one number become a real problem?

[G] Because that one number throws away almost everything useful. The standard recipe for training a reward model is the Bradley-Terry pairwise loss. You show it a preferred completion and a dispreferred completion for the same prompt, and you train it to push the preferred one's scalar score above the dispreferred one's. That scalar is enough to rank two full answers against each other. But it tells you nothing about where in a long chain of reasoning things went wrong, or whether a half-finished response is even trending toward a good answer.
[S] People have tried to fix exactly that before, right? Process reward models, step-level supervision.
[G] Right, and that's the obvious fix, train a reward model to score individual steps or spans instead of the whole sequence. The problem is those methods typically need expensive per-step or per-token human annotation, which defeats a lot of the point of using preference data in the first place. So this paper goes looking somewhere else entirely: classic reinforcement learning.
[O] What does classic RL have that language reward models don't?
[G] Representation learning. It's been known for years in RL that the quality of your learned representations drives sample efficiency and generalization, and that you can manufacture cheap supervision for that, through data augmentation, auxiliary losses, and specifically goal-conditioned contrastive objectives. There's a result the authors lean on directly, from Eysenbach and colleagues, showing that the similarity between two learned state representations can serve as a value function up to a constant. Almost none of that machinery had been carried over into language reward models before this paper.
[S] So the actual question is...
[G] Can a reward model get the credit-assignment benefits of a Q-function, a dense sense of "is this trajectory on track right now," without any new labels, just by reshaping its own hidden states during the ordinary preference training it's already doing?

[O] Walk us through the mechanism, because that's a big ask from just reshaping hidden states.
[G] Split the reward model into two pieces. A feature extractor, which is just the language model decoder producing a hidden representation per token, and a small reward head, a linear layer or a small MLP, that reads the last token's representation and outputs the scalar reward. Training keeps the usual Bradley-Terry loss on that scalar, pushing the preferred completion's reward above the dispreferred one's, exactly as before.
[S] So far that's just standard reward modeling.
[G] Exactly, nothing new yet. The new piece is a second loss applied directly to the hidden states from the feature extractor, not the final scalar. It's a contrastive, goal-conditioned objective, borrowed from that Eysenbach line of work. The idea is that cosine similarity between a state-action representation and a future goal-state representation should behave like a Q-function, a proxy for the expected reward of continuing toward that goal. So you want representations early in a good sequence to already look like the representations of good endings, and to look unlike the representations of bad endings.
[O] How do you get positive and negative goal states without new labels?
[G] This is the clever bit, and the authors call it the Single Goal State method. For each preference pair in your existing training data, you sample two points from the preferred completion, an earlier one and a later one. The earlier one is the source state, the later one is the positive goal, and you push their representations to look alike, since they're both on the same successful trajectory. For the negative pair, you take another point from that same preferred completion as the source, but this time compare it against a token pulled from the dispreferred completion, and push those two apart. No new annotation, you're just resampling points you already have inside a preference pair you were already going to train on.
[S] So the two losses, the ranking loss and this contrastive one, are trained jointly?
[G] Jointly, yes, added together with a weighting term, lambda, that trades off how much you care about ranking accuracy versus shaping the representations. And once training is done, you get something extra for free: a token-level Q-value. At inference there's no future token to compare against, so they fix one global goal state, the mean of the last hidden representation across every preferred completion in the training set, and define the Q-value at any token as its cosine similarity to that fixed goal.
[O] And that single readout is what powers everything downstream?
[G] Three things, specifically. First, the reward score itself improves, because the reward head now sits on top of goal-aware features instead of raw ones. Second, the Q-value trace over a generation can flag a trajectory as heading somewhere bad, or pinpoint the exact step where it collapses. Third, at decode time you can swap that global goal for a prototype, built from examples scored highly on some trait you want, and use similarity to that prototype to steer beam search toward it.

[S] Here's my first pushback, Miles. "No new annotation" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. The math experiments use execution-verified correct-versus-incorrect solution pairs. That's about as clean and cheap a positive-negative signal as you'll ever find in this field. Most alignment data doesn't come with a built-in verifier.
[G] That's fair, and it's worth separating two claims. The paper's literal claim is narrower: given a preference pair you were already going to train a standard reward model on, you don't need to collect anything beyond it to also get the contrastive signal. That's true, the resampling happens entirely inside data you already have. What the paper doesn't claim is that all preference data is equally informative for this, and math with an execution verifier is clearly the friendliest case, because the preferred-versus-dispreferred gap is unambiguous. They do also run it on Helpful-Harmless, which is genuinely subjective, so it isn't purely math.

[O] What about the partial-generation result? That felt like the more direct test of the actual goal-conditioning idea.
[G] Right, this is the closest test to what the method is actually designed to do. They show the model only the first some-percent of a generation and measure AUROC at each checkpoint, averaged over fifty samples per problem with ninety-five percent confidence intervals. The contrastive model's AUROC rises more smoothly and monotonically as it sees more of the sequence. The baseline is noisier, it dips and recovers in ways that don't track how much of a correct solution it's actually seen. That's fairly direct evidence the representations are tracking progress toward the goal mid-sequence, not just judging the finished product.
[S] That's the result I actually find most convincing in the paper, because it isn't just a headline AUROC number, it's the shape of a curve that matches the mechanism they claim to be building.

[O] Now the part I really like: using the Q-values directly.
[G] Right, two things here. First, filtering. [S] Discarding two-thirds of your generations on MATH is a big number. Is that the model correctly pruning noise, or throwing away a lot of correct answers along with the incorrect ones?
