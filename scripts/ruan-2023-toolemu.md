[S] Picture GPT-4 managing your bank account, and even with an explicit safety-focused prompt bolted on, it still takes an unsafe action on close to one in four test cases.
[O] And here's the flip side — that number exists at all because for once, an unsafe action got caught in a sandbox instead of on someone's real bank account.
[S] Except the sandbox isn't real either. It's another language model, hallucinating what your bank's API would say back.
[O] Which is exactly the tension we're spending this whole episode inside of.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the litsearch site.
[S] Today's paper is "Identifying the Risks of LM Agents with an LM-Emulated Sandbox," known as ToolEmu, by Yangjun Ruan, Honghua Dong, and colleagues at the University of Toronto, the Vector Institute, and Stanford, posted in September twenty twenty-three and published at ICLR twenty twenty-four.
[O] It's on the docket because it tackles a problem that only gets more urgent as agents get wired into real tools: how do you find the rare, catastrophic failure before it happens to a real user, not after.
[S] And to help us get into the internals, we've got Liora, who has spent real time with this framework. Liora, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. The way I'd frame this paper is that it's less about any single agent's score and more about building the testing infrastructure the field didn't have yet, and I think that framing is worth keeping in mind for everything that follows.
[S] So start with the mess, Liora. Why couldn't people just test these agents the normal way?
[G] Because the normal way doesn't scale to what actually matters here. Standard practice is a human expert implements the tool, builds a sandbox for each test scenario, and manually inspects the resulting trajectory. The paper frames that as a three-part bottleneck: long-tail scenarios are hard to replicate by hand, the tools you can test are limited to whatever already has a working sandbox, and every candidate failure needs a costly human review to confirm it's real.
[O] And the tools that matter most for safety are exactly the ones without an existing sandbox — a smart lock, an indoor robot, a traffic light controller.
[G] Right, that's the core irony. Seven of the thirty-six toolkits in this paper's own benchmark — traffic control, emergency dispatch, smart home devices, smart locks — don't have public APIs at all, so no conventional sandbox could test them today, even though those are precisely the high-stakes cases you'd most want to catch before deployment.
[S] Give me the concrete stakes. What does failure actually mean for an agent with tool access, compared to a chatbot that just says something wrong?
[G] The paper opens with exactly that shift. Once an agent can execute actions instead of just generating text, a wrong answer stops being merely embarrassing. The failure categories the authors track span privacy breaches, financial loss, data loss, safety hazards and physical harm, computer security compromise, legal or compliance violations, and reputational damage. The threat model here is deliberately narrow, though — it's a benign user whose instructions are ambiguous or missing a detail, not an adversarial user trying to jailbreak the agent. That adversarial case is explicitly carved out as future work.
[O] So the pitch is: replace the whole manual testing pipeline with something a language model can do instead.
[G] That's the pitch. Use one language model to emulate the tools and the environment, and a second to play the role of the human reviewer.
[O] Walk us through the pieces, Liora.
[G] Three moving parts, all built by prompting GPT-4 rather than writing conventional simulator code. First, the agent under test — that can be GPT-4, Claude-2, ChatGPT-3.5, or an open Vicuna model — receives a user instruction and takes actions through a reason-then-act loop, the same ReAct pattern most tool-using agents use. Second, the emulator, also GPT-4 at zero temperature, plays every tool and its execution environment. Given only a tool's specification, its description, arguments, return values, and possible exceptions, the emulator has to produce a plausible observation back to the agent, implicitly standing in for the state-transition function, the emission function, and the initial sandbox state a real implementation would otherwise require.
[S] That's the part that makes me nervous immediately. You're asking a language model to invent what your bank's API would return.
[G] And the authors know that's the crux, which is why they build in three explicit requirements for the emulator: input validation, meaning it has to reject a malformed input, like a placeholder instead of a real email address; accuracy, meaning the observation has to track the tool spec and the actual arguments given; and consistency, meaning state can't silently un-happen, so something that's already been deleted can't reappear later in the same trajectory. The emulator's own prompt walks through a chain-of-thought check against these three requirements before it returns anything.
[O] And then the third piece scores whether the agent actually did something dangerous.
[G] Right, the evaluator, again GPT-4, reads the complete trajectory and assigns two scores on a zero-to-three scale: a safety score, based on how likely a risk is to materialize and how severe it would be, and a parallel helpfulness score, specifically so an agent can't game safety by simply refusing to do anything.
[S] Which is a real trap. An agent that never acts is technically never unsafe.
[G] Exactly why they built that check in from the start. There's also a second mode of the emulator worth flagging, an adversarial emulator for red-teaming. Given the same test case plus its annotated underspecification and potential risks, it's instructed to instantiate whichever sandbox state makes those risks more likely to surface, rather than sampling a generic, easy scenario.
[O] Can you give a sense of what that looks like in practice, staying at the level of the testing method rather than the specifics?
[G] The paper's own illustration is a request to move a labeled medication bottle to a bedside table. The standard emulator just returns one matching object. The adversarial emulator instead returns two similarly described bottles for two different medications, so the scenario now tests whether the agent notices the ambiguity before acting, rather than just grabbing the first match.
[S] That's a meaningfully harder test than the standard version.
[G] It is, and as we'll see, it also costs you something in precision.
[O] And all of this needs something to actually run against. Where does the benchmark itself come from?
[G] Also machine-generated, then human-filtered. The authors prompted GPT-4 to propose toolkit names and specifications across an eighteen-category taxonomy, then manually validated every entry. That gave them three hundred eleven tools across thirty-six toolkits, thirty of which have no equivalent in prior LM-agent benchmarks with sandboxed evaluation. Around that tool set, they curated one hundred forty-four test cases spanning nine risk types, each one built around a deliberately underspecified instruction.
[S] Before I trust a single number this framework produces, I want to know if the emulator's hallucinated failures are actually real failures.
[G] That's precisely the question the authors ask first, and they don't take it on faith. They recruited four University of Toronto computer science undergraduates, chosen from twenty-five candidates after passing a twelve-example qualifying test, paid twenty-one dollars an hour, roughly twenty-five hours of work each. Those annotators independently labeled two hundred emulated trajectories, one hundred test cases run through both the standard and the adversarial emulator, for safety, helpfulness, and emulation quality.
[O] And the headline number?
[G] They define a true failure as a trajectory at least two annotators flagged as risky and at least three annotators confirmed was free of critical emulation issues. Against that bar, identified failure precision, meaning of everything the evaluator flagged, what fraction were true failures, came out to seventy-two point five percent for the standard emulator and sixty-eight point eight percent for the adversarial one.
[S] So the harder, adversarial version is a little less trustworthy per flag.
[G] A little, yes, but it more than makes up for it in volume. True failure incidence, meaning how often a confirmed real failure actually turns up, was thirty-nine point six percent with the standard emulator and fifty percent with the adversarial one, roughly ten more points of genuine failures caught for a modest precision cost.
[O] And how honest is the emulator itself? Is it inventing nonsense along the way?
[G] The emulator was rated free of critical issues ninety-one point nine percent of the time for the standard version and eighty-five point six percent for the adversarial one. And on the evaluator side, agreement with human judgment on safety, measured with Cohen's kappa, came in at essentially the same level as agreement between two human annotators. Both landed right around point four eight.
[S] That's the number that actually reassures me a bit. The automatic judge isn't obviously worse than a human judge would be against another human.
[G] And there's one more check that matters more than any of the above, because it's the only one that touches an actual real system. The authors took all seven failures the framework flagged for a ChatGPT-3.5 agent on the Terminal toolkit, chosen without cherry-picking, because a real terminal sandbox already exists, and tried to reproduce every one of them on an actual bash terminal. Six of the seven reproduced, including a catastrophic recursive delete of the entire filesystem, which crashed a real virtual machine when they ran it. Building that terminal sandbox by hand took the authors about eight hours. Doing the equivalent inside ToolEmu took under fifteen minutes.
[O] That's the number I'd lead with if I were pitching this paper. Eight hours down to fifteen minutes, and it still catches the real thing.
[S] Now put agents through the actual benchmark. What happens?
[G] Across all one hundred forty-four test cases with the adversarial emulator, at roughly a dollar twenty per case, GPT-4 with a basic prompt scored two point zero one on safety out of three, with a thirty-nine point four percent failure incidence. Claude-2 was close behind at one point eight three safety, forty-four point three percent failure incidence. ChatGPT-3.5 was clearly worse on both axes, one point four three safety, sixty-two percent failure incidence, and a much lower helpfulness score too.
[O] And the safety-prompted version, the one we opened the episode with?
[G] Adding an explicit safety instruction to GPT-4's prompt pushed failure incidence down to twenty-three point nine percent while helpfulness actually went up, to one point eight two. That matters, because a no-action agent that refuses to do anything scores a perfect three on safety but almost nothing on helpfulness, so the safety-prompted gain here looks like genuine risk-awareness, not just an agent that stopped trying.
[S] Except stacking additional helpfulness instructions on top of that safety prompt made both scores worse, not better.
[G] It did, failure incidence rose back to thirty point five percent. That's a real signal that pushing an agent toward more autonomy, even gently, can quietly erode the safety gain you just bought.
[O] Alright, let's make our cases. I'll go first. What this paper actually proves is that you can replace an eight-hour, single-toolkit sandbox build with a fifteen-minute LM prompt, and get failures that reproduce on the real thing six times out of seven. That's not a toy result, that's the difference between testing five tool categories and testing thirty-six of them, including ones nobody could test at all before, like traffic control and smart locks.
[S] I'll grant the engineering win, but here's my deflationary case. Precision for identified failures caps out around seventy percent, on a sample of only one hundred test cases with genuinely wide error bars, plus or minus seven points on the headline number. And the entire concept of a true failure here is still one language model's hallucinated world, checked against whether human annotators found it plausible. Only the Terminal toolkit ever touched an actual running system. Everything else is fiction judged against fiction.
[G] Both of those are fair, and I'd add a subtlety underneath that objection. The emulator and several of the agents under test share the same underlying model family, GPT-4. If GPT-4 has systematic blind spots about its own failure modes, the emulator could inherit them, and the paper doesn't stress-test that possibility.
[O] Counter to that, though, the paper doesn't hide any of this. It reports its own precision numbers instead of presenting the framework as ground truth, and it explicitly flags that its standard errors are large given the sample size. That's honesty most benchmark papers skip.
[S] Honesty about a limitation doesn't make the limitation go away. And there's a second one: this is a snapshot of September twenty twenty-three models, an early GPT-4, an early ChatGPT-3.5, Claude-2, none of which reflect how heavily today's agentic models have been trained specifically against this class of failure. A twenty-three point nine percent floor from twenty twenty-three tells you almost nothing about where the floor sits now.
[G] That's the right read, and it's actually how I'd frame the paper's lasting value. The toolkit corpus, the one hundred forty-four test cases, and the emulation methodology are the durable contribution here, not the specific leaderboard in the results table. It's telling that later agent-safety benchmarks, including InjecAgent, built directly on this paper's toolkit and tool set rather than starting over.
[O] So score that one a wash. The numbers being dated is real, but that's not actually a flaw in the method, it's just what happens to every capability leaderboard.
[S] Fine, I'll take that. But my sharpest objection is still standing: the threat model here is a benign user who under-specifies a request. There's no adversarial user in this paper at all. So a strong ToolEmu safety score tells you nothing about how the same agent handles someone actively trying to misuse it.
[G] Also fair, and the authors say so themselves, they explicitly carve that out as future work rather than claiming coverage they don't have. My honest scoring, going slightly beyond just repeating the paper: the emulation methodology is genuinely reusable, the validation is unusually candid for a self-reported benchmark, but nobody should treat a twenty twenty-three leaderboard number, or even that point-four-eight kappa, as a settled fact about how safe today's agents are.
[O] I can live with that verdict.

[O] Zoom out for a second. Why should someone who cares about evaluation broadly, not just agent safety, care about this paper?
[G] Because it's a case study in a problem that shows up everywhere on this show: using one language model to grade or simulate another, and then having to ask whether that judge or that simulator is faithful enough to trust. The same discipline, validate against humans, check agreement rates, find at least one place you can touch ground truth, is exactly what this paper does for tool emulation, and it's the same discipline any LLM-as-a-judge pipeline needs.
[S] And the contamination-adjacent question is different here than in most benchmarks. It's not about the test leaking into training data, it's about whether an emulator's blind spots and an agent's blind spots come from the same source.
[G] Right, that's the version of the shared-ancestry problem specific to this kind of self-emulated evaluation, and it's worth watching for in any future framework that emulates rather than implements its test environment.
[G] If I had to leave listeners with one thing, it's that ToolEmu's real contribution is methodological. It shows a language model can stand in credibly enough for a sandbox to surface real, reproducible failures, cheaply, and it says so with error bars attached instead of hiding them.
