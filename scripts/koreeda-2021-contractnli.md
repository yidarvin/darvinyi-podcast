Today's paper is ContractNLI, A Dataset for Document-level Natural Language Inference for Contracts, by Yuta Koreeda and Christopher Manning, published at Findings of EMNLP in twenty twenty-one.
[O] Joining us is Maeve, a researcher who's spent real time with this dataset and what came after it. Welcome, Maeve.
[G] Glad to be here. This is one of those quieter papers that ended up shaping how a whole subfield of legal NLP frames its problems, so it's worth taking apart carefully.
[S] Before we get comfortable — Maeve, one sentence. What is this paper claiming to solve that nobody had solved before?
[G] It reframes contract review as an inference problem instead of an extraction problem. Rather than just finding where a contract talks about something, the system has to decide whether the whole document entails, contradicts, or simply never mentions a fixed legal claim, and it has to point at the exact sentences that justify that call.
[O] Let's start with why this needed solving at all. The paper opens with an industry number: sixty to eighty percent of all business-to-business transactions run on some written agreement, and a typical Fortune one thousand company is sitting on twenty thousand to forty thousand active contracts at any given time.
[S] And all of that gets reviewed by a human being reading line by line.
[G] Right, that's the fairness problem underneath the cost problem. Review is done manually by professionals, which is expensive, and the authors point out that smaller companies and individuals often just sign without it because they can't afford the review.
[O] So what existed before this paper? Legal NLP wasn't starting from zero in twenty twenty-one.
[G] Two prior efforts get positioned against directly — a lease-clause extraction dataset from Leivaditi and colleagues, and CUAD, an expert-annotated legal contract dataset from Hendrycks and colleagues. Both frame contract review as information extraction: given a question like "is there a restriction on soliciting employees," find the span of text that discusses it.
[S] That sounds useful on its own terms. Why isn't extraction enough?
[G] Because it tells you a clause exists, not what it decides. CUAD hands you the span, and you still have to read it and judge whether it violates your policy. The argument here is that the useful output is the judgment itself — entailed, contradicted, or not mentioned — with evidence attached, not a pointer telling you where to go read something.
[O] So the reframe is: stop asking where the contract talks about this, start asking whether the contract violates this policy.
[G] Exactly, and the mechanism for that judgment is natural language inference — a three-way classification the field already knows from datasets like SNLI and MNLI.
[S] Except standard NLI compares a hypothesis against one short sentence. Here the premise is an entire document.
[G] That's the real technical contribution. The average contract here runs two thousand two hundred fifty-four tokens, and eighty-six percent of documents blow past BERT's five hundred twelve token context limit. You cannot just feed the whole thing to a standard encoder.
[O] And on top of the length problem, you still need the evidence.
[G] Right, that's the second axis. The system also has to point at the spans that justify its verdict, which is an open-domain-question-answering-style retrieval problem, and NLI research had rarely coupled retrieval with inference before this.
[S] Walk me through what's actually in the dataset before we get to the model.
[G] Six hundred seven non-disclosure agreements — NDAs specifically, a single common contract type that's easy to collect and that lets the authors write finer-grained hypotheses than a mixed corpus would allow. Documents came from two sources: Google search across seven English-speaking-country domains, and the SEC's EDGAR filing system. EDGAR was brutal — they manually screened twenty-eight thousand, seven hundred eighty candidate filings and kept only two hundred thirty-six.
[O] That's a rejection rate north of ninety-nine percent.
[G] It is, and it tells you how messy real contract corpora are. On top of the documents, they wrote seventeen hypotheses, reviewed by paralegals — things like "receiving party may share some confidential information with some of receiving party's employees," or "some obligations of agreement may survive termination." And critically, the same seventeen hypotheses are applied to every contract, in training, development, and test.
[S] Every contract gets judged against the identical seventeen claims.
[G] Every one. Each hypothesis gets a label — entailment, contradiction, or not mentioned — plus, when the label isn't "not mentioned," the evidence spans that justify it. A span here is a full sentence or a list item, not a token range, because the authors wanted a lawyer to be able to read the evidence with enough context to trust it, unlike token-level spans in factoid question answering.
[O] And the labels are lopsided, I'm guessing.
[G] Very. A document averages seventy-seven point eight candidate spans, most entailed or contradicting hypotheses have only one or two evidence spans, occasionally as many as nine, and contradiction is the rare label across almost every hypothesis. That imbalance ends up being the whole story of where the models fail.
[O] So how does the model actually handle a two-thousand-token document and still point at exact sentences?
[G] This is Span NLI BERT, and it makes two specific bets against the prior approach. The prior approach, used for things like CUAD, predicted start and end tokens for a span the way SQuAD-style question answering does, and it split long documents into fixed windows with a static stride.
[S] What's wrong with that, mechanically?
[G] Two things. Predicting start and end tokens forces the model to solve span-boundary detection and evidence relevance at once, even though the boundaries are already fixed by sentence structure — you're making it resolve a problem that doesn't need resolving. And a static sliding window can split a span across two contexts, or starve it of surrounding context.
[O] So Span NLI BERT fixes both.
[G] It inserts a special span token before every predefined span and puts a small classifier on each one predicting, yes or no, is this span evidence — ordinary multi-label binary classification instead of boundary regression. For windowing, they use dynamic context segmentation: an algorithm guaranteeing every span appears whole, with a minimum amount of surrounding context, in at least one window, instead of chopping the document at fixed intervals.
[S] And the entailment call itself?
[G] Sits on the classification token, same as any BERT classifier. The two heads train jointly with a combined loss, and there's one subtlety worth stating: when a window contains no evidence span, the entailment-or-contradiction loss for that window gets zeroed out. Otherwise you'd force the model to call "entailed" on a chunk of text that, in that particular window, shows no evidence at all — teaching it the wrong thing.
[O] And since one document turns into several overlapping windows, how do you get back to a single verdict per contract?
[G] Averaging, but a weighted kind. Span probabilities get averaged across every window containing that span. The document-level entailment prediction is a weighted average across windows, weighted by how much evidence probability each window contains, so a window that actually has supporting text counts more toward the final verdict than one that's mostly boilerplate.
[S] Let's get to whether it actually works. Walk me through the headline table.
[G] Two comparisons make the architectural case cleanly. First, the old-style start-and-end BERT-large, the SQuAD approach, gets an evidence mAP of point eight six nine. A plain TF-IDF and support vector machine baseline over spans gets point eight three six. Those are nearly the same number — predicting boundaries instead of classifying predefined spans throws away almost all the advantage of using a transformer at all.
[O] That's a strong point for the architecture argument.
[G] It gets stronger. Span NLI BERT-base, the smaller model, reaches point eight eight five mAP, beating SQuAD BERT-large's point eight six nine, despite the smaller backbone. The architectural change buys more than scaling the backbone did.
[S] Does scale still help within their own architecture, or is bigger just wasted here?
[G] It still helps. Span NLI BERT-large reaches point nine two two mAP and an entailment accuracy of point eight seven five. Best overall combines architecture, scale, and transfer: a DeBERTa-v2-xlarge backbone, further fine-tuned on CUAD's span-identification task, hits point eight nine two NLI accuracy and point nine three six mAP — the top result in the paper.
[O] What about domain pretraining, legal-specific language models? That's usually the intuitive lever to pull.
[G] A wash, and an instructive one. A BERT pretrained from scratch on case law didn't help either sub-task. LEGAL-BERT, fine-tuned on case law and contracts, helped evidence identification but hurt NLI accuracy, point seven nine four against point eight three eight for the plain baseline — domain pretraining bought one axis at the expense of the other.
[S] Now give me the number you buried a minute ago, the one that actually matters.
[G] F one for contradiction. Even the best Span NLI BERT-large model, the one posting point nine two two mAP and looking solved on evidence retrieval, scores point three five seven F one on contradiction, against point eight three four F one on entailment. The aggregate accuracy and the aggregate mAP both look strong. The rare class is where the model is still guessing.
[O] Is that a data problem or a reasoning problem?
[G] Some of both — the authors ran the experiment to find out. They fed the model oracle ground-truth evidence spans, so it didn't have to find the evidence, just reason over it, and contradiction F one for BERT-base jumped from point four nine zero to point six five seven. Real headroom in evidence identification, then. But point six five seven with perfect evidence still isn't close to solved, so contradiction reasoning is hard on its own terms too.
[S] Is the model even using the hypothesis text meaningfully, or pattern-matching on which of the seventeen questions it's answering?
[G] They tested that directly, swapping each hypothesis's actual wording for a meaningless per-hypothesis symbol token. The paper reports a consistent decrease in performance across evidence identification and NLI when they did that, so the wording isn't nothing. But the drop was narrow, which the authors themselves read as the model leaning on shallow cues more than deeply parsing what each hypothesis asks.
[O] Okay, let's argue this properly. My optimist case: this is the first dataset that frames contract review as document-level inference with grounded evidence, at a scale, six hundred seven contracts, that was the largest annotated contract corpus at the time. The architecture insight, span classification over boundary prediction plus proper long-document segmentation, is genuinely portable — it shows up again wherever you need grounded long-document reasoning, not just contracts.
[S] My deflationary case is about trust, not architecture. There's no reported inter-annotator agreement anywhere in the paper. The seventeen hypotheses were reviewed by paralegals, sure, but the actual per-document entailment judgments came from one computational linguistics researcher, helped by two Mechanical Turk workers doing span coverage. Most of the development set, and part of the test set, were labeled by that one person alone.
[G] That's accurate, and it's a real gap. CUAD, which this paper argues against directly, had labels from legal experts, triple-verified. ContractNLI doesn't have that. For a gold standard meant to underpin legal-reasoning claims, the absence of a reported agreement score is a genuine weakness, not a stylistic nitpick.
[S] Second point: the seventeen hypotheses are fixed and shared across every split, including test. So the benchmark measures whether a model generalizes to a new contract under a known question — it never tests generalizing to a new legal question. The authors even admit, in their own words, that the task "could also be addressed by building a text classifier for each hypothesis." That's close to saying this is seventeen-way classification wearing an NLI costume.
[O] I'll grant that's the sharpest limitation in the paper. But fixing the hypothesis set was a deliberate choice to get finer-grained legal claims out of modest data, and the authors say so directly. It's a tradeoff, not an oversight.
[G] Both points are fair. The tradeoff was reasonable for a first dataset in twenty twenty-one, but it means nobody should read this benchmark as evidence a model understands arbitrary legal claims — it's evidence the model can classify these seventeen well, on new paperwork.
[S] Third point, and the one that matters most for anyone deploying this: negation by exception. Contracts routinely state a rule, then carve out an exception, "shall not disclose, except to its employees," sometimes pages apart. On hypotheses where that pattern shows up non-locally, the model's accuracy on the minority label collapses to zero, and weighted accuracy falls from point eight five without the pattern to point four five with it.
[O] Zero.
[S] Zero. Locally, where the exception sits in the same sentence, it's milder but still bad — minority accuracy falls from point seven seven to point four oh. That's the model failing exactly the pattern sophisticated contract drafting uses to bury a real risk.
[G] I'd add the aggregate-metric point on top, because it's a broader lesson for reading benchmark leaderboards. Evidence mAP of point nine two reads as "solved." It isn't the number that determines whether you'd trust this system in a real review — that's contradiction F one at point three five seven, with a standard deviation of point zero three nine over three runs on a test set of only one hundred twenty-three documents, plus minority accuracy going to zero under exactly the pattern that matters most.
[O] Credit to the authors, though — that's a criticism they built into their own paper. They ran the oracle experiment and the exception-clause breakdown themselves.
[S] Agreed, this is an unusually self-critical paper for twenty twenty-one. My skepticism is about what later readers do with the aggregate number, not about the authors hiding anything.
[O] Let's zoom out. If you're building anything that grounds a judgment in a long document — retrieval-augmented pipelines, agentic tools that have to cite their source — this paper's core lesson generalizes: separate "can it find the right passage" from "can it reason correctly given the passage," and report both, because they saturate at very different rates.
[G] Fair generalization, and part of why this recipe stayed a reference point for later legal-benchmark work — the span-token-plus-dynamic-segmentation idea for long documents, and the discipline of reporting a minority-class metric instead of letting accuracy hide it.
[S] One flag for anyone reusing ContractNLI today rather than in twenty twenty-one: it's been public on GitHub for years, the contracts are public filings and search results, and there's no generative large language model baseline in the paper — it predates that era. Any modern score on this benchmark needs a contamination story.
[G] The paper's own contribution stands independent of that risk — it's the framing plus the segmentation recipe. But treat any brand-new leaderboard score against this exact test set with real caution.
[G] If there's one sentence to take from this paper: evidence retrieval in long documents is close to solved, document-level legal reasoning is not, and the gap concentrates exactly where it matters, contradictions and negation by exception.
