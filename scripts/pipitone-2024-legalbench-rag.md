[S] Picture a law firm's AI system pulling the wrong clause out of a three hundred fifty thousand character merger agreement, confidently, and citing it as the controlling term.
[O] That's the nightmare scenario everyone in legal AI worries about, and until this paper, basically nobody was measuring whether it happens.
[S] Nobody was measuring it directly. And it turns out the people who built the measuring stick made an arithmetic mistake in their own flagship results table.
[O] Which is either a footnote or a crisis, depending on whether you think the dataset survives its own author's math error. I think it does, easily.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio. Today we're covering LegalBench-RAG: A Benchmark for Retrieval-Augmented Generation in the Legal Domain, by Nicholas Pipitone and Ghita Houir Alami at ZeroEntropy, posted in August twenty twenty-four.
[S] And we've got a guest who's spent real time inside this benchmark's tables. Rhiannon, welcome to the show.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is a paper I keep coming back to, because the dataset is genuinely important and the results section is a bit of a mess, and both of those things are true at once.
[O] Start with the gap, Rhiannon. Why did legal RAG need its own retrieval benchmark?
[G] Because almost everyone measuring legal language models was grading the wrong half of the pipeline. A retrieval-augmented system has two stages: a retriever that pulls relevant text out of a huge document corpus, and a generator that reasons over whatever the retriever handed it. LegalBench, the field's reference benchmark from Guha and colleagues, hands the model the gold clause directly and asks whether it reasons correctly about it. It never asks whether the system could have found that clause on its own, buried inside a real contract.
[S] So LegalBench is testing reading comprehension with the answer key already open on the desk.
[G] That's a fair way to put it. And the general-purpose RAG benchmarks that do test retrieval, things like RGB, RECALL, and MultiHop-RAG, mostly reward returning a large, roughly-relevant chunk of text. They don't distinguish that from pinpointing the exact sentence that answers the question.
[O] Why does that distinction matter more in law than anywhere else?
[G] Three reasons the paper leans on. Cost and latency, because stuffing a whole contract into the context window to be safe gets expensive fast at scale. Hallucination, because the more irrelevant text rides along in the window, the more a model tends to forget or invent details, and that's a worse failure mode in a legal setting than in, say, customer support. And verifiability: a precise span lets the system cite its source, so a lawyer in the loop can check the claim against the original text in seconds instead of rereading the whole document.
[S] All reasonable. But "we should build this" and "we did build it well" are two different claims, and I want to see the construction before I grant the second one.
[O] Fair, let's get into it. Rhiannon, what's actually in LegalBench-RAG?
[G] The clever part is that they didn't annotate from scratch, they retraced an existing benchmark. LegalBench is built from four expert-annotated source datasets: CUAD, private commercial contracts, ContractNLI, non-disclosure agreements, MAUD, merger and acquisition agreements, and PrivacyQA, consumer privacy policies. Each of those datasets already pairs a clause with a category label, like "does this describe a right of first refusal." LegalBench turns that into a yes-or-no question with the clause supplied. LegalBench-RAG goes back into the original source document, finds the exact character span that clause came from, and turns the annotation category into a natural-language question.
[S] So instead of "here's the clause, answer yes or no," it's "here's the whole contract, go find the clause."
[G] Exactly. Every query takes the form "Consider," then a short description of the document, then a semicolon, then the question itself. One example from the paper: consider the Cologuard Promotion Agreement between Exact Sciences Corporation and Pfizer; does this contract include any right of first refusal, right of first offer, or right of first negotiation. The label isn't yes or no, and it isn't even a document ID, it's a file path and a character range, fifty-one thousand five hundred twenty to fifty-two thousand eight hundred four in that example, and some annotations map to several separate spans rather than one contiguous block.
[O] Where did the document descriptions themselves come from?
[G] GPT-4o-mini generated a short description of each document from its filename and its first and last paragraphs, with the output format checked by regular expression and every description manually inspected afterward. They also ran embedding similarity across every pair of descriptions to catch ones too similar to tell apart, and excluded categories where the source datasets' own annotation precision was inconsistent. Three separate quality-control passes, all documented in the paper.
[S] What's the actual scale here?
[G] Nearly eighty million characters across seven hundred fourteen documents, human-annotated by legal experts throughout. The paper's own count is a little inconsistent, which we might as well flag now: the abstract says six thousand eight hundred fifty-eight query-answer pairs, the body and the detailed statistics table say six thousand eight hundred eighty-nine, and the gap traces to ContractNLI specifically, nine hundred forty-six in one table versus nine hundred seventy-seven in the other. Small, but it's the first sign this paper's bookkeeping isn't as careful as its annotation process.
[O] Still, retracing gold clauses to exact spans across four expert-built legal datasets is not a cheap thing to build.
[G] Genuinely not. The paper points out that CUAD alone, just one of the four source datasets, took over forty lawyers a full year to produce thirteen thousand annotations, and at five hundred dollars an hour for legal expertise, they estimate replicating just that one dataset would cost around two million dollars. That's the case for this benchmark existing at all, before you look at a single number in the results. And because the full corpus is unwieldy for quick iteration, they also release a "mini" version, seven hundred seventy-six queries, a hundred ninety-four from each of the four datasets, over about eight point seven million characters. Every experiment in the paper runs on mini, never the full six thousand eight hundred eighty-nine.
[S] Worth remembering for later: the entire results section is built on roughly eleven percent of the benchmark's own queries.
[O] Let's get to what they actually measured. How does scoring work?
[G] Standard information-retrieval metrics, precision at k and recall at k, applied to spans instead of documents. A pipeline retrieves its top k chunks, and you measure how much of that retrieved text overlaps the gold spans, once as a fraction of what was retrieved, that's precision, and once as a fraction of what should have been retrieved, that's recall.
[S] Overlap measured how, exactly? Characters, whole chunks, something else?
[G] That's actually one of my complaints, and I'll come back to it. The paper never formally defines the unit.
[O] Noted. What did they run it on?
[G] A small grid on the mini set: OpenAI's text-embedding-3-large for embeddings, SQLite-Vec as the vector store, and Cohere's rerank-english-v3.0 as an optional reranker, crossed with two chunking strategies, a naive fixed five-hundred-character splitter with no overlap, and LangChain's Recursive Character Text Splitter, which tries to keep paragraphs and sentences intact.
[S] Headline result?
[G] It's a hard benchmark, full stop. Precision at k equals one sits in the single digits on three of the four datasets, because the ground-truth spans are so tight that even a mostly-correct retrieval that grabs a slightly-too-large chunk gets penalized. The cleanest, most robust signal in the whole paper is the spread between datasets. PrivacyQA, plain-language questions about consumer privacy policies, is by far the easiest, hitting fourteen point three eight percent precision at one and eighty-four point one nine percent recall at sixty-four under the paper's best configuration. MAUD, dense merger-and-acquisition jargon, documents averaging three hundred fifty-one thousand characters, is by far the hardest, two point six five percent precision at one, and recall at sixty-four plateaus below thirty percent.
[O] And the two design conclusions they draw from the grid?
[G] First, that the Recursive Character Text Splitter beats the naive splitter on both precision and recall. Second, framed as the surprising result, that adding the Cohere reranker actually hurts performance rather than helping it, on every dataset. Their explanation is that a general-purpose reranker trained on broad web text doesn't transfer well to dense legal jargon.
[S] The reranker-hurts finding, at least, sounds like something I could believe without a calculator.
[G] It does hold up under recomputation. The splitter conclusion does not.
[O] Rhiannon, you keep hinting at "recomputation." Let's hear it, but let me make the strongest case for the paper first, because I think it earns one. This is the first public benchmark that isolates legal retrieval from legal generation, built by retracing four separately expert-annotated datasets down to the character, something that would cost millions to redo from scratch. The corpus itself, and the difficulty ordering it surfaces, is real signal the field simply didn't have before.
[S] I don't dispute the corpus is real and expensive. My problem is the leaderboard on top of it, and it's wrong in a way a calculator catches. Table four, the naive splitter's aggregate row, reports an overall precision at one of two point four percent. That's below every one of the four datasets feeding into it, the smallest of which is three point three six percent. An average can't fall beneath its own minimum input.
[G] It can't, and if you average the four per-dataset numbers by hand, you get nine point two three percent, not two point four. The recall row has the same problem, it prints seventy-six point three nine percent, where the honest mean is sixty-three point four nine.
[O] Does that actually change the conclusion, or is it cosmetic?
[G] It changes it. The paper claims the Recursive splitter beats the naive splitter on both metrics. But the naive splitter's true average, nine point two three percent precision, sixty-three point four nine percent recall, actually beats the Recursive splitter's printed, and correct, aggregate of six point four one percent and sixty-two point two two percent. The headline finding reverses once you fix the one broken row.
[S] So the single concrete recommendation this paper hands an engineer, use recursive splitting, rests on a broken table. And it gets worse. Table five, Recursive splitter with no reranker, and table six, naive splitter with the Cohere reranker, are supposed to describe two different pipelines. Their numbers are nearly identical across every cell, both aggregating to sixty-two point two two percent recall. That reads like a copy-paste error, not two configurations that happened to tie.
[O] I'll grant that's a real defect. I still think the reranker finding survives it, and that's the more actionable result anyway, tested consistently across all four datasets and all four pipeline combinations: a general-purpose reranker made every single dataset worse, not better.
[S] Granted, that's probably the most trustworthy number in the paper. But zoom out from the tables for a second. Every experiment here runs one embedding model, one reranker, two chunkers, on the eleven-percent "mini" subset, never the full benchmark. No BM25 or hybrid baseline, no legal-tuned retriever, nothing to anchor these percentages against.
[G] I'd add two things the paper's own limitations section concedes. Every query is answered by exactly one document, by construction, so it can't test cross-document or multi-hop retrieval, even though the authors say they found real annotation examples that would need it. And it explicitly doesn't cover structured numerical data or medical records.
[O] What about contamination? These are four datasets that have been public for years.
[G] Worth taking seriously. It's plausible the embedding models tested here saw fragments of CUAD, ContractNLI, MAUD, or PrivacyQA during pretraining. Scoring retrieval rather than generated text blunts that somewhat, since a model still has to locate the right span for a newly templated query, but the paper doesn't address it directly.
[S] One more thing that isn't a bug, it's just never raised: precision and recall trade off differently in law than in general search. Missing a clause in an NDA dispute can be catastrophic, a little extra context is usually just an inconvenience. This paper reports both metrics but never argues which one should matter more for legal use, or whether a higher score here would even produce a better generated answer downstream.
[G] That loop genuinely isn't tested anywhere in this paper. Better retrieval numbers are a reasonable proxy for better downstream answers, but it's an assumption here, not something they demonstrate.
[O] Zoom out for a second. If the parts that do hold up here are real, what changes for legal RAG builders?
[G] Two concrete things. Don't reach for a general-purpose reranker by default on legal text without testing it first, since the one tested here made every dataset worse, not better. And measure retrieval separately from generation before trusting an end-to-end legal RAG demo, because a system can look fluent while silently missing the actual clause a lawyer needed.
[S] And for evaluation practice more broadly, this is a clean case study in why aggregate rows need to be checked, not trusted. A four-dataset equal-weighted average is a simple calculation, and it was still wrong in a way that flipped a headline conclusion. If that slips through in a paper this careful about annotation, it's a reminder to actually recompute the summary statistics in any benchmark paper before repeating its claims.
[O] Rhiannon, does this connect back to LegalBench itself, the benchmark it's built on top of?
[G] A little. LegalBench's own reasoning scores were always implicitly assuming perfect retrieval, since the model gets the clause handed to it. This paper is essentially the missing denominator. If a real system can only find the right clause fourteen percent of the time on the easiest legal dataset, and far less on the hardest, LegalBench's generation numbers were measuring a step that, in production, may never even happen.
[G] If there's one line to take from this paper, it's the authors' own framing: retrieval, not generation, is the unmeasured and probably weaker half of legal RAG, and now there's a real, expensive, expert-built dataset to measure it with.
[O] Mine is that this is exactly the kind of unglamorous infrastructure work the field needs more of, a benchmark built by retracing existing expert annotations down to the character, rather than generating new labels cheaply and hoping they hold up.
