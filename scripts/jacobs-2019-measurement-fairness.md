[S] Which is the whole problem with fairness research, if you ask this paper. Everyone's been arguing about the math, and almost nobody's been asking whether the thing that math is measuring is even real.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a guest scholar sit with one paper until we've actually understood it. Today's paper is "Measurement and Fairness," by Abigail Jacobs at the University of Michigan and Hanna Wallach at Microsoft Research, published at FAccT in 2021.
[S] It's quietly become one of the most cited papers in the fairness literature, north of 500 citations, for a paper that doesn't propose a single algorithm.
[O] Which is exactly why it's on the docket. Joining us is Delphine, a researcher who's spent a lot of time with this paper. Delphine, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is one of those papers people cite as a vibe, "measurement matters," without necessarily internalizing what the authors are actually claiming, which is much sharper than that.
[S] So set the scene. By 2019, when this was first posted, the fairness literature already had a long rap sheet, COMPAS, value-added teacher models, healthcare risk scores, and a whole toolkit of mathematical fairness definitions built to prevent exactly that kind of harm.
[G] Right, demographic parity, equalized odds, predictive parity, individual fairness, an entire cottage industry of definitions, each one formalized, each one debated for its trade-offs. Jacobs and Wallach's argument is that the field kept refining that machinery while skipping a step underneath it entirely.
[O] The step being?
[G] Whether the thing being scored is even a coherent, directly knowable quantity in the first place. The paper's central claim is that quantities like risk of recidivism, teacher effectiveness, and socioeconomic status are what social scientists call unobservable theoretical constructs. You can't point a sensor at someone and read off their recidivism risk. It has to be inferred from something you can observe, an arrest record, a test-score delta, and that inference is never neutral.
[S] That's not a new idea in social science.
[G] Not remotely, it's a century old. Psychometrics, political science, and education research all have an entire discipline called measurement modeling built around exactly this problem, how do you responsibly infer an unobservable construct from observable proxies. The paper's contribution isn't inventing that idea, it's importing it wholesale into computer science, where the authors argue it barely exists at all.
[O] And their diagnosis is that computer science's habit of treating the proxy as if it just were the construct is where a lot of these harms sneak in.
[G] Exactly, collapsing the construct and its operationalization. Once you stop distinguishing recidivism from arrested within two years, you've stopped being able to ask whether that substitution was a good one. Nobody's checking the join.
[S] Walk me through their formal machinery, because I know they actually write down equations for this, which is unusual for a fairness paper.
[G] They do, and they deliberately start with something almost embarrassingly simple: height. Height isn't even a real unobservable construct, but they use it as a toy case because the assumptions are easy to see. You measure someone's height N times with a ruler, and you get a true height plus an error term, h-hat sub n equals h plus epsilon sub n, and the whole thing only works if that error is well-behaved, zero mean, no systematic bias.
[O] And then they break it.
[G] They break it immediately. If your height measurements come from dating-site self-reports instead of a ruler, that assumption falls apart. Toma and colleagues found that men over-report their height more often, and by larger margins, than women do. So the error term isn't noise anymore, it's correlated with gender. Get the error model wrong, and every downstream number is off in a way that tracks a demographic group.
[S] That's a cute toy example. Does it generalize to anything that actually matters?
[G] That's the whole second half of the setup. They walk the same logic up through socioeconomic status, proxied by income, though income misses wealth, education, and occupation, through the SAS-built EVAAS model, the Education Value-Added Assessment System, which infers teacher effectiveness from students' test-score changes, through COMPAS's recidivism score, and through a healthcare enrollment model that infers patient benefit from predicted future costs. Every one of those is a specific, contestable measurement model, dressed up as a neutral number.
[O] So what's the actual toolkit they hand you for catching when one of those has gone wrong?
[G] Two buckets. The first is construct reliability, roughly, precision. Do repeated measurements of something unchanged agree with each other? They focus specifically on test-retest reliability, because it's the aspect most directly relevant to fairness. If a model gives wildly different outputs for the same underlying thing at two different times, that's not the world changing, that's the instrument being noisy.
[S] Which is the 6-to-96 teacher score from the cold open.
[G] Exactly that case. The second bucket is construct validity, which is roughly unbiasedness, and it's explicitly not a pass or fail checkbox, the paper is emphatic that it's a matter of degree, argued for with reasoning, not computed. This is where they do their most original synthesis, pulling together seven distinct aspects of validity from psychometrics, political science, and education into one fairness-oriented vocabulary.
[O] Give me all seven.
[G] Face validity is the sniff test, do the numbers look plausible on their face. Content validity is the big one, and it splits into three: contestedness, whether there's one agreed theoretical understanding of the construct or several competing ones; substantive validity, whether the operationalization uses the right observable properties, and only those; and structural validity, whether it captures the right relationship between them. Then convergent validity, does your measurement agree with other already-validated measurements of the same thing; discriminant validity, does it correlate with other constructs only to the degree they're actually related, and not, say, almost perfectly with race when it has no business doing that; predictive and hypothesis validity, do the measurements support independent downstream facts you'd expect if the construct were real; and finally consequential validity, what actually happens in the world, to whom, once you deploy this measurement.
[S] That's a lot of vocabulary for what's ultimately still a judgment call.
[G] It is a lot of vocabulary. Whether that's a strength or a weakness is basically the debate we should have.
[O] Let's go through the worked examples, because this is where the framework either earns its keep or doesn't. Start with the teacher model.
[G] Tim Clifford, 26 years of teaching experience, well-regarded, scores 6 out of 100 one year on the EVAAS model, then 96 the next. Cathy O'Neil documented this in "Weapons of Math Destruction." Read through a parity-metric lens, there's nothing to even flag, there's no demographic disparity being computed here. Read through test-retest reliability, it's an immediate, obvious failure, no real teacher's effectiveness swings that violently in a year.
[S] And the convergent-validity case?
[G] Sarah Wysocki, a fifth-grade teacher who scored low on the same kind of model despite strong reviews from her principal, her colleagues, and parents. Three independent, differently-obtained signals pointing one way, the model pointing the other. That's a textbook convergent-validity failure, the new measurement doesn't agree with existing, trusted measurements of the same construct.
[O] Now COMPAS, the one everyone already knows.
[G] COMPAS defines recidivism, in its own documentation, as, quote, a new misdemeanor or felony arrest within 2 years. Angwin and colleagues' 2016 ProPublica investigation found Black defendants disproportionately mis-scored as high risk and white defendants disproportionately mis-scored as low risk, and the vendor's response was that COMPAS still satisfied predictive parity. Years of academic argument followed about which parity metric was the right one. The paper's read is that both sides skipped the earlier question, arrests are not crimes, and policing intensity varies by neighborhood and by race. So arrest-within-2-years was never a clean proxy for recidivism to begin with, that's a substantive-validity failure baked in before any parity metric gets computed.
[S] And the healthcare model, Obermeyer and colleagues?
[G] That one's discriminant validity, precisely. The model operationalized who needs the most care as who will cost the most, which sounds almost reasonable until you check who it actually flags, only 18 percent of the patients enrolled were Black, in a population with far higher rates of unmet health need. Obermeyer's team traced it to the fact that Black and white patients with comparable health needs have historically accrued very different costs, because of unequal access to care. Cost correlated with race in a way health need alone doesn't, that's the discriminant-validity red flag, and it should have been checkable before the model ever shipped.
[O] And then the big one, individual versus group fairness.
[G] The paper's most conceptually ambitious move. It argues that debate was never actually a disagreement about which operationalization is more mathematically sound, it's two incompatible theoretical understandings of what fairness even is. No amount of additional math resolves which one you should hold, because it isn't a math question, it's a values question, and the paper's complaint is that dressing it up in equations hides that from the people the equations are about.
[O] Okay, let me make the strongest case for why this matters. Before this paper, if you disagreed with someone about a fairness definition, you were stuck arguing inside the math, parity metric A versus parity metric B, with no way to step outside the equations. This gives you that outside step. You can now say, precisely, we disagree about content validity, or we disagree about which construct you're even operationalizing, and that's a completely different, and more honest, conversation than dueling formulas.
[S] Here's my deflationary case. This is a framework, not an algorithm. It hands you seven ways to say your measurement might be wrong, with no threshold for how wrong is too wrong, and no procedure for resolving disagreement once you've applied it. Face validity is explicitly called a sniff test. Content validity is explicitly a matter of degree. So who adjudicates? If two people disagree about whether COMPAS has substantive validity, this paper gives them richer vocabulary to have that argument, but not a way to end it.
[O] Delphine, does the paper claim to end it?
[G] No, and it's honest about that. It says construct validity is, quote, always a matter of degree, to be supported by critical reasoning, not a yes-no checkbox. That's philosophically defensible, validity genuinely isn't binary in the social sciences either. But it does mean the objection lands: a team building a deployment-bound risk score gets no decision rule from this paper. How many of the seven aspects need to look shaky, and how shaky, before you don't ship?
[S] So which side actually wins here, is this useful, or is it just relabeling the disagreement in fancier language?
[G] Both, partially. It's genuinely useful as diagnosis, every one of those case studies gets sharper once you run it through the seven-aspect lens, you can point at exactly which validity axis failed instead of gesturing at bias in general. Where it's weaker is as prevention. Every example in the paper is a scandal someone else already found and documented, Angwin's COMPAS investigation, O'Neil's EVAAS reporting, Obermeyer's own audit of the healthcare model. The paper never shows the checklist catching something before an outside investigator already had.
[O] That's fair, but that's true of a lot of foundational conceptual papers, they demonstrate explanatory power on known cases first, and the prospective test comes later, from other people picking up the framework.
[S] Sure, but "comes later" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. Years on, has anyone actually run this checklist prospectively and caught something new with it?
[G] The paper itself doesn't claim that, and I'm not aware of that being tested at scale since. That's the honest gap, this is proven as a vocabulary for re-narrating harms you already suspect, more than it's proven as a pre-deployment audit tool.
[O] Even so, having the vocabulary changes what a disagreement even sounds like. That's not nothing.
[S] It's not nothing. It's also not a decision procedure, and I think practitioners reading this paper for "how do I know when to stop" are going to be disappointed.
[O] Where does this actually reach outside fairness research?
[G] Directly into eval and benchmark design generally, which is probably why it keeps getting cited outside the fairness literature specifically. Any time a benchmark claims to measure something like reasoning, or helpfulness, or safety, from a proxy task, you're doing exactly the move the paper describes, operationalizing a construct, and the same seven questions apply. Does the benchmark look plausible. Does it capture the right properties, and only those. Does it correlate with other validated measures of the same capability. Does it vary with things it shouldn't, like surface form or prompt phrasing, instead of the capability itself.
[S] Which is basically construct validity applied to benchmark contamination and construct-irrelevant variance, dressed in the language this field already argues in.
[G] Precisely. The paper never uses those words, because it predates that framing, but the diagnostic move is identical: stop asking only what's the score, start asking is the score even measuring what we claim it measures.
[O] So even readers with zero interest in fairness specifically should walk away with the seven-aspect checklist as a general auditing habit.
[G] That's my honest read, going a step beyond what the paper explicitly argues, the authors wrote this about fairness, but the underlying discipline travels.
[G] If there's one line to take from the paper itself, it's this: a fairness dispute that looks like a disagreement about metrics is very often a disagreement about what was actually measured, and conflating those two questions is where the harm gets in.
