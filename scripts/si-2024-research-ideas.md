[G] Thanks for having me. This is one of those papers everyone cites, and half the citations only remember the headline, so I am glad to get into what is actually inside it.
[S] Naomi, start with why this study needed to exist at all. Hadn't people already tried building research agents?
[G] They had, and that is exactly the gap the authors are pointing at. By twenty twenty-four there was a wave of research-agent papers claiming L-L-Ms could autonomously generate and validate new ideas, but none of them had actually shown the very first step, that an L-L-M can produce a novel, expert-level idea at all. The optimism was running well ahead of the evidence.
[O] Which is a fair complaint about the field generally. Everyone wants to skip straight to "the agent does science" without checking whether it can survive the first five minutes.
[G] Right, and the reason nobody had checked carefully is that idea evaluation is genuinely hard. Qualified experts are expensive to recruit at scale, whether an idea is good is deeply subjective, and even strong reviewers disagree before any experiment has been run. So earlier studies cut corners that quietly break the conclusion, a handful of reviewers, ideas constrained to be short and thin, or letting an L-L-M judge L-L-M ideas instead of a human.
[S] Which is nice foreshadowing, because this paper is going to argue L-L-M judges are not trustworthy for this task either.
[G] Exactly, and they show that with their own agent. The whole design here is expensive on purpose, spending a year and real money to build the measurement instrument the field did not have, recruit enough experts, control the confounders that wreck small studies, and ask one clean question. Are an L-L-M agent's ideas as novel as the ideas expert humans write, under a blind read.
[O] Walk us through the actual design, Naomi. What does controlling confounders mean in practice here?
[G] Three things mainly, topic, format, and style. Topic first, the authors fixed seven N-L-P topics pulled straight from a conference call for papers, bias, coding, safety, multilinguality, factuality, math, and uncertainty. Left free, humans and the agent might just prefer different topics, which would measure taste, not idea quality. So humans pick a topic from the list, and the agent generates a matching idea on that exact topic, making the topic distribution identical across every condition.
[S] And format?
[G] Everyone writes to the same template, title, problem statement, motivation, proposed method, a step-by-step experiment plan, test cases, and a fallback plan. It is modeled on a grant proposal on purpose, because the plan was always for a follow-up study to actually execute some of these ideas into full projects and compare the outcomes.
[O] That follow-up is not in this paper yet, I take it?
[G] Correct, it is pre-registered as the next phase, and the authors are recruiting researchers for it right now. This paper is the ideation half only.
[S] Okay, but the thing that would worry me most is writing style. If a human write-up simply reads more human, reviewers guess the source and the whole comparison is contaminated.
[G] That is the third control, and the cleverest piece of the design. Every proposal, human and machine both, gets run through a style-normalization step, an L-L-M rewriting everything into one neutral voice. In a small pilot, expert judges asked to guess which proposals were machine versus human got it right only fifty percent of the time, chance. And because rewriting could accidentally change what an idea says, the first author personally verified every human proposal to confirm the content survived.
[O] That is a serious amount of manual labor just to buy a clean comparison.
[G] It is, and that is a large part of why this took a year. The agent itself is deliberately minimal, because the goal is measuring what today's L-L-Ms can do, not showing off agent engineering. It runs on Claude three point five Sonnet in three stages. Retrieval issues Semantic Scholar queries, gathering up to one hundred twenty related papers for grounding. Generation, the part people quote most, over-generates four thousand seed ideas per topic, on the logic that quality is rare and inference is cheap, so you cast a huge net and rank afterward.
[S] Four thousand ideas per topic is a lot of casting.
[G] It is, and it does not hold up as well as you would hope. After generation they deduplicate with sentence-embedding similarity, dropping anything above point eight cosine similarity to something already kept. Then ranking, a Swiss-tournament of pairwise L-L-M comparisons, five rounds, the model says which of two ideas is stronger, validated against twelve hundred real ICLR submissions: Claude three point five Sonnet predicted accept versus reject at seventy-one point four percent accuracy, ahead of GPT-4o at sixty-one point one and Claude three Opus at sixty-three point five.
[O] And there is a third condition on top of plain human and plain machine, right?
[G] Right, "AI Ideas plus Human Rerank." The authors did not fully trust their own automatic ranker, so the first author manually picked the best ideas out of the agent's full output instead of relying on the L-L-M's ranking. That condition estimates the ceiling of what the agent could produce if the ranking step were closer to perfect.
[S] And the human side of the comparison, how real is that baseline?
[G] Very real on paper. Forty-nine idea writers, seventy-nine reviewers, twenty-four did both but nobody reviewed their own idea, one hundred four participants total, recruited through NAACL, a large N-L-P Slack, and direct outreach, screened on Google Scholar. Idea writers average twelve papers and four hundred seventy-seven citations each, paid three hundred dollars, with a one thousand dollar bonus for the top five, and ten days to write. Reviewers were paid twenty-five dollars per review and produced two hundred ninety-eight unique blind reviews across novelty, excitement, feasibility, effectiveness, and overall score.
[O] So give us the headline number, Naomi.
[G] Novelty. Treating every individual review as its own datapoint, the largest sample, human ideas average four point eight four on that one-to-ten scale, AI ideas average five point six four, and AI-plus-rerank averages five point eight one. Both machine conditions beat humans at p less than point oh one. And it holds up two more ways, treating each idea as its own datapoint, still significant, and treating each reviewer as a datapoint and comparing within-reviewer differences, still significant. Three ways of slicing the same two hundred ninety-eight reviews, same conclusion.
[S] Robust to the statistical treatment. I will grant that outright, that is exactly the kind of triangulation you want before believing an effect.
[O] And it is not only novelty, AI ideas also score higher on excitement, five point one nine and five point four six, against four point five five for humans.
[G] Right, but here is the authors' own complication. On feasibility, humans score slightly higher, six point six one versus six point three four, though not significantly. And on overall score, only the human-reranked condition clears significance, five point three four versus four point six eight. The agent's own top picks, uncurated, are statistically tied with humans on overall score.
[S] So the win is narrow. Novelty and excitement, yes. Better idea full stop, no.
[G] That is the precise reading, and the authors say so themselves. The part I think gets underdiscussed is that the paper spends its whole second half stress-testing its own agent, and finds two real limits. First, diversity. Remember those four thousand seed ideas per topic? Only about two hundred turn out to be non-duplicates, and the share of genuinely new ideas in each fresh batch decays toward zero the more the agent generates.
[O] Two hundred out of four thousand, that is five percent.
[G] Five percent, yes, so generating more does not scale the way you would hope, you mostly buy repeats. Second limit, the agent cannot judge ideas reliably either. Its own Claude ranker, the best evaluator tested, agrees with expert rankings only fifty-three point three percent of the time, worse than the human reviewers' own agreement rate, fifty-six point one percent. GPT-4o's pairwise judge does worse still, forty-five percent, and the "AI Scientist" reviewer agent from a competing paper comes in at forty-three point three percent.
[S] Which matters a lot, because it means the entire AI Ideas condition, the one everybody quotes, is only as good as a ranker that barely beats a coin flip relative to expert judgment.
[G] Exactly why the human-rerank condition exists, and exactly why it is the only one that beats humans on overall score. The two rankings, the model's top ten and the human's top ten, only overlap on seventeen out of forty-nine ideas. They are picking substantially different winners.
[O] Let me make the strongest optimist case I can, Naomi. This is the first adequately powered, confounder-controlled study on this question, and it came back in the machine's favor on the exact axis that matters most for research, novelty, the hardest thing to manufacture and the thing expert reviewers are trained to hunt for. If a fluent, minimally engineered agent can already out-novel a curated pool of PhD researchers under blind review, that is a genuine capability signal, and today is the worst these systems will ever be.

My read, going a little beyond the page, is that today's ideation agent produces proposals that sound more novel to expert readers, on a genuinely noisy instrument, against a soft human baseline, on exactly the axis those readers are shown to overweight. [O] So if this holds up, what actually changes?
[G] Two things. For anyone building a research agent, stop assuming more inference-time generation just works, over-generate-and-rank hits a wall around a couple hundred genuinely unique ideas per topic, and self-evaluation is not a shortcut yet, the ranker needed a human in the loop to be trustworthy. For evaluation more broadly, this is a template worth stealing outside idea generation, match your conditions on topic and style before comparing human and machine output, or your headline result is a confound in disguise.
[S] And the meta-lesson for anyone reading eval papers in general, watch the gap between what a headline number measures and what the studied population actually represents. Beats expert researchers and beats experts' rushed median submissions under a fifty-six percent agreement instrument are very different claims, even when both are technically true.
