[O] Joining us is Sophie, who has spent real time with this paper. Sophie, welcome. Set the scene for us: what problem is VisualToolBench actually going after?
[G] Thanks for having me. The paper's framing is a distinction between two modes of using vision in these models. "Thinking about images" is the standard mode: the model sees a picture once, and everything after that is text reasoning over a fixed impression of what it saw. "Thinking with images" is different: the model treats the image as a workspace it can act on. Crop a corner, brighten a shadow, zoom into a serial number, and fold whatever comes back into the next reasoning step.
[S] Which sounds like a small distinction until you think about what real users actually hand these systems.
[G] Exactly, and that's the motivating case the authors lean on. Real photos are rotated, underexposed, cluttered, badly framed. The detail that answers the question might be a price printed in six-point font in the corner of a menu. No amount of reasoning about a blurry photo recovers that price. You have to act on the image first.
[O] And the claim is that nothing else really tests this.
[G] That's the gap they identify, and they back it with a comparison table against eight other benchmarks — ScienceQA, MathVista, MMMU, a benchmark called V-star, GTA, ChartQA, MMDU, and m and m's. Each one is scored on five properties: does it use a dynamic vision tool, does it grade with rubrics, is it expert-curated, does it require reasoning, is it multi-turn. VisualToolBench is the only row in that table that checks all five boxes. The static VQA sets never let the model touch the image at all. The benchmarks that do allow tool use, like GTA, restrict it to trivial crops with no rubric grading behind it. And the multi-turn dialogue benchmark, MMDU, doesn't require genuine tool integration.
[S] I'll grant that's a real and somewhat surprising gap. Given how much attention tool-use agents have gotten, I'd have assumed someone had already built the rigorous version of this.
[O] Which is exactly why I think this paper matters more than its low headline number suggests. It's not testing an incremental capability, it's testing a capability nobody else measured cleanly.
[G] Let's get into how they built it, because the construction is where a lot of the paper's credibility comes from. VisualToolBench has one thousand two hundred and four tasks — six hundred and three single-turn, six hundred and one multi-turn — spread almost evenly across five domains: STEM, Medical, Finance, Sports, and Generalist, each right around twenty percent of the set. Built on two thousand eight hundred ninety-three images, and every task is graded against a pool of seven thousand seven hundred seventy-seven weighted rubric criteria.
[O] That's a lot of hand-written grading criteria.
[G] It is, and that's deliberate. There are five task categories. On the single-turn side: Region-Switch Q-and-A, where the answer requires stitching together evidence scattered across different regions of one image — so the model has to crop multiple regions of interest and combine them. And Hybrid Tool Reasoning, where the model has to combine the vision tool with something like a calculator, a code interpreter, or a web search. On the multi-turn side there's Follow-up Test, where the first question is deliberately under-specified and the model has to ask a clarifying question before it can answer; Temporal Visual Reasoning, tracking change across a sequence of images; and Progressive Visual Reasoning, a chain of interdependent questions about the same image, where a later answer depends on getting an earlier one right.
[S] What's actually in the toolbox?
[G] Six tools total, exposed through a standardized API: an image-processing tool for arbitrary crop, edit, and brightness or contrast manipulation, a Python interpreter, web search, a calculator, a tool that fetches page text from a browser, and a historical weather lookup. The image tool is the centerpiece — it's what turns the photo from a fixed slab of context into something the model can iteratively refine.
[O] And crucially, the tasks never tell the model which tool to use.
[G] Right, that's one of their design principles. Implicit tool-use requirements — the model has to infer from context that manipulation is even necessary. That's closer to how a real user would phrase a request. There's also a genuinely clever engineering fix buried in the setup. If you just drop an encoded image into a tool-result message, the model doesn't actually perceive it — it's present in the conversation history but functionally invisible. So after a vision tool runs, the harness injects a separate, explicit user message carrying the transformed image. Without that fix, think-with-images wouldn't really be testable at all.
[S] That's a real infrastructure contribution, honestly, independent of the benchmark's difficulty.
[G] Models get a budget of twenty tool calls per task. For comparison, the human reference solutions the authors wrote typically need fewer than five.
[O] So there's plenty of room to actually get there.
[G] There is. Now, how tasks get admitted is the other half of the design, and it matters a lot for how we should read the results later. Every task starts with a domain-expert contributor who writes the prompt, supplies the image or images, writes a golden answer, a reference tool-use trajectory, and the rubric criteria. Then — and this is the key step — the contributor grades responses from three reference models: o3, Gemini-2.5-pro, and o4-mini. A task only survives if at least two of those three models fail it.
[S] Hold on. Say that again. The benchmark is built by keeping only the tasks that beat two out of three of today's best models.
[G] Correct. That's the selection filter. After that, every surviving task goes through two independent rounds of human review for realism, correctness of the golden answer, and whether dynamic image reasoning is genuinely necessary — tasks that don't require real visual-tool use get discarded even if they passed the model filter.
[O] I actually like that filter. It's the same logic as building a hard test set on purpose so it stays informative for years instead of saturating in six months.
[S] I don't disagree with the motivation. I disagree with what conclusions people will draw from the resulting number, and we should come back to that in the debate.
[O] Fair, let's hold that thought and get to the grading, Sophie, because "rubrics" is doing a lot of work in this paper.
[G] Each rubric criterion falls into one of five families: Visual Understanding, Truthfulness, Instruction Following, Reasoning, and Presentation, and every criterion gets a contributor-assigned weight from one to five. An LLM judge — o4-mini, grading one criterion at a time as met or not met — scores every response against every rubric. From that you get two numbers. Average Rubric Score is the weighted fraction of satisfied criteria, so it's genuine partial credit. Average Pass Rate is much harsher: any criterion with a weight of four or five is designated critical — usually truthfulness or a core piece of visual understanding — and a response only counts as a pass if it satisfies every single critical rubric on that task. Miss one, and you fail the whole task regardless of how much partial credit you'd otherwise earn.
[O] Walk us through an example, because that gating rule sounds abstract until you see it bite.
[G] There's a nice one in the paper. A single-turn generalist task: how much would a gluten-free fourteen-inch pizza with provolone, red sauce, and kalamata olives cost, based on a photo of a menu. The price, the topping count, and the gluten-free surcharge are printed in different corners of the menu, so the model has to crop multiple regions to even see all three numbers. Gemini-2.5-pro in the paper's demonstration case gets the base price wrong — fourteen dollars instead of thirteen — which fails one critical rubric, and its final total is off by a dollar, which fails the other critical rubric. It still earns partial credit on three of five rubrics — recognizing it's a one-topping pizza, correctly adding the three-dollar gluten-free surcharge, and explaining its arithmetic — for a weighted rubric score of point four seven. But because it missed two critical rubrics, the task is scored a flat fail.
[S] So a genuinely close, mostly-correct answer and a total blind guess both register as zero on the pass rate.
[G] That's exactly the tension between the two metrics, and it's worth keeping in mind going into the results.
