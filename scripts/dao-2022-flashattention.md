[O] Here's a claim that should sound impossible: you can make a core piece of every Transformer several times faster, without changing a single number it outputs.
[S] Or here's the more grounded version of that claim: you can make it faster by being smarter about where you put data on a chip, which is a real result, but it's an engineering result, not a new idea about attention itself.
[O] Except that engineering result is why BERT training now beats the official speed record, why GPT-2 trains three times faster, and why models can suddenly see sixteen thousand tokens instead of one thousand.
[S] All true. I just want it on the record early that this paper's cleverness is almost entirely about memory traffic, not about rethinking what attention computes. Let's see if that distinction survives the hour.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an AI optimist, an AI skeptic, and a visiting scholar sit down with one paper at a time.
[S] Today's paper is FlashAttention, Fast and Memory-Efficient Exact Attention with I-O Awareness, by Tri Dao, Daniel Fu, Stefano Ermon, Atri Rudra, and Christopher Ré, out of Stanford and the University at Buffalo, published at NeurIPS in twenty twenty-two.
[O] Joining us is Priya, who has spent a lot of time inside this kernel. Priya, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. This paper is sitting at four thousand, seven hundred eighty-seven citations and counting, and honestly that undersells it — it's less "an influential paper" and more "the piece of infrastructure everyone building long-context models quietly depends on."
[S] Let's ground this before we get to the cleverness. Self-attention is quadratic in sequence length — everyone knows that much. Where exactly does the cost bite?
[G] Concretely: for an input of length N, you form an N-by-N matrix of similarity scores, run softmax over it, then multiply by the value vectors. That N-by-N matrix is the bottleneck — both in floating-point operations and, the paper argues, in something almost nobody was measuring: memory traffic.
[O] And there was already a whole cottage industry trying to fix this before FlashAttention showed up — Reformer, Linformer, Performer, Longformer, Big Bird.
[G] Right — sparse approximations, low-rank approximations, and combinations of the two. All of them cut the asymptotic FLOP count down to linear or near-linear in sequence length. And almost none of them delivered a real wall-clock speedup, or got adopted widely.
[S] That's the detail I want to sit on, because it's a little embarrassing for a whole subfield. Why would cutting the FLOP count not make something faster?
[G] Because on a modern GPU, compute has gotten much faster relative to memory bandwidth. Most operations inside a Transformer are bottlenecked by moving data around, not by doing arithmetic. The authors call this being memory-bound rather than compute-bound, and softmax, plus the masking and dropout wrapped around it, are exactly the kind of memory-bound operations where counting FLOPs measures the wrong thing entirely.
[O] So the whole prior literature was optimizing a number that doesn't track the thing anyone actually cares about.
[G] That's the paper's central diagnosis. The missing principle, in their words, is I-O awareness — carefully accounting for reads and writes between the different levels of GPU memory.
[S] Walk us through that memory hierarchy, because it's the whole ballgame here.
[G] On an A100, there are two tiers that matter. On-chip S-RAM is fast, about nineteen terabytes per second, but tiny — only around twenty megabytes total across the chip. Then there's H-B-M, the high-bandwidth memory that holds your forty to eighty gigabytes of activations and weights, but runs at roughly one and a half terabytes per second. Standard attention writes that full N-by-N score matrix, and the softmax output, out to H-B-M and reads them back multiple times. That shuttling back and forth is the bottleneck.
[O] So the fix is: just don't write the big matrix to the slow memory at all.
[G] That's the entire one-line pitch, yes. FlashAttention never materializes the full N-by-N matrix in H-B-M. It splits the query, key, and value matrices into blocks, loads each block into S-RAM, and does the computation there.
[S] But softmax needs the whole row to normalize correctly. How do you block that without changing the answer you get?
[G] This is the one genuinely non-obvious trick, and it's worth being precise about it: online softmax. For each row, you keep a running maximum and a running sum as you sweep through blocks. When a new block of keys and values arrives, you rescale your accumulated numerator and denominator by the ratio between the old running max and the new one, fold in the new block's contribution, and move on. It's the standard numerically-stable softmax decomposition, just applied incrementally instead of all at once on a fully materialized row.
[O] That's the part I think gets lost in the "fast kernel" framing — exact is doing real work in the title. This isn't a quality-for-speed trade like the approximate methods.
[G] Right, and it carries through to the backward pass too, which is the second half of the trick. Normally you'd need to keep the full score matrix and the softmax output around to compute gradients. FlashAttention instead keeps only the output and those two small running statistics — the max and the sum — and recomputes the scores on-chip during the backward pass from blocks that are already sitting in S-RAM.
[S] Recomputing sounds like more work, not less.
[G] It is more work, measured in FLOPs. On GPT-2 medium's attention, the paper reports seventy-five point two gigaflops for FlashAttention against sixty-six point six for standard attention — more arithmetic. But it needs nine times less H-B-M traffic, four point four gigabytes moved instead of forty point three, and that is what wall-clock time actually tracks. Runtime drops from forty-one point seven milliseconds down to seven point three. Trading FLOPs for memory traffic is a clear net win once memory traffic is the actual bottleneck.
[O] And on its own, before any end-to-end training number — how much does fusing the whole pipeline into one kernel save on the attention computation itself?
[G] Seven point six times, on GPT-2, measured against the standard PyTorch implementation. Matrix multiply, masking, softmax, dropout, second matrix multiply — all fused into a single kernel so nothing round-trips to H-B-M between steps. That fusion is what makes the tiling worth doing in the first place.
[S] And they didn't just measure this empirically.
[G] They proved it. There's a theorem bounding FlashAttention's H-B-M accesses at order N-squared d-squared over M, where d is the head dimension and M is the S-RAM size, against order N-d plus N-squared for standard attention. For the head dimensions people actually use, sixty-four to a hundred twenty-eight, d-squared is many times smaller than M, so that's many times fewer accesses — up to nine times, matching the measurement exactly. There's also a matching lower bound: no exact attention algorithm can asymptotically beat that access count across all S-RAM sizes. So this isn't just a clever kernel — it's provably close to the best any exact method can do.
[S] One thing I want flagged right now, because it matters for the debate later: all of that lives inside one hand-written CUDA kernel, correct? Not something that falls out of ordinary PyTorch.
[G] Correct, and the authors say so plainly in their own limitations section. Writing a new attention variant means writing a new CUDA kernel by hand, in a much lower-level language than PyTorch, and that kernel isn't guaranteed to transfer efficiently to the next GPU generation.
[O] Let's get to what all of this actually bought them, because the training-time numbers are what made this un-ignorable.
[G] BERT-large hits the MLPerf target of seventy-two percent masked-language-model accuracy in seventeen point four minutes with FlashAttention, averaged over ten runs on eight A100s, versus twenty minutes for Nvidia's own MLPerf one-point-one record submission. That's fifteen percent faster than the fastest recorded run anyone had published at the time.
[S] Fifteen percent off an already-optimized, record-holding baseline is a real result. I'll grant that one without a fight.
[G] GPT-2 is the bigger swing: up to three and a half times faster than the Hugging Face implementation, and around one point seven to one point eight times faster than Megatron-L-M, at exactly the same validation perplexity — eighteen point two for the small model, matching to the decimal. Long-Range Arena runs two point four times faster with plain FlashAttention, and two point eight times faster with the block-sparse variant.
[O] And here's the part I think gets undersold: because it's fast and its memory scales linearly instead of quadratically, they could just run longer sequences. That bought them quality, not only speed.
[G] GPT-2 small at four thousand tokens of context trains thirty percent faster than Megatron running only one thousand tokens, while landing zero point seven points better perplexity — seventeen point five versus eighteen point two. On long-document classification it's similar: MIMIC-Three, intensive-care discharge summaries, gains four point three points going from five hundred twelve tokens up to sixteen thousand. E-C-t-H-R, European Court of Human Rights cases, gains eight point five points going from five hundred twelve up to eight thousand. Average those two and you land on the six point four points the abstract quotes.
[S] I want to push on one specific number: Path-X, sixty-one point four percent accuracy. That headline reads as "solved," but it's barely above a coin flip.
[G] Fair correction, and the paper's own language is careful about it — it says better than chance, not solved. Path-X asks a Transformer to decide whether two points in a black-and-white image are connected by a path, fed in one pixel at a time across sixteen thousand steps. Every prior Transformer either ran out of memory or scored at random on it. FlashAttention is the first to clear chance at all, and block-sparse FlashAttention pushes further, to sequence length sixty-four thousand for the Path-256 variant, scoring sixty-three point one percent.
[O] Genuine firsts because nobody else could even attempt that sequence length in the first place, not because the raw accuracy itself is impressive.
[G] Exactly that distinction, and the paper is honest about it.
[O] Alright, let me make the strongest version of the optimist case. This paper didn't invent a new capability out of thin air — it removed a bottleneck that was quietly capping every long-context Transformer built after it. Once attention stopped being the wall, context windows went from a couple thousand tokens to tens and eventually hundreds of thousands, and essentially every serious model since has some descendant of this kernel underneath it. That is about as high a leverage-to-novelty ratio as a systems paper gets.
[S] Here's my deflation. This is an engineering result dressed up as a discovery. Tiling and recomputation are both, in the paper's own words, well-established techniques — blocked softmax already existed, gradient checkpointing already existed. What FlashAttention actually contributed was fusing them into one very well hand-tuned CUDA kernel, for one specific GPU, the A100. Every headline number in this paper is A100-specific. Change the S-RAM size or the GPU generation, and you are rewriting the kernel from scratch.
[G] Both of those points are defensible, and the paper doesn't try to hide the second one. Its own limitations section names compiling to CUDA as the open problem: you need a new hand-written kernel per attention variant, and it isn't portable across GPU architectures. They even point to Halide, from image processing, as the kind of high-level-to-low-level compiler that doesn't yet exist for attention.
[S] So the authors basically agree with my skeptical read.
[G] On portability, completely. But I'd push back on "not a discovery." The I-O-complexity theorem and its matching lower bound are genuine theoretical contributions, not just engineering — they show tiling plus recomputation isn't merely a good kernel, it's asymptotically close to optimal for any exact attention algorithm on any S-RAM size. That result doesn't depend on the A100 specifics at all.
[O] Which is exactly why it generalized the way it did. Stepping outside this specific paper for a second: FlashAttention two and FlashAttention three followed as newer kernels tuned for newer GPU generations, built on the same tiling-and-recomputation argument.
[G] That's true, though worth flagging as outside what this twenty twenty-two paper itself reports — the authors here only promise the concept should generalize, they don't build the sequel. What they do show, in their own words, is that they saw the portability problem coming and named it explicitly rather than pretending it away.
[S] I'll take that as a real point in the paper's favor. My last objection, though: the quality gains — the perplexity improvement, the long-document lift — aren't really from FlashAttention itself. They're from the longer context that FlashAttention's speed and memory savings merely make affordable. The kernel is a speed-and-memory result; calling it a quality result blurs a step that shouldn't be blurred.
[G] That's precisely right, and the paper itself is careful about it — it frames the perplexity and classification gains as coming from modeling longer sequences, with FlashAttention as the thing that makes that affordable. The attention computation itself didn't get smarter. A context length that was previously out of memory became affordable.
[O] I'll still count "made a previously impossible thing possible" as a real win, even if it's one step removed from the kernel itself.
[S] Agreed — I just don't want it counted twice, once as a speed win and again as an independent quality win.
[O] Zooming out, then — what's the actual footprint of this paper, four years on?
[G] It's become close to a default. Long-context language models, and the retrieval and agent pipelines that depend on cheap long context, essentially all lean on FlashAttention or one of its two successor kernels. It reframed how the field thinks about attention efficiency — away from counting FLOPs, toward counting memory movement — and that reframing outlived the specific A100 numbers it was measured on.
[S] It's also a useful cautionary tale for anyone evaluating a systems claim: check whether the reported metric is the one that actually determines wall-clock cost, and check whether exact or approximate is doing quiet work inside a results table. A lot of prior attention papers reported FLOP reductions that had nothing to do with how fast the thing actually ran in practice.
[O] Which, honestly, is a good discipline for judging any efficiency claim in machine learning, not just attention kernels specifically.
[G] If there's one sentence to take from this paper, it's that attention was never really slow because of the arithmetic — it was slow because of how much data it shuffled between fast and slow memory, and once you account for that properly, you can compute the exact same thing, several times faster.
[O] My takeaway: this is the rare optimization paper where exact and faster both hold at once, and that combination is exactly why it became infrastructure instead of a footnote.
[S] Mine: respect the theorem, respect the kernel, but remember that hand-written CUDA tuned to one specific GPU generation is a fragile place for infrastructure to live — and credit where due, the authors flagged that fragility themselves before anyone else did.
