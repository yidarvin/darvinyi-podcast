[S] Picture this: a language model that decides, on its own, mid-thought, that it doesn't know something — stops, fires off a search query, reads what comes back, and keeps reasoning from there.
[O] And nobody wrote it a script for when to search. The only signal it ever receives is whether the final answer is exactly right. That's reinforcement learning with almost nothing holding its hand.
[S] "Almost nothing holding its hand" is a great pitch line. Whether it's a responsible way to train a model that talks to the open internet is a separate question, and I've got several.
[O] Which is exactly why we brought backup today.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where we take one paper from the Litsearch archive and argue about what it actually proves. I'm your optimist host.
[S] And I'm the skeptic host. Today's paper is Search-R-one: Training L-L-Ms to Reason and Leverage Search Engines with Reinforcement Learning, by Bowen Jin and colleagues out of U-I-U-C, UMass Amherst, and Google Cloud AI Research. It's a C-O-L-M twenty twenty-five paper.
[O] And joining us is Julian, who's spent real time with this one. Julian, welcome to the show.
[G] Glad to be here. This paper lands right after DeepSeek-R-one showed that pure outcome-based reinforcement learning can teach a model to reason well. The question this group asked was simple to state and hard to answer: does that same trick still work once the model can reach outside itself and touch a search engine?
[S] Before Search-R-one, how were people actually connecting language models to search? I want the baseline picture before we get to what's new.
[G] Two dominant patterns, and the paper is explicit about both. First, retrieval-augmented generation — R-A-G — retrieve once based on the input, stuff the passages into the context window, generate. Second, treat the search engine as a tool: prompt a model, in the style of ReAct or I-R-CoT, to interleave reasoning and search calls, or fine-tune it to do that on labeled trajectories, the way Toolformer does.
[O] And the paper's complaint with both is basically the same complaint: neither one is actually trained to search well.
[G] Exactly that. R-A-G's retrieval step is fixed and untrained — it just runs once, no matter whether the question needs one hop or five. And the tool-use approaches either lean entirely on prompting, which doesn't generalize past what the model already knows how to do, or they need large, expensive labeled trajectories showing exactly when and how to search, which is hard to scale.
[S] So the pitch is: instead of hand-designing the search strategy, learn it. Where does DeepSeek-R-one fit into that?
[G] DeepSeek-R-one had just shown, a couple months earlier, that you could skip the hand-holding on reasoning itself — no supervised chain of thought data, no process reward model, just a single outcome reward on the final answer — and the model would learn to reason well anyway. Search-R-one's move is to ask whether that same minimalism survives contact with a real, non-differentiable tool sitting in the middle of the rollout.
[O] Which is the genuinely interesting bet here. You're now training through something you can't backpropagate through.
[G] Right, and that's the first of three problems the paper names up front. Can training stay stable once retrieved text — tokens the policy never generated — is sitting inside the sequence being optimized. Second, can the model learn genuinely multi-turn search, not just one retrieve-then-answer step. Third, is a reward this simple actually enough signal to teach useful search behavior at all.
[O] Let's get into the mechanics, then. What does a rollout actually look like?
[G] Four special tokens structure everything. The model reasons inside think tags. When it decides it needs outside information, it writes a query inside search tags. The system pauses generation right there, actually queries the retriever, and splices the results back in wrapped in information tags. Then the model keeps going — it can reason more, search again, however many times it wants — until it commits to a final answer inside answer tags, or it hits a hard cap on the number of search calls, which the paper sets to four.
[S] "However many times it wants" — so nothing in the format tells it when to stop searching, or how many hops a given question needs?
[G] Nothing does. That's deliberate. The authors are explicit that they impose no content-specific bias beyond the structural format itself — no instruction to double-check itself, no nudge toward a particular problem-solving style. They want whatever search behavior emerges to be something the reinforcement learning process discovered, not something they hard-coded in.
[O] So the actual engineering problem becomes: how do you run policy-gradient reinforcement learning over a sequence that's part model output and part search-engine output?
[G] That's the paper's real technical contribution, and it's a small idea that turns out to matter enormously: retrieved-token loss masking. A rollout mixes tokens the model generated with tokens copied straight out of retrieved documents. Compute the P-P-O or G-R-P-O loss over the whole sequence indiscriminately, and you're asking the policy gradient to take credit or blame for text it never produced. The paper shows that badly distorts training. So they define an indicator that's one for model-generated tokens and zero for retrieved tokens, and restrict both the policy-gradient term and the K-L penalty to only the positions where that indicator is one. The retrieved passages still condition what the model generates next — they're just excluded from the gradient itself.
[S] And the reward function sitting on top of all that?
[G] Deliberately bare. Exact match between the predicted answer and the gold answer, full stop. No learned reward model anywhere, and no format reward. DeepSeek-R-one used a format reward to encourage the right tag structure; these authors say they didn't need one, because the model already picked up strong structural adherence on its own.
[O] That's the part I find genuinely striking. You're not telling it how many times to search, you're not rewarding it for searching at all, only for getting the final string right, and multi-turn search behavior falls out anyway.
[S] "Falls out anyway" is doing a lot of work in that sentence, and we'll come back to it. Let's see the numbers first.
[G] They train on a merged Natural Questions and HotpotQA set, and evaluate across seven question-answering benchmarks — three general ones, four multi-hop, including a hard one called Bamboogle — using Qwen two-point-five at three and seven billion parameters, in both base and instruction-tuned form.
[O] And the headline?
[G] Twenty-four percent average relative improvement over R-A-G baselines on the seven-billion model, twenty percent on the three-billion model. Critically, every baseline in that comparison shares the same retriever, the same twenty-eighteen Wikipedia snapshot, the same three retrieved passages per query, and the same underlying Qwen checkpoint. That's not a small thing — plenty of papers in this space let a better retriever or a bigger corpus quietly do some of the work.
[S] Give me one concrete number instead of an average.
[G] Bamboogle, the hardest multi-hop set. R-A-G scores about twenty-one percent exact match. Search-R-one-base scores just over forty-three percent — more than double. On Natural Questions, Search-R-one-base lands near forty-eight percent against R-A-G's thirty-five.
[O] And it beats plain R-one — same recipe, same reward, just no search access — on every single one of the seven datasets. R-one alone averages under twenty-eight percent; give it search, and the average jumps past forty-three.
[S] Now the ablations, because that's where I actually trust a paper. What happens if you strip out the retrieved-token masking?
[G] The seven-dataset average on the seven-billion base model drops from forty-three point one percent down to thirty-four point three. That's a bigger swing than almost any other design choice in the paper. It's genuinely the load-bearing trick, not a minor stability tweak.
[S] And the number of passages retrieved per search call?
[G] They sweep it across one, three, and five. One passage underperforms, at thirty-seven point five percent — not enough recall. Five passages actually does worse than three, forty percent versus forty-three — the paper's read is that extra, less-relevant passages dilute the reasoning context rather than help it. Three is the sweet spot.
[O] What about P-P-O versus G-R-P-O? Does the choice of reinforcement-learning algorithm matter?
[G] G-R-P-O converges faster early on, since P-P-O needs a warm-up period for its critic. But G-R-P-O is also the one that can suffer a late-training reward collapse, which the paper documents directly in its training curves. P-P-O is slower to get going but stays stable, and by the end, both land at comparable final performance.
[S] There's a group-size ablation buried in the appendix I think is more interesting than that P-P-O-versus-G-R-P-O headline. Did you catch that one?
[G] I did, it's a nice one. Bigger sampling groups in G-R-P-O converge faster during training, which is why the main experiments default to a group of five. But when you test generalization to held-out data, group size one — which is just plain REINFORCE — actually generalizes best, at forty-one percent, while group size five generalizes worst, at thirty-five. Faster convergence and better generalization pull in opposite directions here.
[O] There's also a case study I have to bring up, because it's the clearest illustration of what "emergent multi-turn search" looks like in practice. The question is which city and state a fragrance brand's namesake singer was born in — the fragrance is called Curious.
[G] Right, and R-one without search just answers "Houston" — it confidently attributes the fragrance to Beyoncé, who was born in Houston, purely from memory, and it's wrong. Search-R-one searches for the fragrance first, learns it's actually a Britney Spears product, searches again for her birthplace, gets McComb, Mississippi, and then, even though it already has the answer, searches a third time just to confirm the city belongs to that state. The paper calls that self-verification behavior. It wasn't explicitly rewarded, it just showed up.
[O] Okay, let me make the strongest case for why this matters. This is a genuinely minimal recipe: one reward, four tokens, no labeled trajectories, no reward model, and it produces a policy that searches better than R-A-G, better than prompted tool use, and better than the same reinforcement-learning recipe without search, consistently, across model sizes, across base and instruct variants, across two different training algorithms. They even show it keeps scaling, a fourteen-billion model pushes the average past forty-seven percent. That's not a narrow result, that's a recipe that looks portable.
[S] Here's my strongest deflationary case. Every number we just discussed lives inside one closed, static twenty-eighteen Wikipedia snapshot. There is no test against live or open web search anywhere in this paper. Julian, is that a fair characterization?
[G] It's fair. Nothing in the main paper or the appendix touches an online retriever. A closely related paper, R-one Searcher, posted just five days before this one on a very similar multi-hop search-and-reinforcement-learning setup, actually runs its policy against the live Google Search A-P-I on Bamboogle specifically to show it transfers. Search-R-one doesn't run that test.
[S] Second point: exact match is a strict reward for open-ended questions. A correct answer phrased slightly differently from the gold string gets zero credit. Does the paper engage with that at all?
[G] Not directly. No F-one variant, no softer matching is tried here. And it's worth flagging that same R-one Searcher paper ran its own reward ablation and found F-one-based rewards beating exact-match rewards by up to fifty-two point six percent, with exact match producing the shortest, weakest responses of everything it tested. That's not proof Search-R-one is leaving performance on the table, but it's a real, adjacent data point suggesting it plausibly is.
[O] I'll grant that one. Though I'd push back slightly, the fact that the model still learns rich multi-hop, self-verifying behavior under such a stingy reward is itself informative. It suggests the signal doesn't need to be generous to work.
[S] Sure, but "it works" and "it's leaving nothing on the table" are different claims. Third point, and this is the one that worries me most: contamination. Natural Questions, TriviaQA, HotpotQA — these are heavily republished benchmarks Qwen two-point-five's pretraining data has almost certainly seen in some form. Is there any check that the model isn't just recalling memorized answers and dressing them up with a search call?
[G] No such check in this paper. That's a real gap, and it's one a later paper in the same line of work, DeepResearcher, explicitly patches. They add a pass-at-k contamination filter for exactly this reason. Search-R-one doesn't have one. So when the R-one-without-search baseline still scores meaningfully above zero on these sets, some of that is memorized knowledge, and we can't cleanly separate how much of Search-R-one's added lift is "learned to search well" versus "got lucky finding the memorized answer via a slightly different path."
[O] Where I still land optimistic is the ablations themselves. The masking result, the top-k sweep, the group-size trade-off, those are internally controlled comparisons, roughly contamination-neutral by construction, since it affects every arm of the comparison about equally. The mechanism claims survive even if the absolute headline numbers are inflated somewhat by memorization.
[S] That's a fair distinction, and I'll take it. Whether retrieved-token masking matters isn't contaminated by whether Qwen has seen Natural Questions before. Whether this recipe is production-ready against the live web is the question I still don't think this paper answers.
[G] I'd add one more limiting factor while we're being precise: everything here is one model family, Qwen two-point-five at three, seven, and fourteen billion parameters. There's no test on a Llama or Mistral backbone, so we don't yet know whether the recipe is genuinely general or somewhat tuned to how Qwen behaves.
[O] If this holds up under an online-search test and a cross-family check, the implication is pretty large. You'd have a cheap, general way to teach any instruction-tuned model to use a search tool well, without ever hand-labeling a single search trajectory.
[S] And if it doesn't hold up, it's a cautionary tale for exactly the kind of evaluation practice we harp on in this show, a clean-looking benchmark win that's actually clean-looking because the sandbox is static and the training data has likely already leaked into the eval sets. That's a contamination and construct-validity story as much as a modeling story.
[G] That tension is really the throughline of this whole line of work, and it's why the follow-on papers exist. R-one Searcher pushing the reward design and live-search generalization, DeepResearcher pushing the contamination screen. Search-R-one is best read as the paper that established the mechanism was even possible, cleanly, with a controlled comparison, not as the paper that proved it's ready for the open web.
[G] My one-sentence version: retrieved-token loss masking is the mechanism that makes search-and-reason reinforcement learning stable, and almost everything else in this paper is evidence for that one design choice.
[O] Mine: a single exact-match reward, with zero supervision on strategy, is enough to produce multi-turn, self-verifying search behavior. That's a genuinely bigger deal than the headline percentage gains.
