Joining us is Naomi, who's studied this paper and its identification strategy closely. Welcome, Naomi.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is one of those papers where almost everyone knows the headline number and far fewer people know how carefully hedged the paper itself is about that number.
[S] Start with why it exists, Naomi. What was the state of trade economics before this?
[G] Through the nineteen eighties and nineties, the debate was almost entirely about wage inequality, and imports from low-wage countries were a minor character in it. In nineteen ninety-one, low-income countries accounted for just two point nine percent of U.S. manufacturing imports. The consensus explanation for rising wage dispersion was skill-biased technical change, not trade, going back to work by Katz and Autor in the late nineties.
[O] And then China happened.
[G] Then China happened, fast. The low-income-country share of U.S. imports climbed from fifteen percent in two thousand to twenty-eight percent by two thousand seven, with China alone responsible for eighty-nine percent of that increase. The U.S. spending share on Chinese goods rose from point six percent to four point six percent over those same years, with a visible inflection right at China's two thousand one accession to the World Trade Organization. Manufacturing's share of the working-age population fell by a third over that span, from twelve point six percent to eight point four.
[S] That's a correlation. What made the authors think it was more than that?
[G] Partly scale. China ran a current-account surplus averaging five percent of G-D-P through the two thousands, almost exactly matching the U.S. current-account deficit. So American industries faced a sustained rise in import competition with no offsetting rise in demand for U.S. exports to soak it up. That's a very different shock than ordinary trade reallocation.
[O] Why couldn't existing methods just measure that directly?
[G] Because none of them were built for this scale of question. National time series give you far too few data points to separate a trade shock from everything else happening that decade. Industry or plant-level studies, Bernard, Jensen and Schott's work, Liu and Trefler's, can show an import-exposed plant shrinking, but they're silent on where displaced workers end up. And the dominant trade-theory approach mapped industry price changes into wage changes through a Heckscher-Ohlin model, which speaks only to wages, nothing about unemployment, labor-force exit, or disability rolls.
[S] So the fix is a new unit of analysis entirely.
[G] Right. The paper makes the regional economy the unit of analysis, seven hundred twenty-two U.S. commuting zones covering the entire mainland, differing enormously in exposure to Chinese import growth because of which industries they specialized in back in nineteen ninety. Overall manufacturing share explains under a quarter of that variation. What matters is the specific industries, because China's comparative advantage was concentrated. By two thousand seven, China supplied over forty percent of U.S. imports in four narrow industries, and over thirty percent in twenty-eight more, apparel, textiles, furniture, electrical appliances.
[O] Walk us through the actual design, Naomi. How do you turn differential exposure into a causal number?
[G] The paper starts from a small open-economy trade model, monopolistic competition, in the Helpman-Krugman tradition, where a region's wages and employment respond to two Chinese shocks, growth in China's export-supply capability, and growth in Chinese import demand. Because the U.S. runs a large, persistent trade deficit with China, these shocks can change the absolute size of a region's traded sector, not just reshuffle workers between industries. That's what lets a regional import shock show up as a net local employment effect instead of a wash.
[S] And the exposure measure itself?
[G] For each commuting zone, you take every industry's share of that zone's employment, weight it by how much U.S. imports from China grew in that industry nationally, and normalize by the zone's total employment. A zone is exposed if it started out specialized in industries that later saw large import growth from China.
[O] Which sounds clean, but I can already guess the problem.
[S] The problem is endogeneity. Realized U.S. imports from China could themselves respond to U.S. industry demand or labor-supply conditions. A zone with rising local demand in some industry could show both rising apparent exposure and independently rising employment, biasing a naive regression, potentially in either direction.
[G] Exactly the concern, and it's the paper's central empirical move. They instrument realized U.S. import exposure with import exposure built the same way, but using each industry's Chinese export growth into eight other high-income countries instead, Australia, Denmark, Finland, Germany, Japan, New Zealand, Spain, and Switzerland, apportioned using each zone's industry employment shares from a decade earlier, to avoid contemporaneous simultaneity.
[O] So the logic is, if Chinese exports are rising into unrelated rich countries too, that's telling you something about China, not about anything happening inside the U.S. labor market.
[G] That's the identifying logic. The component of rising Chinese imports common to the U.S. and these eight economies is plausibly driven by China's own falling trade costs and rising productivity, its move off central planning, W-T-O accession, opening to multinational investment, rather than anything endogenous to U.S. demand. Correlated demand shocks would actually bias the design toward understating the effect. Correlated technology shocks are harder to rule out, though the estimate holds up well under robustness checks, dropping housing-linked industries only moves the coefficient from point six oh to point five seven.
[S] Is there a first-stage number, something showing the instrument actually has bite?
[G] There is, and it's strong. A one-thousand-dollar predicted increase from the instrument corresponds to an eight-hundred-fifteen-dollar measured increase in actual exposure, a first-stage coefficient of point eight one five, standard error point oh nine two, a t-statistic near nine. The reduced form, exposure predicted by the instrument regressed directly on manufacturing employment share, comes in at minus point three four, standard error point oh seven one, across all seven hundred twenty-two zones.
[O] Give us the headline result, Naomi.
[G] The fully controlled two-stage least squares specification, census-division fixed effects plus demographic and occupational controls, puts the coefficient at minus point five nine six percentage points of manufacturing employment share per one-thousand-dollar rise in decadal exposure, standard error point oh eight seven. That's roughly double the O-L-S estimate, between point one seven and point two seven, consistent with O-L-S being attenuated by measurement error and demand-side simultaneity.
[S] Benchmark that against what actually happened.
[G] Exposure rose eleven hundred forty dollars per worker in the nineties, and a further eighteen hundred thirty-nine in two thousand to two thousand seven. That predicts manufacturing declines of point six eight and one point one percentage points those decades, against observed declines of two point oh seven and two points. So the gross attribution is thirty-three percent of the nineties decline, fifty-five percent of the two-thousands decline, forty-four percent overall.
[O] And that's the number everyone quotes.
[G] It is, but it's a gross figure, attributing all the variation in realized Chinese imports to supply-side forces. Isolating just the roughly forty-eight percent the authors' own decomposition calls supply-driven gives a more conservative sixteen, twenty-six, and twenty-one percent. In levels, that's about five hundred forty-eight thousand manufacturing jobs lost in the nineties and nine hundred eighty-two thousand more in the two-thousands, roughly one and a half million total on the conservative cut.
[S] Here's what surprised me most reading this. No migration response at all?
[G] None that's statistically distinguishable from zero. Commuting-zone working-age population shows no significant response to import exposure in the full-controls specification. Workers stay put. So the manufacturing losses translate almost one-for-one into non-employment rather than being absorbed elsewhere locally. A one-thousand-dollar shock cuts manufacturing employment four point two percent, with no significant offsetting rise in non-manufacturing employment, while raising the count of unemployed and non-participating adults four point nine and two point one percent respectively.
[O] Break down where that non-employment actually lands.
[G] Of the point seven seven point decline in total employment relative to population, about three-quarters is direct manufacturing loss. Of the corresponding rise in non-employment, only about a quarter is measured unemployment, three-quarters is people leaving the labor force entirely. Nearly ten percent of the workers who leave employment land on federal disability insurance.
[S] That's the disability finding people cite constantly.
[G] For good reason. The dollar increase in per-capita disability payments in exposed zones runs more than thirty times the dollar increase in Trade Adjustment Assistance, the program actually designed to compensate trade-displaced workers. T-A-A is temporary. Disability insurance is functionally for life.
[O] What about wages?
[G] Average weekly earnings across a zone fall point seven six log points per one-thousand-dollar exposure, but the effect is concentrated entirely outside manufacturing. Mean manufacturing wages show no significant effect at all, plausibly because it's the lower-productivity manufacturing workers who lose their jobs, leaving a better-paid group behind. Non-manufacturing wages fall almost the identical amount for both college and non-college workers, which reads as a spillover onto local non-traded services, retail, construction, personal services, rather than a within-industry effect.

It's exactly the design you'd want, a real, quantifiable shock, an instrument built from genuinely independent variation, and it doesn't stop at an employment number, it traces the damage through unemployment, labor-force exit, and onto specific transfer programs. That's a complete picture of what a trade shock does to a place, not just an average.

[S] And the deeper issue is what a commuting-zone design can't see by construction. It's a relative comparison. If export-driven demand gains from the same China trade expansion land in a different zone than the import losses, this design is structurally blind to that offset.
[O] Is that just a hypothetical, or has someone actually modeled it?
[G] It's been directly modeled, twice. Caliendo, Dvorkin and Parro, in a two thousand nineteen Econometrica paper, build a full dynamic multi-region trade model and, for the identical two thousand to two thousand seven window, attribute about five hundred fifty thousand manufacturing job losses to the China shock, sixteen percent of the observed decline, roughly half this paper's own supply-driven estimate, and they find the U.S. gains in the aggregate once consumer welfare is counted. Feenstra and Sasahara, in two thousand eighteen, using global input-output linkages, estimate Chinese import competition cost about two million jobs, but that U.S. export growth, driven substantially by services, created about six point six million, for a net gain of roughly one point seven million jobs nationally.
[O] So the honest optimist case isn't that the paper is wrong, it's that the number people repeat is the wrong number to repeat.
[S] That's exactly my point. The local coefficient is real. [G] Both of you are making claims the paper itself would recognize. The local, relative finding, more-exposed commuting zones lost more manufacturing employment, with the damage landing on non-employment and disability rather than migration or manufacturing wages, is well identified and has held up under a decade of scrutiny. What raises my confidence further is the authors' own two thousand twenty-one follow-up, on the persistence of the China shock, which extends the sample to twenty nineteen and finds the adverse effects persisted rather than reversing. But the national headline number was always the load-bearing assumption to interrogate, and that's exactly what the general-equilibrium literature has been doing to it.
[S] Naomi, be honest. If Caliendo, Dvorkin and Parro's number had come out first, would this paper be remembered as fondly as it is?
[G] Probably not with the same one-sentence headline, no. But the local design and the national extrapolation are answering different questions, and the paper is fairly explicit that the aggregate figure depends on how much of realized import growth you're willing to call purely supply-driven. The problem isn't that the authors hid the caveat, it's that the caveat travels much worse than the number does.
