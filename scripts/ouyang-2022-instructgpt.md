[S] Here is the sentence that made this paper famous. A one point three billion parameter model beat a one hundred seventy five billion parameter model in head to head preference. Over a hundred times fewer parameters, and it still won.
[O] And people quote that line like it settles an argument. Bigger isn't everything, alignment matters more. I think it might be one of the most consequential single results in the last several years of language modeling.
[S] I think it's a very specific, very engineered win condition, and if you don't read the fine print you'll walk away believing something the paper doesn't actually claim.
[O] Which is exactly why we brought in someone who has read the fine print for us.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar dig into one paper at a time. Today's paper is "Training Language Models to Follow Instructions with Human Feedback," from Long Ouyang, Jeff Wu, Ryan Lowe, and a large team of colleagues at OpenAI, published in 2022. It's the paper that introduced InstructGPT.
[S] This is the paper that turned reinforcement learning from human feedback into the default recipe for taking a raw language model and making it something you'd actually want to talk to. A huge amount of what came after — every RLHF-trained assistant since — traces its lineage back to these three steps.
[G] Thanks for having me both. One thing up front: I've spent a lot of time with this paper, but I wasn't one of the people who wrote it. So when I say "the authors found" or "they report," that's me describing their work, not claiming any of it myself.
[S] Good ground rule. Marcus, before we argue about the number, what problem was actually broken here?
[G] GPT-3 is trained on exactly one objective: predict the next token on a chunk of internet text. Nothing in that rewards being helpful, truthful, or safe — it just rewards sounding like a plausible continuation. So if you ask it a direct question, it might answer, or it might keep generating more questions, or drift off topic, because to the model all of those look equally valid.
[O] Which is why the field spent years hand-engineering prompts to coax it into behaving. Few-shot examples, magic phrasing, all of it.
[G] Right, and the authors are explicit that prompting is a workaround, not a fix — you're still fighting a misaligned objective one prompt at a time. Their framing borrows a definition from Askell and colleagues: a model should be helpful, honest, and harmless. The direct ancestor is OpenAI's earlier summarization paper, which fine-tuned against human preference rankings for one narrow task instead of a fixed metric. This paper asks whether that trick generalizes across the messy full distribution of real requests — brainstorming, rewriting, extraction, code, question answering — into a model broadly aligned to what the user actually wanted.
[S] So the technique already existed. The ambition here is the scale and the task diversity.
[G] Exactly. Reinforcement learning from human feedback wasn't new. What's new is doing it across this much diversity, on real production traffic, at this scale.
[O] Walk us through the pipeline itself. I know it's three steps.
[G] Three steps, and the order is the whole design. Step one is supervised fine-tuning. The authors hired about forty contractors, screened through Upwork and Scale AI, and had them write demonstrations — real example answers — for around thirteen thousand training prompts, a mix of prompts pulled from the API and prompts the labelers wrote themselves. GPT-3 gets fine-tuned on those demonstrations for sixteen epochs. That alone gives you a decent instruction-follower, and it gives you the starting policy for the next two steps.
[S] Sixteen epochs is a lot for supervised fine-tuning. Doesn't that overfit?
[G] It does, and they say so directly — the model overfits on validation loss after a single epoch. But training longer keeps improving both the reward model score and actual human preference ratings, so they kept going well past where the loss curve says to stop.
[O] Step two?
[G] The reward model. For each prompt, the supervised model samples somewhere between four and nine candidate outputs, and a labeler ranks them from best to worst. That gives you every pairwise comparison inside that ranking. A six billion parameter model is trained to score outputs so the human-preferred one in each pair scores higher — a standard pairwise ranking loss. The reward model dataset ends up around thirty-three thousand prompts.
[S] Why six billion and not the full one hundred seventy five billion, if bigger is supposedly better?
[G] Good catch. A larger reward model was unstable as the value function during reinforcement learning, so six billion was the practical, cheaper choice. There's a subtler detail too. Every comparison from one prompt is correlated with the others, so shuffling them into a normal dataset and training one pass overfits the reward model almost immediately. Their fix was to treat every comparison from a single prompt as one batch element — one forward pass per completion instead of one per pair. That choice is the difference between a reward model that works and one that collapses in an epoch.
[O] That's the kind of detail that never survives the one-paragraph summary of this paper.
[G] It's in section three point five. Easy to skip, but load-bearing.
[S] And step three is the reinforcement learning itself.
[G] Step three is proximal policy optimization, PPO, run against that frozen reward model. But optimize purely against a frozen proxy for long enough and the policy finds ways to game it — outputs that score high without actually being good. So there's a per-token KL penalty pulling the policy back toward the original supervised model, with a coefficient of point zero two. Think of it as a leash: wander too far from the model that generated the reward model's training data, and you pay for it.
[O] Is that InstructGPT?
[G] That's "PPO." InstructGPT proper is a further variant called PPO-ptx, which also mixes in gradients from the original GPT-3 pretraining data during reinforcement learning — eight times as many pretraining examples as reinforcement learning episodes. That's specifically there to fix something they call the alignment tax, which we'll get to. Unless the paper says otherwise, "InstructGPT" means PPO-ptx.
[S] So three models in one lineage, each one patching a specific failure mode of the last.
[G] That's a fair way to put it.
[O] Give me the headline number properly, because I want to say it right on air.
[G] On their own API prompt distribution, judged by human labelers: the one hundred seventy five billion InstructGPT model is preferred over the one hundred seventy five billion GPT-3 model eighty-five percent of the time, plus or minus three points. Against a few-shot-prompted GPT-3 — GPT-3 given a hand-crafted prefix specifically to coax it into instruction-following mode — it's still preferred seventy-one percent of the time, plus or minus four. And the one point three billion PPO-ptx model does come out ahead of the full one hundred seventy five billion GPT-3 baseline too.
[S] Preferred by whom, exactly, and on what?
[G] By the same roughly forty contractors — mostly English-speaking, based in the United States or Southeast Asia — rating prompts drawn from OpenAI's own API traffic, with the test-set customers excluded from training. That distinction matters, and we'll come back to it.
[O] Beyond the head-to-head win rate, what actually changed about the outputs?
[G] Concretely: InstructGPT follows explicit constraints stated in an instruction roughly twice as often as GPT-3. On closed-domain tasks, like summarization, where the answer should only contain information present in the input, InstructGPT hallucinates — makes up facts not in the source — about twenty-one percent of the time, versus forty-one percent for GPT-3. Almost half.
[S] What about truthfulness more broadly, not just closed-domain generation?
[G] On TruthfulQA, InstructGPT gives truthful and informative answers roughly twice as often as GPT-3, and that gap holds even on the subset of questions that weren't adversarially chosen to trip up GPT-3 specifically. On toxicity, using RealToxicityPrompts, InstructGPT produces about twenty-five percent fewer toxic outputs than GPT-3 — but only when it's explicitly prompted to be respectful. Without that instruction, the advantage mostly disappears. And if you prompt it to be maximally toxic, InstructGPT is actually more toxic than GPT-3, not less. It follows that instruction too.
[O] That's a genuinely interesting nuance. It's not "safer by default," it's "more steerable in both directions."
[G] Exactly right. And on bias — Winogender and CrowS-Pairs — there's no improvement at all. The paper is blunt about it: helpfulness moved, toxicity moved a little under the right prompt, bias didn't move.
[S] And the "alignment tax" you mentioned earlier?
[G] Plain PPO, without the pretraining mix, regresses on several public NLP benchmarks versus GPT-3 — SQuAD version two, DROP, HellaSwag, and French-to-English translation on the twenty fifteen WMT set. That's the cost of optimizing hard against the RLHF objective. PPO-ptx substantially closes those gaps, even surpasses GPT-3 on HellaSwag, but still lags on DROP, SQuAD, and translation.
[O] Does the headline result hold up with people who weren't part of the labeling process?
[G] That's one of the stronger checks in the paper. They ran a held-out group of labelers who produced zero training data, and the preference gap for InstructGPT over GPT-3 held at roughly the same size. They also cross-validated the reward model across five labeler groups: it predicts held-out labelers' preferences at about seventy percent accuracy, only a few points below its accuracy on labelers it trained on. For context, inter-annotator agreement itself was seventy-two point six percent among training labelers and seventy-seven point three percent among the held-out group. Even humans agree with each other only about three-quarters of the time on this task.
[S] Seventy-three percent agreement is not a high bar for a ground-truth signal.
[G] It's worth sitting with. It roughly matches the researcher agreement rate in the earlier summarization paper this one builds on, so it's not unusually low for subjective preference data. But it does cap how much signal any reward model trained on this data can possibly recover.
[O] One more number for the record. They also beat instruction-tuned baselines, not just raw GPT-3, right?
[G] Right — InstructGPT beats a GPT-3 model fine-tuned on FLAN seventy-eight percent of the time, and one fine-tuned on T0 seventy-nine percent of the time, both plus or minus four points. So this isn't only a story about beating an untuned base model.
[O] Let me make the optimist case, because it's genuinely strong. This alignment run is cheap. The authors report the total compute for the full one hundred seventy five billion pipeline — supervised fine-tuning plus PPO-ptx — at roughly sixty-five petaflop-days, against three thousand six hundred forty petaflop-days to pretrain GPT-3 in the first place. Under two percent of the pretraining cost, and it buys more helpfulness than scaling the model up over a hundred times. If that ratio holds elsewhere, the field's incentive should tilt toward investing in alignment, not just bigger pretraining runs.
[S] I'll grant the compute point, it's real and it's in the paper. Here's my deflation: "preferred by our labelers, on our prompt distribution, following our instructions" is a preference-alignment claim, not a general capability claim, and the paper practically says so itself. They admit outright that their training prompts are, quote, designed to be used with InstructGPT models — meaning the evaluation distribution favors instruction-tuned models by construction, and GPT-3 gets graded on a format it was never built for. Not fraud, but a much narrower claim than "aligned models beat huge models," and the narrow version is the one that got remembered.
[G] Both points are fair, and the authors address the second one directly — they also evaluate on prompts submitted straight to GPT-3 models, not to InstructGPT, specifically to reduce that bias, and the ranking holds up there too. So it isn't purely an artifact of a friendly test set. The deeper issue is one the paper names and can't fully resolve: reward model overoptimization. You're running PPO against a frozen six billion parameter proxy for human preference — push far enough and the policy learns to exploit whatever the reward model gets wrong, the textbook Goodhart failure. The KL penalty is the only guardrail, and there's no sweep here of how far you can push before the reward diverges from true quality. A later OpenAI paper on reward model overoptimization filled exactly that gap.
[O] So the headline claim survives its own evaluation, but the mechanism producing the gains is still somewhat unaudited within this paper.
[G] That's fair. One more limit the authors are unusually candid about: "truthfulness" here means TruthfulQA plus a closed-domain hallucination rate — real signal, but a small slice of what honesty means. And in their own discussion section they say plainly that they're aligning to a specific, non-representative group: forty contractors, mostly English-speaking, mostly in the United States or Southeast Asia, filtered through instructions written by OpenAI's own researchers, applied to whichever customers happened to be on the API waitlist — a waitlist that itself started from OpenAI's own professional networks. They explicitly say they're not claiming this group's preferences are the right ones to align to.
[S] That's the sentence I'd put on a slide. It's rare for a paper this influential to state its own scope limitation that plainly.
[O] Point to the skeptic on scope. But I still think the durable story is that RLHF works, it's cheap relative to pretraining, and it generalizes to held-out labelers.
[G] I think both readings coexist without contradiction. The method proved durable — nearly every RLHF pipeline since traces its lineage to these three steps. But the specific number, eighty-five percent, or "one point three billion beats one hundred seventy five billion," is a statement about preference on a curated distribution, not a general capability ranking. Conflating the two is exactly the mistake the paper's own limitations section tries to head off.
[S] Does any of this connect to how models get evaluated today, or is it mostly historical at this point?
[G] It connects directly. Every complaint about a modern judge-model evaluation setup — the judge has blind spots, the policy being trained can learn to exploit them, human rater agreement caps how clean your ground truth can be — is present here in miniature, with a reward model standing in for the judge. That seventy-three percent inter-annotator agreement number is basically the same problem later work on rubric-based and judge-based evaluation spends years trying to tighten. Human preference as a training signal, noise and scope limits included, first got proven out at real scale here, and a lot of the contamination and overoptimization concerns in later RLHF work descend directly from caveats already sitting in this paper's own discussion section.
[O] It's also the direct ancestor of the "alignment tax" framing people still reach for — measuring not just whether alignment helps on the target distribution, but what it costs everywhere else. Their finding that the tax can be mostly, if not entirely, mitigated by mixing in pretraining gradients is still the basic move people use today.
[G] If I had to leave you with one sentence: this paper showed that optimizing directly for human preference, in three careful steps with a KL leash to keep the policy honest, beats raw scale on the metric that actually matters to users — but it is very precise about whose preferences, on what distribution, and it says so itself.
[O] Mine: this is the paper that made "align it" a real alternative to "make it bigger," and the field has spent every year since finding out exactly how far that trade goes.
[S] And mine: read the fine print on "eighty-five percent" before you repeat it. It's a real result, but it's a preference win rate on a curated distribution, not a scoreboard for raw capability. Full writeup, figures, and the eval critique are up on the litsearch site.
