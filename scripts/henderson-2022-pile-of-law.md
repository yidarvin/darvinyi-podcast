[O] And the case that struck down bans on interracial marriage, Loving versus Virginia, scores as clearly toxic on the same four tools, because it quotes the statute it's overturning.
[S] So your filter would delete the civil rights case and keep the one arguing people can be property.
[O] That's not a bug you patch with a better word list. That's a definition of "toxic" that never had a theory of context built into it.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a guest scholar take one paper apart.
[S] Today's paper is Pile of Law: Learning Responsible Data Filtering from the Law and a two-hundred-fifty-six-gigabyte Open-Source Legal Dataset, from Peter Henderson, Mark Krass, Lucia Zheng, Neel Guha, Christopher Manning, Dan Jurafsky, and Daniel Ho, at Stanford, published at the twenty twenty-two NeurIPS Datasets and Benchmarks track.
[O] Joining us is Priya, who's read this one closely. Priya, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. It's really two papers stapled together: a genuinely useful dataset release, and a much more ambitious argument about where filtering rules should come from in the first place.
[S] Worth saying up front, since this got flagged to us under "safety": this isn't about censoring training data. It's about who gets to decide what's filtered, and the authors' answer is, courts already decided a lot of this, so ask them.
[O] Priya, set up the actual problem. Why does a paper like this need to exist?
[G] Because content filtering has been, in the authors' words, "largely a matter of researcher discretion." They open with a table comparing the filters behind CCNet, C4, GPT-3, Gopher, and The Pile. None of them explicitly filters for what the paper calls P-I-I, personally identifiable information. Toxicity handling ranges from a crude word list in C4 to what The Pile's own documentation calls "ad-hoc source deletion."
[S] Five major pretraining efforts, five different improvised answers to "what do we take out."
[G] And the deeper issue isn't just inconsistency, it's that undesirable content is contextual. Whether a slur is toxic depends on who's saying it and to whom. Whether a fact is a privacy violation depends on what the speaker expected to happen to it. A word list can't see any of that, so it either misses real harm or deletes speech doing legitimate work, like a court quoting a segregationist statute specifically to strike it down.
[O] And overcorrecting has its own cost. The paper points out that the Census Bureau's move to differential privacy actually introduced errors into how Congressional districts got redrawn.
[S] So under-filtering leaks harm, over-filtering breaks real downstream uses, and the field's response has mostly been vibes.
[G] Their argument is that this exact tension, transparency against privacy and offense, isn't new. Courts and legislatures have litigated it for centuries, in public, with appeals and revisions attached. Their line, more or less verbatim, is that there's no need to reinvent law.
[O] Walk us through what's actually in the dataset before we get to the clever part.
[G] Two hundred fifty-six gigabytes of English-language legal and administrative text, from thirty-five sources. Court opinions and filings, contracts and SEC filings, immigration and veterans appeals decisions, statutes, regulations, casebooks, legislative records, even the legal-advice subreddit. The property that matters isn't the size, it's that every one of those thirty-five sources already passed through some filtering process, because it was published by a court or agency operating under that jurisdiction's own privacy and toxicity norms.
[S] So the dataset is, by construction, a record of thousands of real filtering decisions already made by real institutions.
[G] Exactly. Instead of a research team improvising a list of banned words, you inherit redaction calls made by judges, under public scrutiny, with an appeals process behind them.
[O] And the second contribution is cataloguing those decisions properly.
[G] They build a comparison table across U.S. federal courts, U.S. administrative agencies, German courts, Chinese courts, and Canadian courts. Some rules are close to universal: juvenile names get pseudonyms almost everywhere, dates of birth and account numbers get redacted almost everywhere. Those are bright lines any researcher could adopt today at essentially no cost.
[S] Where do the systems actually disagree?
[G] German courts strip all litigant names from civil judgments as a matter of course. U.S. federal courts almost never redact criminal defendants' names from the public record. Canada expunges most criminal records after good behavior, and Germany does something similar, and has actually fined people for republicizing an expunged record. Privacy protection isn't one dial, it's a values choice, and different democracies have set it differently.
[O] Their prescription is to treat the U.S. court standard as a floor, then make contextual calls above it.
[G] Right, a deliberately modest claim. Adopt the near-universal redactions everywhere, and don't assume you're done just because you cleared that bar.
[S] Cataloguing norms is still just a literature review, though. What's the actual machine-learning contribution?
[G] Three case studies showing the contextual part can be learned, not just hand-coded. First: the Board of Immigration Appeals sometimes publishes decisions with the applicant's full name, sometimes with pseudonymous initials. The authors split cases into paragraphs, mask the reference to the applicant, and train a distilled BERT model to predict which way that decision should go. It gets roughly eighty percent F one.
[O] That's the easy part. What's clever is what they do next.
[G] They take the Bias in Bios dataset, which has no pseudonymity question at all, and prepend one sentence to each biography marking the subject as an asylum seeker, a torture survivor, a nonviolent offender, or a violent offender. Then they measure how much that single sentence shifts the model's pseudonymity prediction.
[S] And?
[G] Asylum and torture markers push the prediction toward pseudonymity. Criminal-offense markers push it away. That tracks federal regulations almost exactly: disclosures tied to asylum or torture are shielded, but disclosure is explicitly permitted once a criminal proceeding is involved. The model recovered a real, codified legal distinction just from patterns in paragraph-level text.
[O] They go a step further with something they call the Jane Doe score.
[G] The zero-shot version. Litigants in sensitive cases, harassment suits especially, often get referred to as Jane Doe. They collect around five hundred paragraphs using that pseudonym, generate a hundred alternative versions of each swapping in a real name sampled from nineteen-ninety census frequencies, and ask whether a model trained on this corpus finds "Jane Doe" more natural than the real name.
[S] More natural how, exactly?
[G] Pseudo log likelihood under a masked language model, essentially how surprised the model is by the sentence. A model pretrained from scratch on Pile of Law ranks Jane Doe about three points higher than a plain BERT-large baseline on true pseudonym cases. The authors flag that their own model is also slightly more biased toward Jane Doe in general, but say the gap survives after accounting for that.
[O] So pretraining on legally-filtered text doesn't just inherit the norm, it seems to transmit it into the model's own preferences.
[G] That's their claim, though it's worth remembering this is an intrinsic proxy score, not a task where the model actually has to decide whether to redact something.
[S] And the third case study?
[G] A cross-agency comparison. They run an off-the-shelf HIPAA-style scrubber over veterans appeals and labor department decisions. It flags almost everything: ninety-nine percent of decisions get flagged for containing dates, and physician names get flagged in ninety-six percent of veterans cases and every single labor case. But underneath that, twenty-six percent of labor department cases actually name a physician, versus zero point three six percent of veterans cases, because physician fraud is a much bigger issue in workers' compensation than in veterans' disability. A blunt sanitizer erases exactly the distinction that mattered.
[O] Let's get to the toxicity results, since that's the part that reframes how people should think about these filters.
[G] They run four standard toxicity classifiers, profanity-check, Perspective, Detoxify, and Toxigen, over Supreme Court opinions, and measure agreement with Cohen's kappa. At the sentence level, agreement is low, and it actually rises as you move toward the present, meaning older legal language sits furthest from what these filters were trained on.
[S] Low agreement isn't shocking by itself. What makes it damning?
[G] The disagreement isn't just drift, it's conceptual. Between nineteen fifty and nineteen seventy, right as legal segregation was ending, there's a spike in sentences flagged toxic in civil rights cases specifically, because those opinions are quoting the segregationist laws they're overturning.
[O] Which brings Dred Scott back in.
[G] The paper has an exact table. Dred Scott versus Sandford scores point two nine, point five oh, point two six, point five four across the four filters, none confident. Loving versus Virginia, quoting an anti-miscegenation statute specifically to invalidate it, scores point five two, point five four, point six oh, point nine four. And Hunter versus Erickson, striking down a discriminatory ballot measure, scores as low as point zero two on one filter and point eight one on another.
[S] So depending which filter you pick, you might delete Hunter and Loving entirely and leave Dred Scott sitting in the training corpus.
[G] That's the authors' own framing, and it's hard to argue with once you see the numbers side by side.
[O] There's also a result about how much the scoring window itself changes the outcome.
[G] They take the five thousand sentences Toxigen rates most toxic, then add two sentences of context on each side, clamped to document boundaries. The toxicity score drops by fifty-five to fifty-seven percent, absolute, with a ninety-five percent confidence interval, from that context alone.
[S] So the boundary you draw around a sentence does as much work as the content itself.
[G] Which matters enormously for document-level filtering, since the scoring window is usually an engineering afterthought, not a policy decision.
[O] And then there's the pretraining result, showing the corpus is actually usable, not just a governance exercise.
[G] They train a BERT-large equivalent from scratch on a thirty-gigabyte sample, the Pile of Law BERT model. On CaseHOLD, a legal holding-prediction benchmark, it reaches seventy-five point oh F one, beating a vanilla BERT-large at seventy-one point three, and closing in on CaseLaw-BERT, a model built specifically for legal text, at seventy-five point four, or seventy-eight point five when fine-tuned.
[S] So broad pretraining on a big, diverse legal corpus gets you most of the way to a domain-specialized model.
[G] That's the headline they'd like you to take. Whether it's the whole story is worth pushing on.
[O] Time for the actual debate. My optimist case: this paper permanently changes how you should think about toxicity filters. The Dred Scott versus Loving inversion alone should retire the idea that these classifiers measure some neutral property of text. And they show, with real numbers, that context-aware filtering can be learned instead of hand-coded. That's a genuinely new research direction, not just a complaint about existing tools.
[S] I'll grant the descriptive half completely. The filter-disagreement result is airtight, and it's already the example people reach for. My skepticism is entirely about the prescriptive claim, "look to the law." The immigration classifier gets roughly eighty percent F one, but compared to what, the word-list filters everyone's already running? There's no baseline. Same for the Jane Doe score, it's an intrinsic language-model signal, not a test of whether a model actually redacts anything correctly on a real task.
[G] Both points land, and the authors are upfront about it. They call their own experiments "not comprehensive," explicitly framed as setting a research agenda rather than delivering a validated system. There's no head-to-head against the ad-hoc pipelines they're critiquing, and no human-redaction baseline either.
[O] Fair, but that's reasonable follow-up work, not a flaw in the core idea.
[S] Here's my bigger concern: contamination, running the opposite direction from a normal benchmark. Pile of Law is a pretraining source, and CaseHOLD, the benchmark used to show it works, is drawn from the same case-law universe that feeds the corpus. Some of that seventy-five versus seventy-one point three gap could be in-domain familiarity, not better legal reasoning.
[G] That's a real gap. They don't audit train-test overlap at all, a strange omission for a paper this careful everywhere else.
[O] Fair, though the gap over vanilla BERT is still notable.
[S] "Notable" is doing a lot of work in that sentence.
[G] The other limitation the authors state themselves, in an appendix, is that this is almost entirely a U.S. legal analysis. And by their own comparison table, U.S. law is the most transparency-favoring, least-redacting system of the ones they surveyed.
[O] Might still be the right place to start, though, since it's the most legible ruleset for English-speaking researchers.
[S] Or "the law already solved this" quietly becomes "American law already solved this," a much narrower claim than the title suggests.
[G] There's a point buried in their ethics statement worth surfacing. They explicitly chose not to blindly redact names, because a sentence like "In Brown, Justice Warren wrote" becomes something like "in a masked case, a masked justice wrote," if you strip names naively, and you lose the legal information the sentence exists to convey. That's their argument for why context-sensitive filtering has to replace blanket redaction, not just supplement it.
[O] Which is basically a working version of an idea privacy researchers call contextual integrity, that whether an information flow violates privacy depends on the norms of the context it's moving through, not some fixed sensitivity label on the fact itself.
[G] The paper never uses that term, to be clear, but the mechanism they're describing, learning when disclosure is appropriate from how a specific institution has always handled it, is a computational instance of exactly that idea.
[S] And "the data already encodes the norm" cuts both ways. Their own civil-rights finding shows the corpus also encodes the eras when the law's own norms were the problem.
[G] Which the authors seem to accept. They're explicit that they're not claiming law is a perfect ethical guide, just a more principled starting point than an improvised word list. They also built in an actual governance mechanism, not just an argument: a takedown pathway through the dataset's Hugging Face listing, so if something slipped through that shouldn't be public, someone can request its removal.
[S] That's a real point in their favor. A lot of dataset papers gesture at ethics; fewer ship an actual removal process.
[O] So what changes if this holds up?
[G] For evaluation practice specifically: content filters and safety classifiers need documented, contestable ground truth, not just an F one score in isolation. The Hunter, Loving, Dred Scott comparison is a template other domains could copy, find cases where your classifier's stated definition and the outcome any reasonable person expects come apart, and report that gap explicitly instead of averaging it away.
[S] It's also a caution for anyone using off-the-shelf toxicity or P-I-I scores to manufacture benchmark labels. If four respected classifiers can't agree whether the same Supreme Court sentence is toxic, and the score moves fifty-five percent just from widening the context window, treating that score as ground truth anywhere else is risky.
[O] And for practitioners, the takeaway is almost boringly practical: before building a bespoke filter, check whether some institution has already made, and published, that exact judgment call.
[G] My one-line summary: filtering pretraining data doesn't have to be improvised, because centuries of legal practice already encode context-aware judgment calls about privacy and offense, and this dataset shows those judgment calls can be learned directly from the text, not just hand-coded.
[O] Mine is that the Dred Scott versus Loving result alone earns this paper its citations. It's the clearest evidence I've seen that today's toxicity classifiers encode a definition, not a measurement.
[S] Mine is more of a homework assignment. Before anyone adopts "look to the law" as an actual filtering strategy, someone needs to benchmark it against the ad-hoc methods it's supposed to replace, and check CaseHOLD for contamination.
