[O] Isla, start with the argument. What's wrong with the benchmarks we already have?
[G] The authors' complaint is that almost every widely used benchmark tests already-solved problems. Think MMLU, GSM8K, MATH, GPQA, closed-ended questions with a known correct answer. They were genuinely useful for showing models could acquire basic reasoning skills, but once a benchmark saturates, the field's reflex is just to build a harder version of the same thing. Humanity's Last Exam is the extreme case, thousands of frontier questions across every subject, explicitly positioned as the final academic benchmark.
[S] But final doesn't mean it changes the underlying failure mode.
[G] Exactly the authors' point. Every problem on it is already solved by someone. Topping the leaderboard means matching a known key, not producing new knowledge. So doing well optimizes for narrow point-gains, not scientific advancement.
[O] What about the newer open-ended benchmarks? Agentic coding, ML engineering tasks, those feel less like trivia.
[G] They get closer, but the authors argue they still fall short. DSBench and MLE-bench put agents on Kaggle-style engineering tasks, but every task has a ceiling on its score. PaperBench, EXP-bench, and the Automated LLM Speedrunning Benchmark test whether a system can reproduce a result that's already been achieved, science-flavored, but still judged against a known target. Even LLM-SRBench, which is about rediscovering scientific equations, mostly rearranges equations that already exist under controlled data.
[S] So the gap they're pointing at is specific. Nobody has a benchmark whose objective is an open, unsolved frontier, where beating the benchmark literally is the scientific contribution.
[G] That's the gap. And it's a fair one to name, even before you look at what they build to fill it.
[O] So what do they actually build?
[G] They call it a progress-oriented benchmark, a problem environment whose objective is itself a live target of scientific progress, so that state-of-the-art performance on the benchmark is state-of-the-art performance on the underlying science. Their first instance wraps the community NanoGPT speedrun, the modded-nanogpt project started by Keller Jordan. The challenge is to train a GPT to a cross-entropy validation loss of three point two eight or better on the FineWeb dataset, on a single eight-GPU H100 node, as fast as possible.
[S] And that's been a real, ongoing community effort, not something the authors invented.
[G] Right, that's the point. Since June of twenty twenty-four, the record has fallen from forty-five minutes to under three minutes, driven by real algorithmic advances like the Muon optimizer and various precision changes. Those advances generalized well beyond the speedrun itself, into language modeling more broadly. That history is exactly why the authors picked it. Improvements here tend to be improvements to the whole pretraining stack, not toy wins.
[O] So it's not that they built a new problem, they wrapped an existing one and made it a proper benchmark.
[G] Yes, standardized data slice, standardized reference model and training harness, and then a rich reward surface. Not just final loss and time. Step-level incremental metrics, code-section profiling for the forward pass, backward pass, optimizer steps, and data loading, the fifteen most expensive CUDA kernels and CPU operations, plus token throughput and peak memory. The idea is to give an optimizer, human or AI, fine-grained signal to act on.
[S] Who's the optimizer here? Walk me through the actual agent.
[G] They fork OpenEvolve, which is an open-source implementation of the AlphaEvolve-style evolutionary coding loop. Four pieces run in a cycle. A database of past programs and their scores, using island evolution, separate populations that periodically exchange their best performers, to keep both quality and diversity. A prompt sampler that pulls together a set of top programs and a set of diverse, non-elite programs into one prompt, and asks the model for changes formatted as search-and-replace blocks, the same style the aider coding tool uses. An evaluator. And a pluggable language model that proposes the edits.
[O] What's the actual score it's chasing?
[G] A single scalar, step-average training time multiplied by final validation loss, lower is better. That's what ranks programs in the database. And there's a nice trick for creativity called two-stage meta-prompting. Instead of asking the model directly for a code edit, they first ask it for a natural-language idea sketch, emphasizing novelty, and only then re-prompt it to implement that sketch as a code edit. Decoupling the idea from the implementation is meant to reduce cognitive load and raise creativity.
[S] Doesn't that also increase the chance the model breaks something?
[G] It does, and the paper is upfront about that trade-off, we'll get to it in the results. There's also a fast error-catcher. Candidate programs get a quick, cheap execution to check they compile and run, and if not, they're bounced back to the model for fixing, up to three fast retries, before a full evaluation run.
[O] This is a system that lets a model rewrite arbitrary training code. That sounds like a reward-hacker's dream.
[G] Which is why anti-gaming is really the crux of the whole design. Two guards matter most. First, runtime injection. No matter what the model edits, the environment overrides the training and validation data slices and the validation sequence length at evaluation time, so you can't just train or test on an easier slice. Second, immutable core logic. The environment injects its own cross-entropy loss function, so a model can't swap in a trivial, non-equivalent loss that's easier to minimize, and it freezes the training-document and causal masks, so tokens can't cheat by attending across document boundaries or into the future.
[S] Is that airtight?
[G] No, and the authors say so directly. They flag that this rules out a class of legitimate loss-function innovations as a necessary cost, and that verifying full functional equivalence of a rewritten training script is still future work. So the current guardrails stop the obvious exploits, not every conceivable one.
[O] Okay, so does it actually find anything?
[G] The headline run seeds the system with the leading NanoGPT code as of August twenty twenty-five, and runs the o3 model for ninety iterations, producing roughly nine hundred candidate programs. Branching factor of ten per iteration, an elite archive of twenty, meta-prompting turned off for the first twenty iterations to build a strong pool of parents before switching it on.
[S] And the outcome?
[G] A new state-of-the-art training time. One hundred seventy-two point six eight seconds, at a validation loss of three point two eight, beating the one hundred seventy-five point two second record that stood going in, and the one hundred seventy-six point seven second record before that. The paper calls that improving on the previous record by three seconds, and says the margin mirrors the size of the gains humans had been making by hand.
[O] What was the actual discovery?
[G] Something concrete and legible. Casting down the precision of the optimizer's operations on the large hidden-layer weight matrices, keeping the Muon optimizer's hidden matrices at sixteen-bit precision, while leaving the Adam-optimized tensors at thirty-two-bit. And meta-prompting mattered a lot here. Turning it on sharply lowered the rate of programs that ran successfully, but raised the quality of the ones that did, and the state-of-the-art discovery only happened after it was switched on.
[S] What about comparing models against each other, that's the part I actually want scrutinized.
[G] They ran three more frontier models, GPT-5 Thinking, Gemini 2.5 Pro, and Claude 4, for fifty iterations each, same setup otherwise. Claude 4 posted the fastest time, one hundred seventy-three point two eight seconds, ahead of GPT-5 Thinking at one hundred seventy-four point four eight, and Gemini 2.5 Pro at one hundred seventy-four point seven eight. All three beat the prior one seventy-five point two record.
[O] So Claude wins.
[G] On the raw endpoint, yes, but the authors immediately hedge that. GPT-5 Thinking's trend line was still heading down at fifty iterations, suggesting it would likely close the gap or overtake with more compute. Gemini's trend was flat, which they read as weaker idea generation. And Claude, despite winning, struggled the most to produce programs that even ran, its run is dominated by buggy candidates, yet it still came out ahead when it worked. Every model, by the way, shows a spike in buggy programs right after iteration twenty, exactly when meta-prompting turns on.
[S] So the winner is doing the least reliable job of actually following instructions.
[G] That's a fair read, and it's part of why the authors keep repeating that comparison is a diagnostic, not the goal. They also highlight two ideas that emerged during evolution and at least compiled and ran. GPT-5 proposed a sliding-window attention variant where heads share a small core of key-value blocks and split the rest into complementary stripes, same coverage with fewer operations. Gemini proposed something they call an AdaptiveComputeBlock, which routes tokens through subpaths of varying cost, small MLPs up to full attention, based on a learned difficulty predictor. Neither is shown to actually improve loss or time. The authors call them not groundbreaking by themselves, and use them as evidence the environment surfaces novel ideas at all.

[O] Isla, zoom out for the eval community listening to this. Why should someone who cares about benchmarking generally care about a GPU speedrun?
[G] Because the design pattern is portable, even if this exact environment isn't the point. If you can specify a real optimization target, not get the right answer but move a number that reflects genuine capability, with instrumentation rich enough to reward partial progress, and guardrails that block the obvious ways to cheat it, you get something static, closed-ended benchmarks structurally can't give you. A ceiling that keeps moving because the target itself is open. The authors' own proposed next steps go toward post-training and alignment environments, and math environments judged by a mix of LLM-as-judge and formal theorem provers like Lean, instead of an answer key.
[S] Though the contamination story is different here, which is worth flagging. There's no leaked answer key to worry about, because the objective is open-ended, beat a time. The integrity risk shifts entirely to reward hacking, which is exactly why the runtime injection and the frozen loss and masks matter so much. And the authors admit those guardrails aren't complete yet, there's no functional-equivalence check on a rewritten training script, so a sufficiently clever model could still probably find a loophole they haven't anticipated.
[G] Which is a very benchmarking-methodology-shaped problem. You've traded a solvable, well-understood integrity threat, leakage, for a less solvable, less well-understood one, reward hacking against a flexible optimization target. That trade-off is worth taking seriously as this pattern spreads to other domains.
[G] If you remember one thing, the paper's real contribution is the reframing. Evaluate models on objectives that are themselves open scientific targets, not on how many already-solved puzzles they can match. The NanoGPT demo is an honest, small-scale proof that the idea can work, not a settled empirical result.
