[O] Here is the sentence that stopped me cold in this paper. They throw out Monte Carlo tree search, they throw out the value network, they throw out process reward models, all the machinery everyone assumed you needed for o1-style reasoning, and they still land at the ninety-fourth percentile on Codeforces.
[S] Or, read it the other way. They throw out three expensive things and never tell us how big the model is or how much compute it took. That is not the same as proving you did not need them.
[O] Fair, but the results are not vaporware. Seventy-seven point five on AIME 2024, ninety-six point two on MATH-500, essentially matching OpenAI's o1 on its own turf.
[S] Matching o1's reported numbers. Not a controlled head-to-head. We are going to spend real time on that distinction.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist and a skeptic argue about a paper until a guest sorts out who is right.
[S] I am the skeptic, and today's paper has one of the more interesting evidence gaps I have seen from a serious lab.
[O] I am the optimist, and I think the core idea here, that scaling how long a model gets to think is itself a scaling axis, is one of the more important claims of the last year.
[O] The paper is Kimi K-one-point-five, Scaling Reinforcement Learning with LLMs, from the Kimi Team at Moonshot AI, posted in January 2025 the same week as DeepSeek-R1.
[S] And joining us to referee is Priya, who has spent time in the weeds of this report.
[G] Happy to be here. The short version is that Moonshot AI set out to test whether reinforcement learning, not more pretraining data, could be a second scaling axis for language models, and they built a full multimodal system to test it, from an SFT warm-up through a long RL phase to methods for compressing the result into something cheap to run.
[S] Let's start with the actual gap. Why does this even matter, why not just keep scaling pretraining?
[G] Because pretraining scaling laws, the Kaplan and Hoffmann lines of work, describe how to spend a fixed compute budget against a fixed pool of high-quality text. That pool is finite. RL offers something structurally different, a model that generates its own training signal by exploring with rewards, so it is not capped by how much internet text exists.
[O] And OpenAI's o1 had already shown that RL-trained long chain of thought reaches competition-math and competitive-programming territory.
[G] Right, but o1's recipe was never published. The paper is blunt, quote, prior published work has not produced competitive results, end quote. Open attempts leaned on heavy machinery, tree search over reasoning steps, learned value functions, process reward models scoring every intermediate step. The field assumed you needed all of that.
[S] And Kimi's whole pitch is that assumption was wrong.
[G] That is the central empirical claim, yes. Long context scaling plus a fairly ordinary policy-gradient update, with no tree search, no value network, no process reward model, gets you there.
[O] Let's get into how. What is actually different about the training loop?
[G] Four pieces, really. The headline one is scaling the reinforcement learning context window up to one hundred twenty-eight thousand tokens. A longer context buys the model more implicit search steps inside a single rollout, room to try something, notice it is wrong, backtrack, and recover, all inside one continuous chain of thought instead of an explicit search tree.
[S] But generating a hundred twenty-eight thousand token rollout on-policy has to be brutally expensive. How do you afford that at scale?
[G] Partial rollouts. Each rollout gets a fixed token budget. If a trajectory runs over, the unfinished tail gets saved to a replay buffer and picked up again next iteration, instead of being thrown away or forced to finish immediately. Only the newest segment needs fresh on-policy generation, earlier segments are reused. Because rollout workers run asynchronously, short trajectories keep flowing while a handful of workers grind through the long ones. That is what makes long-context RL affordable rather than a nice idea on paper.
[O] That is clever infrastructure, but the part I actually want to understand is the no-value-network claim. That feels like the bigger conceptual bet.
[G] At each iteration, the current policy is frozen as a reference model, and they optimize a relative-entropy-regularized objective, a variant of online mirror descent, maximizing expected reward minus a K-L penalty that keeps the new policy close to the reference. That has a closed-form optimal solution, and taking logs produces a surrogate loss whose gradient looks like an ordinary policy gradient using the mean reward of several sampled responses as a baseline, plus an off-policy regularization term.
[S] So functionally it resembles REINFORCE with a baseline, sampled off-policy from the frozen reference model.
[G] Exactly that, and no critic network anywhere in the loop. Here is the justification, and it is the most interesting argument in the method section. Imagine a partial chain of thought where the next step could go one of two ways, one leads straight to the correct answer, the other contains an error but the model later catches it and recovers. A value function trained on standard credit assignment would score that erroring step negatively and suppress it. But that erroring-and-recovering step is exactly the behavior you want a long chain-of-thought model to learn. So a value network, they argue, is not just unnecessary here, it is actively counterproductive.
[O] That is a genuinely elegant argument. It reframes exploration mistakes as data rather than noise to be punished.
[S] It is elegant, and I want to flag something Priya can maybe settle. What is this compared against? Because "we removed the value network and it still worked" is not the same as "we removed the value network and it worked better."
[G] Fair challenge. Honest answer: it is compared against one thing, ReST, an earlier method that fits the model to its own best sampled responses without applying negative gradients to wrong ones. Kimi's method beats ReST clearly on sample efficiency. But the paper never runs a head-to-head against PPO or GRPO, the actual dominant RL algorithms in this space. Neither is even cited as a comparison.
[S] So the claim "you don't need a value network" rests on beating one fairly dated baseline, not on beating the methods people actually use today.
[G] That is a precise way to put it, and it is a real gap in the ablation story.
[O] Okay, two more method pieces before results. What stops the model from just rambling forever once RL rewards long reasoning?
[G] A length penalty. They noticed an overthinking phenomenon, response length balloons during training because longer reasoning helps accuracy, but it gets expensive and humans dislike the rambling. Among correct answers, shorter responses get rewarded and longer ones penalized, and incorrect long answers get penalized outright, using a normalized length score across sampled responses per problem. Critically, this gets warmed up gradually. Turning it on too early stalls training entirely.
[S] And the fourth piece?
[G] Curation and sampling. Curriculum sampling starts on easier problems and shifts to hard ones once the model has footing, since spending compute on problems it has zero chance of solving wastes rollouts. Prioritized sampling oversamples problems with lower success rates, concentrating effort where the model is weakest. One more detail matters for the eval read later. For math, where two algebraically identical answers can look totally different as text, they compare a classic value-head reward model against a chain-of-thought reward model that reasons before it judges. On manual spot checks, the classic model hit eighty-four point four percent agreement, the chain-of-thought version hit ninety-eight point five. The chain-of-thought reward model is what actually drives the RL signal.
[O] Let's get to the results, because that is where this paper is either impressive or it isn't.
[G] The long chain-of-thought flagship lands squarely in o1 territory. Ninety-six point two on MATH-500, seventy-seven point five on AIME 2024 pass at one, ninety-fourth percentile on Codeforces, seventy-four point nine on MathVista, against o1's reported ninety-four point eight, seventy-four point four, ninety-fourth percentile, and seventy-one point zero. Kimi edges ahead on three of four.
[S] And where does it lose?
[G] MMMU and LiveCodeBench. o1 scores seventy-seven point three on MMMU against Kimi's seventy point zero, and sixty-seven point two on LiveCodeBench against sixty-two point five. A tie with edges in both directions, not a clean sweep, which is actually more credible than "beats o1 on everything."
[O] The number that really jumped out at me was the short chain-of-thought result. That is the distillation story.
[G] Right, this is the long2short piece: folding the long-CoT model's thinking skill into a cheap, non-thinking model. Four methods: model merging, literally averaging the weights of a long-CoT and short-CoT model, shortest rejection sampling, sampling a problem eight times and fine-tuning on the shortest correct answer, DPO with the shortest correct response as the preferred sample against longer ones, and a dedicated long2short RL phase reapplying the length penalty with a much smaller rollout budget. The resulting short model hits ninety-four point six on MATH-500, sixty point eight on AIME, forty-seven point three on LiveCodeBench.
[S] And the headline framing in the abstract is "up to plus five hundred fifty percent" over GPT-4o and Claude 3.5 Sonnet.
[O] Which sounds enormous.
[S] It sounds enormous because GPT-4o scores nine point three on AIME and Claude 3.5 Sonnet scores sixteen. Any model not near the floor produces a triple-digit percentage gain against those two. That framing is technically true and rhetorically doing a lot of work.
[G] Going slightly beyond just restating the paper here: the fairer comparison is the strongest non-reasoning baseline in their own tables, DeepSeek-V3, at thirty-nine point two on AIME. Sixty point eight versus thirty-nine point two is still a big, genuine gap, just far less cinematic than five hundred fifty percent, and the paper had that fairer number available and chose the dramatic one for the abstract.
[O] Even the toned-down version is real. What convinced me the long2short story isn't just marketing is the token-efficiency chart, plotting accuracy against response length, where the short-CoT k1.5 variants sit to the left of DeepSeek-V3 at equal or better accuracy.
[G] That's the cleanest evidence something specific is happening, not just scale. The long2short-RL variant hits sixty point eight on AIME using an average of only three thousand two hundred seventy-two tokens per response, beating merging, DPO, and shortest rejection sampling on that efficiency frontier. Of the four methods, it's the one worth remembering.
[S] What about the ablations, the actual controlled comparisons? Those matter more to me than the headline table.
[G] Two are worth flagging. First, they trained a small and a mid-sized model on identical data to separate "bigger model" from "longer context." The larger model starts ahead, but the smaller model closes most of the gap by generating longer chains of thought under RL. The larger model stays more token-efficient at a given accuracy, so with a fixed inference budget a bigger model with shorter context wins, but if you're constrained on model size, a smaller model with longer context is a viable substitute. Second, curriculum sampling clearly beats uniform sampling once the hard-problem phase kicks in, roughly zero point six one versus zero point five five accuracy by the end of training.
[O] So where does the skepticism actually bite? Let's do the debate properly.
[O] My case for the optimist side: this is a lab showing its work in a field where OpenAI showed none. They publish the length-penalty formula, the mirror-descent derivation, the reward-model spot-check numbers, the infrastructure for overlapping training and inference. And the central idea, that thinking length is itself a scaling axis, independent of heavier search machinery, is a real conceptual contribution other labs have since built on.
[S] My case for the skeptic side: the paper's entire ablation story runs on small and mid-sized internal models, and every headline number comes from an undisclosed flagship. We are never told the parameter count, the architecture, or the training compute. So "you don't need MCTS, value networks, or process reward models" is demonstrated as an existence proof at unknown scale, not a controlled comparison at fixed scale. It is entirely possible the flagship's headline numbers are mostly explained by raw model size and RL compute rather than the recipe.
[G] Both points are fair, and the paper does not resolve that tension. I'd add two items the paper itself surfaces without addressing. First, the o1 numbers are OpenAI's own published figures, not a re-run under matched conditions, and o1's test-time compute budget is unknown, so "matching o1" is same-benchmark, not same-conditions. Second, and this genuinely surprised me: Kimi k1.5 was posted the same week as DeepSeek-R1, the other prominent open o1-class reasoning model, and R1 is never cited or compared. DeepSeek only appears as the DeepSeek-V3 non-reasoning baseline.
[O] That omission does feel notable given the timing.
[S] It is the single fact that makes me most suspicious of how the comparisons were curated. You'd expect a paper this careful about method to acknowledge its most direct concurrent competitor.
[G] On contamination, credit where due, because this is an area labs often skip entirely. For code, they evaluate on date-windowed LiveCodeBench slices, one window for short-CoT, a later window for long-CoT, specifically to dodge training-data leakage. That's better hygiene than most reports bother with. But the math benchmarks get no equivalent windowing, a real asymmetry given how much of the headline claim rests on math scores.
[O] And the chain-of-thought verifier scoring ninety-eight point five percent, that number is the entire foundation of the RL signal.
[G] It is, and it's worth being precise about it. That figure comes from a manual spot check by the same team that built the reward model, not an independent audit or a stress test against adversarial edge cases. If that verifier has systematic blind spots, the whole RL signal inherits them silently, and the paper has no experiment that would surface that.
[S] My scorecard: strong on transparency about the recipe, weak on transparency about scale, weak on independent verification of the piece the whole method depends on.
[O] Mine: the long2short result and the context-scaling result both survive scrutiny, even if "plus five hundred fifty percent" does not. Win on the mechanism, concede the win on disclosure.
[G] That's roughly where I'd land, plus one thing slightly past the paper itself. The simplicity claim matters less than the demonstrated fact that RL-trained long chains of thought reliably improve with more context and more rollouts. That trend has held up in follow-on work from other groups, which is more convincing than anything internal to this one report.
[S] What does this mean for how people should evaluate reasoning models going forward?
[G] Two lessons transfer directly. If you lean on an LLM verifier to score RL rollouts, report its accuracy on adversarial or near-miss cases, not just a friendly spot check, since the verifier's blind spots become the model's. And the date-windowed contamination check for code is a pattern worth copying broadly, applied to every static benchmark cited, not just the convenient ones.
