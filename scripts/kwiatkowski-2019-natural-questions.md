[S] Marcus, set the scene. What was actually broken about question answering benchmarks before this one?
[G] The dominant benchmarks at the time, SQuAD, SQuAD two point oh, NarrativeQA, HotpotQA, all shared one structural flaw. The annotators who wrote the questions had already read the passage that contained the answer.
[O] Which sounds harmless, but apparently it is not.
[G] It leaks. Annotators unconsciously borrow vocabulary from the evidence paragraph when they write a question, so the question and the answer end up lexically close. Robin Jia and Percy Liang showed in twenty seventeen that you could break SQuAD systems just by inserting a distractor sentence that should not change the answer at all. The model was matching words, not reasoning.
[S] SQuAD two point oh tried to patch that by adding deliberately unanswerable questions.
[G] Right, but the authors of this paper make a sharp point about that fix. A question designed to be unanswerable can usually be spotted with very little reasoning. That is a different skill from genuinely not knowing whether a real, messy, information seeking question has an answer on a given page.
[O] And there is a human upper bound problem too, right? I remember HotpotQA got called out for something like this.
[G] Yes. HotpotQA computed its human ceiling by taking four annotations per example and picking, after the fact, whichever split into one prediction and three references gave the best score. The authors here point out that is not representative of the real evaluation distribution, and it is not clear it is even achievable by an actual system. Natural Questions was built specifically to fix that, keep the evaluation distribution fixed, and estimate an upper bound a model could in principle reach.
[S] Okay, walk us through how they actually built it.
[G] Every question starts as a real, anonymized, aggregated query that multiple people actually typed into Google search. Not written by an annotator, an actual search log. Heuristics filter it down, questions have to be at least eight words long, and pattern rules pick out things like who, when, or where followed by a modal verb, or phrases containing a categorical noun phrase.
[O] Eight words feels like a deliberate choice.
[G] It is, the authors say they wanted longer, more complex queries as a tougher test of understanding. Once a query survives the filter, they run it through search, and if a Wikipedia page shows up in the top five results, that question and that page become one example. So the model does not get a hand picked paragraph, it gets an entire Wikipedia page and has to find the answer, or decide there is not one.
[S] That is already closer to a real product than SQuAD ever was.
[G] Right, and that is explicit in how they frame the task, close to an end to end application. Annotation then happens in three stages, all by one annotator. First, is the question even good, meaning fact seeking, judged before they have even seen the page. Second, long answer identification, they pick the earliest HTML bounding box, a paragraph, a table, a list, a list item, that contains everything needed to infer the answer, or they mark no answer. Third, short answer identification, they pull out the specific entity or entities, or flag yes or no, or say no short answer exists.
[O] And crucially, not answering is a legitimate outcome, not a failure of the annotator.
[G] That is the point the authors keep hammering. Across the corpus, annotators find a long answer for forty-nine percent of examples, and a short answer or yes-no for thirty-six percent. The other fifty-one percent get no answer labeled, and they keep those in the dataset instead of discarding them, because deciding whether to answer at all is treated as part of the task.
[S] How big is this thing, and who is doing the labeling?
[G] The public release is three hundred and seven thousand, three hundred seventy-three training examples, each with a single annotation. Then seven thousand, eight hundred thirty development examples and seven thousand, eight hundred forty-two test examples, both five-way annotated. A special subset of three hundred two examples got twenty-five-way annotations, specifically to study how much humans disagree with each other. Roughly fifty annotators did the work, averaging about eighty seconds per example.
[O] Why five annotators for the actual evaluation sets, and not just one?
[G] Two reasons. First, aggregating five is much more robust than trusting one person's judgment call. Second, five is cheap enough to scale to thousands of items. Of the long answers people do find, seventy-three percent are ordinary paragraphs, nineteen percent are tables, and the rest, about seven percent combined, are lists, list items, or table rows.
[O] Let us get to numbers. How good are the annotations themselves?
[G] They ran a precision audit. They sampled one hundred thirty-nine non-null answers and had four experts, the people who wrote the annotation guidelines, independently grade each one as Correct, Correct-but-debatable, or Wrong, then meet and reach a consensus call. Long answer precision came out to ninety percent, short answer precision to eighty-four percent.
[S] That is the accuracy of the labels. What about consistency between different annotators on the same question?
[G] That is where the twenty-five-way study comes in, and it is the most interesting result in the paper to me. They track what fraction of the twenty-five annotators give some non-null answer for a given question. When more than eighty percent agree there is an answer, the expert judges call those answers overwhelmingly correct. When fewer than twenty percent think there is an answer, those tend to be wrong. In between, the disagreement is almost entirely the correct-but-debatable category, meaning reasonable people genuinely differ, not that anyone is making an error. One example the authors give, for a question about where blood goes after leaving the right ventricle, twenty-five annotators produced eleven distinct short answers that were each individually defensible.
[O] So disagreement is not noise, it is signal.
[G] That is the authors' framing, yes. And it lets them build a super-annotator, from the twenty-five-way data, they use twenty of the annotations to simulate an aggregated, higher confidence prediction, and score it against reference annotations the same way a model would be scored. That super-annotator reaches ninety point zero precision, eighty-four point six recall, eighty-seven point two F one on long answers, well above a single annotator's seventy-three point four F one.
[S] Hold on, Marcus, is not that a little bit of a magic trick? You are using twenty annotators to define the ground truth, and then acting surprised that it beats one annotator.
[G] It is a fair challenge, but the argument is not just more data outvoting one person. If you have a model that is a good approximation of the true answer distribution, predicting from that distribution provably beats sampling a single draw from it, that is a Bayes-optimal result, not an artifact of pool size. The authors confirm it empirically too, by approximating the distribution from twenty-way annotations and showing it outscores a single annotator on the same metric.

On the actual system side, the best baseline for long answer selection, a pipeline combining a Decomposable Attention model with a Document Reader, gets fifty-four point eight F one on the development set. The best short answer system, an adapted Document-QA model, gets thirty-five point seven F one.

One more number worth knowing, seventy-nine percent of the Wikipedia pages in the development set also show up in training. A baseline that just copies the most frequent training annotation for a page scores twenty-seven point three F one with zero reading comprehension involved.
