[S] Or, more skeptically: it wins on seven benchmarks where every answer has been sitting on the internet, unchanged, since long before either model existed. That is a very different claim from "we no longer need real search."

[S] And I'm the skeptic host. Today's paper is Zero-Search: Incentivize the Search Capability of L-L-Ms without Searching, by Hao Sun and colleagues at Tongyi Lab, Alibaba Group, posted in twenty twenty-five.
[O] And joining us is Priya, who has spent real time with this one. Priya, welcome to the show.
[G] Glad to be here. And since we're doing this properly up front: I'm not one of the authors, so whenever I say "the authors show" or "the paper argues," that is exactly what I mean, never "we."
[S] So Priya, set the scene for us. Why does teaching a model to search need reinforcement learning at all, instead of just prompting it well?
[G] Because prompting alone does not teach a model when to search, and one-shot retrieval-augmented generation retrieves once and hopes it grabbed the right passage. The lineage here runs through Search-R-one, R-one Searcher, and ReSearch, all of them training a policy to interleave reasoning and search calls, rewarded only on whether the final answer is correct. Zero-Search inherits that whole format, down to the same think, search, and answer tags Search-R-one used.
[O] So what is actually broken in that lineage that this paper is trying to fix?
[G] Two things, both named explicitly. First, once you move past cheap local Wikipedia corpora, the way Search-R-one and R-one Searcher trained, to real commercial search, the way DeepResearcher and WebThinker did, the returned documents become uncontrollable. Sometimes useful, sometimes garbage, with no way to schedule the difficulty on purpose. Second, cost. A single Zero-Search training run generates on the order of sixty-four thousand search requests. At commercial pricing, the paper prices that out at five hundred eighty-six dollars and seventy cents for one run's worth of Google queries.
[S] Sixty-four thousand requests for one run is already eye-watering. Multiply that across every ablation and every model size in a real research program, and you can see immediately why a lab would want an alternative.
[G] That is exactly the pitch. And the core insight, in the authors' own words, is that an L-L-M has already absorbed enough of the internet during pretraining that it can generate a plausible search result on its own, the two are separated mostly by writing style, not by substance.
[O] That is a bold thing to claim. A search engine's index and a language model's internal memory are not obviously the same object.
[G] Right, and closing that gap is exactly what the method has to do.
[O] Okay, so how do you actually turn a language model into a convincing fake search engine?
[G] Two stages. First, they collect real interaction trajectories, the policy actually talking to a live search engine, extract the query-document pairs, and use an L-L-M as a judge to label each returned document as useful, meaning it contains enough to answer the question, or noisy, meaning it does not. Then a lightweight supervised fine-tuning pass, S-F-T, teaches a separate frozen model, the simulation L-L-M, to emit either kind on command. The prompt template literally has a useful-or-noisy slot you flip.
[S] Isn't that just relocating the reward-hacking problem, though? Now you're trusting a judge model's labels, then fine-tuning on those labels, then trusting that taxonomy holds up at scale.
[G] Fair challenge, and it is worth saying plainly: the paper does not test the judge's own reliability. It reports that the resulting simulator produces documents matching the intended label, and leaves it there. That is a real gap in what gets verified, not something the paper resolves.
[O] So once you have a simulator you can dial between useful and noisy, what do you actually do with that dial?
[G] You build a curriculum. The probability of serving a noisy document rises over training on an exponential schedule: mostly useful documents early, and by the back half of training, noisy documents become steadily more common. The paper backs this specific ordering directly against a flat, fifty-percent-noise baseline: curriculum rollout scores thirty-four point four seven average exact match against thirty-two point five nine for the random version, same backbone.
[S] Walk me through why that ordering has to be easy-then-hard, and not noisy-first or a flat mix the whole way through.
[G] Because the policy has two things to learn at once: the mechanical format of thinking, searching, and answering, and the judgment of recognizing bad retrieval and recovering from it. Start with noise and it never locks in the basic format. Feed it only useful documents forever and it never builds robustness. The curriculum banks the easy skill first, then forces the hard one once the easy one is solid.
[O] What about the reward signal itself? That is usually where these search-agent papers quietly go wrong.
[G] They tried exact match first and got real reward hacking: the policy padded its answers with extra words to raise the odds the correct string appeared somewhere inside a longer response. They switched to a token-level F-one overlap between prediction and ground truth instead, which balances precision against recall and does not reward padding. And there is one more stabilizing trick: document tokens the simulation L-L-M returns are masked out of the loss entirely, so gradients only flow through tokens the policy itself chose to write.
[S] Why does that masking matter so much? It sounds like a training detail, not a headline idea.
[G] Because a rollout sequence mixes model-generated tokens with retrieved-document tokens, and optimizing over both indiscriminately means asking the policy gradient to take credit or blame for text it never wrote. The ablation shows this is not cosmetic: masking is worth about a point and a half of average exact match, thirty-six point zero seven against thirty-four point five three, same backbone. And the framework is not tied to one optimizer, they report REINFORCE, P-P-O, and G-R-P-O, with REINFORCE coming out slightly ahead, matching what Search-R-one itself found.
[S] Let's get to the actual head-to-head. What is the headline number against Search-R-one?
[G] Seven benchmarks: Natural Questions, TriviaQA, and PopQA for single-hop questions, HotpotQA, two-Wiki, MuSiQue, and Bamboogle for multi-hop, across three model families, Qwen two-point-five at seven billion and three billion parameters, and LLaMA three-point-two at three billion, each in base and instruction-tuned form. On the seven-billion Qwen base model, Zero-Search averages forty point nine three exact match across all seven sets. Search-R-one-base, trained against real Google Search the entire time, averages thirty-nine point five one.
[O] Does it sweep every single one of those seven, or is the average hiding something?
[G] It does not sweep them all, and that is worth naming. On that same seven-billion comparison, Search-R-one actually edges Zero-Search on Natural Questions, forty-three point four against forty-two point four, and on two-Wiki, thirty-seven point two against thirty-four. But Zero-Search leads clearly on TriviaQA, sixty-six point four against sixty-one point four, on PopQA, sixty point four against fifty-four point six, and on Bamboogle, thirty-three point three three against thirty point five six. Five wins out of seven, two narrow losses, and the average still favors Zero-Search.
[S] That is a more honest picture than just quoting the average. What about the smaller models, does the pattern hold, or was seven billion doing all the work?
[G] It holds. LLaMA three-point-two at three billion, Zero-Search-base averages thirty-six point zero seven against Search-R-one-base's thirty-four point two one. Consistent across model families, not a one-model fluke.
[O] There is also a training-dynamics story here I find more convincing than the final-score table.
[G] The reward curves, yes. On Qwen three billion, both methods climb together for roughly the first fifty steps, but Zero-Search pulls ahead afterward with a visibly smoother curve, fewer of the sharp dips Search-R-one shows. There is a companion figure tracking interaction turns per rollout on LLaMA three billion: turns drop sharply in the first fifty steps as the policy learns not to waste calls, then reward and turn count climb and plateau together once it has locked in an efficient strategy. The authors' explanation is direct: uncontrolled real search results can derail a policy early and under-challenge it late, and the curriculum sidesteps both failure modes by design.
[S] There is also a scaling result buried in here that I think undersells itself.
[G] Table four. With the three-billion Qwen base as the policy, a fine-tuned seven-billion simulator already matches real Google Search, thirty-three point five three against thirty-two point eight one average exact match, and a fourteen-billion simulator surpasses it outright, thirty-four point four seven. A simulator that is only prompted, not fine-tuned, at those same sizes, lags well behind both, so it is specifically the fine-tuning step closing the style gap, not raw scale on its own.
[O] And the cost side is where this turns genuinely dramatic, if the numbers hold up in practice.
[G] They are stark. The fourteen-billion fine-tuned simulator, the one that beats Google outright, costs seventy dollars and eighty cents in GPU time for roughly sixty-four thousand queries. The seven-billion version costs thirty-five dollars and forty cents, the three-billion version seventeen dollars and seventy cents. Real Google Search, for the identical query volume, costs five hundred eighty-six dollars and seventy cents.
[O] So the best-performing option on the table is also the cheapest, by almost a factor of ten. That is the kind of number that changes what a small lab can afford to even attempt.
[S] If it generalizes past this exact evaluation setup. Which is precisely what I want to press on next.

[S] Gladly. Every one of the seven benchmarks tests facts that were settled years before either model existed, who came second in a bike race in twenty eighteen, a Victorian musician's birthplace, the title of a nineteen-nineties novel's sequel. Priya, is there anywhere in this paper that tests something time-sensitive, something that happened after the model's pretraining cutoff?
[G] No. Nothing in the main results or the appendix touches a live-events or post-cutoff benchmark. All seven sets are static, Wikipedia-or-web-sourced, and years old.
[S] Then here is my deflationary reading: the paper's own selling point is that the simulator has acquired, quote, extensive world knowledge during pretraining. That is exactly the property that fails the moment a question needs something newer than the pretraining cutoff, a score from last night, someone who died last month. A real search engine's whole comparative advantage is freshness, and this evaluation suite structurally cannot surface that advantage, because nothing in it is fresh. Calling this "search capability without searching" oversells what has actually been shown, which is closer to "static-fact recall without searching."
[O] I will grant the gap is real, but I do not think it erases the result. Within the regime the paper actually tested, long-tail multi-hop factual questions that people ask search engines constantly, a model trained for seventy dollars beat one trained for five hundred eighty-seven. That is not nothing, and a lot of real deployed question-answering traffic looks more like Natural Questions than like breaking news.
[S] Second problem. Priya, is there any contamination check anywhere in this paper, any test for whether the base models had already memorized these exact question-answer pairs during pretraining?
[G] There is none. No decontamination pass, no held-out-era split, nothing. And it is a sharper issue here than in an ordinary L-L-M evaluation, because Zero-Search's whole mechanism is an L-L-M generating documents from its own memorized world knowledge. If the policy is doing something structurally similar on the evaluation side, recalling rather than genuinely searching, then part of what gets measured is how well reinforcement learning elicits recall the model already had, not information-seeking behavior.
[O] That is a real symmetry, and I had not thought about it framed quite that way. Point to the skeptic on that one.
[S] Third and last. The paper compares against exactly one reinforcement-learning search baseline, Search-R-one, and explicitly declines to run the two other contemporaneous methods it cites by name, R-one Searcher and ReSearch. How defensible is that choice, Priya?
[G] More defensible than it sounds at first. The stated reason is that Search-R-one avoids elaborate reward design or data selection, which lets them re-score every reinforcement-learning method under the identical F-one reward for a clean, apples-to-apples comparison. That is good experimental hygiene. But it does mean the claim "Zero-Search beats reinforcement-learning-based search training" rests on one baseline, not the three the related-work section names.
[O] So here is my honestly updated position: the mechanism itself, the curriculum, the useful-or-noisy control, the loss masking, is genuinely well-supported by its own ablations. What I will concede is that "replaces real search" is a bigger claim than "matches real search on static-fact benchmarks with no contamination check," and those are not the same sentence.
[S] That is basically all I wanted out of this conversation. Not that the method is fake, the numbers inside its own test conditions are real, just that the test conditions were drawn exactly where the method's core assumption cannot be told apart from the truth.
[G] If I can add the thing the paper itself names as a limitation, since it is easy to miss: deploying the simulation L-L-M is not literally free. You still need GPU servers running it continuously, which is real infrastructure cost even if it sits far below commercial API pricing. The authors say so themselves.
[O] Which matters for the evaluation angle we keep coming back to on this show. If simulated environments become the default way agentic reinforcement learning gets trained, search, tool use, code execution, then contamination and construct-validity questions like the ones we just raised are not a footnote, they are the whole ballgame for whether these numbers transfer to actual deployment.
[S] Right. A simulator that is honest about its own knowledge cutoff is a feature. A simulator nobody has checked against fresh, out-of-distribution queries is a blind spot wearing a feature's clothing.
