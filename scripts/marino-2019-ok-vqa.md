[S] Here's an uncomfortable fact buried in a footnote of the original VQA dataset. Researchers asked crowd workers how old someone would need to be to answer each question, and more than seventy eight percent of those questions could be answered by a ten year old.
[O] Which sounds damning until you realize somebody built the fix. OK-VQA hand-filtered its questions down to ones a ten year old, or honestly most adults, genuinely can't answer from the picture alone.
[S] And when they ran the best vision-language models of the day against it, the strongest one collapsed to twenty six percent, on a benchmark where that same model had just scored over seventy two percent on ordinary VQA.
[O] Not a small dip. That's a benchmark doing exactly what it promised: proving pixels alone weren't the bottleneck everyone assumed.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a guest scholar take apart one paper from the litsearch dot darvinyi dot com atlas. I'm your optimist host.
[S] And I'm the skeptic. Today's paper is OK-VQA: A Visual Question Answering Benchmark Requiring External Knowledge, by Kenneth Marino, Mohammad Rastegari, Ali Farhadi, and Roozbeh Mottaghi, published at CVPR in twenty nineteen.
[O] Joining us is Oliver, who's spent real time in this paper's tables and its Wikipedia retrieval baseline. Oliver, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is a simple idea executed with real discipline: if you want to know whether a model can reason beyond the image, you first have to build questions the image genuinely cannot answer.
[S] Let's start with why this needed to exist. What was actually wrong with the VQA benchmarks that came before it?
[G] Most of them, VQA, VQA two, COCO-QA, Visual Madlibs, Visual seven W, CLEVR, mostly test recognition dressed up as reasoning: counting objects, naming colors, identifying what's in the picture. The paper's own evidence is that age annotation statistic, drawn from ten thousand VQA questions.
[O] So the authors aren't inventing a new complaint. They're pointing at a number already sitting in someone else's dataset.
[G] Exactly, so the paper doesn't have to argue "trust us, current benchmarks are too easy." The evidence was already published by somebody else.
[S] There had been earlier attempts at knowledge based VQA. KB-VQA and FVQA both tried to force in an outside knowledge requirement.
[G] Right, but both tie every question to one fact pulled from a small, fixed knowledge base, subject relation object triplets like "dog is mammal," with the question authored around a chosen triplet. So you're testing whether a model can consume a fact somebody handed it, not whether it can figure out what to look up.
[O] And triplets are a narrow way to represent knowledge. "Teddy bears are named after Theodore Roosevelt" doesn't reduce cleanly to one relation.
[G] It doesn't, and both datasets were small. FVQA has five thousand eight hundred twenty six questions over two thousand one hundred ninety images, KB-VQA just two thousand four hundred two over seven hundred. OK-VQA's gap is a dataset large enough to train modern models, genuinely unanswerable from the image, enforced by human filtering rather than a template, and tied to open knowledge instead of a fixed triplet store.
[S] So the model has to decide what to look up, not just consume a fact it's handed.
[G] That's the whole design philosophy in one sentence.
[O] Walk us through how you build a dataset like that, Oliver, because "filter out the easy questions" undersells the process.
[G] It starts from random COCO images, reusing COCO's own eighty thousand, forty thousand train and validation split. Round one, MTurk workers write a question given an image, the same "try to fool a smart robot" prompt the original VQA dataset used, but told not to ask what's in the image or how many of something there is, and to require outside knowledge.
[S] Did that prompt work, or did people just ignore the instructions?
[G] Partly ignored. It produced a lot of noise, basic counting questions, questions answerable without the image, some nonsensical ones. So they manually filtered the raw pool, from eighty six thousand seven hundred collected questions down to thirty four thousand nine hundred twenty one that plausibly required real external knowledge.
[O] That's more than half the pool thrown out before round two even starts.
[G] Right, and round two is where five independent MTurk workers each answer every surviving question image pair, giving five answer annotations per question. The original VQA dataset used ten; OK-VQA uses five, which matters later for how the accuracy metric gets computed.
[S] Then there's a de-biasing pass, the part I find most interesting.
[G] It targets the shortcut VQA two later targeted at scale. Their example: in snowy images, "what season is it?" defaults to "winter," even though other images, with autumn leaves, have different answers. So a question is dropped if one answer is the single most common answer for more than five instances of that question, and any question with zero inter-annotator agreement is dropped too.
[O] And that's what brings it down to the final size.
[G] Nine thousand nine training questions plus five thousand forty six test questions, fourteen thousand fifty five total, over fourteen thousand thirty one unique COCO images. There's also a separate labeling pass: five MTurk workers each assign one of ten fixed knowledge categories, things like Vehicles and Transportation, Plants and Animals, Science and Technology. No majority category, it gets bucketed as "Other."
[S] And that's before we even get to the retrieval baseline they built.
[G] Right, ArticleNet. For a question image pair, it builds Wikipedia search queries from question words plus labels from an ImageNet classifier, a Places2 scene classifier, and a COCO object detector. It hits the Wikipedia search API for the top article per query, then keeps the five sentences most relevant by word overlap.
[O] So it's not reading the whole article, just the parts that look relevant.
[G] Right. A GRU based network, a kind of recurrent network, reads the retrieved title and candidate sentences, trained to predict whether and where the ground truth answer appears. At inference it answers with the highest scoring word in the highest scoring sentence.
[S] How well does that work on its own, before you fuse it with anything?
[G] Badly, five point two eight percent, because it can only answer with a word in its own imperfectly retrieved sentences. But fused in, MUTAN plus AN and BAN plus AN feed ArticleNet's top sentence hidden states into a memory network, concatenated with the VQA model's own fusion hidden state before the final classifier.
[O] So the weak retriever still contributes something once it's not doing all the work alone.
[S] Let's get to the numbers, because this is where the paper does its real work.
[G] Start with scale. Table one compares OK-VQA to prior knowledge VQA datasets: fourteen thousand fifty five questions over fourteen thousand thirty one images, dwarfing FVQA and KB-VQA while staying genuinely knowledge based, unlike VQA two, which has one point one million questions but, the authors argue, mostly doesn't need outside knowledge at all.
[O] And the dataset itself is genuinely diverse, not filtered down to one narrow slice.
[G] Twelve thousand five hundred ninety one of the fourteen thousand fifty five questions are unique phrasings, drawn from seven thousand one hundred seventy eight unique question words, with fourteen thousand four hundred fifty four unique answers. The top two thousand most frequent answers cover only eighty eight point five percent of the test set, a much longer answer tail than closed vocabulary VQA benchmarks typically have.
[S] What about the category breakdown? You mentioned it's uneven.
[G] Very. Plants and Animals is the largest at seventeen percent, Vehicles and Transportation next at sixteen percent, then Cooking and Food at fifteen percent and Sports and Recreation at twelve percent. Science and Technology is the smallest named category at two percent. And "Other," the no majority bucket, absorbs twelve percent of all questions, larger than six of the ten named categories.
[O] Now the headline result. What happens when you actually run current models on this?
[G] Every state of the art VQA model degrades sharply. MUTAN, the strongest pure vision language baseline, scores only twenty six point four one percent overall. BAN scores twenty five point one seven. Compare that to seventy two point four one percent, the best open ended result from the twenty eighteen VQA Challenge, on standard, non knowledge VQA.
[S] That's the drop we opened the episode with.
[G] Right, and there's a second number that's arguably more damning. A question only baseline, no image at all, still reaches fourteen point nine three percent. So the entire margin a strong image aware model buys you over ignoring the picture is only about eleven to twelve points, much narrower than the corresponding gap on ordinary VQA.
[O] What does adding ArticleNet's retrieval buy you on top of that?
[G] A small, real lift. MUTAN goes from twenty six point four one up to twenty seven point eight four percent with ArticleNet fused in. BAN goes from twenty five point one seven up to twenty five point six one. The oracles, take whichever system's answer is correct, reach twenty eight point four seven percent for MUTAN and twenty seven point five nine for BAN, suggesting the retrieved knowledge carries complementary signal the fusion isn't fully extracting.
[S] Is that lift uniform across categories, Oliver, or does it depend on what Wikipedia covers well?
[G] Very uneven. The contribution is largest for Brands and Companies, up from eighteen point nine five to twenty three point nine five percent, and for People and Everyday Life and Science and Technology. For Plants and Animals it actually hurts, thirty point four four down to twenty nine point seven. Wikipedia is apparently a better source for corporate trivia than animal facts.
[O] And there were a couple of ablations too.
[G] Two. Swapping MUTAN's visual backbone from ResNet one fifty two down to ResNet eighteen costs little, twenty six point four one down to twenty three point six four. But dropping the image entirely costs nine to eleven points, so visual grounding matters, but better image features aren't the bottleneck, retrieval and reasoning are. And a scale ablation trains MUTAN on five percent up to the full training set, and the score climbs steadily to twenty six point four, with no sign of saturating.
[S] Meaning more knowledge VQA training data would probably keep helping.
[G] That's exactly the authors' own read.
[O] Okay, my strongest case for this benchmark. It found a real, measurable gap, seventy two percent down to twenty six, using vision language models that were genuinely state of the art. It's built on a large, diverse image set, it engineered out the same answer distribution bias VQA two would later fix at scale, and it shipped an actual retrieval baseline instead of a leaderboard with no proposed direction. That's a benchmark doing its job.
[S] Here's the deflationary case. The entire premise rests on one design decision: a human decides whether a crowd written question "requires external knowledge." That filtering removed the majority of raw submissions, and the paper describes no adjudication protocol for the judgment call itself, no second annotator layer re-confirming borderline cases, no reported inter-rater agreement for that filter, only for the separate answer agreement filter used to remove bias.
[O] Is that a problem in practice, or just a missing statistic in the write-up?
[S] In practice, because "requires knowledge no pixels supply" and "requires common sense one annotator didn't consciously register as inferable" sit on a spectrum. Recognizing a building as a church versus knowing churches hold services on Sundays. A single filterer's intuition decided which side of that line tens of thousands of questions fall on, with zero measured agreement behind it.
[G] You're both pointing at real parts of the same dataset, and the problem compounds. The ten category knowledge taxonomy inherits the identical issue one level up. Five workers each assign a category, no majority questions default to "Other," which absorbs twelve percent of the dataset, more than six of the ten named categories. That's a visible signal of category boundary disagreement the paper never quantifies, and every per category claim in the results, including "ArticleNet is particularly helpful for brands, science, and cooking," rests on labels whose consistency was never measured.
[S] So even the finding I found most interesting, the uneven per category lift, is standing on unaudited ground.
[O] Oliver, isn't that close to invalidating the whole category analysis, not just weakening it?
[G] A real limitation rather than a fatal one. The baselines themselves, MUTAN and BAN, were genuinely current state of the art models reused faithfully, and the ResNet depth ablation is a fair sanity check that the drop isn't just weak image features. What's unaudited is the label deciding which bucket a question falls in, not the accuracy numbers underneath.
[S] Fair, but that's still the label the whole per category story depends on, and nobody measured whether two humans would even agree on it.
[G] Agreed, score that to the skeptic. Where I'd add a concern the paper couldn't have anticipated: this is a twenty nineteen benchmark measured against models with zero external text access at inference. The facts these questions require, teddy bears and Theodore Roosevelt, cows producing milk, twenty three chromosome pairs, are exactly the kind of widely documented trivia a modern language model has memorized outright, over images drawn from COCO, one of the most heavily reused image sets in vision language pretraining.
[O] So a model today scoring well on OK-VQA might not be retrieving and reasoning at all.
[G] It might just be pattern matching a familiar stock photo to parametric trivia it already knows, a fundamentally easier capability than the retrieval and fusion problem the twenty nineteen baselines actually struggled with. There's no held out re-annotation on fresh images, no text only versus image ablation, that would let you separate the two today.
[O] That's a fair hit, and not the authors' fault. It's what happens to any knowledge benchmark that outlives the knowledge cutoff of the models being tested on it.
[S] One more point for the skeptic. ArticleNet itself is a shallow keyword search plus GRU pipeline, scoring only five point two eight percent alone. That likely understates how much a stronger dense retrieval system could close the gap, which makes "even retrieval barely helps" partly an artifact of a weak retriever, not an inherent property of the task.
[G] Agreed, the fairest single caveat on the whole results table. Read the numbers as "this particular retrieval pipeline, in twenty nineteen," not as a ceiling on what retrieval augmented VQA can do in general.
[O] Zooming out, what should someone building an eval today take from this?
[G] Publish your inter-annotator agreement, especially for the judgment call that defines your benchmark's core label. "This question requires external knowledge" does as much work here as any accuracy number, and it's the one thing without a measured reliability score.
[S] It's also an honest example of a problem every knowledge grounded benchmark inherits eventually. The underlying facts don't stay hard forever. A genuine retrieval challenge against fixed twenty nineteen models becomes an easier memorization test against a model trained on most of the internet.
[O] Which is really the contamination conversation the field is still having, with pretrained knowledge standing in for a leaked test set.

The filtering methodology and category taxonomy are reusable. The raw accuracy number on a modern model is not telling you what it used to.
