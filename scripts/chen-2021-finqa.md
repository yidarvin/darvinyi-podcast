[S] Picture an entry-level analyst who reads every number in a filing correctly, but chains them together wrong roughly four times out of ten.
[O] Or picture a system that, a year earlier, could not attempt this task at all, and now clears half the questions a random crowd worker manages.
[S] Both readings describe the exact same benchmark, and there's a ninety-one percent human expert score sitting right above both of them.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an AI optimist, an AI skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the litsearch atlas. Today it's FinQA, A Dataset of Numerical Reasoning over Financial Data, from Zhiyu Chen, Wenhu Chen, and colleagues at U C Santa Barbara, JPMorgan, Penn State, and Carnegie Mellon, published at EMNLP twenty twenty-one.
[S] Joining us to walk through it is Bianca, a researcher who has studied this dataset closely. Bianca, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. This paper is really two contributions stacked together, a genuinely hard dataset and an evaluation trick I think more benchmarks should have stolen from it by now.
[O] Let's start with the gap. Why did finance need its own benchmark when numerical-reasoning question answering already existed?
[G] Because reading a company's financials is a numerical-reasoning task wearing a reading-comprehension costume. To answer something like what was the change in shares vested, an analyst has to find numbers scattered across a table and the surrounding prose, then chain several steps together, a ratio, a difference, sometimes a unit conversion. Before this paper, benchmarks like DROP tested discrete reasoning over Wikipedia paragraphs, and MathQA and MaWPS tested self-contained word problems, but almost all of them needed only one arithmetic step, and none of them required real domain expertise just to write a sensible question.
[S] So the earlier datasets were testing arithmetic. This one wants to test judgment.
[G] That's exactly the authors' framing, and they're explicit that the stakes are different too, a wrong answer in financial analysis can cost real money, which is not true of a trivia question about Wikipedia.
[O] There was a table-and-text dataset before this too, HybridQA. What did that miss?
[G] HybridQA and TabFact both spanned tables and text, but neither was built around calculation. FinQA is the first to combine real earnings filings, multi-step arithmetic, and an explicit, gradeable reasoning program rather than just a final number.
[S] Let's get into how they actually built it, because eight thousand two hundred eighty-one expert-written questions is not a cheap dataset to produce.
[G] It's built on FinTabNet, S and P 500 earnings reports from nineteen ninety-nine to twenty nineteen with the tables already annotated. The authors filtered down to clean, reasoning-friendly pages, at most one table per page, nothing over twenty rows, no nested or catalog-style layouts, which left twelve thousand seven hundred nineteen candidate pages. Then, instead of crowdworkers, they hired eleven US-based finance professionals off Upwork, CPAs and MBAs, interviewed each one on four sample pages, and trained them on a purpose-built annotation tool.
[O] That's a real hiring pipeline for a dataset paper, not just a task posted to a crowd platform.
[G] It shows in the pay too, about two dollars a question, which worked out to roughly thirty-five dollars an hour, and the whole collection took eight weeks. For each page, an annotator writes up to two questions, and for each question supplies three things, the question itself, a gold reasoning program of up to five steps, and the exact supporting sentences and table rows. That yields eight thousand two hundred eighty-one examples over two thousand seven hundred eighty-nine report pages, split seventy-five, ten, and fifteen percent for training, validation, and test, with no report crossing a split.
[S] Walk me through the reasoning program itself. What does explainable actually buy you here?
[G] It's a small domain-specific language, ten operations total. Six are math, add, subtract, multiply, divide, a greater-than comparison, and an exponent. Four are table aggregations, table sum, table average, table max, and table min. An argument is either a number pulled straight from the report, a table row name, or a pointer back to an earlier step's result. Ratios dominate, divide alone is about forty-five percent of all operations, subtract about twenty-eight, add about fifteen, multiply under six, which tracks with what ratio-heavy financial analysis actually looks like. And most chains are short, fifty-nine percent of programs are a single step, thirty-three percent are two steps, and only about eight percent need three or more.
[O] And because the program itself is annotated, you can grade the reasoning, not just whether the final number happens to land right.
[G] That's the part I think is underrated. FinQA scores two separate things, execution accuracy, which checks only the final number, and program accuracy, which replaces every argument with a symbol and checks whether two programs are mathematically equivalent. A model can't back into full credit by guessing a number that happens to be correct through the wrong reasoning.
[S] Okay, and the baseline model, FinQANet, how does it actually attack this?
[G] Two stages. A full report page can run past two thousand tokens, well beyond what a BERT-era encoder can handle directly, so first a retriever turns every table row into a templated sentence, something like the risk-free interest rate of 2006 is 5 percent, scores every candidate fact against the question with a BERT classifier, and keeps the top three, reordered by where they appear in the document. That retriever alone reaches eighty-nine point six six percent recall at the top three, ninety-three point six three at the top five, well ahead of a T F I D F baseline's eighty-two point nine one.
[O] So the generation step only ever sees a handful of retrieved sentences, not the whole page.
[G] Right, and then an L S T M decoder builds the program token by token, choosing at each step from three sources, numbers or row names pulled from the retrieved text, the domain-specific language's special operator tokens, or a step-memory token, like pound zero or pound one, that points back at an earlier result. Every time the decoder closes out one operation, that step's memory embedding gets updated, so the running reasoning path carries forward into the next step. Grammar masks at decoding time keep every generated program structurally valid, so it always executes cleanly.
[O] Let's get to the number everyone's going to remember. What did the best system actually score?
[G] FinQANet with a RoBERTa-large encoder, sixty-one point two four percent execution accuracy, fifty-eight point eight six percent program accuracy, on the test set. Worth flagging up front, the paper's own abstract quotes sixty-five point zero five percent, but every results table and every breakdown in the body of the paper uses sixty-one point two four. That's a real inconsistency inside the paper itself.
[S] So which number should people actually cite?
[G] Sixty-one point two four, since that's the one backed by the actual results table rather than the abstract's summary line.
[O] Either way, that beats the general crowd. Non-expert workers only reached fifty point six eight percent.
[S] But experts reached ninety-one point one six on execution, eighty-seven point four nine on program accuracy. That's roughly a thirty-point gap between the best model and a working professional.
[G] And the ablations explain a good chunk of where that gap comes from. Skip the program entirely and just generate the final number directly, and you get zero point three percent, essentially nothing. Feed the whole page into a Longformer with no retrieval step at all, and you get twenty-one point nine, length alone floods the generator with distractor numbers. Scale helps steadily, BERT-base fifty percent, BERT-large fifty-three point five two, RoBERTa-base fifty-six point one, RoBERTa-large sixty-one point two four. FinBERT, a model actually pretrained on finance text, only reaches fifty point one, no better than plain BERT-large, because its pretraining corpus is only around thirty million words, too small to matter here.
[O] What happens if you just hand the model the gold retrieved facts instead of making it retrieve them itself?
[G] It jumps to seventy percent execution accuracy, sixty-eight point seven six program accuracy. So roughly nine points of that thirty-point human gap is retrieval error alone, not reasoning error.
[S] What about the reasoning itself, once retrieval stops being the bottleneck?
[G] Two different breakdowns matter here, and they're easy to conflate. First, restrict the model's evidence to just the table, ignoring text entirely, and it scores forty-five point eight one percent. Restrict it to just the text, ignoring the table, and it drops to fifteen point eight. Both are far below the sixty-one point two four you get from using both, so cross-modal evidence genuinely matters, it's not decoration. Second, and separately, group the actual questions by what kind of evidence they need, table-only, text-only, or genuinely mixed, and the model scores sixty-seven point three eight on table-only questions, fifty-four point eight six on text-only, and only forty-three point eight on the mixed ones, which is the realistic case. That hardest, mixed category is only fourteen point one five percent of the whole dataset. By reasoning depth, one-step programs score sixty-seven point six one, two-step fifty-nine point zero eight, and anything over two steps collapses to twenty-two point seven eight. Programs that need an implicit constant, like a one thousand to convert billions into millions, drop to forty-three point eight eight.
[O] So it's not that the model can't read a table. It's multi-step chaining and mixed-source retrieval that break it.
[G] The authors' own error analysis backs that up. They hand-checked fifty mistakes, fifteen percent were retriever failures, and of the rest, roughly half were financial-knowledge gaps, not knowing what a term meant, and half were pure numerical-reasoning slips, wrong year picked, a unit mismatch, that kind of thing.
[O] Let me make the optimist case. This is exactly the kind of benchmark that ages well. Program accuracy rewards real reasoning instead of lucky guessing, and the fact that a twenty twenty-one supervised pipeline already beat the untrained crowd by ten points, on a task that needs real financial training just to write the question, tells you the underlying capability was already there and just needed sharpening.
[S] Here's my deflationary case. Every single baseline in this paper is pre-LLM, BERT, RoBERTa, FinBERT, an L S T M decoder, a Longformer. There's no GPT-3 few-shot baseline, no chain-of-thought, no tool use, and all of those existed in some form by twenty twenty-one. So that dramatic thirty-point human gap isn't really a statement about what language models can do on financial reasoning. It's a statement about what one specific retrieve-then-generate pipeline could do in the exact year this paper shipped.
[G] You're both right about different pieces of this. S's point about the baselines is just where the paper stops, there's no chain-of-thought or tool-use comparison anywhere in it, and the financial-reasoning work that followed, FinanceBench included, showed that gap narrows a lot once you add better prompting and tool use. But O's point about program accuracy holds regardless of which model generation is being tested. That metric design doesn't go stale the way the baseline numbers do.
[S] Fair, and I'll go further, actually. I think the paper is honest about its own limits in a way a lot of benchmark papers aren't. They filtered out nested tables, tables over twenty rows, catalog layouts, and capped programs at five steps. That's a deliberately narrow slice of what a real financial document looks like.
[O] Is that a knock or a strength, Bianca?
[G] Both. It's a strength because it makes the task tractable enough to grade cleanly at all. It's a limitation because that narrowing cuts hardest against exactly the slice, table-plus-text questions, where the model already struggles the most. I'd flag two more things before anyone leans on the headline gap. The human expert ceiling rests on just two annotators scoring two hundred questions, which is a thin upper bound, not a large validated one. And there's contamination to think about, these are public S and P 500 filings collected in FinTabNet, which sit squarely inside anything scraped for modern web pretraining. The genuinely novel annotation is the reasoning program, not the underlying filing text, so a model that's simply memorized these reports is a real leakage risk for anyone benchmarking a modern system against this exact test set.
[S] So even the thirty-point framing needs an asterisk on both ends, a thin human ceiling and possibly contaminated documents.
[G] Right, and before fully trusting a modern re-run of this comparison, I'd want a genuine few-shot or chain-of-thought baseline, a larger multi-annotator human study, a held-out split covering the nested and irregular tables they excluded, and ideally a time-split test, filings published after a given model's training cutoff, to actually probe memorization instead of assuming it either way.
[O] Does any of this connect to evaluation practice more broadly, or is it a one-off for finance?
[G] It connects directly. Program accuracy through symbolic equivalence is an early, domain-specific version of something a lot of reasoning benchmarks now do, grading the chain instead of just the endpoint, and FinanceBench and the financial-reasoning papers that followed inherited that expert-annotated, gold-program recipe wholesale.
[S] It's also a nice case study in how fast a human-ceiling benchmark can go stale once the baseline generation underneath it changes.
[G] Which is exactly why I'd treat the specific sixty-one versus ninety-one comparison as a twenty twenty-one artifact, and treat the dataset design itself, the annotation process, the domain-specific language, the program-accuracy metric, as the durable contribution.
[G] If there's one thing to take from the paper itself, it's that finance professionals could be hired to build a benchmark hard enough to separate reasoning from memorization, years before that became standard practice.
[O] My takeaway is that a twenty twenty-one pipeline already beating the untrained crowd on a task requiring real financial judgment is a genuinely underrated data point for how fast this capability moved.
[S] Mine is a caution. Don't trust a headline human gap number without checking how many humans set the ceiling, and how old the model doing the comparing actually is.
[O] Thanks for walking through this with us, Bianca. For the full results tables, the figures, and the rest of the critique, the writeup is up on the litsearch site.
[G] Thanks for having me.
