[S] Good, because we're going to hold you to what's actually on the page. Elena, start us at the beginning. What's broken about how models currently get aligned to follow instructions?
[G] The core problem is that reinforcement learning only has a clean target where the reward is cheap and unambiguous. On verifiable tasks, math, code, format constraints, a program can check the answer, so R-L has something solid to climb. Open-ended instructions people actually type, like "list Airbnbs in Singapore for two people under a budget, and add tips if listings aren't available," have no answer key at all. The field's standard fix is R-L-H-F, a reward model trained once on a fixed notion of helpfulness and harmlessness that grades everything.
[O] So the issue isn't that R-L-H-F doesn't work, it's that it applies the same yardstick to every prompt?
[G] Exactly, and the authors walk through why every existing fix is structurally limited. Verifiable-only R-L can only reinforce what a program can check, ignoring subjective things like topicality or tone. Reward models are flexible, but their notion of reward is arbitrary and opaque, which is precisely what makes them hackable. Distilling preferences from a bigger prompted model forces that model to silently decide what to grade on, collapsing what they call the generator-verifier gap. And even multi-criterion rubrics like UltraFeedback apply the same four fixed principles, instruction-following, helpfulness, truthfulness, honesty, to every prompt, so they're rarely comprehensive for any one instruction.
[S] So their fix is more prompting? Just have the model write its own rubric per instruction?
[G] That's the bet, yes. A rubric is automatic, it needs no human annotation, and unlike a reward model or four fixed principles, it's genuinely specific to the instruction in front of it. They call the method Reinforcement Learning from Checklist Feedback, R-L-C-F for short.
[O] Give us the shape of an actual checklist item. It's not fifty pages of philosophy, right?
[G] Not at all. Take that Singapore Airbnb example from the paper's own figure. Item one, does the text provide a list of Airbnbs in Singapore, weight one hundred out of one hundred. Item two, does each listing accommodate two people, weight one hundred. Item three, does the text provide useful tips if real-time listings aren't available, weight seventy-five. Each one is a plain yes or no question, checked against one specific candidate response.
[S] Let's get into how they actually build and grade these things, because that's where I expect the real design choices are hiding.
[G] There are two separate machines here: how you write the checklist, and how you grade a response against it. For writing, they compared two recipes. Direct just prompts a language model to pull a checklist straight out of the instruction. Candidate-based is smarter. It defines a requirement as anything that, when missing, makes a response fail. So first sample responses of varying quality, good, mediocre, bad, then ask the model to write down every way those responses could go wrong, and assign each requirement an importance weight from zero to a hundred.
[S] Direct sounds cheaper. Why bother with the extra step?
[G] Because direct checklists tend to just parrot the instruction back at you. High on what they call naturalness, weak on objectivity and comprehensiveness. When GPT-4o and human annotators scored both approaches on InFoBench instructions, candidate-based checklists won on objectivity, atomicity, and overall preference, fifty-six percent preferred versus thirty-eight percent for direct. It costs a little naturalness and buys a lot of rigor. And later in the paper, that intrinsic quality gap actually shows up in the trained model too, candidate-based checklists produce better R-L results, not just prettier checklists.
[O] And this is where WildChecklists comes from?
[G] Right. They ran the candidate-based recipe over one hundred thirty thousand instructions pulled from WildChat, real crowdsourced conversations with assistants, and released the resulting checklists as a public dataset. Qwen2.5 seventy-two-B-Instruct is the model doing the writing, and later, the grading.
[S] Same model writing and grading. Flag that, we'll come back to it.
[G] We will. One more design wrinkle worth knowing. Early on, optimizing purely for checklist completion made the model pad every response with a long preamble overview, a classic reward-hacking tell. So they added two universal requirements to every checklist, stay on topic, and match the tone the instruction calls for, each weighted at a full hundred points, specifically to close that loophole.
[O] Okay, I have a weighted checklist. How does a candidate response get scored against it?
[G] Two graders, deliberately redundant so each covers the other's blind spots. First, an AI judge, again the seventy-two-B model, scores each item zero to a hundred, averaging twenty-five samples per item rather than taking one shot, since a single call is noisy and the mean tracks quality better than the mode. Second, for items that are exactly checkable, "does the response contain the letter R at least three times," the model writes a small verification program instead, only when it's confident it can check the requirement precisely, and it abstains on subjective items like "is this sentence coherent." Where a program exists, its score is averaged in with the judge's.
[S] Twenty-five samples per item, times every item, times every candidate response. That has to be the expensive part.
[G] It is, it's the computational bottleneck of the whole pipeline, and we'll get to exactly how expensive later. Once every item has a score, they're combined into one number with a weighted average across items. For training, they sample two responses per instruction at a fairly high temperature for real diversity, score both, and keep only the forty percent of pairs where the gap on at least one criterion is largest, near-identical pairs get discarded since they carry no signal. What survives becomes a preference pair for DPO.
[O] So no reward model anywhere in the loop, a checklist score standing in as the reward, and then a completely standard DPO run on top of that.
[G] Exactly. Qwen2.5-seven-B-Instruct trains in about three hours on a single eight GPU H100 node, two epochs, batch size ten twenty-four. The framing the authors like is that this is an extreme mixture of evaluators. A classic reward model effectively gives you one implicit criterion, UltraFeedback gives you four, Constitutional A-I maybe sixteen, and checklist feedback draws from an effectively unbounded pool, choosing a different subset of criteria for literally every instruction.

[S] For our listener who lives in evaluation and benchmarking, what's the actual takeaway here, not the headline, the mechanism?
[G] That RewardBench-style accuracy and R-L-teaching quality are different properties, and treating them as interchangeable is a mistake this field keeps making. If you're building an evaluator to guide training, test whether it actually improves downstream policies, not just whether it agrees with static human preference labels. The other transferable idea, even outside R-L, is the confidence-gated verifier, let the grader default to a program when it can check something exactly, and default to a model only when it genuinely can't. That's a cheap pattern any pipeline mixing hard and soft constraints could borrow.
[O] And it's one more data point for the broader shift away from a single frozen reward model, toward something closer to per-example, generated rubrics, a trend showing up in evaluation work well beyond just this paper.
[G] If you remember one thing, remember this. The paper's own claim, properly scoped, is that a per-instruction, weighted, yes-or-no checklist, graded by a judge and a confidence-gated verifier together, is a denser and harder-to-hack reward for R-L than a single learned scalar, even when that scalar is a better judge in isolation.
[O] My takeaway is that the real ceiling here is scale of criteria, not this specific seven-B result. Once rubrics can grow without retraining anything, that's the part I'd bet on generalizing well past this paper.
