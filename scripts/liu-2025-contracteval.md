[G] Thanks for having me.
[S] And it earns its spot on the docket because it's the first current stress test of whether a model you run on your own hardware can actually do legal work, not just talk about the law.
[O] Set the scene for us, Wynne. Why does this need its own benchmark?
[G] Picture a company buying another company. Before the deal closes, the buyer's lawyers have to comb through every contract the target has signed, hunting for landmines: who actually owns the intellectual property, does a contract auto-renew in a way that traps the buyer, is liability capped or not. The paper notes that step alone can run into the hundreds of thousands of dollars, often just to confirm there's nothing wrong.
[S] That's expensive, but it's still just reading. Why hasn't legal natural language processing already solved this?
[G] Because most legal natural language processing wasn't built for this shape of problem. Case retrieval, judgment prediction, legal text generation, those are document-level or generative tasks. Contract review is span-level extraction: the model has to point to the exact sentence inside a document that can run past three hundred thousand characters. The two closest prior datasets, CUAD and ContractNLI, were built around 2021 and mostly used to benchmark BERT-era models. Nobody had a current read on how 2025 language models handle it.
[O] And there's a confidentiality wrinkle that makes this more than academic.
[G] Right. Law firms carry strict client-confidentiality obligations, so a lot of them are wary of shipping a client's contract to a proprietary API. That pushes the field toward locally-deployed, open-source models that never let the data leave the building. So the real question ContractEval is asking isn't just "are language models good at this," it's: can an open model you run yourself match a proprietary one on legal-risk extraction, without the contract ever touching someone else's server.
[S] Which is a much sharper, more falsifiable question than "are LLMs good at law."
[O] Walk us through what ContractEval actually built, Wynne.
[G] It's built on the test split of CUAD, the Contract Understanding Atticus Dataset. That gives four thousand, one hundred twenty-eight contract-and-question pairs, spanning forty-one clause categories, drawn from one hundred two unique contracts. Context lengths run from around six hundred characters up to over three hundred thousand. The labels come from law students who did seventy to one hundred hours of specialized training under practicing-lawyer supervision, so the annotation quality is genuinely high.
[S] And I noticed the data's lopsided.
[G] Very. About thirty percent of the pairs are positive, meaning a clause actually exists, and seventy percent are negative, meaning there's no matching clause for that question. That imbalance shapes how you should score this, and it shapes which metrics the authors chose.
[O] What does the model actually have to do?
[G] The framing mirrors what a junior associate does. You hand it the full contract as context, plus a targeted question, something like "on what date is the contract effective," and it has to return only the sentence or sentences that answer it, verbatim, not a paraphrase, or output the exact string "no related clause" if nothing applies. That's compared against CUAD's human-highlighted span.
[S] How many models, and which ones?
[G] Nineteen. Four proprietary: GPT four point one, GPT four point one mini, Gemini two point five Pro Preview, and Claude Sonnet four. And fifteen open-source models across the DeepSeek, Llama, Gemma, and Qwen three families, including Qwen three at four B, eight B, and fourteen B, each tested with its thinking mode both on and off, plus FP eight and AWQ quantized variants of the eight B model. Every model had to support at least a one hundred twenty-eight thousand token context window just to qualify, since the longest contract blows past that. That's actually why they excluded SaulLM, a legal-specialized model whose context tops out at thirty-two thousand tokens.
[O] There's a cost angle here that I think gets underrated.
[G] The paper estimates running GPT four point one over the entire CUAD test set costs around fifty dollars, at two dollars per million input tokens and eight dollars per million output tokens. A local open model has no per-token fee at all. That's the practical case for open source, entirely separate from the confidentiality argument.
[S] Now the part I actually respect: they refuse to boil this down to one number.
[G] That's the paper's best design choice. Three lenses. First, correctness, via F one and F two. A true positive requires the prediction to fully cover the labeled span, nothing partial. F two gets reported specifically because of that thirty-seventy imbalance, it weights recall four times as heavily as precision, because missing a real risk clause is worse than flagging one that isn't there. Second, output effectiveness, the Jaccard similarity between the predicted and ground-truth token sets, computed only on the positive cases, which catches a model that finds the right clause but buries it in a paragraph of extra text. Third, "laziness," the false "no related clause" rate, how often a model bails and claims nothing's there when something actually is.
[O] Three very different failure modes, all hiding behind what would otherwise be one score.
[S] So who wins?
[G] On correctness, the proprietary models lead outright. GPT four point one mini tops the table at an F one of point six four four, an F two of point six seven eight, essentially tied with GPT four point one at point six four one and point six seven two. Claude Sonnet four lands at point five two three F one, Gemini two point five Pro at point four nine seven. The best open model is Qwen three eight B in thinking mode, at point five four zero F one, about sixteen percent below GPT four point one. At the very bottom, DeepSeek R one Distill Qwen seven B and Qwen three four B in thinking mode both score under point one F one, close to total failure on the task.
[O] But that's not the full picture, and this is where it gets interesting.
[G] Flip to Jaccard, the conciseness metric, and the ranking scrambles. Gemini two point five Pro is actually the best of anyone there, at point five zero six. And Gemma three twelve B, an open model, hits point four four six, which edges out GPT four point one mini's point four three five and nearly matches GPT four point one's point four seven two. Meanwhile, the Qwen three eight B variants that led on F one drop to point three nine one or lower on Jaccard. They're finding the right clause, but padding it with extra text.
[S] So "best" depends entirely on which failure mode you care about.
[G] Exactly, and the laziness axis spreads the field out even further. Gemma three four B never once says "no related clause" when it shouldn't, a false rate of exactly zero. The proprietary models all stay low too, Gemini at point zero one one, Claude Sonnet four at point zero two five, both GPT four point one variants around point zero seven. But several open Qwen three models in non-thinking mode are alarmingly quick to give up. Qwen three eight B AWQ tops the field at just over thirty percent false abstention. Qwen three eight B FP eight sits at about twenty-eight and a half percent. Even Llama three point one eight B Instruct misses on more than one in five questions.
[O] That's the number that would actually worry me if I were the lawyer relying on this.
[G] It should. A missed clause isn't a rounding error, it's the exact thing you're paying the review to catch.
[S] What about the size and reasoning story?
[G] Two counterintuitive findings. First, within the Qwen three family, the eight B model beats both the smaller four B and the larger fourteen B on F one. Correctness has diminishing, even negative, returns to scale here, though Jaccard, the conciseness score, keeps climbing with size. Second, turning on thinking mode trades correctness for conciseness, and the swing can be enormous: Qwen three four B falls from an F one of point four one one with thinking off to point zero seven five with thinking on. The authors' read is that step-by-step reasoning leads the model to over-explain or wander into irrelevant clauses on what's fundamentally a precise extraction task. Quantization tells a smaller version of the same story, FP eight and AWQ save GPU cost but shave some accuracy, and that drop worsens once thinking is switched on.
[O] And then there's the category breakdown.
[G] Which might be the most operationally important result in the paper. Comparing GPT four point one mini against Gemma three twelve B across all forty-one clause categories, both models approach or exceed point nine F one on common clauses like Governing Law and Parties. But on rare, high-stakes categories, Uncapped Liability, Joint IP Ownership, Notice Period to Terminate Renewal, both models collapse to near zero. Those are precisely the clauses whose omission could blow up a deal.
[O] Okay, let me make the optimist case. A model you can run on a single GPU, no data leaving the building, is landing an F one in the point five range on real contract clauses, and the frontier proprietary model is hitting roughly junior-associate quality. Eighteen months ago that comparison wouldn't have been close.
[S] I'll push back hard on the framing "open lags proprietary." Look at which open models they actually tested, capped at fourteen billion parameters. No Llama seventy B, no Qwen three thirty-two B or two hundred thirty-five B, no full DeepSeek V three or R one. The real frontier of open weights sits an order of magnitude above anything in this table. So when the paper says proprietary beats open source, isn't that partly just restating that large beats small?
[G] That's a fair point, and one the paper doesn't fully own. The honest way to state the finding is frontier-API beats single-GPU-local, not that open weights as a category can't do this task. The fourteen B cap is a defensible deployment constraint, that's genuinely what a small firm can run on one machine, but it does mean you can't cleanly separate open-versus-closed from small-versus-large in these numbers.
[S] Second issue, and I think it's bigger: the true-positive rule is binary. A prediction has to fully cover the labeled span or it's scored as a complete miss, no partial credit for a clause that's ninety percent right but clips a boundary token.
[O] Does that cut in one direction, though, or does it hurt everyone equally?
[G] It's not symmetric, and it's worth sitting with. The rule only penalizes under-coverage, clipping the span, on F one. Padding extra text around a correct span still counts as "fully covering" it, so over-inclusion gets charged to Jaccard instead, never to F one. That's exactly why the verbose Qwen three eight B variants keep a decent F one but a low Jaccard. And it reframes that dramatic thinking-mode collapse we just discussed, Qwen three four B falling from point four one one to point zero seven five. Part of that could be a genuine reasoning deficit, but part could be a measurement artifact: if a thinking model's output includes its reasoning trace instead of the verbatim clause, the string match simply fails, even if the model located the right clause. The paper doesn't test for that, and it doesn't rule it out either.
[S] So we genuinely don't know how much of "thinking hurts correctness" is a real finding versus a parsing problem.
[G] Correct, that's an honest gap.
[O] What about contamination? CUAD's been sitting on the open web since 2021.
[G] And it's sourced from SEC EDGAR filings, fully public. Every model in this study was pretrained on web text through 2024 or 2025, so it's entirely plausible some of these contracts, maybe even some annotation patterns, were seen during pretraining. Part of the proprietary lead could be memorization of EDGAR filings rather than a clause-extraction skill gap. The paper doesn't probe this at all, and for a 2025 benchmark built on a four-year-old public dataset, that's a live confound, not a hypothetical one.
[S] And it's single-run, single-prompt, no variance sweep, even though the authors themselves say in their own discussion that prompt sensitivity is an open problem for these models.
[G] Right, that's in their own limitations section. So take the absolute F one numbers as a floor, not a ceiling, until someone runs the variance check.
[O] One more thing I want to give the paper credit for: it doesn't hide the ball on Gemma three four B's perfect laziness score.
[G] It doesn't oversell it either, to be fair. A false rate of zero isn't unambiguously good, a model that simply never says "no related clause" avoids every abstention error by construction, and that's part of why its F one is only point one eight eight on a dataset that's seventy percent negative. You don't get credit for never guessing wrong on the one answer type you never give.
[O] Step back, why should someone outside legal tech care about this?
[S] Because it's a clean case study in construct validity for evaluations generally. A single F one score here would have completely hidden the laziness problem and the verbosity problem. Any benchmark that collapses several real failure modes into one number is probably hiding something similar.
[G] And the contamination point generalizes too. Any 2025 benchmark built on a dataset that's been public for years needs a memorization check before you trust the headline gap, whether that's contracts, code, or anything else scraped from the open web.
[O] For me the narrower, actionable takeaway is this: if you're a small firm deciding whether to run something locally today, this paper is a genuinely useful, if slightly depressing, first map. Just don't trust an open model's silence.
[G] That's the paper's own conclusion too, treat "no related clause" from a small open model with real suspicion until it's been fine-tuned on legal data.
