Eighty examples moving a one hundred seventy-five billion parameter model is either the most efficient alignment result of twenty twenty-one, or a very well-instrumented case of two people teaching a model to agree with them.

[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where we take one paper from the atlas and actually argue about it. Today's paper is "Process for Adapting Language Models to Society," PALMS, with Values-Targeted Datasets, by Irene Solaiman and Christy Dennison, published at NeurIPS 2021 while both were at OpenAI.
[S] It's on the docket because it's an early, unusually direct answer to a question every alignment paper since has had to deal with: if you want a large pretrained model to behave according to some set of values, do you need a bigger dataset, or a smarter one?
[O] And to help us dig into it, we've got Daniel, who's spent a lot of time with this paper. Daniel, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. This one's fun to revisit, because it's small in every dimension except the claim it's making.
[S] Set the scene for us. Why does this paper exist in the middle of twenty twenty-one?
[G] GPT-3 had been out for about a year, and by then there was a solid stack of evidence that it reproduced ugly patterns from its training data — negative associations with race, with gender, with religion. The "stochastic parrots" paper had just landed making a related point, that scale alone doesn't fix this. So the problem the authors are staring at isn't "does the model have biases." That was already established. It's "what do you actually do about it that isn't retraining the whole model from scratch."
[O] Which is expensive, and also doesn't obviously solve the underlying problem, because "desirable behavior" isn't one fixed target to train toward.
[G] Exactly, and the authors are explicit about that. They pick a lens — U.S. English, informed by U.S. and international human rights law — and they say directly that this is one cultural frame, not a universal one. The paper's contribution is meant to be a process you could point at other value sets, not a claim that theirs is the correct set.
[S] Which already tells you where the fight in this episode is going to go.
[O] Patience. Let's earn it first. Daniel, walk us through the actual process. What does PALMS mean mechanically?
[G] It's seven steps, designed as a loop. Step one: pick sensitive topics. They generated a long list and grouped it into eight high-level categories, things like "Human Characteristics and Behavior." Step two: write a position statement for each category — what should the model actually say here. For that human-characteristics category, the position is roughly that beauty and likeability are subjective, and the model shouldn't reinforce narrow standards.
[S] So far this is just two people deciding what's true.
[G] Correct, and that's steps one and two, plainly. Step three is writing prompts, question-answer format, eighty of them total for training — seventy on broad topics like history or government policy, and ten specifically targeting categories where early testing showed the model was weak. Step four is writing the completions, the actual answers, and this part is worth noting: they hired a professional writer, gave them outlines derived from the position statements, and had the writer produce answers between forty and three hundred forty words each.
[O] So that's the whole training set. Eighty question-answer pairs, professionally written to spec.
[G] That's it. Step five is fine-tuning GPT-3 on those eighty examples, at eight different model sizes, from one hundred twenty-five million parameters up to one hundred seventy-five billion. Step six is evaluation, and step seven is iterate — go back and patch steps two through four based on what the evaluation shows. They report completing one full round of that iteration before locking in the results the paper reports.
[S] And the evaluation side is where I want detail, because "we fine-tuned on eighty things and it worked" needs a control, or it's not really evidence of anything specific.
[G] They built one. Alongside the values-targeted model, they trained a control model on one hundred neutral snippets pulled from a private corpus of books and Wikipedia — length-matched to the values-targeted set, but not selected for any stance at all. The point is to separate two possible explanations: maybe any decent fine-tuning data improves behavior, or maybe it specifically has to be values-targeted data.
[O] That's a genuinely good design choice. Without that control, you can't tell those two explanations apart.
[G] Agreed, and then three scoring methods on top: the Perspective API for an automated toxicity score, three human raters scoring one to five on how well each answer matches the stated position, and a qualitative co-occurrence check — what's the single most common word the model associates with a given religious, racial, or gender category.
[O] Worth being precise about the mechanics of each, since researchers listening will want the exact knobs.
[G] Good idea. The toxicity score is an average of four Perspective API sub-scores — toxicity, severe toxicity, threat, and insult. The human ratings come from three raters hired through Surge AI, blind to which model produced which output, scoring five weakness-targeting prompts per category, with completions generated at temperature point seven. And the co-occurrence check samples eight hundred completions per prompt using nucleus sampling at a top-p of point eight — the same setup the original GPT-3 paper used for its own bias probes, so the numbers are at least comparable to that baseline.
[S] Let's get to numbers, then. What actually moved?
[G] Toxicity dropped for the values-targeted model at every single model size, and the gap was widest at the largest sizes. The base model's toxicity was highest of the three, values-targeted was lowest, and the control model landed in between. So decent fine-tuning data helps a little, and values-targeted data helps a lot more.
[O] And the human evaluation scores?
[G] Same direction, and this is the paper's headline result: the values-targeted model's adherence score was consistently higher than both base and control, and the size of that improvement itself grew as the base model grew larger. Their read is a rough scaling relationship — bigger models need proportionally fewer curated examples to shift by the same amount.
[S] That's the flashy claim. What about capability? Did they check whether the model got worse at everything else in exchange for getting nicer on the targeted topics?
[G] They reran the same capability probes used on the original GPT-3 paper, comparing the one hundred seventy-five billion parameter values-targeted model against base, and reported results within about one percent accuracy. Their argument is that the intervention is narrow enough not to bleed into general capability.
[O] And generalization — because eighty examples is a tiny net to throw over a huge space of possible prompts.
[G] Right, so they checked that directly: thirty-four of their forty test prompts had no close match anywhere in the eighty-example training set. Performance stayed strong regardless, and their interpretation is that the model is extrapolating the desired behavior from patterns already sitting in GPT-3's pretraining data, rather than memorizing eighty specific answers.
[S] I'll flag one thing in the qualitative results that the paper itself flags, to its credit. In the co-occurrence checks, the values-targeted model stops associating "Muslim" with the word "Brotherhood," which reads as progress. But the top association becomes "Heterosexual" instead. That's not becoming unbiased. That's trading one bias for a different one that happens to look better to these two particular authors.
[G] That's an accurate read, and the paper says almost exactly that itself — the pattern throughout is less overtly harmful, not bias-free. The race results follow the same shape. Base models across the board surface derogatory words for "Black" — "Monster," "Thug" — where the values-targeted models surface "All American." For "White," the largest base model's top word is "Supremacy," and the largest values-targeted model's is "Canadians." Cleaner, but still a specific, chosen substitution rather than an absence of any association at all.
[S] Which is a good moment to note the control model doesn't clean any of that up. On race and religion, the control behaves almost identically to base — still surfacing "Supremacy," still surfacing slurs — which is more evidence that generic high-quality fine-tuning data isn't doing the targeted work here. It really does need to be values-targeted.
[O] Okay. Let's actually argue this out. My case for why it matters: at the time, the dominant intuition was that behavior change requires scale — more data, more compute, bigger interventions. This paper shows a highly targeted, eighty-example dataset moves a one hundred seventy-five billion parameter model's behavior more than a matched, high-quality control does, and the effect gets bigger, not smaller, as the model scales up. That's a genuinely useful, somewhat counterintuitive empirical result, and you can draw a fairly straight line from this to later fine-tuning-based alignment techniques that bet on small, curated data beating brute-force retraining.
[S] I'll grant the empirical shape of that. Smaller, curated, targeted data outperforming a matched neutral control is a real result, and it's a clean piece of experimental design. But here's my problem: who decided what "good" behavior looks like on beauty, gender, religion, and race? The same two people wrote the position statements, guided the writer's completions to match those positions, and wrote the rating guide the human evaluators used to score against those same positions. That's not external validation of reduced harm. That's measuring whether the model learned to agree with its own authors.
[G] Both points land, and to the paper's credit, it doesn't hide from either one. On the circularity, the authors are unusually candid — there's a line stating outright that the positions they use reflect one cultural lens, shaped by U.S. law and industry priorities, both of which are crafted by large, powerful institutions. They're not claiming universal correctness anywhere in the text. But that honesty doesn't change the underlying mechanism: "the model learned to match a rubric its authors wrote" is what's happening under the hood, whatever framing surrounds it.

[G] It's a real gap, and there's a second one that compounds it: the eighty-example values-targeted dataset itself was never released. There's a footnote citing a data policy. Fine-tuning on non-toxic text, controllable generation with toxic-expert subtraction, sentence-level debiasing — all mentioned, none benchmarked against. We don't actually know if PALMS beats those, or just beats doing nothing.

[S] So where does that leave us? Proof of concept, or actual evidence of reduced harm?
[G] My read, staying close to what the paper itself supports: it's a strong proof of concept for the mechanism. Small, curated, values-targeted fine-tuning data measurably shifts behavior on the targeted axis, more so at scale, without a large capability cost. It is not independently verified evidence of reduced real-world harm, because the whole loop — deciding the target, writing to it, grading against it — runs through the same two authors, and the one artifact that would let someone else check the work was withheld.

[O] Zoom out for a second, Daniel. Where does this sit relative to what came after it?
[G] It's a genuine precursor. The core intuition — that a small, carefully written dataset can steer a large pretrained model's behavior more efficiently than throwing more undifferentiated data at it — is the same intuition underneath instruction tuning, reinforcement learning from human feedback, and later constitutional-style approaches. PALMS doesn't use preference comparisons or a reward model, it's just supervised fine-tuning on eighty hand-written pairs, but the shape of the bet is identical.
[S] And for anyone doing evaluation work specifically, this is a nice early case study in a problem that never went away: when your grader and your target are designed by the same people, your metric can look great while telling you very little about how it generalizes to anyone else's values.
[G] That's the through-line I'd leave people with. The construct being measured and the process defining the construct aren't independent here, and that's worth remembering every time a paper reports a clean number against a rubric its own team wrote.
[G] If there's one sentence to take from the paper itself, it's this: significantly adjusting a large language model's behavior is feasible with a small, hand-curated dataset, and that effect gets stronger, not weaker, as the model gets bigger.
