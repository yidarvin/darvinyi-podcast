[O] Marcus, start with the gap. Why did nobody ask this cleanly before?
[G] Because it's genuinely hard to ask without cheating. The paper's framing is that the single most expensive decision in empirical science is choosing which experiment to run next. Beam time on an accelerator, a months-long crystallization screen, a materials synthesis campaign, each one commits real money before any data comes back. If a model could reliably predict outcomes, you'd prune the dead ends and concentrate resources on what's likely to pay off. And the authors argue LLMs should be well suited to this, since they've absorbed most of the scientific literature and can reason about complex systems.
[S] So why didn't the existing "AI for science" benchmarks already measure this?
[G] Because they measure adjacent things. Literature review, paper drafting, reproducing the code and results of a paper that's already published, generating novel hypotheses. All of those are about summarizing or re-deriving what's already known. None of them ask the forward-looking question: given an experimental setup you've never seen, what will the measurement actually show.
[O] And the reason that's hard to test is data leakage.
[G] Exactly, and this is the paper's central design move. Any experiment old enough to appear in a benchmark is old enough to be sitting in the training data, so a "prediction" is really just recall. SciPredict sidesteps that by pulling every task from a paper first posted after March thirty-first, twenty twenty-five, which postdates the training cutoff of every model they evaluate, and they disable web search during evaluation so models can't just go look up the paper. What's left, in principle, is genuine predictive reasoning from first principles plus whatever context they're given.
[S] I want to push on that cutoff claim later, because I don't think it does everything the paper implies. But first, walk us through what a task actually looks like.
[G] Four hundred five tasks, spanning thirty-three specialized subfields, nine in physics, ten in chemistry, fourteen in biology, weighted so biology is about half the set and physics and chemistry split the rest. Each task hands the model the skeleton of a real experiment: the system under study, the conditions imposed, the measurements taken, any interventions, and asks for the outcome. Questions come in three formats. Multiple choice, about forty percent of the set. Free-form, about thirty-two percent. And numerical value, about twenty-eight percent.
[O] Why three formats instead of just picking the easiest one to grade?
[G] Because they capture different capabilities. Multiple choice lets you isolate the right answer among plausible alternatives and it's easy to grade automatically. Free-form tests whether the model can explain an outcome in its own words the way a scientist would. Numerical tests whether it can produce a quantity, not just a qualitative direction. Grading is format-specific: exact match for multiple choice, an expert-set acceptable range for numerical answers that absorbs measurement noise, and for free-form, an LLM judge, specifically Gemini three Pro, scoring against a one-to-ten expert-written rubric, cross-checked against GPT-five point two with no statistically significant difference found between the two judges.

[G] Correct, and it's a clean, almost stark result. [O] Marcus, that's a huge tell. It means the bottleneck isn't "does the model have the knowledge somewhere in its weights." It's "can it tell what's relevant right now."
[G] That's exactly the paper's read, and I think it's right. It also explains something you'd otherwise find puzzling: format matters more than model identity. Multiple choice is consistently the easiest format, free-form lower, numerical lowest. And when the authors take a multiple-choice question and rewrite it as a matched open-ended prompt, same experiment, options removed, accuracy drops across essentially every model family. That gap is the tell that headline multiple-choice scores reflect recognition, comparing candidates and picking the closest match, rather than the open-ended generation a real research workflow actually demands.
[S] Now give me the headline finding. The calibration one.
[G] This is the paper's real center of gravity. Human experts are sharply calibrated. Their accuracy climbs from about five percent on questions they rate as infeasible to predict, without running the experiment, up to about eighty percent on questions they rate as feasible. They know which outcomes follow from established principles and which genuinely require the lab. Models show none of that structure.
