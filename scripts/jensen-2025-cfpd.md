[S] Elena, start with the gap. Why do we need a new benchmark here at all?
[G] Because almost every evaluation we run on these models is the wrong instrument for this problem. Benchmarks overwhelmingly grade capability against a verifiable answer key: can the model reason, recall a fact, write correct code. Meanwhile the authors point out that a twenty twenty-four White House memo directed the US government to harness AI models for national security systems, the Department of Defense's Combined Joint All-Domain Command and Control program is being built around AI-enabled decision support, and the State Department has its own hub encouraging diplomats to experiment with these tools. None of that gets tested by a math benchmark.
[O] So the risk isn't that the model gets an answer wrong, it's something else entirely.
[G] Right, the authors call it deployment bias: the risk of applying an off-the-shelf model to a use case far outside what it was built and trained for. Foreign policy has no answer key. Whether to escalate a border dispute or intervene in a humanitarian crisis is, in the paper's own words, complex, politically driven, and subjective.
[S] What existed before this? Surely someone tried to study AI and foreign policy before twenty twenty-five.
[G] Mostly crisis simulations, putting models into agentic wargames against each other or against a scripted opponent. Those studies are evocative. Some found models will escalate all the way to nuclear use. Others, running a similar setup, found the opposite. A third showed that simply rewording a prompt, while keeping the same meaning, could flip the recommended course of action entirely.
[O] That last one is the real problem, honestly. If prompt wording alone flips the outcome, the wargame isn't measuring the model, it's measuring the prompt.
[G] Exactly the failure mode CFPD is trying to design around. Wargames are bespoke, hard to reproduce, and too cumbersome to rerun every time a new model ships. CFPD's bet is that you can fix a scenario set and a metric, skip the ground truth entirely, and measure the distribution of choices instead of their correctness.
[S] Walk me through what's actually in the dataset.
[G] Four hundred expert-crafted scenarios, one hundred each across escalation, intervention, cooperation, and alliance dynamics. They were written and triple-reviewed by a team of post-doctoral and advanced PhD international-relations specialists. Scenarios start actor-agnostic, Actor A and Actor B, and then get real country pairs swapped in. Because most scenarios admit many eligible country dyads, those four hundred base scenarios expand to sixty-six thousand, four hundred seventy-three individual questions, spanning two hundred sixteen qualified actors.
[O] That's an enormous multiplier from four hundred base cases.
[G] It is, and it's deliberate. The four domains break down further into fourteen sub-dimensions and twenty-eight codified response comparisons. Take escalation: the options are No Use of Force, a middle Threat of Force tier, and Use of Force, which itself splits into four sub-types pulled straight from the Militarized Interstate Dispute dataset: Attack, Blockade, Declaration of War, and Clash. Cyber operations get coded into that middle threat tier, below the use-of-force threshold, following the IR literature on escalation firebreaks.
[S] And the other three domains?
[G] Intervention is No, Middle, or High intervention across humanitarian crises, disaster relief, conflict-zone aid, and regime intervention. Cooperation is Cooperative versus Non-cooperative, over joining, violating, or enforcing agreements. Alliance dynamics tests Balancing, the reference category, against Bandwagoning, Buck Passing, and Power Maximization, straight out of the realist international-relations canon.
[O] Who are the countries actually being advised?
[G] Five core qualified actors get equal representation across every domain: the United States, China, the United Kingdom, India, and Russia. Beyond those five, other actors are drawn from the top forty countries by military expenditure, for scenarios without a geographic constraint. Here's a detail I like: some pairings are deliberately counter-factual. France and Germany get cast as adversaries in places. That's intentional. The authors want to know whether a model just echoes the current geopolitical status quo, or actually reasons about the structural situation described in the prompt.
[S] That's a real design strength. It's testing whether the model is pattern-matching current headlines or engaging with the scenario as written.
[G] And it connects to the other thing they controlled hard for: prompt phrasing. Prior research showed minor wording changes can flip model responses, so every scenario was reviewed by at least two people who didn't write it, specifically to strip language that nudges toward one answer and to confirm both options are roughly equally reasonable.
[O] How do you score something with no ground truth, though? What's the actual number you end up with?
[G] Because some scenarios have far more eligible country dyads than others, a handful recur over fifteen hundred times, most under a hundred, raw counts would let a few high-combinatorial scenarios dominate. So they normalize per scenario first: compute each scenario's selection rate for a given response, then average those rates evenly across the domain. That keeps a scenario with fifteen hundred instances from drowning out one with twenty.
[S] And how do open and closed models actually get run? I assume you can't treat GPT-4o and Llama the same way.
[G] You can't, and this matters a lot later. Open models, the two Llama 3.1 sizes, Mixtral 8x22B, Qwen2 72B, were run at temperature zero with guided decoding, which forces selection of the single highest-probability labeled option. Closed models, GPT-4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, Gemini 1.5 Pro, can't be constrained that way, so they were sampled five times each and reported as a mean plus or minus one standard deviation, with their free-text answers manually encoded to A, B, C, or refusal, because closed models tend to add explanations, hallucinate extra options, or just decline. Those closed-model runs happened on December thirteenth, twenty twenty-four. Uncertainty everywhere is quantified with a bootstrap: resample the scenario set with replacement, recompute the mean, take the two-point-five and ninety-seven-point-five percentiles as a ninety-five percent confidence interval.
[O] And the dataset itself, can I go download these four hundred scenarios?
[G] No, and that's deliberate too. It's withheld for contamination reasons, plus they plan to rerun this exact recipe on future model releases without worrying those releases trained on the benchmark. What they publish instead is the full methodology, the recipe rather than the ingredients.
[S] Give me the headline number. Where do models actually disagree?
[G] Escalation, by far. It's the only domain where variation between models reaches statistical significance. On the three-choice version, Claude 3.5 Sonnet is the most restrained model in the entire study, picking No Use of Force sixty-seven point six seven percent of the time. GPT-4o is close behind at sixty-one point four eight percent.
[O] And on the other end?
[G] Llama 3.1 8B Instruct picks outright Use of Force forty-one point three six percent of the time. Qwen2 72B is right behind it at thirty-three point four six percent. Both are statistically distinguishable from GPT-4o and Claude.
[S] Say the Llama number again, against its own sibling model.
[G] Forty-one point three six percent for the eight-billion-parameter Llama, versus fifteen percent for the seventy-billion-parameter version of the same model family. That's a twenty-six point three six percentage-point gap between two models trained the same way, differing mainly in scale.
[O] More parameters, less escalation. That's a striking within-family result on its own, independent of anything about cross-vendor comparisons.
[G] It is. Gemini 1.5 Pro is the great equivocator of the group, it parks fifty-four point eight three percent of its answers in that middle Threat of Force tier, regardless of which country it's advising. Refusals, across the board, are rare: GPT-4o at zero point zero three percent, Gemini at zero percent, Claude at one point one seven percent on this three-choice version.
[S] What about the other three domains?
[G] Real but weaker divergence. On three-choice intervention, both Llama models and Qwen2 72B pick High Intervention near or above fifty percent of the time. Gemini favors the middle option at a rate twenty-six point one four percentage points higher than the least likely model, Llama 3.1 8B Instruct. GPT-4o is the least interventionist overall, choosing No Intervention twenty-eight point six percent of the time. On cooperation, every model recommends cooperative action to all five core countries more than seventy-five percent of the time, Claude is the most cooperative with the tightest confidence intervals, Mixtral the least, but none of those cross-model gaps are statistically significant. On alliance dynamics, Balancing dominates for everyone, partly because it's the built-in reference category.
