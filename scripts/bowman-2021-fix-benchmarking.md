[O] Here's a sentence that should worry anyone who trusts a leaderboard: by 2019, SuperGLUE's organizers had to throw out most of the candidate benchmarks they'd collected, because BERT was already beating a majority vote of human annotators on them.
[S] And yet those same superhuman models fall apart the second you ask them something slightly different from the training distribution. Something is broken, and I don't think it's the models.
[O] Stick around, because by the end of this episode you'll understand why the community's favorite fix at the time, adversarial data collection, gets called out by name as treating the symptom instead of the disease.
[S] And why the authors think some of that superhuman score isn't a superhuman model at all. It's a broken definition of ground truth.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a guest scholar dig into one paper from the evaluation and benchmarking literature.
[S] Today's paper is "What Will it Take to Fix Benchmarking in Natural Language Understanding," by Samuel Bowman of New York University and George Dahl of Google Research, published in 2021.
[O] It's a position paper. No new model, no new dataset, just a diagnosis of why N-L-U benchmarks stopped being useful, and four criteria for fixing that. Joining us is Elena, who's spent a lot of time with this paper. Elena, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is one of those papers that reads like common sense in hindsight, but nobody had actually written the checklist down before.
[S] Let's set the scene. What was actually broken in 2021?
[G] The core symptom is saturation. On G-L-U-E, the nine-task benchmark that basically defined the pretraining era, leaderboard performance had approached or exceeded estimated human performance on all nine tasks. SQuAD two's reading comprehension leaderboard had long passed human annotators as well.
[O] Which sounds like great news. We solved reading comprehension and sentence understanding.
[S] Except the same systems collapse on cases specifically designed to probe those exact skills. The paper cites the HANS diagnostic and CheckList, both showing top models failing simple, targeted tests of the very abilities the leaderboard claims they've mastered.
[G] Right, and the authors put it sharply: our systems have significant room to improve, but we have no guarantee our benchmarks will detect that improvement when it happens. A broken metric doesn't just mislead you, it discourages newcomers from trying to contribute, and it gives specialists room to cherry-pick evaluation settings that quietly mask a lack of progress.
[O] So the field's fix at the time was DynaBench. Ask crowdworkers to write examples that fool a live reference model, and keep collecting adversarial data forever.
[S] Which guarantees hard examples. That part works.
[G] It guarantees hard examples in the short term, yes. But Bowman and Dahl's argument is that collecting examples current models fail on is, in their words, neither necessary nor sufficient for a useful benchmark. It can even create a perverse incentive: a new model tops the leaderboard by producing different errors than the adversary, not fewer errors, because the examples that would expose its new mistakes were never collected.
[O] That's a genuinely clever point. You could game an adversarial leaderboard just by being wrong in a new way.
[S] So if adversarial filtering isn't the fix, what's the alternative?
[G] They start by separating two ideas people conflate: a task and a benchmark. A task is an abstract language skill in a fixed input-output format, say multiple-choice reading comprehension. A benchmark is one concrete attempt to measure that task with a specific dataset and metric. Their claim is there's no general proof that a given benchmark actually captures its task, so the whole job of benchmark design is narrowing that gap as much as possible.
[O] And that's where the four criteria come in.
[G] Exactly. First, validity: outscoring another system should be real evidence you're better at the task, not evidence you found a shortcut. The authors are refreshingly honest here, they say they know of no simple test to certify validity, only necessary conditions, like covering the full range of linguistic variation and being free of the annotation artifacts that let a model reach near-human scores without doing the underlying reasoning.
[S] Second criterion?
[G] Reliable annotation. Test labels have to be correct, which fails in three ways: careless mislabeling, underspecified guidelines, and the subtle one, legitimate disagreement, where reasonable annotators genuinely disagree.
[O] Give me the fun example.
[G] The paper actually has one. Does "Ed ate a burrito" entail "Ed ate a sandwich"? Most people say no, but there's a real regulatory and legal literature arguing a burrito counts as a sandwich. That's legitimate disagreement, not noise.
[S] Third and fourth?
[G] Statistical power, meaning the benchmark has enough examples and enough difficulty to reliably detect a real gap between two models. And fourth, bias disincentives: a benchmark should reward a model without harmful social biases over an equivalent one that has them, not accidentally reward the biased shortcut.
[O] Four clean criteria. I like that this isn't just "benchmarks bad," it's an actual rubric.
[S] It's a rubric, but notice what it rules out along the way. They walk through naturally-occurring data, expert-authored examples, plain crowdsourcing, and adversarial filtering, and argue none of the four reliably satisfies validity on its own.
[G] The adversarial filtering critique is the sharpest of those. They call it mode-seeking rather than mass-covering: by systematically throwing out everything the reference model already solves, you shrink the dataset's diversity and coverage, which makes validity harder to hit, not easier. Though they're careful to say adversarial competitions, comparing how hard it is to fool different systems, are a fine complement, as long as you don't use a model to filter the actual test set.
[O] Given all that, what do the authors actually recommend doing instead?
[G] For validity, the most promising direction is hybrid: start from crowdsourced data, then bring in domain experts or computational linguists to patch gaps and remove artifacts. They point to Hu and colleagues' OCNLI project, where small interventions during crowdsourcing, like paying a bonus for avoiding overused words and constructions, meaningfully improved data quality without killing off annotators' creativity.
[S] That's a modest, unglamorous fix. I like that about it.
[O] Let's go through the receipts. Start with validity and saturation.
[G] Already covered the headline: all nine G-L-U-E tasks at or above human performance. But the SuperGLUE story is even more telling. When SuperGLUE solicited candidate benchmarks from the community in 2019, the organizers had to exclude the large majority of submissions because BERT was already at or above a majority vote of human crowdworkers on them. Of the eight tasks that did leave BERT real headroom, every single one is now effectively saturated too.
[S] So the goalposts didn't just move, the field ran out of goalposts twice in a row.
[G] That's a fair way to put it.
[O] Now the annotation piece. This is the one that surprised me most.
[G] It should. Across several textual entailment datasets, twenty percent of examples are significantly ambiguous, according to Pavlick and Kwiatkowski's analysis. And in Natural Questions, thirty-six percent of short-answer annotations differ significantly from the majority answer. When a benchmark is full of legitimately disputable examples, a model that just learns to predict the majority label can out-score individual humans who are honestly reporting their own judgment. That's an illusion of superhuman understanding, built entirely out of annotator disagreement.
[S] Which reframes half the "superhuman" headlines from that era as a measurement artifact, not a capability milestone.
[O] Harsh, but probably fair. What about statistical power?
[G] This is the criterion I think gets underrated. With a reliable, hard dataset, detecting a one-point absolute gain, eighty percent to eighty-one percent, only takes a few thousand examples. But the same five percent relative improvement near the ceiling, ninety-eight percent to ninety-eight point one percent, needs two orders of magnitude more data, according to Card and colleagues. As models improve, the benchmark has to get radically bigger just to keep detecting real progress.
[O] And that costs real money.
[G] It does. Even on optimistic assumptions, forty-five seconds to label an existing example, one minute to write a new one, only ten percent of examples discarded for disagreement, a ten-way-annotated set of five hundred thousand examples costs over one million dollars at fifteen dollars an hour. For scale, the paper notes public estimates put GPT-3's training cost above ten million dollars. Adequate power is buyable, but expensive enough that it concentrates the field's attention on a handful of high-resource languages and tasks.
[S] And the fourth criterion, bias? I want the incentive-design point, because that's the one people forget.
[G] The state-of-the-art T5 model shows far more sensitivity to irrelevant gender cues than humans do, on SuperGLUE's Winogender diagnostic. But here's the part that stings: SuperGLUE actually requires you to compute that bias metric before it releases your other test-set results, and a large majority of papers reporting SuperGLUE numbers simply omit the unflattering bias figure anyway.
[O] So the measurement existed, and people just didn't report it.
[G] Correct. Measurement without an obligation to disclose it changes very little.
[O] Before we move to the debate, I want the hardest part of the bias criterion: how do you even enumerate which biases to test for in the first place?
[G] That's the part the authors are honest about too. They point to something easy to miss from a US-centric view: caste is a major axis of discrimination in hiring in India, with real legal recognition there, but it carries none of that recognition in the United States, where it's easy for non-specialist bias researchers to overlook entirely. Their point is that choosing which biases to measure is inherently political, there's no neutral, complete list, but that's not an excuse to measure nothing.
[S] Time for the debate. I'll go first, because I think the paper's own prescription didn't survive contact with reality. They bet against adversarial and out-of-distribution evaluation, and for doubling down on static, I-I-D benchmarks made bigger, harder, and better annotated. The field didn't take that fork. We got harder held-out exams like G-P-Q-A and Humanity's Last Exam, contamination-resistant suites like LiveCodeBench, and human-preference arenas like Chatbot Arena, often pursued by the same labs at the same time.
[O] Sure, but I'd push back on calling that a loss. The diagnosis is the load-bearing part of this paper, and it aged extremely well. Benchmark saturation, annotation artifacts, superhuman-as-artifact, all of that became mainstream vocabulary within a couple of years, and the whole train-test contamination panic of the LLM era is basically the validity criterion restated for a new generation of models.
[S] I'll grant the diagnosis. I'm harder on the cure. Even careful, expert-written exams now saturate within a release cycle or two. If "make it bigger and harder" bought only a year of headroom instead of a decade, that undercuts the paper's central remedy on its own terms.
[G] Both of you are right about different halves of the paper, and that split is exactly how I'd score it. The four criteria are still, five years later, the cleanest checklist anyone has for asking whether a benchmark deserves trust. Practically every major evaluation controversy since, from contamination in MMLU to disagreement over LLM judges, maps onto one of those four boxes. Where the paper is more exposed is the bet it makes against the field's actual trajectory: it treats adversarial and out-of-distribution methods as a distraction from the real fix, and the field pursued both the real fix and the distraction at once.
[O] So the paper won the argument about what matters, and lost the argument about which methods should be used to get there.
[G] That's a good summary. I'd add one limitation nobody's mentioned yet: this is entirely a paper about N-L-U classification, single labels over fixed inputs. The dominant problem after 2022 is grading open-ended generation, and this paper is silent on that by design, the authors explicitly set aside anything outside labeled classification tasks.
[S] Which is a big blind spot in hindsight, because grading generation is exactly where their own reliable-annotation worry comes back with a vengeance, except now the annotator is often another model.
[G] Right, an LLM judge is an unreliable annotator by a different name, and this paper doesn't have anything to say about how to certify one. That's not really a flaw so much as a boundary they drew on purpose, but it does mean the paper can't be used off the shelf for today's biggest evaluation headache.
[O] So if I'm building a benchmark in twenty twenty-six, what do I actually take from this?
[G] Use the four criteria as a pre-registration checklist before you collect a single example. Ask up front how you'll demonstrate validity, how you'll handle annotator disagreement instead of pretending it doesn't exist, whether your planned sample size can detect the effect sizes you actually expect near the ceiling, and what bias metric you're going to require, not just offer, alongside the headline number.
[S] I'd add: budget for the long tail. The paper's own math says a five percent relative gain near saturation needs two orders of magnitude more data than the same relative gain lower down. If you don't plan for that upfront, your benchmark expires the moment it starts getting interesting.
[G] And SuperGLUE's own compliance failure is the cautionary tale here. You can require a bias metric and still watch most papers quietly skip reporting it, which is why the paper's real institutional point, that measurement without an obligation to disclose it does very little, might be its most underappreciated line. It's not a data-collection problem, it's a publication-norms problem.
[O] That's a good note to end on: this isn't just a checklist for collecting data, it's a checklist for what a field is willing to enforce.
[G] If there's one sentence to take from this paper, it's that benchmarking N-L-U is broken in specific, diagnosable ways, and adversarial data collection treats the symptom rather than the disease.
[O] My takeaway is more optimistic: this paper gave the field a shared vocabulary, validity, reliable annotation, statistical power, bias disincentives, and that vocabulary outlived every specific benchmark it was written about.
[S] Mine is a warning label: read the four criteria as a checklist, not a solved problem. Five years on we still don't have a benchmark that clears all four, and the paper's own prescription for getting there didn't survive first contact with the field.
