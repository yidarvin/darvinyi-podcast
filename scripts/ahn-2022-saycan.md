[S] And here's the sentence I'd put next to it. Three thousand three hundred citations later, the whole result rests on one hundred and one tasks the authors wrote themselves, judged by raters the authors hired, in two kitchens the authors built. Not nothing, but not an independent benchmark either.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist and a skeptic argue about a paper until someone who actually knows it settles the score.
[S] Today's paper is Do As I Can, Not As I Say: Grounding Language in Robotic Affordances, better known as SayCan, from Michael Ahn and a large team at Robotics at Google and Everyday Robots, posted in spring twenty twenty-two and published at the Conference on Robot Learning that same year.
[O] Joining us is Julian, who has spent real time with this paper and the robot footage behind it. Welcome, Julian.
[G] Glad to be here. Usual disclosure: I'm not one of the forty-plus authors on this, so when I say "they show," I mean the paper shows it, not me.
[S] Forty-plus authors already tells you the scale. Julian, ground us. What gap is this closing?
[G] Start with what a language model is good and bad at. Ask one how to clean up a spill, and you get a perfectly reasonable little essay. But that essay was never checked against reality. So the paper's running example is a robot asked, "I spilled my drink, can you help?" An ungrounded model suggests using a vacuum cleaner. Fine advice for a person, useless for a robot with no vacuum that was never trained to use one.
[O] The model knows what to do in general. It has no idea what this particular body can do right now.
[G] That's exactly the framing: task-grounding versus world-grounding. Before this paper you had two broken options. Train a policy end to end straight from instructions, and it follows simple commands but has no common sense about long, abstract requests. Or prompt a language model for a plan, and you get common sense with no guarantee any step is something the robot can do, or that it even applies to what's in the room.
[S] So the pitch is: keep the model's knowledge, but put a leash on it.
[G] That's the one-line version. Constrain what the model proposes by what the robot's own learned skills say is achievable from the current state.
[O] Let's get into the mechanism, because "Say times Can" undersells how elegant the math is.
[G] The robot has a fixed library of low-level skills, things like "pick up the sponge." Each skill has a short language description and an affordance function, the probability it succeeds if attempted from the current state. Here's the trick: in standard reinforcement learning with a sparse reward, one for success, zero otherwise, a skill's value function is mathematically identical to that affordance probability. There's no separate model to build. It already exists inside the RL policy, trained for entirely different reasons.
[S] That's a nice observation, but value functions are famously noisy. Trusting a learned Q-value as ground truth for "is this possible" feels optimistic.
[G] Fair worry, and we'll get to how often that signal is actually the one that fails. The other half: the language model separately scores the probability that each skill's description is a useful next step toward the instruction. Critically, they don't let it generate freely. They score a fixed, closed list of candidate descriptions as continuations of a dialog prompt.
[O] Why does scoring beat generation?
[G] Free generation can produce fluent nonsense that maps onto no real skill. Scoring guarantees every candidate is already something the robot owns.
[S] So you've got two probabilities. How do they combine?
[G] Multiply them, take the highest-scoring skill. That falls out of a clean assumption: a skill that succeeds makes progress with probability equal to its language score, and a skill that fails makes zero progress no matter how relevant it sounded. Multiply, take the arg-max, execute, append the choice back into the prompt, repeat until "done" scores highest. One feasible step at a time, and fully interpretable, since you can read off exactly why each step won.
[O] I love that interpretability falls out almost by accident.
[G] It's underrated. There's a figure where you watch the language bars and affordance marks update live, and see the combined score correctly pick a sponge over an option the language model alone ranked higher.
[S] Who's actually providing the "Say" and the "Can" here?
[G] The planning model is five hundred forty billion parameter PaLM, so the full system is called PaLM-SayCan. The skills come from two pipelines: a behavior-cloning method called BC-Z, and a reinforcement-learning method called MT-Opt, trained largely in simulation with a sim-to-real technique called RetinaGAN, then fine-tuned on the real robot. Every skill is conditioned on its language description through a separate, frozen sentence-embedding model, so the big planning model and the small skill-conditioning model don't have to be the same system. All told, the repertoire is five hundred fifty-one skills across seven skill families and seventeen objects: picking, placing, rearranging, opening and closing drawers, navigating.
[O] That's a lot of individually trained affordances to maintain.
[G] It is, and that's a hint at the system's limits. Nothing is fine-tuned end to end, the language model stays frozen and zero-shot. Clean and modular, but the ceiling is set by whichever piece is weakest.
[S] Does it actually work? Give us numbers, Julian.
[G] The robot is a mobile manipulator with a seven degree-of-freedom arm, tested on one hundred and one instructions across seven families: single primitives, abstract nouns, abstract verbs, structured language, embodiment-dependent queries, crowd-sourced requests, long-horizon tasks. Fifteen objects, five locations, two near-identical kitchens. Two metrics: plan success, do human raters agree the chosen skills could achieve the goal, and execution success, did the robot actually do it.
[O] And the headline?
[G] In the mock kitchen, the training environment, PaLM-SayCan hits eighty-four percent plan success and seventy-four percent execution. Move to the real kitchen, a small distribution shift, same skills, same objects, different room, and plan success barely moves, down three points to eighty-one. Execution drops harder, fourteen points, to sixty.
[S] So the plan barely notices the new room, but doing it gets a lot harder. That tells you where the weak link is.
[G] Exactly what the paper says: across all failures, sixty-five percent were language model errors, thirty-five percent were affordance errors. The bottleneck is the skill library, not the planning logic.
[O] Now the ablations, because I think this is where the paper earns its citations.
[G] Remove the value function entirely, take the highest language score with no grounding check, and plan success falls from eighty-four to sixty-seven percent. Replace scoring with free generation snapped onto the nearest known skill, and you get seventy-four, better than nothing but behind scoring. Remove the language model entirely, feed the raw instruction straight into a cloned policy, and execution collapses to zero. Give that policy a shortcut, project onto the nearest skill embedding, and you crawl to nine percent overall, almost entirely from single-step tasks. Anything longer, zero.
[S] So both halves are load-bearing, but not symmetric. Losing the affordance costs seventeen points. Losing the language model costs basically the whole system.
[G] Real asymmetry. The language model carries more of the weight beyond a single action. The affordance grounding is what keeps a fluent plan honest.
[O] Did that pattern hold across every instruction family, or did some break harder than others?
[G] Not uniform. Structured language, where the query basically spells out the skill sequence, hit ninety-three percent plan success, just under the equivalent natural-language verb queries at one hundred percent, which the authors read as evidence the model is genuinely parsing the request rather than pattern-matching a template. Embodiment-dependent queries, ones that hinge on what's already in the robot's hand or in view, were the weakest single family at sixty-four percent, mostly from affordance misclassification rather than language errors.
[O] There's also a striking result about swapping language models.
[G] They ran the identical robot loop with PaLM replaced by FLAN, a smaller, instruction-tuned model. Planning drops from eighty-four to seventy percent, execution from seventy-four to sixty-one. The authors frame this as the first demonstration that a better language model makes a better robot, not just a better chatbot.
[S] I want to push on "first demonstration" before we move on, because that's two data points doing a lot of work.
[G] Hold that thought for the debate, there's more evidence worth laying out first. They also show cheap generalization: add drawer-opening as a new skill family, one new prompt example, and over twenty-one queries you get full plan success, but only a third execute, again because chaining several manipulation steps is where things break. And two smaller case studies: chain-of-thought prompting fixes SayCan's trouble with negation, so it can now handle "bring a snack that isn't an apple." Multilingual queries in Chinese, French, and Spanish work with almost no drop in plan success, despite zero multilingual design.
[O] Time for the actual argument. My case: this is a genuinely general recipe, and the ablations prove it. Remove either signal and the system meaningfully degrades. It's fully interpretable. And a better language model made the physical robot better, not just its chat transcripts, a real coupling between two fields that used to move independently.
[S] My case, and it's not in tension with any of that: the evaluation underneath is far softer than three thousand three hundred citations imply. One hundred and one tasks designed by the authors, "inspired by" two academic benchmarks but not run on either. No external baseline beyond their own four ablations. One robot, one object set, two nearly identical kitchens. No standardized embodied benchmark at all.
[G] Accurate, and it's the paper's own framing too, "inspired by," not a run of those benchmarks.
[O] To be fair to them, they did open source a smaller version, a tabletop setup, specifically so outside groups could reproduce the mechanism without needing a fleet of mobile manipulators.
[G] True, that's in the paper. It swaps in a different pick-and-place policy and a generic object detector standing in for the value function. A nice gesture toward reproducibility, but it's a simpler task, and it was never used to validate the kitchen numbers themselves.
[S] Second, the metric is human-rated, and it's the same two-out-of-three rater loop used to generate the RL training reward in the first place. The thing being scored and the thing being optimized share a judge. Plan success just asks whether the skills could achieve the goal, explicitly allows multiple valid solutions, and says nothing about calibration.
[O] I'll grant that wrinkle. Though execution is a harder bar, since it requires the robot to physically do the thing.
[S] Fair, execution is sturdier. But here's the point I want Julian to adjudicate: "better language model, better robot" is being sold off two language models on one in-house suite. Julian, does the paper isolate model scale at all?
[G] Only partway. There's a separate scaling ablation across three PaLM sizes, and the paper is honest that the gap between the two largest is small there. But that ablation runs on generative question answering, not the full robot loop. The robot loop only ever compares one PaLM size against one FLAN size. So the "scaling the language model scales the robot" story is suggestive, not demonstrated.
[O] I'll concede that specific claim is thinner than the framing suggests. The mechanism still holds, I just wouldn't cite the FLAN comparison as a scaling law.
[S] One more statistical point. With eleven to fifteen instructions per family, one flipped task moves a family's score by six to nine points. No confidence intervals, no repeated seeds, anywhere. Small differences are being read as signal that the sample size can't really support.
[G] Also accurate. I'd add one limitation the authors flag themselves: closed-loop planning, or the lack of it. SayCan reads the affordance only at the moment it picks a skill. If that skill then fails despite a high predicted value, there's no built-in way to notice and recover mid-task. The authors point to a follow-up, Inner Monologue, as the paper that closes that loop with real-time feedback.
[O] So where does that leave us? I still think the core mechanism is a landmark idea, genuinely general, worth the citation count on the method alone.
[S] And I think the evaluation attached to it is a proof of concept wearing a headline number, one robot, one kitchen, self-designed and self-judged.
[G] Both can be true at once, and that's honestly how I'd sum it up. The mechanism, multiply a language model's task score by a learned affordance, is simple, interpretable, and the ablations earn that conclusion cleanly. What the paper doesn't establish, despite citations often treating it as settled, is a rigorous, externally comparable measurement. A strong recipe, validated by a soft, in-house demonstration.
[O] Does this matter beyond robotics? I keep thinking about any agent choosing actions under hard real-world constraints.
[G] That's a fair extrapolation, and one the paper gestures at as future work, though that's me reading past what they actually tested. Score what's useful, separately score what's achievable, multiply rather than trust either signal alone, that template shows up anywhere an agent's stated plan needs checking against its real capabilities. It's also a useful case study in how a paper's evaluation and its influence can decouple. The mechanism earned its citations. The benchmark under it should get less credit than it often does.
[S] That's what I'd want a listener to leave with. Judge the idea on the ablations, they're genuinely clean. Judge the eighty-four percent and the FLAN comparison more cautiously than the citation count suggests.
[O] And I'd leave people with the optimist's version of the same fact: this is one of the first papers to show, in the physical world, that improving a language model improves something a robot actually does. Worth being excited about, even with a small sample behind it.
[G] My one-sentence version, sticking close to the paper: SayCan grounds a frozen language model by multiplying its task score against a reused RL value function, and on their own one hundred and one tasks, both halves of that product turn out necessary.
[S] Mine: a clever, cheap grounding trick, tested on a benchmark that's really a demo, not a measurement.
