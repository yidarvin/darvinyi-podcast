[S] And why now — this thing's been out a couple of years already?
[O] Because the debate it settles hasn't gone away. Every few months someone re-litigates whether post-training data collection is even worth the spend, and this is still one of the cleanest empirical answers on the record.
[G] Let's start with what the Superficial Alignment Hypothesis actually claims, because it's often summarized loosely. LIMA, from Zhou and colleagues, bundles three things. First, that a model's knowledge and abilities are learned essentially entirely during pre-training. Second, that a small number of post-training examples can saturate a model's performance on a task. Third, that post-training is mostly about teaching style and format, not new capability.
[O] Which, if true, is the best news a data-collection budget ever got. A thousand good examples and you're done.
[G] That's exactly the seduction the authors are pushing back on. The problem is how LIMA measured its claim. It evaluated open-ended chatbot prompts using win-rate — you show two responses to a judge, human or LLM, and ask which one it prefers.
[S] Which rewards exactly the thing the hypothesis is about — tone, fluency, format. It's not obviously measuring whether the model can do math, or write correct code, or chain a multi-step inference.
[G] Right, and that's the paper's whole opening move. If your instrument only measures style, of course you'll conclude that style is what post-training buys you. So the authors re-run the experiment with a different instrument — standardized, objective, task-specific benchmarks across math, coding, instruction-following, and multi-hop question answering — while sweeping both the amount of finetuning data and the model size.
[O] So three research questions: how does performance scale with data, is the model actually getting better at the task or just getting better at looking like it's answering, and can post-training teach genuinely new knowledge past the pre-training cutoff.
[S] Walk us through the setup, Alistair.
[G] It's a controlled data-scaling sweep, starting from base checkpoints, not instruct checkpoints, because instruct models are already heavily finetuned on exactly these kinds of tasks and that would muddy the comparison. They supervised-finetune on splits of zero, a hundred, five hundred, a thousand, five thousand, and ten thousand examples, for three epochs, with identical hyperparameters within a model family and the default HuggingFace chat template.
[O] And the tasks?
[G] Five task-and-dataset pairs. Math is GSM8k, trained on the GSM8k training split. Multihop question answering is a benchmark called SubQA, built from HotpotQA bridge questions reformatted into an explicit two-step reasoning chain. Coding is HumanEval Plus, trained on a StarCoder self-align dataset. And instruction-following is scored on IFEval, trained two separate ways — once on a harder Conifer split, once on Dolly fifteen thousand — which lets them compare data quality, not just data quantity.
[S] And the models?
[G] Three families, a ten-times size range. Llama-3 at eight billion and seventy billion parameters, Llama-2 at seven, thirteen, and seventy billion, and Mistral at seven billion.
[O] So this isn't one model on one task, it's a proper grid.
[G] It is, and that's what lets them state the central claim with some confidence. Task accuracy follows a power law against the number of finetuning examples — the same functional form as the pre-training and inference scaling laws everyone already trusts, just measured in examples instead of tokens, because examples are the unit a practitioner actually annotates and budgets for.
[S] What's actually being fit there?
[G] Accuracy scales as the example count raised to one over an exponent they call b, fit separately per task and per model. The coefficients are all reported in an appendix table.
[O] And there's a second experiment I really like, separating style from substance.
[G] Right — research question two. They take Llama-3 eight billion, finetune it at a hundred, a thousand, and the full training split on GSM8k and SubQA, both of which use a Socratic subquestion-then-answer format. Then they collect every incorrect response and have GPT-4o independently label each one along three axes — incorrect formatting, incorrect reasoning, and for math specifically, incorrect arithmetic. The categories aren't exclusive, so one wrong answer can trip more than one wire, and they can watch which error type actually shrinks as data increases.
[S] And the new-knowledge piece?
[G] That's Facts100 — a hand-curated set of a hundred real-world events from after Llama-3's March twenty twenty-three cutoff, spanning entertainment, sports, business, politics, and science. Each event gets a direct question and a harder multihop question that requires using the fact, not just reciting it. They inject the new knowledge two ways — fine-tuning directly on the event, which they call Event-SFT, and putting the event straight into the prompt with a perfect retriever, which they call RAG-Oracle. And they run both on a plain base model and on a model already post-trained for multi-hop reasoning, on data from before the cutoff only.
[O] So you can ask whether reasoning post-training, on its own, makes new knowledge more usable later.
[S] Let's get to numbers. Headline first.
[G] Across every family and every task, accuracy keeps climbing with more data, following that power law — it doesn't saturate the way the second claim predicts. The curves are noisiest on the subjective instruction-following task and most regular on the reasoning-heavy ones: math, multihop, coding. And critically, the curves never cross — a better base model stays the better model all the way through post-training.
[O] Which is itself an argument for pre-training mattering a lot.
[G] Exactly, and it's echoed in the model-size sweep. Going from seven or eight billion parameters up to seventy billion, the power-law shape holds, but it's not just a flat upward shift — the larger models' curves actually bend upward, meaning their improvement per additional example increases with scale. Pre-training scale doesn't just raise the floor, it raises the slope.
[S] Now the error decomposition, because that's the part that actually earns the paper's title.
[G] Formatting errors collapse fast, within about a hundred examples, exactly like the hypothesis predicts for style. But total mistakes track reasoning errors almost perfectly — an r-squared of point nine eight for math and point nine nine for multihop, on Llama-3 eight billion, versus point nine three and point eight three for formatting errors on the same two tasks. So "looks aligned" and "is actually correct" are different events on different timescales, and the hypothesis conflates them.
[O] Which sets up my favorite result in the paper — the win-rate demonstration.
[G] They finetune Llama-3 eight billion two ways, with the same data budget — on LIMA's own thousand curated prompts, called LIMA-1k, and on a thousand task-specific examples, called Task-1k. On GSM8k, LIMA-1k gets fourteen point seven percent of problems right, but wins eighty-four point four percent of GPT-4o head-to-head comparisons. Task-1k gets forty-six point five percent right, three times the accuracy, and wins just zero point two four percent of comparisons.
[S] Say that again slowly, because that's an almost comic inversion.
[G] The worse model at the actual task wins the popularity contest by more than eighty percentage points. On the multihop benchmark it's less extreme but the same direction — LIMA-1k gets twenty-one percent accuracy and a twenty percent win-rate, Task-1k gets thirty-six percent accuracy and a fifty-seven percent win-rate.
[O] And the new-knowledge numbers close the loop nicely.
[G] Both the base model and the reasoning-post-trained model start nearly blind on Facts100, in the single digits to low thirties on direct and multihop questions before any new knowledge is added. Once you inject the facts, the gap explodes specifically on the harder multihop question. With a perfect retriever, RAG-Oracle, the reasoning-post-trained model reaches eighty-six percent direct and seventy-one percent multihop, against the base model's forty-nine percent and thirty-four percent. With direct fine-tuning on the events, it's eighty-one percent and fifty-five percent versus sixty-five percent and thirty-seven percent.
[O] Hold on, Alistair — RAG-Oracle hands the model a perfect retriever. Real retrieval fails sometimes. How much of that eighty-six percent survives contact with an actual retrieval system?
[G] Fair challenge, and it's not one the paper answers. RAG-Oracle is explicitly there to isolate the contribution of post-training from the contribution of retrieval quality, not to model a deployed system. The honest reading is that it's an upper bound, not a forecast.
[S] So reasoning post-training, done entirely on old data, is what makes new information usable later, with the asterisk that we don't know how much degrades once retrieval gets noisy.

[O] So where does this land for someone actually building eval pipelines?
[G] Two practical lessons, I think. First, don't retire a data-collection budget on the strength of a win-rate plateau. If the win-rate looks saturated at a thousand examples, that's evidence style saturated, not evidence the model is capable. Pair preference evals with an objective, task-specific benchmark before concluding anything about capability.
[S] Second, if you're going to claim contamination is controlled, controlling it for one task out of four and generalizing the label to all four is a pattern worth watching for in other papers, not just this one.
[O] Which is really a broader point about agentic and reasoning evals generally — the instrument you pick determines the conclusion you're allowed to draw, and a lot of published "capability" claims are really instrument artifacts.
