[G] Because on-policy methods like TRPO, PPO, and A3C have a structural cost, they throw away every batch of experience after one gradient step. That's fine for stability but brutal for sample efficiency, especially as tasks get harder. [S] So the target is precise. Off-policy for the sample efficiency, but somehow not brittle. Those two properties hadn't shown up together in one algorithm before this.
[G] Right, and it's worth saying Haarnoja himself had already tried a version of this with soft Q-learning a year earlier. [O] So this paper's pitch is: take the entropy idea that's already shown up in inverse reinforcement learning and optimal control going back over a decade, but wire it into an actual actor-critic loop that's provably well-behaved.
[G] That's the pitch, and it's the first off-policy actor-critic method inside the maximum-entropy framework, by the authors' own claim.
[S] Let's get into the mechanism, because "reward plus entropy" is easy to say and harder to actually optimize well.
[G] The derivation starts in the tabular setting, which is what lets them prove anything at all. They define a soft Bellman backup that replaces the usual value function with a soft state value, expectation of Q minus log pi, so the entropy of the action distribution is literally subtracted into the value target. Then policy improvement isn't gradient ascent on Q directly, it's projecting the policy toward the exponential of the current Q-function using a KL divergence, restricted to whatever tractable policy class you've chosen, Gaussians in practice.
[O] And that's not just intuition, they actually prove it converges?
[G] Two lemmas and a theorem. Soft policy evaluation converges to the true soft Q-value for a fixed policy, soft policy improvement never produces a worse policy under the KL projection, and alternating the two, soft policy iteration, converges to the optimal policy within whatever policy class you picked. That's the theoretical backbone, and it's a genuine advance over soft Q-learning, which only converges as well as its sampler approximates the true posterior. Here convergence holds regardless of how the policy is parameterized.
[S] Fine, but you said yourself the exact version only runs in the tabular case. What actually ships in continuous control?
[G] Three function approximators trained jointly by stochastic gradient descent, a state-value network, a soft Q-network, and a policy network. The value network minimizes a squared residual against the entropy-adjusted Q-target. The Q-network minimizes a Bellman residual against a slowly-updated target value network. And the policy is trained by reparameterizing the action as a deterministic function of the state and a noise sample, so the Q-function's gradient can backpropagate straight through the actor instead of relying on a higher-variance likelihood-ratio estimator.
[O] So what does that reparameterization actually buy you, practically?
[G] Lower-variance gradients, and it's the same trick DDPG uses to get a deterministic policy gradient, just extended here to any tractable stochastic policy rather than a point estimate. That's a meaningful generalization on its own.
[S] Here's what I want to know, though, what's actually doing the work when this thing is stable and DDPG isn't. Because the entropy story is elegant, but elegant stories aren't always where the engineering gains come from.
[G] Fair question, and the paper is honest about it, two independent Q-networks, taking the minimum of the pair for both the value target and the policy gradient. That's borrowed explicitly, they cite Hasselt's double Q-learning and Fujimoto and colleagues' concurrent TD3 work, to counter the positive-bias problem that plagues single-Q actor-critic methods. They even note the algorithm can solve the twenty-one-dimensional humanoid with just one Q-function, but two significantly speeds up training on the harder tasks.
[O] So it's entropy for exploration and stability, plus clipped double-Q for the bias problem, running together.
[G] And a target value network updated by exponential moving average with a smoothing constant tau, one environment step per one or more gradient steps, everything trained off a replay buffer since both the value estimators and the policy tolerate fully off-policy data.
