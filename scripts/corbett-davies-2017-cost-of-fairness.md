[S] Here's a sentence that should make everyone uncomfortable: the single fairest-looking rule a judge could adopt is also the one that produces the biggest racial gap in who gets locked up.
[O] And here's the flip side — every rule designed to close that gap does it by locking up fewer dangerous people and releasing more of them back onto the street.
[S] That's not hypothetical, it's the actual finding, in actual numbers, from the same Broward County data everyone was already fighting about after the ProPublica investigation.
[O] Which is exactly why this paper is worth the commute — it doesn't pick a side in that fight, it prices out what each side costs.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper at a time. Today we're covering "Algorithmic Decision Making and the Cost of Fairness," by Sam Corbett-Davies, Emma Pierson, Avi Feller, Sharad Goel, and Aziz Huq, presented at KDD in 2017.
[S] Joining us is Renata, who has studied this paper and the fairness literature around it closely. Renata, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is one of the papers that turned the COMPAS fight from a public scandal into a formal research question, so there is a lot worth unpacking.
[S] Renata, set the scene for us — where does this paper land in 2017?
[G] In May 2016, ProPublica published Machine Bias, showing that COMPAS flagged black defendants who did not reoffend as high risk at roughly twice the rate it flagged white defendants who did not reoffend.
[G] That set off two separate lines of academic response. One was impossibility results — Kleinberg, Mullainathan, and Raghavan, and separately Alexandra Chouldechova, proved that calibration and balanced error rates across race groups cannot both hold whenever the underlying reoffending rates differ by group.
[O] So the math says you cannot have everything.
[G] Right. The other line of response was construction — several groups built algorithms that provably satisfy one fairness definition or another. Feldman and colleagues repaired scores toward statistical parity, Kamiran and colleagues did something similar for conditional parity, and Hardt, Price, and Srebro post-processed decisions to equalize odds.
[S] So by late 2016 you have "you cannot have it all" on one side and "here is how to guarantee any one definition you like" on the other.
[G] Exactly, and neither answers the question a judge or a legislator actually has: if I pick this fairness constraint over the rule that maximizes public safety, what do I give up? That is the gap this paper fills.
[O] That is a genuinely useful reframe — stop treating "satisfies definition X" as the finish line, and start treating it as one candidate policy with a price tag attached.
[S] Walk us through the actual formalism, Renata.
[G] The setup is constrained optimization. Every defendant has visible attributes — age, priors, COMPAS score — and a decision rule maps those attributes to a probability of detention between zero and one.
[G] The authors define immediate utility as the expected benefit of crimes prevented by detention, minus a cost term, c, times the number of people detained, where c is the cost of detaining one person, in units of crime prevented, somewhere between zero and one.
[O] So c is basically "how bad is it to lock up someone who would not have reoffended, relative to how bad is it to release someone who would have."
[G] That is the right intuition. Because utility decomposes that way, it is maximized pointwise — detain exactly the people whose true probability of reoffending exceeds c. Theorem three point two shows that is provably a single, uniform threshold on risk, applied the same way regardless of race.
[S] Meaning the safety-maximizing rule is automatically race-blind.
[G] Race-blind in its inputs, yes — it never looks at group membership. The authors then ask what happens if you also require one of three fairness properties. Statistical parity means equal detention rates across race groups. Conditional statistical parity means equal detention rates within strata of "legitimate" factors — in their application, just prior-conviction count, binned into four groups. Predictive equality means equal false positive rates, the specific pattern ProPublica flagged.
[O] And the headline theoretical result is what happens to the optimal rule under each of those.
[G] For all three, the utility-maximizing rule that also satisfies the constraint is still a threshold rule on risk — but the threshold is different per race group, and for conditional statistical parity, per stratum of legitimate factors too. You go from one threshold for everyone to multiple thresholds, one per group.
[S] Which is the mechanical source of the tension — a single threshold and a set of group-specific thresholds are structurally different policies, and whenever the two groups' risk distributions differ in shape, not just on average, those two policies diverge.
[G] That is precisely the mechanism, and it is worth being precise about what drives it — this is not about groups having different average risk. The paper builds a simulated example where two populations have identical mean risk but different tail shapes, and a single threshold still produces a detention-rate gap.
[O] That is the part I think gets lost in the popular version of this debate — people assume unequal outcomes under a race-blind rule must mean unequal average risk. This paper shows that is not the only way it happens.
[S] Fair, though I would note the theorem does not prove the two rules always diverge — it just says they generally will, since it would be a coincidence for the thresholds to land exactly on top of each other.
[G] Correct, and the paper says that explicitly. The coincidence case is possible in principle but unlikely, which is why they turn to real data next.
[O] Let us get to the real data — what is the actual dataset?
[G] The same Broward County extract ProPublica compiled — three thousand three hundred seventy seven defendants assigned COMPAS scores within thirty days of arrest, tracked for two years for violent recidivism.
[S] And they do not just take COMPAS's score at face value.
[G] No — they refit their own risk model, an L one regularized logistic regression with Platt scaling, using all available features except race. It actually edges out COMPAS on the same held-out data: an AUC of point seven five versus COMPAS's point seven three.
[O] So the refit model is both more accurate and race-blind by construction, and adding race back in does not improve it further.
[G] Right, they note that explicitly — adding race as a feature does not move the AUC at all. Then, for each fairness constraint, they find the utility-maximizing rule that satisfies it while detaining exactly the same thirty percent of defendants COMPAS itself flags as medium or high risk, so the comparison isolates the constraint's cost with total detention held fixed.
[S] Give us the numbers.
[G] Averaged over one hundred random seventy-thirty train-test splits: enforcing statistical parity means 17 percent of the people who end up detained are actually low risk — they would be released under the safety-maximizing rule — and swapping them for an equal number of high-risk defendants who get released instead produces an estimated 9 percent increase in violent recidivism. Predictive equality is a bit cheaper — 14 percent low-risk detainees, a 7 percent increase. Conditional statistical parity, conditioning on prior convictions, is the cheapest of the three — 10 percent, 4 percent.
[O] So fairness has a real price, and it is not the same price for every definition — conditioning on a legitimate factor like priors gets most of the fairness benefit at less than half the cost of statistical parity.
[S] Sure, but let us flip it around, because the paper does too — what does the safety-maximizing single threshold cost in racial disparity?
[G] That is Section 5, and it is the sharper half of the paper. Detaining the same thirty percent of defendants with one uniform threshold means detaining 40 percent of black defendants versus 18 percent of white defendants. And among defendants who did not go on to reoffend — the exact group ProPublica's investigation was about — 32 percent of black defendants get detained versus 14 percent of white defendants.
[O] So the "fair," race-blind rule reproduces the ProPublica disparity almost exactly.
[G] It does, and the authors are explicit that this is not the algorithm sneaking race in through the back door — it follows purely from the two groups having different risk-score distributions, driven substantially by differing rates of prior arrests. They connect it to a concept from economics called infra-marginality: detention rate and false-positive rate are statistics about people above a threshold, so they can differ across groups even when the threshold itself is identical.
[S] There is also a calibration result I want flagged, because it might be the sneakiest part of the paper.
[G] That is Section 6 and Figure 3. COMPAS scores turn out to be well-calibrated by race — among defendants who scored a seven, 61 percent of black defendants and 60 percent of white defendants reoffended, about as close as calibration checks get. But the authors then construct a hypothetical score for a favored group by adding noise to true risk estimates and refitting. The resulting score is still perfectly calibrated, but by construction, nobody in that group crosses the detention threshold.
[O] So calibration alone cannot catch that kind of engineered discrimination.
[G] It cannot, and that is the same warning Hardt, Price, and Srebro raised about what they call oblivious fairness measures — statistics computed only from scores and outcomes, without looking at how the score was built, can be gamed.
[O] Okay, I want to make the strongest case for this paper, because I think it is an unusually honest piece of work. It does not tell policymakers what to do — it hands them an actual menu with prices attached. "Here is what each fairness definition costs, in the same units, on the same data everyone already argued about." That is genuinely rare in this literature, which is otherwise mostly "here is my definition, adopt it."
[S] I will grant the menu is useful. My problem is with what is on the menu, and what currency it is priced in. The entire cost column is denominated in one objective — arrest-based violent recidivism — that the authors themselves flag as a biased proxy. If policing intensity differs by neighborhood race composition, the "safety-maximizing" threshold is partly optimizing against how hard a neighborhood gets policed, not against true underlying risk.
[G] That is a fair reading of the paper's own Discussion section — they raise it directly and do not fully resolve it. Their pushback is that this concern is weaker for violent crime specifically than for minor offenses, citing separate work suggesting arrest patterns for violent offenses track victim reports and self-reported offending reasonably well.
[S] That is a mitigation, not a rebuttal. It says the bias is probably smaller for violent crime, not that it is zero, and the paper does not attempt to bound how much of that 9, 7, and 4 percent cost column would shrink if you corrected for it.
[O] Sure, but that is true of basically every empirical fairness paper — you work with the outcome data you have. The alternative is publishing nothing until someone solves measurement of true criminal propensity, and that is not coming.
[S] I would push further than measurement, though. The framing itself — public safety as the objective, fairness as a side constraint you pay to satisfy — is a normative choice, not a neutral starting point. Frame it the other way and fairness is the objective, with the incremental crime from satisfying it as the acceptable cost of not perpetuating an unequal system. The paper's own vocabulary quietly picks a side.
[G] That is a real critique, and worth separating from the math, because the math itself is symmetric — Theorem three point two holds no matter which side you call the objective and which side you call the constraint. What is not symmetric is the paper's Discussion section, which leans toward the single-threshold rule as the constitutionally safer default, citing strict-scrutiny case law under the Equal Protection Clause.
[O] Which is a legitimate legal-risk argument — race-specific thresholds are far more likely to get challenged and struck down in court.
[S] It is a legitimate argument about litigation exposure. It is not evidence that the uniform rule is the substantively fairer policy, and I think the paper blurs those two claims more than it should, especially given that the authors themselves cite Hardt and colleagues' point that a facially race-blind rule can still discriminate by design if the inputs feeding it are degraded per group.
[G] That is a genuinely sharp tension inside the paper. Figure 3 is the authors demonstrating that a calibrated, race-blind-looking score can hide engineered discrimination, and then the Discussion leans on race-blindness as a point in the uniform rule's favor. The paper does not fully reconcile those two threads itself.
[O] I will concede that one. Where I still land on the optimist side is the contribution itself — before this paper, "cost of fairness" was a slogan. After it, it is a number you can compute on your own data, with your own cost parameter, and argue about honestly instead of past each other.
[S] One more thing worth flagging for anyone doing evaluation work generally, not just criminal justice — this is a case study in how a headline number can be exactly right on its own terms and still misleading, if the outcome variable underneath it is a contaminated proxy for what you actually care about.
[G] That generalizes well beyond pretrial detention. Any time an evaluation pipeline uses a measured outcome as a stand-in for a true, unmeasured construct — arrests standing in for offending, clicks standing in for satisfaction, moderation flags standing in for actual harm — this paper's structure applies. State the objective, state the constraint, and remember the cost you compute is only as trustworthy as the proxy underneath it.
[O] The calibration result generalizes too — it is an early, very clean example of a metric that looks clean in aggregate but does not certify what people assume it certifies. That shows up constantly in modern evaluation, where a benchmark score can be well-calibrated on average and still hide systematic gaps a single number will not surface.
[S] Which is why I would want, for any of today's fairness-flavored eval tools, the same discipline this paper models — state the objective, state the constraint, state the cost, instead of reporting one number and calling it fair.
[G] If there is one sentence to take from the paper itself: satisfying a fairness definition is not free, it has a measurable public-safety cost, and the cheapest constraint here — conditioning on legitimate factors — is not automatically the ethically obvious choice, it is just the one that happened to be cheapest on this dataset.
[O] My takeaway: this paper gave the fairness debate a shared unit of account, and shared units of account are how contested policy fights eventually become tractable ones.
[S] Mine: a shared unit of account is only as honest as what it is measured in, and this paper's own Discussion section admits the unit — arrest-based public safety — is exactly the piece nobody has pinned down.
