[O] So you have "this tool over-flags Black defendants" and "this tool's scores mean the same thing regardless of race," both backed by numbers from the same Broward County dataset.
[S] And before this paper, nobody had a formal account of why a calibrated tool could also produce unequal error rates. It read like a fight over spreadsheets.
[G] That's exactly the gap Chouldechova closes.
[O] Walk us through it, Maya.
[G] The setup is almost embarrassingly simple. There's a risk score, S, for a given defendant. There's group membership, R, Black or White in this data. And there's the outcome, Y, whether the person actually recidivates within two years. The paper formalizes what Northpointe and Flores meant by fair. A score is test-fair, or well calibrated, if the probability of reoffending given the score is the same across groups, for every possible score value.
[S] And empirically that roughly holds. Figure one plots observed recidivism rate against the ten COMPAS decile scores, split by race, and the two curves track each other closely.
[G] Closely, with a caveat I want to flag now and come back to. The confidence intervals actually separate a bit at the top few deciles. But broadly, yes, calibration looks like a reasonable description of COMPAS.
[O] Okay, so if the scores are calibrated, why would the error rates differ at all?
[G] This is the heart of the paper. To connect calibration to false positive and false negative rates, Chouldechova coarsens the continuous score into a binary high risk, low risk classifier by picking a cutoff. Once you do that, calibration implies something specific: the positive predictive value, meaning the probability you actually reoffend given you were flagged high risk, has to be equal across groups too.
[S] That's a real, checkable constraint. Calibration at every score value forces calibration after thresholding.
[G] Exactly. And now there's a second quantity nobody gets to choose: each group's own recidivism prevalence, meaning what fraction of that group actually reoffends. Put those two together, a fixed positive predictive value and a fixed prevalence, and you can solve algebraically for the false positive rate. It comes out as a function of the group's prevalence, the positive predictive value, and the false negative rate. Nothing exotic, it's basic conditional probability, but nobody had written it down as one clean identity before.
[O] And the punchline of that identity is what, exactly?
[G] If two groups have different prevalence, and the positive predictive value is fixed across them because the tool is calibrated, the false positive and false negative rates cannot also be equal across those groups. That's not a possibility. It's a mathematical guarantee.
[S] I want to be precise about what that does and doesn't claim. It's not saying calibration causes bias in some moral sense. It's saying calibration and equal error rates are two different mathematical properties, and once base rates diverge, you cannot have both at the same time. Northpointe wasn't lying about calibration. ProPublica wasn't lying about the false positive gap. Both are true, and they're forced apart by algebra the moment recidivism prevalence differs by race.
[G] Worth adding, this exact algebraic point was independently discovered around the same time by Kleinberg, Mullainathan, and Raghavan, working completely separately. Two teams, the same equation, weeks apart.
[O] That's a good sign it isn't some artifact of how Chouldechova happened to set up the problem.

[G] The paper is careful to say it does not tell you which criterion to pick, calibration or error rate balance. [S] Which, for anyone doing evaluation work more broadly, should sound familiar. This is a metric validity problem wearing a criminal justice costume.
