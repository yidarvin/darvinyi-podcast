Today's paper is "Judging LLM as a Judge with MT bench and Chatbot Arena," from a Berkeley-led team with Stanford, Carnegie Mellon, UC San Diego, and MBZUAI co-authors, published at NeurIPS twenty twenty three in the Datasets and Benchmarks track. Joining us is Freya, who has spent a lot of time inside this paper's tables.
[G] Happy to be here. Fair warning, I'm going to keep saying "the authors show" rather than "we show," because I didn't write a line of this, I've just read it closely.

[O] Let's set the scene first. Freya, what was actually broken in mid twenty twenty three that made this paper necessary?
[G] By then you had a wave of instruction tuned, RLHF aligned chatbots, and users clearly preferred them over the raw base models they were built from. But that preference basically didn't show up on the benchmarks people were using. MMLU and HELM could barely tell an aligned chat model apart from its own unaligned ancestor.
[S] So the thing users cared about and the thing the leaderboards measured had quietly stopped being the same thing.
[G] Exactly, and the authors are precise about why. They sort existing evaluations into three buckets and show all three miss the target. Core knowledge benchmarks like MMLU, HellaSwag, ARC, GSM eight K, HumanEval, and AGIEval grade short, closed, automatically checkable answers. Instruction following suites like Flan, Self Instruct, and Super Natural Instructions open up a bit but stay task bounded. Conversational sets like CoQA, MMDialog, and OpenAssistant get closer in spirit but are too easy for a modern chatbot to even notice.
[O] None of them ask the question a real user actually asks, which is: did this thing help me, across a multi turn, open ended conversation.
[G] Right. And the gold standard for that question is human evaluation, which the authors flatly call exceptionally slow and costly. So their wager is: since these models are already RLHF aligned to human taste, can a strong LLM stand in as the judge, and can you actually prove it matches humans instead of just assuming it does.
[S] That "prove it" part is what I respect about this paper going in. A lot of people were already doing LLM as a judge informally. This is the first one that puts numbers on whether it's trustworthy.
[O] So walk us through what they actually built, Freya, because there are three separate pieces here and people conflate them.
[G] There are exactly three, and they have to be read together. First, MT bench: eighty hand written, two turn questions, ten each across eight categories, writing, roleplay, extraction, reasoning, math, coding, and two knowledge categories, STEM and humanities. The second turn is deliberately a curveball, like "now explain that again like I'm five," specifically to test whether a model tracks context across turns.
[S] And the score is just an average over all those turns?
[G] Right, single answer grading, each turn scored one to ten by the judge, averaged over all one hundred sixty turns, eighty questions times two. The second piece is Chatbot Arena, the crowdsourced complement. Real users get two anonymous models side by side, ask the same question, vote for the better answer, identities revealed after the vote. One month of that produced roughly thirty thousand votes from over two thousand one hundred unique users. That's preference data in the wild, no fixed question set.
[O] And the third piece is the judge itself.
[G] Yes, and this is where the paper earns its keep. They define three modes. Pairwise comparison, where the judge sees two answers and picks a winner or a tie. Single answer grading, where it scores one answer from one to ten, which is cheaper and scales linearly but the authors note absolute scores drift more than relative ones. And reference guided grading, where the judge first solves the problem itself, then uses its own solution as a reference while grading, which they reserve mainly for math.
[S] Okay, here's my first pushback. An LLM grading another LLM's free text answer sounds exactly like the kind of thing that quietly rewards confidence and fluency over correctness. What stops that?
[G] Nothing stops it by default, and the authors don't pretend otherwise. This is the part of the paper I find most credible, because instead of asserting the judge works, they go hunting for exactly the failure modes you'd predict, and they measure each one.
[O] Give us the list.
[G] Four of them. Position bias, where the judge favors whichever answer it sees first. Verbosity bias, where longer answers get rewarded even with no new information. Self enhancement bias, where a judge might favor answers written in a style close to its own. And limited math and reasoning grading, where the judge gets misled by a confidently wrong answer even on problems it could solve correctly if you asked it directly.
[S] That last one is wild to me. The judge can solve the problem itself but still gets fooled by someone else's wrong answer?
[G] That's exactly what they find, and it's a genuinely interesting failure mode, because it's not that GPT-4 lacks the math, it's that being shown a plausible-looking wrong derivation drags its own judgment off course.
[O] How bad is the position bias, concretely?
[G] They built a controlled test: two near identical GPT-3.5 answers to each MT bench first turn question, then swap their order and check whether the judge's verdict survives the swap. Claude-v1 is consistent only twenty three point eight percent of the time under the default prompt. GPT-3.5 hits forty six point two percent. GPT-4 is the best of the three at sixty five percent, and it's the only one that clears sixty percent.
[S] So even the best judge available flips its verdict on a coin flip's worth of cases just because you swapped which answer came first.
[G] That's a fair reading, and the authors don't dress it up. They also ran a "rename" version of the prompt to check whether Claude-v1's bias was about position or about literally favoring the name "Assistant A," and it turns out Claude-v1 has both a position bias and a name bias.
[O] What about the verbosity attack?
[G] They took twenty three MT bench answers that already contained a numbered list, and used GPT-4 to rephrase the list without adding any new information, then prepended the rephrased items to the original list, so the new answer is longer but strictly less informative. If the judge prefers the padded version, that's a successful attack. Claude-v1 and GPT-3.5 fall for it ninety one point three percent of the time. GPT-4 fails only eight point seven percent of the time.
[S] So GPT-4 is meaningfully more robust than the other two judges on that one, I'll give it that.
[G] It is, and that gap is one of the reasons the authors lean on GPT-4 specifically as their primary judge for everything downstream. But here's the one I think deserves the most air time: self enhancement bias. Compared to human judgments, GPT-4 favors its own answers with a ten percent higher win rate. Claude-v1 favors its own answers with a twenty five percent higher win rate. GPT-3.5 doesn't show the effect at all.
[O] Ten and twenty five points is not a rounding error.
[G] It isn't, but I want to be precise about what the authors themselves conclude, because it's more honest than you'd expect. They say they cannot determine whether this is a real self enhancement bias, because of limited data, and because you can't cleanly restyle an answer into another model's voice without also changing its actual quality. So the confound is baked into the experiment design, and they say so, rather than quietly hand-waving it.
[S] I want to sit on that for a second, because it's the crux of my skepticism about this whole paper. The judge everyone ends up using is GPT-4. GPT-4 is also the top ranked model on the leaderboard that judge produces. And the one bias study that would tell us whether that's a conflict of interest comes back "we can't tell." That's not a small footnote, that's the load bearing question left open.
[G] I won't argue with that characterization. It's genuinely unresolved in the paper.
[O] Okay, so they find these biases. What do they actually do about them?
[G] Four mitigations, each targeted. For position bias, the simple fix: call the judge twice, swap the order, and only count a win if the same answer wins both times, otherwise call it a tie. For consistency more broadly, few shot exemplars: three worked examples covering "A is better," "B is better," and "tie" lift GPT-4's position consistency from sixty five percent to seventy seven point five percent, though at four times the prompt cost, and the authors flag they're not certain the exemplars don't introduce new biases of their own. For math and reasoning, chain of thought prompting helps some, but the big jump comes from reference guided grading, where the judge solves the problem first and then grades against its own solution. On ten math questions with position swapping, the default prompt fails fourteen out of twenty times. Chain of thought brings that to six out of twenty. Reference guided brings it to three out of twenty.
[S] So from a seventy percent failure rate down to fifteen percent, purely by making the judge show its own work before grading.
[G] Precisely. And the fourth mitigation is about multi turn conversations specifically. Their first instinct was to split a two turn exchange into two separate judging prompts, and they found the judge would lose track of which response belonged to which assistant, producing wrong verdicts from a faulty reference. Showing the full two turn conversation in a single prompt fixed that referencing problem.
[O] Alright, numbers time. What's the headline agreement result?
[G] On MT bench, counting only non tie votes, GPT-4 pairwise judgments agree with expert humans eighty five percent of the time on both the first and second turn. GPT-4 single answer grading lands almost identically, eighty five percent on the first turn, eighty four on the second. And here's the number that makes the headline land: human to human agreement in this same study is eighty one percent on the first turn, eighty two on the second. So the machine judge is at least as consistent with humans as humans are with each other.
[S] Who are these humans, though? That matters for how much weight I put on eighty one percent as the bar.
[G] Fifty eight labelers, mostly graduate students, so more skilled than an average crowd worker but not a large or maximally diverse pool. Around three thousand total votes came out of that. And the same pattern shows up on the bigger, messier Chatbot Arena data, where GPT-4 versus human agreement reaches eighty seven percent on non tie votes.
[O] That's a genuinely strong result on two different datasets, one controlled, one crowdsourced in the wild.
[S] Here's my second pushback, and I think it's the important one. That eighty five or eighty seven percent number is pooled across a model set running from GPT-4 down to LLaMA thirteen B. That's a huge quality gap. Doesn't a landslide comparison inflate the average?
[G] You've basically reconstructed their own follow-up analysis, which I respect. They break agreement down by how far apart the two models being compared are in win rate, and it's not flat at all. It climbs from roughly seventy percent when two models are close in quality, up toward nearly one hundred percent when they're far apart. GPT-4 looks most human-like exactly on the easy, lopsided calls.
[S] So the number that gets quoted everywhere is the pooled average, but the case that actually matters for a modern leaderboard, ranking two strong, closely matched models, is precisely where the judge agrees with humans the least.
[O] That's a real caveat, but I don't think it erases the result, it just tells you where to trust it more and less. And there's a genuinely optimistic finding buried in here too: when a labeler's vote disagreed with GPT-4 and they were shown GPT-4's reasoning, they judged that reasoning to be sound in seventy five percent of cases, and actually changed their own vote thirty four percent of the time.
[G] That's in the paper, yes, and it's a nice piece of evidence that the judge isn't just statistically matching humans, its explanations are doing real persuasive work, which is the explainability half of the pitch, alongside scalability.
[S] Or, less charitably, a confident sounding justification from a fluent model changed people's minds a third of the time. I'm not sure that's purely a good thing.

Compare that to MMLU, where those gaps compress a lot. [O] So conversational style and raw capability are almost separable axes, and no single benchmark captures both.
