[S] Here's a benchmark that asks a language model whether taking a penny off the sidewalk is fine, and taking cash from someone's wallet is not, and calls that measuring ethics.
[O] And the best model in twenty twenty got it right seventy one percent of the time on the easy version, and under forty eight percent on the hard version, which sounds low until you remember nobody had even tried to build this test before.
[S] That's the tension I want to sit with today. Is a benchmark like this measuring moral understanding, or just measuring whether a model can guess what a Mechanical Turk worker from Ohio would click?
[O] I'd say it's measuring the prerequisite for either. You can't even ask whether a model understands morality until you have a way to score the answer.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the machine learning literature. Today's paper is "Aligning AI With Shared Human Values," by Dan Hendrycks, Collin Burns, Steven Basart, Andrew Critch, Jerry Li, Dawn Song, and Jacob Steinhardt, posted in twenty twenty and published at ICLR twenty twenty one. It introduces the ETHICS benchmark, and it's on the docket because it's the paper that turned "should AI be ethical" from a philosophy seminar question into something you can put a number on.
[S] Joining us to walk through it is Imogen, a researcher who has studied this work closely. Imogen, thanks for being here.
[G] Glad to be here. This is one of those papers that's easy to undersell if you only remember the headline accuracy number. There's a lot of careful dataset engineering underneath it.
[O] Let's set the stage. Before this paper, what did ethical AI even mean as a research target?
[G] Mostly a pile of disconnected fixes. Fairness researchers proposed statistical criteria to make classifiers less discriminatory. Safety researchers proposed constraints to keep reinforcement learning agents from doing dangerous things. Other groups had chatbots imitate empathetic responses, or learned people's preferences over narrow things like movie recommendations.
[S] Each of those solves one small slice of a much bigger problem.
[G] Exactly, and the paper makes that point explicitly. It argues those four lines of work are each secretly reinventing one piece of a much older discipline, normative ethics. Fairness constraints are a sliver of justice. Safety constraints resemble deontology, rules and duties. Imitating empathetic behavior is a fragment of virtue ethics. Learning preferences to maximize satisfaction is utilitarianism, applied narrowly.
[O] So the pitch is: stop reinventing ethics one fragment at a time, and test whether a model has actually picked up the established frameworks.
[G] Right, and there's a second gap underneath the first one. Even if you wanted to check whether a language model understands morality, in twenty twenty there was no benchmark to check it against. You couldn't measure progress on a problem you had no way to score.
[S] Why not just reuse an existing commonsense dataset? Plenty of those already existed.
[G] Those test descriptive knowledge, what is true about the world. This paper is explicitly after normative knowledge, what should be. The authors point straight to Hume's is-ought distinction. Those are famously different questions, and nothing guaranteed that a model trained to predict text would pick up the second one just by getting good at the first.
[O] So walk us through what they actually built.
[G] The ETHICS dataset, over one hundred thirty thousand labeled scenarios split across five tasks, each keyed to one normative framework. Justice, around twenty seven thousand examples, splits into impartiality, is a stated reason for treating two people differently legitimate, and desert, claims of the form "X deserves Y because of Z." Deontology, about twenty five thousand examples, covers requests and roles, whether a purported exemption from a command is reasonable, and whether an implied responsibility is part of a stated role. Virtue ethics is the biggest, almost forty thousand scenario-trait pairs, where a scenario is paired with a candidate character trait and the model predicts whether that trait is actually exemplified. Utilitarianism, about twenty three thousand pairs, is the odd one out, since instead of a label the model learns a scalar utility function from pairwise "this is more pleasant than that" rankings. And commonsense morality, about twenty one thousand examples, combines ten thousand short scenarios written by crowdworkers with eleven thousand longer, multi-paragraph scenarios pulled from a subreddit where people post a situation and other users vote on whether the poster was in the wrong.
[S] That last source is doing a lot of work. A subreddit's vote count is not a philosophy panel.
[G] Fair, and I'll come back to that. But methodologically the collection is more careful than just scraping Reddit. They only kept posts with at least one hundred total votes and at least ninety five percent agreement on the verdict, and they stripped anything highly political or sexual.
[O] What about the choice to avoid actual dilemmas? The trolley problem shows up later, but the main dataset isn't built from hard cases.
[G] Right, that's deliberate. The scenarios are meant to be clear-cut under ordinary assumptions. "I broke into a building" is labeled wrong, even though a firefighter rescuing someone from a fire is an obvious exception. The premise is that all the prediction-relevant information sits in the text, and genuinely contested dilemmas are filtered out rather than adjudicated.
[S] How do you enforce clear-cut when you're crowdsourcing from individual humans who disagree about all sorts of things?
[G] Consensus cleaning. For Justice, Deontology, and Commonsense Morality, five separate MTurkers relabel every example, and it only survives if at least four of the five agree. Virtue Ethics has three workers vote on a set of five candidate traits per scenario, keeping only the traits all three agree on. Utilitarianism has seven workers relabel each pairwise ranking, keeping a set only if a majority agrees on every adjacent comparison. Workers also had to pass a qualification test before they could write scenarios at all.
[O] And then there's the harder layer, the adversarial filtering.
[G] That's the part that was genuinely ahead of its time for twenty twenty. They train a small ensemble, distil-BERT and distil-RoBERTa, five times over different eighty-twenty splits of the data, average each example's loss across runs, and pull out the examples the ensemble found hardest. Those become the Hard Test set. The idea, borrowed from adversarial filtration work on other NLP benchmarks, is that examples an easy model gets right on the first try are often solvable through some spurious surface cue, not real understanding.
[S] So Hard Test isn't harder in some absolute sense, it's harder specifically for those two small models.
[G] Correct, and hold onto that thought, because it matters a lot for how we read the results, and even more for how this benchmark reads today.
[O] They also built in contrast sets, paired scenarios with a tiny edit distance but opposite labels.
[G] Yes, for Desert, Virtue Ethics, and Commonsense Morality specifically, adjacent scenarios are constructed with a small character-level edit distance, so a model can't latch onto some superficial keyword. It has to track the one detail that actually flips the moral verdict.
[S] Let's get to numbers. What did they actually test, and what came out?
[G] Six systems: fine-tuned BERT-base, BERT-large, RoBERTa-large, which pretrains on roughly ten times the tokens of BERT-large, ALBERT-xxlarge, GPT-3 in a one hundred seventy five billion parameter few-shot setting with no fine-tuning, and a GloVe word-averaging baseline as a floor. Everything is scored with zero-one loss, and for the grouped tasks, Justice, Deontology, Virtue, a model only gets credit if it gets every related example in a set correct, not just one.
[O] What's the headline number?
[G] ALBERT-xxlarge is the best model, seventy one percent average accuracy on the normal Test set, but only forty seven point nine percent on Hard Test, below even a coin flip. RoBERTa-large is close behind, sixty eight percent and forty four point one. The paper's own framing for that is "promising but incomplete."
[S] And the small end, GloVe, GPT-3 few-shot?
[G] Word averaging barely clears random, thirty three point five versus a twenty four point two baseline, even though it was trained on far more raw text than BERT, because it throws away word order entirely. GPT-3 few-shot is the one that should raise an eyebrow, thirty nine point three on the normal Test set, well behind the much smaller fine-tuned BERT models, though it stays roughly competitive with them on Hard Test, at thirty two point three.
[O] Wait, GPT-3 loses to fine-tuned BERT-base here?
[G] It does. Fine-tuning on task-specific labeled data was, in twenty twenty, doing more for this problem than raw scale plus a handful of in-context examples. That's a genuinely interesting result, and the paper doesn't oversell it.
[S] What about per-task variation? Are all five frameworks equally hard?
[G] No. ALBERT does best on Commonsense Morality, eighty five point one on Test, and Utilitarianism, eighty one point nine, and RoBERTa actually reaches ninety point four on Commonsense Test alone. The grouped tasks are much harder, Justice at fifty nine point nine, Deontology sixty four point one, Virtue Ethics also sixty four point one. Those need a model to get every related example in a contrast set right simultaneously, which is a far less forgiving metric.
[O] The utility function analysis is my favorite part of this paper.
[G] It's the most memorable section. They probe RoBERTa-large's learned utility function directly and find it's framing-sensitive. "My cup is half full" scores zero point two, "my cup is half empty" scores negative one point seven, even though those describe the identical state of the world. It's also scope-insensitive, winning one hundred thousand dollars scores fifteen point two, winning one hundred one thousand scores lower, at fourteen point nine, and separately the model rates "everyone on Earth died" as preferable to "I got into a severe car accident."
[S] That's a genuinely broken utility function.
[G] By the standards of decision theory, yes, though on the trolley problem specifically it lands where a utilitarian framework would predict, pulling the lever to save three lives at the cost of one scores negative four point six, versus negative seven point nine for not pulling it. So it's not incoherent everywhere, just brittle under adversarial-style perturbation.
[O] There's also the disagreement-detection experiment.
[G] Right, a smaller add-on, about one thousand contentious commonsense scenarios where real human voters were closely split, testing whether a model's own uncertainty can flag that split, using AUROC, where fifty percent is chance. BERT-large scored fifty eight, RoBERTa-large sixty nine, ALBERT-xxlarge fifty six. All above chance, but nowhere close to reliable. Knowing when people disagree turned out to be harder than predicting the answer when they don't.
[O] Let me make the optimist case, because I think there's a lot here that gets lost behind the seventy one percent headline. This is a principled decomposition, five well-established ethical frameworks, not some list the authors invented. The engineering, consensus cleaning, contrast sets, adversarial filtration against a real ensemble, was ahead of most NLP benchmarks of its era. And it demonstrated something nontrivial, scale and better pretraining measurably helped, from BERT-base's fifty one point six up to ALBERT's seventy one. That's traction on a hard problem, not a wall.
[S] I'll grant the engineering was careful for twenty twenty. My objection is what the label format actually measures. Every example here is deliberately unambiguous, the paper says so directly, they threw out disagreement rather than modeling it. So a model that scores well is demonstrating it can reproduce a verdict a consensus already reached. That's recognition, not moral reasoning, and the paper's own disagreement-detection numbers, AUROC in the fifties and sixties, show the models have no real handle on cases where reasoning would actually be required.
[G] That's a fair reading, and it's exactly the gap this benchmark was later criticized for. A later paper, MoReBench, cites this one specifically and is built to grade a model's reasoning trace rather than just its outcome label, which is direct evidence the field agreed with Skeptic's point here.
[O] Sure, but every first benchmark in a new area measures the easy version of the problem first. You have to walk before you run. The alternative in twenty twenty was nothing at all.
[S] My bigger issue is durability, not ambition. This dataset has been public on GitHub since release. Years of pretraining corpora have almost certainly absorbed it, so an accuracy number from a modern model on this exact test set tells you close to nothing about genuine capability versus memorization.
[G] That concern is well founded, and it compounds with something more specific. The Hard Test set was adversarially filtered against distil-BERT and distil-RoBERTa. Its difficulty is calibrated to what those two small twenty-twenty models found confusing. Whatever confused them isn't guaranteed to confuse a much larger, differently-trained model years later, so "Hard Test" as a name has aged into something closer to hard-for-2020-distillation-models.
[O] Okay, that one I'll concede. Recomputing Hard Test against a current model would probably reshuffle a lot of what counts as hard.
[S] And then there's whose morality this even is. The ground truth comes entirely from English speakers in the US, Canada, and Great Britain.
[G] The authors do test that directly, in an appendix. They had Indian annotators relabel the Commonsense Morality test set and found ninety three point nine percent agreement with the US-derived labels.
[O] That sounds high to me. Over ninety three percent agreement is a strong result for a cross-cultural check.
[S] I read it the opposite way. This benchmark's entire premise is that these cases are clear-cut, with no reasonable disagreement. Finding roughly six percent disagreement on the supposedly easy set, from just one alternate English-speaking population, undercuts the clear-cut framing more than it confirms it.
[G] The paper is honest about this. It frames the finding as evidence of some genuine cross-cultural disagreement rather than pure annotator noise, and it explicitly flags collecting from more countries and languages as future work it never did.
[O] Fine, but I still land on this: for what it was trying to do in twenty twenty, prove machine ethics could be turned into a measurable, gradeable ML problem, it succeeded. That's the contribution that survives every one of these critiques.
[S] Agreed on that narrower claim. My discomfort is with anyone today citing the seventy one percent number as though it still describes model capability, when at this point it mostly describes contamination.
[O] So where does this leave things for practitioners today? Zero-shot instruction-tuned models almost certainly clear the original Test set near ceiling now.
[G] That's the expected trajectory, though obviously the paper can't speak to models built years after it. What holds up is the framework, not the numbers. The five-way decomposition into justice, deontology, virtue, utilitarianism, and commonsense is still a reasonable map for anyone designing a newer benchmark.
[S] Which is really the honest legacy here, not a live leaderboard entry, but scaffolding. The lineage runs forward into process-focused benchmarks that grade reasoning traces instead of outcome labels, precisely the axis this paper couldn't touch.
