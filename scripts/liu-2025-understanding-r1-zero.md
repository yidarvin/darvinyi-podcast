[O] Here's a claim that broke my brain a little: the most famous curve in the whole DeepSeek-R1-Zero story, the one where response length climbs and climbs as reasoning supposedly "emerges," might be partly a bug.
[S] Not partly. The paper we're covering today traces it straight to a normalization term buried inside G-R-P-O's loss function, and once you delete that term, most of the "emergence" just stops happening.
[O] "Most" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. The fixed version still reaches the same accuracy, just with far shorter answers, and that's not nothing.
[S] Sure, but it means a chunk of what people were calling proof of reasoning emerging from nothing was actually an artifact of how the gradient was computed. That's the whole episode in one sentence.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a guest scholar dig into one paper from litsearch dot darvinyi dot com.
[S] Today's paper is "Understanding R1-Zero-Like Training: A Critical Perspective," by Zichen Liu and colleagues at Sea AI Lab, the National University of Singapore, and Singapore Management University, published at COLM twenty twenty-five.
[O] And joining us is Julian, a researcher who's spent real time inside this paper's appendices. Welcome, Julian.
[G] Thanks for having me. And to be clear up front, I didn't write this paper, I've just read it closely enough to argue about it with the two of you.
[S] Let's set up why it needed to exist. What exactly did DeepSeek-R1-Zero claim, and why did it need auditing?
[G] R1-Zero's claim was that you could skip supervised fine-tuning entirely, apply reinforcement learning directly to a base model with only a rule-based reward for a correct final answer, and reasoning would emerge on its own. Two things became the iconic evidence: response length climbed steadily through training, and the model spontaneously started saying things like "wait" and "let me reconsider," what the community called the Aha moment.
[O] That's a genuinely exciting claim if it holds, pure reinforcement learning, no human demonstrations, discovering long chain-of-thought from a blank base model.
[G] It is, and a wave of open replications followed almost immediately, nearly all built on the Qwen2.5 model family. That's exactly where this paper drives its wedge: nobody had cleanly separated how much of the behavior comes from the base model, which might already be a strong math solver, and how much comes from the reinforcement learning itself.
[S] That's the confound I'd flag immediately. You can't credit an algorithm for a capability the base model already had.
[G] Exactly, and there's a second gap. Prior studies had already argued there might be no real Aha moment, because the open-source base models they tested already contained self-reflection keywords. But none had tested DeepSeek-V3-Base, the six hundred eighty-five billion parameter model the actual R1-Zero was trained from. So the authors hosted it themselves.
[O] That's a real gap to close, arguing an effect isn't real without testing the one model where the effect was first reported.
[G] Right. So they probe six base models before any reinforcement learning touches them: Qwen2.5-Math at one point five and seven billion parameters, plain Qwen2.5-seven-B, Llama-3.1-eight-B, DeepSeek-Math-seven-B, and DeepSeek-V3-Base. For each, they ask: can it answer questions, can it explore correct trajectories, and does it already self-reflect.
[S] Start with the template finding, because I gather it complicates the "no template needed" excitement around Qwen.
[G] It's actually the surprise that drives that excitement. A base model is trained for sentence completion, not question answering, so normally you need a template to turn it into something that behaves like a policy answering a question. Llama and DeepSeek both need one. But Qwen2.5 models hit a one hundred percent answering rate with no template at all, and dropping the template lifts Qwen2.5-Math's accuracy by roughly sixty percent over standard four-shot prompting.
[O] Sixty percent is a huge jump for just removing a prompt wrapper.
[G] It is, and the authors' explanation is that Qwen2.5-Math was likely pretrained on concatenated question-and-answer text, meaning the so-called base model is already functioning like a fine-tuned model before any reinforcement learning. DeepSeek-V3-Base, by contrast, has the lowest no-template answering rate of any model tested, a genuinely purer base model, and it still shows the Aha moment, the piece missing from the earlier no-real-emergence studies.
[S] So the headline "R-L discovers reasoning" claim already needs an asterisk before we've even gotten to the R-L algorithm.
[G] That's the authors' own reading, yes. They call it a pretraining bias, and they're explicit that it's a hypothesis, not a proven mechanism, since they don't have access to Qwen's actual pretraining corpus.
[S] Isn't that a convenient hedge, though, Julian? "We can't check it" is a comfortable place to land a strong claim.
[G] It would be, if that were all they offered. The sixty percent jump is real, measured, and reproducible; only the explanation for it is inferred rather than proven. What they're careful about is claiming certainty on the mechanism, not on the effect.
[O] That distinction seems worth holding onto for the rest of this conversation.
[O] Okay, let's get into the algorithm itself. What exactly is biased about G-R-P-O?
[G] G-R-P-O samples a group of responses per question, scores each with a reward, and computes an advantage by subtracting the group mean and dividing by the group's standard deviation. Then it normalizes each response's loss by that response's own length, one over the number of tokens. With the K-L penalty set to zero, since a rule-based verifier makes it unnecessary, that leaves two normalizers doing quiet damage.
[S] Walk through both, because I want to know exactly which one causes the length blowup.
[G] The first is the response-level length bias, and it's the one that drives the length story. For a correct response, dividing by length means a shorter answer gets a larger per-token update than a longer one, so the policy is nudged toward brevity when it's right. For an incorrect response, the same division means a longer wrong answer is penalized less per token than a short one, so the policy drifts toward writing longer wrong answers. That asymmetry, repeated over thousands of steps, is the engine behind "response length keeps growing."
[O] And the second bias?
[G] The question-level difficulty bias, from dividing by the standard deviation across the group. Questions that are nearly all-right or all-wrong have a standard deviation close to zero, so dividing by it inflates their weight in the update. The objective ends up distorted by how easy or hard a question happens to be, rather than treating every question consistently.
[S] So what's the fix, and is it really as simple as it sounds?
[G] It really is. They call it Doctor G-R-P-O, for "G-R-P-O Done Right," and the fix is to delete both normalizers. No division by response length, no division by the standard deviation. The authors show algebraically that what's left is the plain Monte Carlo policy gradient with a mean baseline, the actual unbiased P-P-O objective, and that it's equivalent to REINFORCE Leave-One-Out up to a constant scale factor that just folds into the learning rate.
[O] That's a satisfying fix, not a new architecture, just deleting two lines that shouldn't have been there.
[G] And it's not only G-R-P-O's problem. The authors checked several popular open-source P-P-O implementations, the libraries T-R-L, OpenRLHF, and verl, plus the replication codebases SimpleRL-Zero and Open-Reasoner-Zero, and all of them normalize the loss by response length too. That misalignment predates G-R-P-O entirely. Their best guess is it leaked in from the pretraining convention of averaging loss over a fixed context length, sensible there but a silent length bias once sequence length is what you're optimizing.
[S] That's the part of this paper I'd bet survives five years from now, a bug hiding in five different codebases that nobody had traced to its source.
[G] Putting the diagnosis together with the base-model finding gives what they call the minimalist recipe: take Qwen2.5-Math-seven-B, train it with Doctor G-R-P-O on MATH questions at difficulty levels three through five, using the Qwen-Math template, and you get a model they call Oat-Zero-seven-B, trained in twenty-seven hours on eight A one hundred GPUs.
[O] Let's get to the numbers, because "new state of the art" is a strong claim.
[G] At a matched three-thousand-token budget, Oat-Zero-seven-B averages fifty-one point four percent across five math benchmarks, and posts forty-three point three percent on AIME twenty twenty-four, the standout number. SimpleRL-Zero-seven-B averages forty-six point six percent, Prime-Zero-seven-B forty-eight percent, Open-Reasoner-Zero-seven-B forty-three percent, at the same budget. But look at where the win comes from: Oat-Zero dominates AIME, more than fifteen points ahead of the next-best model, while sitting mid-pack on MATH-500 and Minerva Math.
[S] So the average is being pulled up almost entirely by one benchmark.
[G] Largely, yes. And what I find more interesting isn't the accuracy number, it's what happens to response length. Doctor G-R-P-O and vanilla G-R-P-O land on similar training reward and benchmark accuracy, but G-R-P-O keeps inflating incorrect-response length even after reward plateaus, climbing from roughly one thousand tokens to about one thousand eight hundred, while Doctor G-R-P-O holds flat. An appendix ablation repeats this across three seeds and reports Doctor G-R-P-O's accuracy edge as small but statistically significant, with token efficiency the much larger, more consistent effect.
[O] So the honest headline isn't "Doctor G-R-P-O makes the model smarter," it's "Doctor G-R-P-O makes the model equally smart without the overthinking."
[G] That's exactly how I'd put it, and it's the paper's most durable contribution.
[S] What about the template and question-set experiments, did those change your read on anything?
[G] They reinforced the elicitation story. Reinforcement learning lifts every template to a comparable plateau around forty percent, given a suitable question set, but the path differs. With the Qwen-Math template, training on GSM-8K, a much simpler, out-of-distribution grade-school set, nearly doubles accuracy on the harder benchmarks. The authors' reading: because the Qwen2.5-Math base already has the underlying ability, reinforcement learning mostly reinforces behaviors that are already there, not new knowledge. Their own words are that a mismatched template can destroy the capability before reinforcement learning reconstructs it.
[O] There's a nice complementary experiment on Llama, though, that argues the other direction, that pretraining, not the algorithm, sets the ceiling.
[G] Right, and it's one of my favorite results here. Plain Llama-3.2-3B barely moves under reinforcement learning, three point three up to six point eight percent average. Continually pretraining it on math first changes everything: Llama-3.2-3B-FineMath reaches fourteen point eight percent after reinforcement learning, and a version pretrained on concatenated question-answer text, called Oat-Zero-3B, reaches twenty point seven percent. The same G-R-P-O length blowup reappears on Llama, fixed the same way. Domain knowledge baked into pretraining raises the ceiling; the algorithm mostly determines how efficiently you get there.
[O] Let me make the strongest optimist case, because I think it survives scrutiny. Even after every caveat, this paper hands the field two things that are just true regardless of the state-of-the-art claim: a correctly diagnosed bug quietly inflating length across five codebases, and a one-line fix that recovers the same accuracy with far fewer tokens. That's real, immediately actionable, and already widely adopted.
[S] I'll grant every word of that. My skepticism is entirely about the "new state of the art" half. Start with the evaluation budget: every baseline is capped at three thousand tokens, chosen because Qwen2.5-Math's base context window is four thousand. Models trained for longer outputs get hobbled by that cap. R1-Distill-Qwen-seven-B scores only twenty-eight point five percent at three thousand tokens, but fifty-four point seven percent at its native eight-thousand-token budget, which beats Oat-Zero's fifty-one point four percent outright.
[O] The paper does report both numbers, though, that's not hidden.
[S] It's reported, but easy to miss under a headline that says state of the art. The claim is scoped to zero-R-L peers at a short budget that structurally favors a method whose entire selling point is shorter responses. Julian, is that a fair read, or am I being too harsh?
[G] It's a fair read, close to how I'd frame it myself. I'd add one layer: AIME twenty twenty-four, the benchmark driving most of the headline gain, is only thirty problems, so forty-three point three percent is roughly thirteen out of thirty. A couple of problems either way swings that score several points, and the main results figure reports point estimates with no error bars, the multi-seed significance analysis lives in an appendix, not the headline chart.
[O] Okay, that one lands. Small benchmarks deserve error bars in the figure people actually screenshot.
[S] And there's a deeper issue underneath both of those, the pretraining hypothesis itself.
[G] Right, and this is the part the authors handle with real intellectual honesty. They say outright they lack access to Qwen's pretraining corpus, so the already-SFT-like explanation is their best inference, not a verified fact. Oat-Zero-seven-B is built on exactly that base model. So a meaningful share of its apparent gain is plausibly the model recovering ability it already had, not reinforcement learning teaching it something new, which is, ironically, the exact deflationary argument the rest of the paper makes about everyone else's results.
[S] So the paper's own method partially undermines its own headline number.
[G] I'd say it complicates it rather than undermines it, but yes, that tension is real and the authors don't hide from it.
[O] That feels like a generous read to me, Julian. If the base model already had the ability, isn't "reinforcement learning improved a math model" just true regardless of mechanism, and the paper's own framing oversells it?
[G] That's a fair pushback, and I won't fully talk you out of it. But "R-L improved a model" and "R-L discovered reasoning from scratch" are different claims with very different implications, and this paper's real contribution is showing those got conflated. I'd call that clarifying, not overselling. One more limitation worth naming: every result here is math, whether either bias generalizes to code or any other domain is simply untested.
[O] I'll concede the state-of-the-art framing is doing more work than the evidence cleanly supports. I still think the diagnosis is the paper's real contribution, and that part I'm not backing off of.
[S] Agreed, and for the record that's not a small concession. A correctly identified optimization bug that's been silently shaping five codebases' worth of training runs is genuinely valuable, headline aside.
[O] Zooming out, what should this change about how the field runs these evaluations going forward?
[G] A few habits, I think. Always report baselines at their native generation budget alongside any matched-budget comparison; burying the eight-thousand-token number in an appendix table is technically transparent but practically misleading. Treat "the base model already has this skill" as a standing hypothesis to test, not a footnote, whenever anyone claims R-L taught a model something new. And small competition benchmarks like AIME need error bars in the primary figure, not just the appendix, once people start citing the point estimate as the finding.
[S] I'd add one more, closer to my usual complaint: audit your own training code for silent normalization bugs before you credit an algorithm with an emergent capability. This paper found the same length-normalization bug in five different codebases nobody had checked.
[O] And to be fair to the field, this is exactly the kind of finding that's easy to act on, Doctor G-R-P-O is a two-line diff, not a new architecture, so there's no excuse for it not propagating.

[G] If there's one sentence to take from this paper, it's that "pure reinforcement learning discovers reasoning from scratch" is the wrong story. What's really happening is a strong base model having latent ability elicited, plus an optimizer bug that was mistaken for emergent behavior.
