[O] So where does GAIA fit, Eleanor? I thought that was the closest comparison point.
[G] It's the closest, and the authors say so directly. GAIA has multi-step tool use and human-verified answers. But it has no step-wise labels, and a lot of its tasks lean on web browsing, multi-modality, and reading odd file types, specialized capabilities that muddy whether a failure is about reasoning or about, say, parsing a PDF. ToolComp deliberately stays text-only to isolate the thing it actually wants to measure.
[S] So the gap they're targeting is specifically multi-step, human-verified, programmatically checkable, and, the one nobody had, labeled step by step.
[G] That's the whole pitch. Get all four, and you can finally tell whether a model got the right answer for the right reasons, and where exactly a ten-step trajectory goes off the rails.
[O] Walk us through what's actually in the benchmark, Eleanor.
[G] It's four hundred eighty-five prompts, split into two subsets. ToolComp-Enterprise, two hundred eighty-seven prompts, opens up all eleven tools, things like a calculator, wiki search, Google search, Wolfram Alpha, current and historical weather, intra-day and daily stock lookups, a stock symbol search, and a Python interpreter. ToolComp-Chat, one hundred ninety-eight prompts, restricts the model to just Google search and Python, the minimal kit a general chatbot actually ships with.
[S] Why bother with two subsets instead of just one big pool?
[O] And how do you generate a prompt like that at scale, once you need dependent tool chains and a checkable answer?
[G] Both good questions. They wanted to separate two things, can a model juggle a large, expressive toolkit the way an enterprise agent would, versus can it do multi-step reasoning with almost nothing. And on generation, it's few-shot prompting first, then an iterative filter, rejected prompts get folded back in as negative in-context examples to push the generator harder, and finally a human refinement pass.
[S] But the annotation loop that comes after is the part I actually want detail on, because that's where the step-level labels come from.
[G] Right, that's the distinctive part. They use the ReAct format, Thought, Action, Action Input, then a tool Observation, repeated in a loop, preceded by an overall Action Plan. A policy model, FireFunction-V1, which was the strongest open tool-use model at the time, proposes the plan and then each step. A human annotator marks every single component, the plan, the thought, the action, the action input, as Correct or Incorrect.
[O] And if it's wrong?
[G] The annotator rewrites it themselves, and that human-corrected step gets fed back into the model's context before it generates the next step. So the model is essentially being walked forward by a human co-pilot who fixes every wrong turn in real time. The loop ends when the model, or the annotator, calls Finish. What you get out the other end, per task, is a golden trajectory plus a paired record at every step, the model's original attempt and the human's correction, side by side.
[S] That pairing is what becomes the training signal later.
[G] Exactly, and it's also directly how they grade a model's step-wise judgment, show it the human-corrected step and the model's original wrong step, swap the order to cancel position bias, and see if it can pick the better one.
[O] What about quality control? Four hundred eighty-five prompts isn't huge, how do they keep it clean?
[G] Manual inspection of every example, then three trusted annotators do a final re-review pass. There's also a clever last step, they evaluate the whole benchmark with four strong models, GPT-4o, GPT-4 Turbo, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, and Llama 3.1 four-oh-five B, find the handful of prompts where every single model disagrees with the ground truth, and re-examine exactly those, because that's where a mislabeled prompt is most likely hiding.
[S] Smart. Use disagreement as a diagnostic for label noise, not just model weakness.

[S] And how well does that step-level judgment track final-answer accuracy? If it's the same signal twice, why bother collecting both?

[O] Now the part I actually came for, does process supervision help you train something, not just evaluate it?
[G] This is the second half of the paper. They take Llama 3.1 eight B Instruct, bolt on a linear reward head, and train two reward models on synthetic data, an outcome-supervised one, the ORM, that scores a whole trajectory, and a process-supervised one, the PRM, that scores every step. On raw base-model generations, the PRM hits forty-two point six five percent rank at one accuracy, versus twenty-three point eight nine for the ORM. Call it a nineteen-point gap.
[S] Rank at one meaning?
[G] Generate thirty candidate trajectories, let the reward model pick its favorite, check whether that pick is actually correct. On generations from a fine-tuned model, the PRM pushes rank at one up to sixty point two five percent, which is nearly the generator's own pass at thirty ceiling, an eleven-point edge over the ORM there. And the PRM wins at every training-data scale they tried, ten, twenty-five, fifty, and a hundred percent of the data.
[O] Any design choices that mattered inside the PRM itself, Eleanor?
[G] Two big ones. First, what gets supervised, rewarding the full ReAct step including the tool's Observation, its actual output, wins at sixty point two five percent, ahead of the same thing without the observation at fifty-five point four three, and well ahead of finer sub-step labels, which land around forty-nine and forty-five percent. Coarser labels that include the tool's real output generalize better than over-detailed ones. Second, how you combine per-step scores into one trajectory score, max is dramatically the best aggregator at sixty point two five percent, while min, average, and product all collapse to the low-to-mid twenties.
[S] Why does max win so decisively?
[G] Because min and average punish any single wrong step even when the model recovers, and product biases toward short trajectories. Max avoids both traps.
