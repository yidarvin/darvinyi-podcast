[O] Which is exactly why I love this paper. Instead of just complaining about that problem, it hands you the actual reasoning chain as data, subject, relation, object, so you can mechanically check whether a model chained two facts or just got lucky.
[S] Except that chain comes out of seven hundred ninety-nine hand-written templates, so before I get excited, I want to know whether we're testing reasoning or testing pattern-matching against a fixed vocabulary.
[O] That's the whole debate today. Welcome to Litsearch Audio. Today we're digging into "Constructing A Multi-hop QA Dataset for Comprehensive Evaluation of Reasoning Steps," by Xanh Ho, Anh-Khoa Duong Nguyen, Saku Sugawara, and Akiko Aizawa, presented at COLING twenty twenty. It's the paper that introduced 2WikiMultiHopQA.
[S] And joining us to referee is Rupert, a visiting scholar who's studied this dataset's construction pipeline closely. Rupert, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. The annotation design is the real contribution here, so it's worth being precise about what the evidence field can and can't prove, and where the templates behind it become a liability.
[O] Let's set the stage first. Before this paper, how many datasets could genuinely claim to test multi-hop reading?
[G] Four, over textual data. ComplexWebQuestions and QAngaroo paired web or Wikipedia text with a knowledge base, but neither recorded why an answer was correct, there's no annotation of the reasoning path at all. HotpotQA, from Yang and colleagues in twenty eighteen, added sentence-level supporting facts, a list of which sentences justify the answer. Then R4C, from Inoue and colleagues, went further and added a semi-structured explanation of how those sentences combine, but R4C has only four thousand, five hundred eighty-eight questions, nowhere near enough to train an end-to-end model on.
[S] So HotpotQA is the elephant in the room. What's actually wrong with its supporting facts?
[G] They tell you which sentences a model used, but that's a binary classification task, a sentence is relevant or it isn't. It can't tell you whether the model composed two facts into a new one, or just noticed the right sentences and pattern-matched from there. Min and colleagues showed in twenty nineteen that a large share of HotpotQA's comparison questions are solvable single-hop, by exploiting type-consistent surface patterns in the distractor paragraphs. You don't need to bridge anything, you just need to notice the shape of the sentence.
[O] And this fits a pattern the field had already been burned by. Sugawara and colleagues, twenty eighteen, one of this paper's own authors among them, showed that a lot of reading comprehension datasets are quietly full of easy questions answerable from just the first few words. And Jia and Liang, back in twenty seventeen, showed you could append one adversarial distracting sentence to a passage and watch strong SQuAD readers collapse, average F one across sixteen published models fell from seventy-five point four percent down to thirty-six point four percent.
[S] So the diagnosis by twenty twenty is: multi-hop datasets either don't explain their answers at all, explain them too shallowly to catch shortcuts, or explain them well but are too small to train on.
[G] That's exactly the gap this paper is aiming at. Large enough to train on, and annotated with something stronger than supporting sentences.
[O] So how do you build something stronger than supporting sentences?
[G] The core idea is to fuse two sources: the summary section of a Wikipedia article, the short description at the top, as text about an entity, and Wikidata, a structured knowledge base of subject, relation, object triples about that same entity. Every question is generated from one or two of those triples, with a hard constraint, the subject and object entities have to actually appear as text spans inside the matching Wikipedia paragraphs. That grounding keeps a knowledge-base fact checkable against real prose, instead of a database lookup dressed up as a question.
[S] And the payoff for that fusion is the evidence field?
[G] Right. Alongside the question, the context paragraphs, and sentence-level supporting facts borrowed from the HotpotQA playbook, every example now also carries evidence, the specific triples that were chained to produce the answer. Where a HotpotQA-style annotation would just point at two sentences, this dataset points at two explicit triples connected through a shared bridge entity, subject, relation, object, subject, relation, object, and you can check that mechanically.
[O] Walk us through the four question types, Rupert, because that's where the design gets interesting.
[G] Comparison questions compare two entities from the same category on a shared property, who was born first. Inference questions take two triples, their own example is Abraham Lincoln's mother, Nancy Hanks Lincoln, and her father, James Hanks, and apply a hand-verified logical rule, mother then father becomes maternal grandfather, so the surface question reads naturally, who is the maternal grandfather of Abraham Lincoln. Compositional questions chain two triples through a bridge entity with no named logical relation, their example is who is the founder of the company that distributed the film La La Land, resolving through its distributor, Summit Entertainment, to founder Bernd Eichinger. And bridge-comparison questions combine both moves, a bridge entity in each of two chains, then a comparison, needing up to four gold paragraphs at once.
[S] The inference questions sound like the cleverest piece, but also the trickiest to get right without quietly breaking. How do they avoid that?
[G] Very deliberately. They mined candidate logical rules with an association-rule-mining system called AMIE, then manually verified twenty-eight of them against Wikidata. Even then, some failed the checks, their example is that place-of-birth plus country doesn't safely imply nationality, because a paragraph about someone's birthplace usually already states their nationality too, so the two-hop requirement collapses on its own. They also restricted themselves to relations with a single valid value, because if a person has three children, "who is the grandchild" has three possible right answers, which breaks the one-answer-per-question design entirely.
[O] And the bridge-question construction has that neat two-paragraph constraint from the writeup.
[G] Yes, this is the mechanism meant to force genuine two-hop reading. For a chain e, r one, e one, r two, e two, the paragraph describing e has to mention the bridge entity e one, and the paragraph describing e one has to mention the final answer e two. Crucially, neither paragraph is allowed to leak the other hop, the first paragraph can't already contain e two, the second can't already contain e. If it could, you wouldn't need to bridge anything at all.
[S] What about scale? Template-based generation tends to run out of road fast.
[G] They built seven hundred ninety-nine compositional templates, from thirteen first-relation properties crossed with twenty-two second-relation properties, across fifteen entity types, plus sixty-two bridge-comparison templates, and a smaller set of comparison and inference templates explicitly pruned of anything Min and colleagues had already flagged as solvable single-hop. Distractor paragraphs reuse HotpotQA's recipe, bigram T F I D F retrieval of the fifty most similar paragraphs, narrowed to the top eight by entity type, or top six for bridge-comparison, shuffled with the gold paragraphs into a ten-paragraph context.
[O] And the headline number?
[G] One hundred ninety-two thousand, six hundred and six questions. Split into a hundred fifty-four thousand, eight hundred seventy-eight easier training examples, and twelve thousand, five hundred seventy-six each for a harder training slice, a dev set, and a test set, with the split decided by how well a single-hop model could already solve each question. Across all of it, seven hundred eight distinct answer types show up, dominated by yes-or-no at just over thirty-one percent, dates at almost seventeen percent, films at thirteen and a half percent, and humans at close to twelve percent.
[S] Let's get to whether it actually works. Numbers, Rupert.
[G] Two evaluations matter here. First, difficulty. They ran the exact same HotpotQA baseline, Yang and colleagues' twenty eighteen bi-attention reader, on both datasets side by side. On HotpotQA it gets forty-four point four eight exact match and fifty-eight point five four F one on the answer. On this dataset, the same model drops to thirty-four point one four exact match and forty point nine five F one. The supporting-fact score actually goes up slightly, twenty-six point four seven exact match versus twenty point six eight on HotpotQA, but the joint metric, which multiplies all three sub-scores together, falls from ten point nine seven exact match and forty point five two F one on HotpotQA down to nine point two two and twenty-six point seven six here.
[O] And since human performance is comparable across both datasets, that gap is the authors' evidence they've packed in more genuinely hard questions.
[G] That's their argument, yes. The second test is more targeted, and it's borrowed directly from Min and colleagues' methodology, a single-hop BERT reader, one that physically cannot combine two paragraphs. On HotpotQA it scores sixty-four point six F one. On this dataset, fifty-five point nine F one, an eight point seven point drop. Since a single-hop model can't bridge anything, that drop is read as evidence that a larger share of these questions genuinely require the second hop.
[S] Fair, that's a reasonably clean shortcut probe. What about the full baseline, the one that has to do all three tasks together?
[G] That's their extended model, same bi-attention backbone, plus a new branch that first predicts a triple's relation from the question, then predicts the subject and object spans from the context. On the test split, answer exact match is thirty-six point five three, F one forty-three point nine three. Supporting facts, twenty-four point nine nine exact match, sixty-five point two six F one. Evidence generation, just one point oh seven exact match, fourteen point nine four F one. And the joint score, which needs all three right together, comes out to zero point three five exact match, five point four one F one.
[O] That evidence score is the tell. The model basically can't produce the full correct triple set.
[G] Right, and the breakdown by question type makes that starker. On the test split, joint exact match is essentially zero for comparison, inference, and bridge-comparison questions, and only zero point eight four percent even on the model's best category, compositional. Evidence exact match is zero everywhere except compositional, at two point five seven percent.
[S] So where does the human ceiling land, and how big is the real gap?
[G] They ran a hundred-sample human study, three annotators per sample. Human average joint F one is sixty-six point six nine, human upper bound seventy-five point two five. The model's joint F one on that same subset is nine point seven nine. But the sharpest single number is the evidence task specifically, human upper-bound F one there is seventy-eight point eight one, the model's is sixteen point seven four. That's the widest human-model split of any of the three sub-tasks, wider than answer prediction, wider than supporting facts.
[O] Meanwhile the supporting-fact task looks almost solved, mid-sixties F one for the model.
[G] Which the authors themselves flag as a trap. In their own words, the supporting-fact task is, quote, incapable of evaluating the reasoning and inference skills of the model, because it's just a per-sentence binary classifier. It can score well without composing anything at all, which is the exact criticism this whole paper is trying to move past.
[O] Okay, my strongest case: this is the first large-scale multi-hop dataset where you can mechanically check whether a model actually chained two facts, not just whether it cited the right sentences. That's a real methodological upgrade over HotpotQA, and the same-baseline, same-model comparison across both datasets is about as clean an apples-to-apples test as you get in this literature, lower answer and joint scores here despite comparable human ceilings, plus an eight point seven F one drop for a single-hop reader that literally cannot cheat by reading one paragraph.
[S] I'll grant both of those are genuinely well-designed probes. But here's my deflationary case. Every one of these one hundred ninety-two thousand questions comes out of a closed, hand-authored template vocabulary, seven hundred ninety-nine compositional templates, sixty-two bridge-comparison templates, plus the comparison and inference sets. That's a fixed, learnable question surface. And the paper never runs a template-holdout split, train on some templates, test on templates the model never saw, to check whether these scores survive that. Without it, I don't know how much of "the model is bad at this" is really "the model hasn't memorized this particular sentence shape yet."
[G] That's a fair reading of what's missing, and it's worth being precise about what the evidence field does and doesn't prove. It verifies which triples a model surfaces. It does not verify that the model composed them rather than pattern-matched a template, for instance spotting that a date-of-birth slot wants a date-shaped span in one of two paragraphs, without ever actually traversing the bridge entity. That's the same comparison-question shortcut Min and colleagues found in HotpotQA, just one level removed.
[O] Doesn't the single-hop BERT probe partly answer that concern, though? If a model that can't even see both paragraphs already loses eight point seven F one, that's evidence the shortcut isn't the whole story.
[S] It's evidence against the crudest shortcut, single-paragraph pattern matching. It's not evidence against a subtler one, a two-paragraph model that's learned the template distribution rather than the underlying relations. Those are different failure modes, and this paper's experiments only rule out the first.
[G] There's a second issue, the evidence metric itself is brittle: exact match against Wikidata's canonical entity string. The authors' own section five point four admits names are ambiguous, one person can have several valid aliases, and the ground truth records only one. When they manually audited a hundred training samples for a related mismatch problem, eight out of a hundred had a genuine inconsistency between the Wikipedia sentence and the Wikidata triple. There's no relaxed or alias-aware variant offered, so some fraction of "wrong" evidence predictions are probably naming variation, not reasoning failure.
[O] That changes how I read that seventy-eight versus seventeen F one number. It's probably still a real gap, but it's not as clean as the headline makes it look.

The headline baseline is Yang and colleagues' pre-BERT bi-attention reader from twenty eighteen, BERT only shows up narrowly, as the single-hop shortcut probe, never as a full competing baseline across the answer, supporting-fact, and evidence tasks together. This paper published in November twenty twenty, when BERT-class readers were already standard, so that sixty-six point six nine human-versus-model joint F one gap is measured against a model already a couple of generations behind the state of the art.
[G] The paper is honest about not testing that, but it does mean the true model-versus-human gap under a contemporary reader is genuinely unknown from what's in this paper. It could be much smaller than the headline number suggests.
[O] Zooming out, where does the evidence-triple idea land for how the field builds benchmarks now?
[G] It pushed multi-hop and knowledge-grounded QA toward treating the reasoning path itself as a checkable artifact, not an afterthought, the same question anyone evaluating chain-of-thought or tool-use traces from language models is asking today, can you verify the steps, not just the final answer.
[S] Which is exactly why the closed-template critique generalizes.
