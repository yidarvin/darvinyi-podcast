[S] Let's set the scene. Where does BLEU actually break down, and why did that motivate this specific fix?
[G] BLEU's whole design is n-gram precision. Count how many of the candidate's n-grams appear in the reference, at orders one through four, then combine those with a geometric mean. It never measures recall directly, because recall is awkward to define against multiple references, so BLEU substitutes a brevity penalty that just punishes short output.
[O] Which is a hack. It's approximating something it should be measuring directly.
[G] That's exactly the authors' argument. And the geometric mean has a second problem: if any single n-gram order scores zero, which happens constantly on one sentence, the whole product collapses to zero. BLEU was designed for corpus-level aggregate scoring, not sentence level, and the paper says outright that using it that way is unfair to the algorithm.
[S] So they're building a metric that's fair to sentence-level use by definition, then noting that BLEU isn't fair when forced into that role. Worth flagging early, because it shapes how we should read every comparison table later.
[G] It's a fair point to hold onto. The authors' own earlier work, from 2004, had already made the system-level case that recall matters. This paper is the sequel, pushing the same idea down to the segment level, because that's where you catch small differences between systems, or between versions of your own system during development.
[O] And that's the practical stake. If your metric only works in aggregate, you can't use it to guide day-to-day development decisions.
[S] Walk us through what METEOR actually computes, Marcus.
[G] It starts with an alignment, a strict one-to-one mapping between unigrams in the candidate and unigrams in the reference. That alignment is built in stages, and each stage runs two phases. Phase one, a matching module proposes candidate pairs. Phase two, METEOR keeps the largest subset of those pairs that forms a valid alignment, and if there's a tie, it keeps the subset with the fewest crossing matches, the way you'd count crossed lines if you drew the two strings on rows and connected every matched pair.
[O] So it's explicitly rewarding word order, not just inferring it indirectly from n-gram overlap the way BLEU does.
[G] Right. The modules run in a fixed priority. By default: exact surface match first, then Porter stemming, so "computers" matches "computer," then WordNet synonymy, which the authors themselves call a poor-man's synonym detector, since it never disambiguates word sense. Each stage only touches words the earlier stages left unmapped.
[S] And once you have the alignment, how does that become a score?
[G] Precision is matched unigrams over candidate length, recall is matched unigrams over reference length. Then comes the move that actually matters. They combine precision and recall with a harmonic mean, weighted nine to one toward recall. The formula is ten times precision times recall, divided by recall plus nine times precision, so recall dominates almost completely.
[O] Nine to one is a huge thumb on the scale.
[G] It is, and that ratio was chosen deliberately, because the authors' earlier work had already shown recall was the stronger predictor of human judgment. Then there's the fragmentation penalty. Matched words get grouped into the fewest possible contiguous chunks: one chunk if the whole sentence matches in order, as many chunks as there are matches if nothing sits adjacent. The penalty is zero point five times the ratio of chunks to matched unigrams, cubed, capped at zero point five, so in the worst case the final score gets cut in half.
[S] Cubing that ratio is a strange design choice. Why cube it?
[G] The paper is candid here. It says directly that the penalty's parameters, the zero point five coefficient and the cube, were determined through some experimentation with development data, but have not yet been trained to be optimal. That's the paper's own phrasing. It's a hand-tuned function, not a learned one.
[O] So the headline formula is honest about being a first pass.
[G] It is. One more piece worth knowing: with multiple references, METEOR scores the candidate against each one separately and keeps the best score. The authors themselves flag that as a weak use of multiple references, and it's on their own list of things to fix.
[O] Let's get to the numbers, because this is where the paper makes its case.
[G] They evaluate on the DARPA TIDES 2003 data released for this workshop: six hundred sixty-four Arabic-to-English sentences across six systems, nine hundred twenty Chinese-to-English sentences across seven systems, each with four reference translations, each translation scored by two human judges on adequacy and fluency, one to five, averaged into a combined score.
[S] And the first table is the one everyone quotes.
[G] System-level correlation on the Chinese data. BLEU comes in at point eight one seven, NIST at point eight nine two. Plain precision alone is actually worse than BLEU, at point seven five two. But recall alone, nothing else, hits point nine four one. Precision and recall combined as a plain F one gets point nine four eight. The recall-weighted F-mean gets point nine five two. And full METEOR, with the fragmentation penalty added on top, reaches point nine six four.
[O] That's the whole argument in one table. Recall was the ingredient BLEU was missing, and it wasn't subtle.
[S] System level is the friendly table, though. What happens at the level METEOR was actually built for, segment by segment?
[G] There the numbers are much smaller across the board. Chinese follows the same shape: point two eight six, point three two zero, point three two seven, point three three one. The paper calls these segment-level correlations, quote, still rather low.
[O] So every component helps, but the ceiling is modest.
[G] That's the honest reading. There's also a module ablation: run METEOR with only exact matching, then add Porter stemming, then swap in WordNet-based stemming instead, then the full stack with synonymy on top. On Arabic, exact-only gets point three one two, adding stemming lifts that to roughly point three three, and the full stack with synonymy reaches the final point three four seven. Chinese shows the same shape, ending at its own point three three one. Stemming does most of the lifting; synonymy adds a smaller final increment.

[G] Right, this one's easy to miss. A group at the MITRE Corporation normalized the human judgment scores to reduce disagreement between judges, and when METEOR is scored against those normalized labels instead of the raw ones, correlation rises from point three four seven to point four zero three on Arabic, and from point three three one to point three six five on Chinese. That's a meaningful chunk of the gap between METEOR's score and a perfect correlation, and it's sitting in the human labels, not in the metric.

Every embedding-based and LLM-judge metric that came later, BERTScore, BLEURT, COMET, G-Eval, is downstream of the diagnosis in this paper: score at the segment level, weight recall, use real alignment instead of blind counting.

[G] And I'd add one more limit: everything here is still surface-form matching, widened by a stemmer and an undisambiguated WordNet synonym lookup. A correct paraphrase outside WordNet earns nothing, and the synonymy module is English-only, so it can't help when the target language isn't English. The authors' own future-work section names these same gaps: train the penalty formula on data instead of hand-setting it, move past plain synonymy toward broader semantic relatedness, and weight different mapping modules instead of just prioritizing them in a fixed sequence.
[O] So where does this land for people building eval today?
[G] The throughline is that METEOR is the pivot point in the automatic-metric lineage, the bridge from pure n-gram counting to learned, embedding-based scorers, and eventually to LLM-as-judge evaluators. It's still reported alongside BLEU in machine translation papers two decades later, mostly as a lexical sanity check rather than the primary metric anyone optimizes toward.
[S] The methodological lesson generalizes past MT, too. Recall-weighting, explicit alignment instead of blind overlap, and being honest that a metric behaves differently at different granularities, that's a checklist worth applying to any new automatic eval metric, including the LLM-judge scorers people are shipping now.
[G] And one more habit worth copying: the paper names its own weak spots out loud, the small system count, the untrained penalty constants, the noisy human labels, rather than burying them. That candor is part of why it held up.

[G] If there's one line to take from this paper, it's that measuring recall through explicit alignment, not just counting overlapping n-grams, was the fix machine translation evaluation actually needed.
