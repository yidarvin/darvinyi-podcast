[O] Picture a language model that fails at nine-digit addition almost every single time, and then, with no change to its architecture at all, starts getting it right ninety-three percent of the time.
[S] The catch is what "no architecture change" is hiding. That jump came from bolting on step-by-step supervision the authors had to construct by hand, for every task. That is not free, and it is not zero-shot.
[O] Sure, but that is exactly why this paper matters. It is the moment someone shows a plain Transformer, no new memory module, no learned halting mechanism, can be taught to think longer just by changing what it is asked to output.
[S] Taught being the operative word. Hold that thought, because by the end of this episode I want to know how much of that thinking survives once you take away the hand-written training wheels.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the litsearch site. Today's paper is "Show Your Work: Scratchpads for Intermediate Computation with Language Models," by Maxwell Nye, Anders Johan Andreassen, Guy Gur-Ari, and colleagues at MIT and Google Research, posted at the very end of November, twenty twenty-one.
[S] Joining us is Julian, who has spent a lot of time with this paper and the line of work it kicked off. Julian, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. Worth saying up front, I did not write this paper, so anywhere I say "the authors show," I mean exactly that, not "we."
[O] And it earns its slot on the docket for one big reason: this is widely read as the direct supervised ancestor of chain-of-thought prompting, which showed up only a few months later and reshaped how people talk to language models.
[S] Before we get into the guts of it, Julian, set the actual problem. Why do these models fail at something as basic as addition in the first place?
[G] The authors' diagnosis is architectural, not about missing knowledge. An ordinary Transformer answers in a single forward pass, a fixed number of layers, a fixed amount of computation, no matter how hard the specific input is. GPT-3 already struggles with few-shot addition past three digits, and a companion paper on this same model family found language models cannot reliably predict what a Python function will output, even functions they can write correctly themselves.
[O] Which is such a strange asymmetry. The model can generate working code but cannot run it in its head.
[G] Exactly the tension the paper opens with. And it is not limited to language models. Specialized recurrent and graph networks, built specifically for predicting program outputs, still fail to generalize once a program contains a loop.
[S] So people had already tried to fix this at the architecture level.
[G] Several times over. Neural Turing Machines add external memory the model can read and write. Universal Transformers and PonderNet add a learned halting mechanism, so the network can decide to spend more computation on a harder input. Both are legitimate answers to "give the model adaptive computation time." Both also require designing and training a new architecture from scratch.
[O] And this paper's whole pitch is: what if you do not touch the network at all?
[G] That is the gap. Keep an ordinary, pretrained, off-the-shelf Transformer, and get something functionally like adaptive computation purely by redesigning the task, what the model is trained to output, not how it is built.
[S] So walk us through the mechanism itself. What is a scratchpad, concretely?
[G] Disarmingly simple. Let the model produce an arbitrary sequence of intermediate tokens before its final answer, and train it on that expanded target with completely ordinary supervised likelihood, nothing exotic in the loss. For long addition, the scratchpad writes out the grade-school carrying algorithm one column at a time.
[O] Walk me through it. If I ask it to add twenty-nine and fifty-seven, it does not just output eighty-six.
[G] Right. It first works the ones column, nine plus seven equals six, carry one. Then the tens column, two plus five plus the carry equals eight, carry zero. Only after writing that out does it produce the final answer, eighty-six. At training time the model sees input, scratchpad, and target together. At test time it only sees the input, and has to generate the scratchpad and the answer itself, by sampling or beam search.
[S] And the authors claim this buys three specific things, not just a vague "it thinks longer."
[G] They are precise about it. Adaptive computation time, the model can generate as many scratchpad tokens as the problem needs. Externalized state, intermediate values live as tokens it can attend back to, instead of being compressed into hidden activations. And reduced error compounding, because each intermediate state is quantized down to discrete tokens and sampled, rather than propagated as continuous recurrent state the way a Neural Turing Machine does.
[O] That third one is subtle. You are saying discretizing the intermediate state is a feature, not a loss of precision.
[G] That is the claim, yes. Small errors get corrected at each discrete step instead of silently accumulating through a continuous hidden state.
[S] Does this generalize past hand-designed tasks like addition, or is every new problem a bespoke scratchpad format?
[G] Both, in this paper. There is algorithm induction, hand-designed scratchpads for addition and for polynomial evaluation, where each term of the polynomial is computed and summed separately. And there is the more general version, learning to execute arbitrary code, where the model predicts a full execution trace, the sequence of source-code lines run, interleaved with the state of every local variable after each line, encoded as a JSON dictionary.
[O] So the same recipe, scaled from "carry the one" all the way up to "trace a real program."
[G] That is the arc of the paper. And all of it uses the same family of dense, decoder-only Transformer language models, ranging from two million parameters up to one hundred thirty-seven billion, the same model family used in the companion program-synthesis paper this show has already covered.
[S] Let's get to actual numbers, Julian, because "dramatically improves" is doing a lot of work in that abstract.
[G] Start with addition. Models were fine-tuned on one- to eight-digit problems, then tested in-distribution up to eight digits, and out-of-distribution on nine- and ten-digit problems they never saw in training. In-distribution, once you cross a critical model scale, scratchpad models solve essentially all of it, while the no-scratchpad baseline plateaus around a third correct even at the largest size tested.
[O] And that is the boring case. The interesting one is out-of-distribution.
[G] Right, that is where the gap is starkest. On nine-digit addition, the scratchpad model reaches ninety-three percent, while the baseline stays near zero regardless of scale. On ten-digit, scratchpad still gets fifty-two percent, baseline again near zero throughout.
[S] So the baseline is not struggling, it is flatlined.
[G] Completely flat, across every model size tested. A striking result on its own terms.
[O] What about polynomial evaluation, the slightly harder toy task?
[G] Tested two ways. Few-shot, using the full one hundred thirty-seven billion parameter model with four example problems in the prompt. And fine-tuning, using an eight billion parameter model trained for two thousand steps. Few-shot, direct prediction gets eight point eight percent, scratchpad gets twenty point one percent. Fine-tuning, direct prediction gets thirty-one point eight percent, scratchpad gets fifty point seven percent. Scratchpad wins both regimes, by a wide margin.
[S] And then the real test, actual Python code.
[G] Two versions. First, synthetic programs, simple loops and if-statements, four hundred training programs, two hundred held out for test. Few-shot, direct prediction eleven percent, scratchpad twenty-six point five percent. Fine-tuned, direct twenty percent, scratchpad forty-one point five percent.
[O] You flagged that twenty-six point five with an asterisk when you sent over notes.
[G] Good catch, because it is a genuinely interesting wrinkle. In the few-shot condition, the model kept reusing the original function's variable name in its final trace line instead of writing the expected label "output." Scored the naive way, accuracy was close to zero. The authors noticed the pattern and modified the scoring to accept the renamed variable. That is disclosed clearly in the paper, but it means that one number is not computed the same way as the other three in that table.
[S] Good, that is exactly the kind of thing I want flagged. Now the one that actually matters for anyone doing real work, human-written code.
[G] This is MBPP, real crowd-sourced Python problems with test cases, the same benchmark from the companion synthesis paper. And this is where the story gets a lot less clean. Trained on only the original three hundred seventy-four training tasks, scratchpad actually loses to direct prediction, five point one percent against ten point three percent.
[O] Wait, it loses?
[G] It loses. Scratchpad tracing needs far more supervision to pay off than a single answer token does, and at that data scale there is not enough of it.
[S] So how do they turn that around?
[G] Data augmentation. They sample eighty candidate programs per task from the model itself, run them through a real interpreter, and keep the ones that execute cleanly. That produces seventeen thousand new programs, self-generated but interpreter-verified. Trained on that augmented set, scratchpad more than triples, to seventeen point three percent. The identical augmentation actually hurts direct prediction, which drops to five point one percent.
[O] That is a genuinely lovely result. More data helps the method that can use step-by-step signal, and actively confuses the one that cannot.
[G] Then they stack in two more sources: six hundred seventy thousand, nine hundred four traces extracted from Project CodeNet, and roughly nine million single-line Python transformation examples. The best combination pushes scratchpad to twenty-six point six percent of tasks fully correct, forty-six percent measured per individual test example rather than per full task.
[S] Twenty-six percent, on code that is supposedly toy-level, crowd-sourced, entry-level Python.
[G] And be precise about the denominator too. The official MBPP test split is five hundred tasks. The authors only evaluate on the subset whose gold execution trace fits inside the model's five hundred twelve token generation window, across all three test cases, which shrinks it to two hundred twelve tasks, forty-two percent of the official set.
[O] Okay, let me make my strongest case. Forget the exact percentages for a second. What this paper demonstrates is that an ordinary, unmodified Transformer already has more latent computational ability than one forward pass lets it use. You do not need a new architecture, you need a better output format. That is a genuinely deep result, and it is exactly the insight chain-of-thought prompting runs with a few months later, except chain-of-thought gets a similar benefit with zero fine-tuning, just a prompt.
[S] My strongest case, then. Everything in this paper is supervised on gold step-by-step targets the authors had to construct or mine, by hand for addition and polynomials, and at industrial scale for Python: six hundred seventy thousand CodeNet traces, nine million single-line examples, eighty self-samples per task. That is a mountain of engineering for a twenty-six percent task-level number on entry-level code. And the paper explicitly names Neural Turing Machines, Universal Transformers, and PonderNet as the architectural alternative in its own related work, then never runs any of them on these same tasks. "We do not need a new architecture" is asserted, not demonstrated head to head.
[O] Sure, but the comparison that is in the paper, same model, same fine-tuning budget, with and without a scratchpad, is a completely clean ablation. That is the right control, and it is the one that supports the core claim.
[S] Julian, adjudicate. Does the paper earn "adaptive computation without an architecture change," or does it only earn "scratchpads beat no scratchpad, on the same model"?
[G] Honestly, both readings have real support. The direct-versus-scratchpad comparisons are genuinely clean, same base model, same budget, at every scale tested, so the ablation itself is trustworthy. What is not in the paper is any head-to-head number against the competing architectures it cites, so "you do not need a new architecture" is the authors' interpretation, not a measured result. There is a second thing worth putting on the record, an ablation the authors ran themselves. They took a scratchpad-trained model and then fine-tuned it to skip the scratchpad and answer directly. If the benefit were just "more training signal seen," that model should still do well. It does not, performance drops right back toward baseline.
[O] Which tells you the improvement is really coming from the token-by-token computation happening at inference time, not just from having seen more supervision during training.
[G] That is exactly the authors' own reading of that result. One page in an appendix, but it is doing real argumentative work, and it is the strongest evidence in the paper for the "computation, not just supervision" point you two are arguing about.
[S] Fine, that one lands in the optimist's favor. I will take the point.
[G] There is also a genuine cost the paper is upfront about. They checked whether all this trace training wrecks the model's ordinary coding ability, and it does cost something: few-shot synthesis accuracy drops from sixty-two percent for the original model down to fifty-four percent after scratchpad training. Not catastrophic, but not free.
[O] Which is a fair tax to flag, but fifty-four is still most of sixty-two.
[S] Big picture, Julian, why should someone who evaluates models for a living care about a twenty twenty-one workshop paper?
[G] Two reasons. First, the lineage. This genuinely is the direct supervised precursor to chain-of-thought, which drops the fine-tuning requirement entirely a few months later and becomes the default way people prompt reasoning models today, right through to modern test-time compute methods. Second, there are two small methodological habits worth stealing from it. One is that appendix ablation isolating "extra supervision" from "extra inference-time computation," which is exactly the confound anyone evaluating a reasoning-trace method still has to rule out. The other is simply remembering that a windowed subset, here forty-two percent of the official MBPP test set, is not the same claim as "solves MBPP."
[O] And the paper is honest about what it has not solved. Learning to use a scratchpad without direct supervision is explicitly future work in its own limitations section. The authors even sketch a reinforcement-learning approach, reward for the right answer, penalize excess scratchpad tokens.
[S] Which is basically describing the next several years of the field, without knowing it yet.
[G] If there is one sentence to take from the paper itself, it is this: the authors show that letting a Transformer write down its work, quite literally, unlocks multi-step computation that same architecture cannot do in one pass, without changing a single layer.
[O] My own takeaway: this is the unglamorous ancestor everyone should know by name, because once you see why a hand-supervised scratchpad on Python execution traces works, chain-of-thought and today's whole test-time-compute wave stop looking like a trick and start looking like the same idea, minus the fine-tuning bill.
[S] Mine is a caution to carry into the next paper that claims "adaptive computation": check whether the gain came from more inference-time steps or just more supervision, the way this paper's own appendix did, because that check is rarer than it should be.
