[S] Elena, start with why this was even considered hard. Chess fell to a computer in the nineties. Why did Go hold out for two more decades?
[G] It's really a search-space argument. The paper frames every perfect-information game as a tree with roughly b to the d possible move sequences, where b is how many legal moves you have each turn and d is how long the game runs. Chess is around b equals thirty five, d equals eighty — big, but a machine can prune that with a good evaluation function. Go is roughly b equals two hundred fifty, d equals one hundred fifty. That's not a bigger tree, it's a different universe of tree.
[O] Just to put a number on "different universe" — the paper notes something like two times ten to the one hundred seventieth possible board positions on a nineteen by nineteen board. That's not a search problem anymore, that's a search space bigger than there are atoms to search with.
[G] The two standard fixes both failed here. Depth reduction, cutting the tree short and plugging in an approximate evaluation function, is how chess and checkers engines worked, but nobody had a Go evaluation function good enough to do that. Breadth reduction, using Monte Carlo rollouts, random playouts averaged to the end of the game, powered the best existing Go programs. Even the strongest of those, combined with policies trained to imitate human moves, only reached what the paper calls strong amateur play.
[S] So the honest state of the art going into this was "good amateur," not "professional," and everyone expected that gap to close over years, not months.
[G] That's exactly the framing. The paper's own words are that near-exhaustive search in Go was previously believed to be at least a decade away from professional-level play.
[O] So walk us through the fix. What did they actually build?
[G] Three networks, trained in sequence, each one built on the last. First is a supervised-learning policy network — thirteen convolutional layers — trained on thirty million board positions from the KGS Go server, just predicting which move a human expert actually played next.
[S] And how good is "just predicting a human move," concretely?
[G] Fifty seven percent top-one accuracy on held-out positions, against a prior state of the art from other research groups of forty four point four percent. The paper shows something important alongside that: small gains in move-prediction accuracy translate into large gains in playing strength, so this proxy task is tightly coupled to what actually matters downstream.
[O] Which is exactly what an evaluation-minded listener wants — a proxy metric that actually predicts the thing you care about.
[G] There's also a second, much cheaper network trained alongside it — a linear "fast rollout" policy, only twenty four point two percent accurate, but able to pick a move in two microseconds instead of three milliseconds for the full network. Cheap enough to run thousands of times inside a tree search.
[S] Okay, so stage one gets you a network that plays like a strong human imitator. That's not superhuman on its own.
[G] Right, which is why stage two exists. They take that same policy network and improve it with self-play reinforcement learning — it plays games against randomly selected earlier versions of itself, and instead of learning to match a human move, it does policy-gradient ascent on the actual outcome: a reward of plus one for a win, minus one for a loss.
[O] And how big is the swing from just changing the objective, from "imitate humans" to "win games"?
[G] Head to head, the reinforcement-learning network beats the supervised network it started from more than eighty percent of the time. With no search at all, just the raw policy, it beats Pachi — the strongest open-source Go program at the time, running one hundred thousand simulations a move — eighty five percent of the time. The prior supervised-only state of the art managed just eleven percent against Pachi and twelve against Fuego.
[S] So self-play reinforcement learning alone is most of the story here, before search even enters the picture.
[G] It's a huge chunk of it. Then stage three is a value network, trained to predict who's going to win from a given board position, as a single number. And this is where the paper hits a real methodological snag worth dwelling on.
[O] Go on.
[G] Their first attempt trains the value network on complete games from that same human data set. It overfits badly — point one nine mean squared error on the training set, point three seven on the test set. Consecutive positions in one game are nearly identical, differing by a single stone, but they all share the same final outcome as a label, so the network memorizes games instead of learning to evaluate positions.
[S] Classic near-duplicate leakage, just inside a regression target instead of a train-test split.
[G] Exactly, and the fix is a fresh self-play data set of thirty million positions, each sampled from a separate game, so no two are near-duplicates. That closes the gap to point two two six train and point two three four test — strong evidence the bottleneck was memorization, not capacity.
[O] So now you've got a policy network for narrowing which moves to consider, and a value network for judging a position without playing it out to the end. How do those fuse into search?
[G] That's Monte Carlo tree search, and it's three signals working together, not one. Every edge in the tree carries a visit count, an action value, and a prior probability copied from the supervised policy network. Selection walks down the tree favoring high action-value moves, with an exploration bonus that decays the more an edge has already been visited. At a new leaf, you evaluate two ways at once: a forward pass of the value network, and the outcome of a fast rollout using that cheap linear policy to the end of the game. Those two get mixed with a weighting parameter, and the blended score is backed up through every edge you traversed.
[S] And they tested pure value-network evaluation, pure rollout evaluation, and the fifty-fifty blend against each other directly?
[G] They did, and the blend won at least ninety five percent of games against either pure variant. The two signals are catching different things — they're complementary, not redundant.
