[O] This one earned its spot on the docket because it isn't just another leaderboard — it's an argument about how you should even score something as messy as a ten-thousand-word research report.
[S] Julian, before the rubric machinery, set the table for us. Why does deep research need a whole new benchmark?
[G] Because the obvious way to grade it, matching against one fixed correct answer, misses what these systems actually do. A benchmark like Humanity's Last Exam or GAIA gives you a question with a single correct string, something like naming a material from its band gap and dislocation density. Deep Research agents don't work that way. They read across dozens of sources and write a report that can run five thousand to fifty thousand tokens, and there's no single correct version of that report.
[O] So the short-answer benchmarks are really testing retrieval, not synthesis.
[G] Exactly, and the field noticed that gap and tried to fill it, but the fixes created a new problem. DeepResearch Bench, for instance, grades against rubrics and reference reports that were themselves generated by a language model. The authors argue that's circular — you use a model to write the answer key, then use a model to grade against it, and a human judgment of what a good answer looks like never actually enters the loop.
[S] And the benchmarks that do use humans tend to narrow the scope so far that it stops looking like real deep research — scoring only a Related Works section, say, or restricting everything to AI topics so an agent-as-judge setup can cope.
[G] Right, ExpertLongBench and Mind-to-Web-2 both do versions of that. They're well built, but they trade domain diversity for tractability. The pitch here is that real users point these systems at everything, business plans, medical questions, career advice, so a benchmark should reflect that breadth, and the rubrics grading it should be human from the first draft, not merely human-checked after a model wrote them.
[O] Walk us through how they actually built it, then, because twenty-eight hundred hours is a lot of labor to spend on a benchmark.
[G] It's a deliberately slow three-stage pipeline. Expert one drafts a prompt and an initial rubric. Expert two reviews it independently and the pair iterates until they agree. Then expert three comes in cold and makes a final, independent pass. No single person's judgment ships alone.
[S] What counts as an expert here, though? That word is doing a lot of work in a paper like this.
[G] Fair to press on, and worth being precise about. The paper defines an expert as someone with a strong STEM background who's skilled at task design and evaluation, not necessarily a domain specialist for every prompt, and participants only worked on topics they were personally familiar with. So it's closer to a generalist research-methods expert than, say, a licensed physician grading a medical answer.
[O] That's a lighter bar than some sibling benchmarks that use credentialed specialists.
[S] It is, and we'll come back to that. What did the actual prompt set end up looking like?
[G] A hundred and one single-turn prompts across nine domains. AI and machine learning is the largest slice at about seventeen percent, then historical analysis, business planning, technical documentation, general consumer research, hypotheticals and philosophy, STEM, creative writing, and current events, each with its own rubric rather than a shared one.
[O] How many criteria per prompt are we talking about?
[G] Between twenty and forty-three, averaging about twenty-six, for two thousand, five hundred ninety-three criteria total. Every criterion gets an integer weight from negative five to positive five. A weight of four or five, either direction, marks it mandatory, meaning the response is fundamentally broken without it. Anything from negative three to positive three is a nice-to-have. Negative weights punish specific failure modes: uncited blanket claims, off-topic tangents, factual errors.
[S] So a good answer isn't just hitting the positives, it's avoiding the named ways of being bad.
[G] Exactly, and the criteria sort into six axes: explicit requirements, implicit requirements, synthesis of information, use of references, communication quality, and instruction following. The two biggest by far are implicit requirements at almost forty percent of all criteria, and explicit requirements at roughly twenty-eight percent. That split is deliberate — the benchmark is built to reward inferring what wasn't asked, not just answering what was.
[O] And how do you turn twenty-six hundred criteria into one score without it becoming an arbitrary weighted average?
[G] The grading is a language model acting as judge, tested two ways. Ternary grading gives each criterion one of three verdicts, satisfied, partially satisfied, or not satisfied, worth one, one-half, and zero. Binary grading collapses that middle option to zero, so partial credit disappears entirely. The task score is the weighted sum of the verdicts, divided by the total positive weight available, normalized to the maximum a response could theoretically earn.
[S] Did they just trust one judge model to run all of that?
[G] No, they ran three: GPT-5, Claude Sonnet four point five, and Gemini two point five Pro, and validated every one of them against nine human expert annotators grading three hundred and three responses by hand, using Macro F-one across the three verdict classes. That validation step turns out to be one of the more interesting parts of the paper, arguably more interesting than the leaderboard itself.
[S] Let's get to the leaderboard anyway. What did the three commercial systems actually score?
[G] Under ternary grading, Gemini's Deep Research leads at sixty-seven point seven percent compliance, OpenAI's Deep Research follows at sixty-six point four percent, and Perplexity's Deep Research trails at fifty-six point six percent. Switch to binary grading and every number drops: sixty-one point five, fifty-nine point seven, and forty-eight point seven percent.
[O] No system clears seventy percent either way, and the authors say that lines up with what other benchmarks are finding too.
[G] They point to LiveResearchBench, where the best systems stay under seventy-four percent, and DeepResearch Bench, where nobody clears fifty percent. The authors read that convergence as evidence of a real, shared ceiling rather than something specific to their own grading.
[S] Convergence across three benchmarks agreeing something is imperfect isn't the same as proving why it's imperfect. What's actually driving the failures?
[G] That's where the criterion-level granularity earns its keep. Implicit requirements fail roughly forty-five to forty-nine percent of the time, and synthesis of information fails around twenty-five to twenty-nine percent, both far above references, communication, and instruction following, which all sit under about twenty percent. The pattern is nearly identical across all three systems, which is what lets the authors argue this is architectural rather than a quirk of one product.
[O] So they're all decent at retrieving facts and writing clean prose, they just don't reliably guess what you didn't say out loud, or stitch sources into one coherent argument.
[G] Precisely, and there's a genuinely surprising twist when you split criteria by importance. On most axes, mandatory criteria fail more than optional ones, which tracks, the hard requirements are harder to hit. But on implicit requirements it flips: optional criteria fail sixty point four percent of the time against forty-six point three percent for mandatory, under ternary grading. Systems clear the basic implicit bar but miss the subtler judgment calls that separate a merely adequate answer from an excellent one.
[S] What about citations? That's usually where these long reports either shine or fall apart.
[G] There's a clean trade-off. Gemini's Deep Research produces around a hundred and eleven citations per report at eighty-one percent accuracy. Perplexity's Deep Research goes the other way, ninety percent accuracy but only about thirty-one citations. Nobody manages both volume and precision at once.
[O] There's also that domain inversion in the discussion section that surprised me, the gap between organizational structure and technical precision.
[G] Right, agents hit around eighty-five percent on organizational structure but fall below forty percent on what the authors call technical nugget coverage, even while scoring a strong seventy-six percent on open-ended consulting-style questions. So the systems are better at looking well-organized than at being precisely correct on technical specifics, which cuts against the intuition that technical prompts should be the "easy," well-defined ones.
[S] Does a longer report just game the rubric, though, given how much these things write?
[G] Only weakly, and the authors are careful here. The correlation between token count and score sits around point two five to point two eight for Gemini and ChatGPT, and it isn't even statistically significant for Perplexity. Since scoring is criterion-based rather than holistic, a longer report mostly reflects covering more ground, not padding.
[O] Now the part I actually find most interesting, the human alignment numbers.
[S] Same, this is where the paper does something unusually self-critical.
[G] It is. Binary grading reaches point seven two to point seven six Macro F-one against human judges, with Gemini two point five Pro as the best judge at point seven six, which actually beats HealthBench's own reported figure of point seven zero nine. Ternary grading, the scheme with partial credit, only reaches point five three to point five seven, roughly a twenty-point gap. The authors' own conclusion is that partial credit adds ambiguity without adding discriminative power.
[O] So in their own words, don't lean too hard on the ternary numbers.
[G] That's the honest reading. One more finding worth keeping: they tested whether short inline examples on a criterion help agreement, and they do, by about three to four percent. But when an LLM was used to automatically rephrase or expand the expert-written rubrics, agreement collapsed by fifteen to twenty percent. For Gemini specifically, binary agreement fell from point seven six down to point five six four. Expert-written brevity beats machine-generated elaboration, badly.
