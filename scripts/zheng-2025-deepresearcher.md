[O] Picture an AI agent that, after being trained purely to get the right answer, spontaneously decides the honest move is to say "I don't know" instead of guessing.
[S] Now picture that same agent being marketed as a "deep research" system, when what it actually does, benchmark after benchmark, is answer short trivia questions in a word or two.
[O] Both of those are true about the same paper, and that gap is exactly why I wanted to build an episode around it.
[S] Then let's dig in, because I have real questions about what "deep" is doing in that title.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an AI optimist, an AI skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from litsearch dot darvinyi dot com.
[S] Today's paper is DeepResearcher: Scaling Deep Research via Reinforcement Learning in Real World Environments, by Yuxiang Zheng and colleagues at the G-A-I-R lab, Shanghai Jiao Tong University, presented at E-M-N-L-P twenty twenty-five.
[O] Joining us is Priya, who's studied this paper closely. Priya, welcome. Why build an episode around this one?
[G] Happy to be here. Because it's the first open recipe that trains a research agent with reinforcement learning against the actual live internet, rather than a cleaned-up stand-in for it. That sounds like a small distinction. As we'll get into, it turns out to be the whole ballgame.
[S] Set up the "before" picture for us, Priya. What was the state of the art, and why wasn't it good enough?
[G] Two camps. First, prompt-engineered agents — Gemini Deep Research, OpenAI's Deep Research, Grok's DeeperSearch, and open projects like MetaGPT, OpenManus, and OWL. These chain search calls and reasoning steps together, but a human engineer designed the exact workflow. The paper argues that makes them brittle: rigid behavior patterns, weak generalization to anything outside the scripted routine, and constant manual tuning just to keep them working.
[O] And the commercial systems that actually work well are black boxes. OpenAI has said they use reinforcement learning for their research agent, but none of the methodology is public.
[G] Right, and that's part of the gap this paper tries to close in the open. The second camp is reinforcement learning applied to search — Search-R-one, R-one-Searcher, ReSearch. These train the model instead of scripting it, which is the right instinct. But they train inside a local retrieval-augmented generation sandbox, a fixed, usually Wikipedia-based corpus the model searches through a retriever.
[S] Which sounds fine — Wikipedia covers a lot of ground.
[G] It does, until the answer isn't in the corpus, or the corpus is stale, or the question needs synthesis across domains the corpus never touched. The core argument here is that this controlled setup, everything you need already sitting in a fixed knowledge base, quietly falls apart the moment you leave the lab. Real webpages are noisy, structurally inconsistent, and some of them are actively trying to stop you from reading them.
[O] So the actual contribution is refusing that shortcut, training against the messy, live web instead of a tidy stand-in for it.
[G] Exactly. As far as the authors can tell, nobody had scaled reinforcement learning for a research agent against the real open web before this paper. That's the gap they set out to close.
[S] Walk us through the machinery, then. What's the model, and what's the training loop?
[G] The backbone is Qwen two point five, the seven billion parameter instruct model, not huge, which is itself a claim about how much of this is algorithm rather than scale. They train it with an algorithm called G-R-P-O, Group Relative Policy Optimization, inside a training framework called verl.
[O] Explain G-R-P-O for a second. How does it differ from more familiar reinforcement learning from human feedback setups?
[G] The main simplification is dropping the separate critic network. For each question, the model generates a group of rollouts, sixteen of them here, and the average reward across that group becomes the baseline. A rollout's advantage is just how much better or worse it did than its siblings on the same question. Cheaper and more stable than training a critic alongside the policy.
[S] And mechanically, what does one rollout look like?
[G] An interleaved loop: the model thinks, issues a web-search call as a JSON request, reads back a list of titles, U-R-Ls, and snippets, thinks again, optionally issues a browse call to read a full page, and eventually commits an answer. Each rollout gets up to ten tool calls before it has to answer. Every training step samples two hundred fifty-six questions, times sixteen rollouts each.
[O] What happens on the browsing side? That's usually where these systems get sloppy, dumping a huge wall of text into context.
[G] That's exactly what they avoid. Browsing is handled by a separate reading agent with its own short-term memory. It reads a page segment by segment, the way a person skims, and after each segment decides whether to keep reading or bail. If the first chunk looks unproductive, it just stops. That's the opposite of retrieval-augmented generation's usual move, handing the model pre-chopped passages regardless of whether they're any good.
[S] What's the reward actually built from? Because that decides everything the model learns to value.
[G] It's deliberately minimal, outcome-only, no process reward, no learned reward model. If the output format breaks, missing tags or malformed structure, the reward is a flat negative one. Otherwise, the reward is the word-level F one score between the model's final answer and the gold short answer. And every tool-response token, the search results, the page text, is masked out of the loss, so the model is never trained to imitate the internet, only to act on it.
[O] I want to flag something for later. That reward structure looks like it punishes saying "I don't know" exactly the same as a wrong guess, since both score zero F one. Hold that thought, we're coming back to it.
[G] Good instinct.
[S] What about training data? Where do multi-hop research questions with verifiable answers even come from?
[G] They repurpose four open-domain question-answering sets: NaturalQuestions and TriviaQA for single-hop questions, HotpotQA and 2Wiki for multi-hop ones. After filtering, the final training set is eighty thousand examples at a ratio of one to one to three to three across those four, so seventy-five percent of the data is deliberately multi-hop.
[O] And contamination. A seven billion parameter model trained on huge internet corpora probably already knows who wrote Hamlet without searching anything.
[G] They take that seriously. Two filters: first, DeepSeek-R-one screens out time-sensitive, subjective, or harmful questions. Second, and this is the more rigorous move, they sample ten answers from the base model per question, pass at ten, and discard any question the base model can already answer correctly without search. What survives is a set that genuinely requires going out and looking something up.
[S] That's a real upgrade over just trusting a benchmark at face value.
[G] It is, and it matters more here than in most reinforcement learning papers. If contamination slips through, the model only looks like it's learning to search, when it's actually just recalling.
[O] Last piece of the machinery. What was the actual pain of hitting the live web at this scale?
[G] Substantial. A single training batch can fire something like four thousand concurrent searches and page fetches, so they run a fifty-node distributed cluster just to service tool calls during rollouts. Real search A-P-Is and websites throw anti-crawling defenses and rate limits, on the order of two hundred requests per second in some cases, so they built retry logic and a seven-day cache for repeated search queries, which also keeps the Google Search A-P-I bill under control.
[S] So a real fraction of this paper is systems engineering, not machine learning.
[G] Genuinely so, and the authors are upfront about it. They frame that engineering as one of the paper's core contributions, not a footnote.
[O] Let's get to the actual numbers. What's the headline?
[G] Across seven open-domain question-answering benchmarks, DeepResearcher improves up to twenty-eight point nine points over prompt-engineered baselines, and up to seven point two points over the strongest reinforcement-learning agent trained on retrieval-augmented generation. Two metrics run throughout: word-level F one, which is literally the training reward, and something they call M-B-E, model-based evaluation, where GPT-four-oh-mini judges each answer as correct or incorrect against the gold answer.
[S] Why do you need a second metric if F one is what you're optimizing?
[G] Because F one punishes a correct answer phrased differently from the gold string. If the gold answer is "nineteen sixty-nine" and the model says "in the year nineteen sixty-nine, during the Apollo program," raw word overlap can shortchange it. M-B-E is meant to forgive that kind of rewording.
[O] Give us the in-domain numbers.
[G] On the four datasets used in training, NaturalQuestions, TriviaQA, HotpotQA, and 2Wiki, DeepResearcher leads M-B-E on all four: sixty-one point nine, eighty-five, sixty-four point three, and sixty-six point six. It pulls clearly ahead specifically on TriviaQA and 2Wiki.
[S] Here's my first flag, though. On NaturalQuestions its F one is thirty-nine point six, actually below Search-R-one-base's forty-five point four. Same pattern on HotpotQA, fifty-two point eight versus fifty-five point nine. So on the metric that's literally the training signal, it sometimes loses.
[G] Accurate, and worth sitting with. That competitor, Search-R-one-base, was trained and evaluated with a retriever pointed straight at the relevant Wikipedia corpus, an easier task. DeepResearcher has to find that same information somewhere on the open internet. The paper is transparent about the asymmetry, and staying competitive under a much harder setting is a fair point in its favor, but it does mean the F one story is murkier than the M-B-E story, and the paper leads with M-B-E.
[O] Where does the case get genuinely strong?
[G] Out of domain. On MuSiQue, Bamboogle, and PopQA, none of which were in training, DeepResearcher leads both F one and M-B-E on all three. Bamboogle is the cleanest test, because its answers aren't reliably sitting on Wikipedia. There, DeepResearcher scores seventy-two point eight M-B-E against R-one-Searcher's sixty-five point six, that's the headline seven point two point gap, and against Search-R-one-base's fifty-seven point six.
[S] And R-one-Searcher gets web access at inference time too, if I have the setup right?
[G] It does, and that's the detail that makes Bamboogle the load-bearing result. Even when R-one-Searcher, which the paper describes as trained using local retrieval-augmented generation, is allowed to query the real web at inference, it still performs substantially worse than DeepResearcher. The paper's read is that it's the training environment, not just what you're allowed to access at test time, that builds the skill.
[O] That's a genuinely persuasive piece of evidence for the whole thesis.
[G] It's the strongest single data point in the paper. One more result worth mentioning: training dynamics. F one climbs steadily from around zero point three seven five to roughly zero point five five over training, and as it improves, the model makes more tool calls and writes longer reasoning traces, especially on four-hop questions, where the tool-call count is still rising at the end of training rather than flattening out.
[S] So it's not saturating within the compute they spent.
[G] Correct, which the authors read as headroom for further scaling, though they didn't test that further in this paper.
[O] Time for the actual argument. My optimist case: this paper shows real research behaviors — planning, cross-validating a fact across multiple sources before committing, catching itself when a search goes off track, and declining to invent a precise number it doesn't have — all emerging from a reward that's just word overlap plus a format penalty. Nobody hand-wrote those behaviors in. That's a genuinely exciting signal about what falls out of a simple outcome reward against a hard enough environment.
[S] My skeptic case: this is marketed against Gemini Deep Research and OpenAI's Deep Research, products that write long, cited, multi-page reports. But every benchmark here is short-answer trivia, scored by word overlap or by an unvalidated language-model judge. Call it what it is, an excellent multi-hop web question-answering agent, not a deep research system in the sense the title implies.
[G] You're both pointing at something real. On the framing complaint, the paper is honest about it in its own conclusion. The authors say long-form answers would need a more sophisticated reward than F one, and they never claim to have built a report-writer. The title does more marketing work than the evaluation supports.
[S] Then that point's mine.
[G] Fair, take it. But on the optimist side, the emergent behaviors are real in the sense that they're documented, specific trajectories in the paper's own case studies, not vague claims. The reflection example, where the model searches for one historical figure, gets results about a completely different person with a similar name, and explicitly re-queries, is a legible, verifiable behavior right there in the transcript.
[O] I'll take that one, then.
[S] I'll allow it, with a footnote: those are illustrative examples, not a measured rate. We don't know what fraction of the time the model behaves honestly versus just guessing. And here's the sharper problem, the reward function gives zero credit for "I don't know," identical to a wrong guess. It's a little surprising honest abstention shows up at all under a reward that never rewards it.
[G] That's a genuinely good catch, and the paper doesn't resolve it. It shows one honesty trajectory as a qualitative case study, not a quantified abstention rate across the eval set.
[O] Okay, one more for my side. The GPT-four-oh-mini judge issue cuts both ways, though, right? It's not obviously biased toward DeepResearcher.
[G] Not obviously, but it's also never checked against human labels in this paper. When F one and M-B-E disagree, and they do on NaturalQuestions and HotpotQA, the paper leads with the metric that favors its own system, without independently establishing the judge is trustworthy. That's a methodological gap, not proof of bias, but the headline ranking partly rests on an unvalidated instrument.
[S] Which is my last point. There's no clean ablation. Same model, same training data, retrieval-augmented environment versus live-web environment, all else held constant, that experiment doesn't exist in this paper. The comparisons run across different systems with different training setups, so the flagship claim, that real-environment training is a fundamental requirement, rests on a pattern of deltas, not one clean before-and-after.
[G] Also fair. Bamboogle is the closest thing to a controlled comparison, since R-one-Searcher gets web access at inference and still loses badly, but even there, the two systems weren't trained identically apart from the environment. I'd call the causal claim well supported, not proven.
[O] I'll take the loss on "proven" and keep "well supported."
[S] Zooming out. If this holds up with more scaling, what actually changes?
[G] Two things. First, it's a template: outcome-only reinforcement learning against a real, imperfect environment produces more robust behavior than the same recipe against a sanitized one, and that principle plausibly generalizes past search agents. Second, for anyone doing evaluation work specifically, this paper is a small case study in the failure modes to watch for, a language-model judge used for a headline result without validation, and a contamination check that's rigorous but scoped to one base model.
[O] It's also a nice illustration of why benchmark choice matters so much. Bamboogle carries the whole generalization argument because it's the one dataset not fully anchored to Wikipedia. Swap it out and the story gets murkier fast.
[G] Right, and that's a broader lesson for agentic evaluation generally. A hundred and twenty-five held-out examples are doing a lot of argumentative work here. Worth noticing whenever a paper's strongest claim leans on its smallest test set.
[G] If there's one sentence: this is the first open recipe for training a research agent against the real web with reinforcement learning, and it shows that the environment you train in, not just the one you deploy in, is what builds the skill.
[O] My takeaway: an outcome-only reward against a hard, messy environment produced planning and honesty nobody explicitly taught it, that's a bigger deal than the raw benchmark scores alone.
[S] Mine: read "deep research" here as "multi-hop web question answering," trust the out-of-domain numbers more than the in-domain ones, and go check the full writeup on litsearch dot darvinyi dot com for the figures and the exact tables.
