[G] Thanks for having me. I've spent a lot of time with this paper, and I don't think many of the people citing MedQA today have really sat with the eighteen-textbook corpus underneath it, which is really the paper's whole point.
[S] Set the stage for us, Julian — why did the authors think the field needed another multiple-choice dataset in twenty twenty?
[G] By then, models like BERT, RoBERTa, and ALBERT had nearly saturated the popular reading-comprehension benchmarks, but those benchmarks hand the model a passage that's already known to contain the answer. The authors wanted open-domain question answering instead, where a system has to both find the evidence in a large text collection and reason over it — that's the harder setting the field calls OpenQA.
[O] And the existing multiple-choice OpenQA sets, ARC and OpenBookQA, weren't hard enough?
[G] The authors' own complaint is that those sets need only elementary or middle-school knowledge — their example question is literally "which object would let the most heat travel through". Compare that to a licensing exam question, and you can see why they wanted a harder source.
[S] So the pitch is professional medical board exams as a ready-made, expert-level question bank.
[G] Exactly, and it's a genuinely different animal from the other medical QA datasets on the market at the time. LiveQA, Medication QA, and MedQuAD pull consumer health questions and retrieve an answer snippet from a health website. BioASQ is span extraction, like SQuAD. emrQA ranks lines from electronic medical records with an automatically generated ground truth. None of those datasets the authors compare against in their own related-work table are formulated as open-domain multiple choice. MedQA is the only one.
[O] Walk us through what one MedQA question actually looks like, Julian.
[G] Every instance has three parts: a question, several answer candidates, and a shared document collection the model has to search for evidence in. The questions come in two flavors — a single fact lookup, like "which symptom belongs to schizophrenia", or a full patient vignette: age, history, exam findings, labs, then "which of the following is the most likely diagnosis". The authors sample and annotate a hundred questions per language and call the vignette style "type two" — it needs multi-hop reasoning, chaining symptoms to a diagnosis before you can even go looking for the right textbook passage.
[S] And these aren't invented questions, they're scraped from real exam content.
[G] Real national board exams in three jurisdictions, plus freely available mock tests: the USMLE for the United States, the MCMLE for mainland China, and the TWMLE for Taiwan. Twelve thousand seven hundred twenty-three USMLE questions, thirty-four thousand two hundred fifty-one MCMLE questions, fourteen thousand one hundred twenty-three TWMLE questions, sixty-one thousand ninety-seven in total, split eighty-ten-ten into train, development, and test.
[O] That's a genuinely large, professionally sourced multiple-choice set. What's the catch?
[G] Copyright. To comply with fair-use law, the authors shuffled the option order and randomly deleted one wrong option for the USMLE and MCMLE questions, which turns the real exam's five-option format into four options, one right and three wrong. TWMLE already had four options natively, so it wasn't touched that way, but its questions were originally in traditional Chinese and get machine-translated into English with Google Translate before the model ever sees them, so they can be answered against the same English textbook corpus as USMLE.
[S] So the chance floor moves from twenty percent on the real exam to twenty-five percent in this dataset. That's not nothing.
[G] It isn't, and I'll come back to that when we get to the eval read. The other half of the dataset is the document collection: for USMLE and TWMLE, eighteen English medical textbooks widely used by USMLE takers, OCR'd into roughly two hundred thirty thousand paragraphs and about twelve point seven million tokens. For MCMLE, thirty-three official simplified-Chinese licensing-exam textbooks, OCR'd into about a hundred sixteen thousand paragraphs and close to fifteen million characters.
[O] And then two families of baseline get thrown at that corpus.
[G] Rule-based first: PMI scores each option by how strongly its word patterns co-occur with the question's word patterns in the corpus, no training involved. Then two information-retrieval variants — IR-ES is off-the-shelf Elasticsearch running BM25, and IR-CUSTOM adds a re-weighted BM25 with a medical-term filter called MetaMap that strips non-medical words out of the query, since a type-two vignette is full of clinical noise a search engine doesn't need. The neural family follows the DrQA recipe: a document retriever, which here is just the best IR system, surfaces the top passages, and a document reader scores each option against them. Readers range from a non-pretrained recurrent model called MAX-OUT up to fine-tuned BERT, RoBERTa, BioBERT, and ClinicalBERT for English, with matching whole-word-masked BERT and RoBERTa variants for Chinese.
[S] What's actually doing the work in that reader, mechanically?
[G] MAX-OUT encodes the retrieved context and the question-plus-option pair separately, pools each into a vector, combines them with their element-wise product and absolute difference, passes that through a hidden layer, and scores it. The pretrained readers are simpler — concatenate context and question-option pair with the standard BERT separator tokens, take the classifier token's hidden state, and score it with a single linear layer, softmax across all four options.
[O] Let's get to the scoreboard. What actually won?
[G] On the Chinese set, MCMLE, RoBERTa-Large with whole-word masking hit seventy point one percent on test, well clear of chance at twenty-five. On the two English-language sets, BioBERT-Large was best: thirty-six point seven percent on USMLE test, forty-two percent on TWMLE test.
[S] Thirty-six point seven is barely half of seventy. That's a huge language gap for what's nominally the same task.
[G] It is, and it's one of the paper's own headline findings. But the number I'd actually put in front of a listener is this: on USMLE, the custom IR baseline, no training at all, scored thirty-six point one percent on test. Plain BERT scored thirty-four point three. ClinicalBERT, fine-tuned on real clinical notes, scored thirty-two point four, worse than plain information retrieval. Only BioBERT-Large edges past the IR baseline, and only by six-tenths of a point.
[O] So on the hardest language, a decade of pretraining barely buys you anything over a keyword search.
[S] That's the sentence I wanted said out loud. What breaks it?
[G] The authors had two medical doctors annotate whether the top twenty-five retrieved paragraphs actually contain the evidence, on a hundred sampled questions per dataset. For USMLE, sixty-eight percent of the time there's no usable evidence anywhere in those twenty-five paragraphs. For MCMLE, that number is four percent. For TWMLE, it's about twenty-three percent.
[O] And that maps directly onto the question-style split you mentioned earlier.
[G] Precisely — ninety-eight percent of the sampled USMLE questions are the multi-hop, patient-vignette type, versus twenty-five percent for MCMLE and thirty-one percent for TWMLE. A vignette buries the diagnostic signal in a paragraph of common symptoms, so a keyword retriever chasing something like "cough" or "increased urinary frequency" pulls back miscellaneous distractors instead of the one textbook passage about the actual disease.

[O] So what does this mean for how we should read a MedQA number today?
[G] Mechanically, almost nobody runs the retrieve-then-read pipeline this paper evaluates anymore, modern usage typically skips retrieval entirely and asks the model to answer straight from parametric knowledge. That's a different task than the one these baselines were scored on, even though the questions are identical.
[S] Which means every current model card citing MedQA is implicitly claiming a validity this twenty-twenty paper never tested for that use case, and it's carrying no contamination audit, no human anchor, and no oracle-retrieval control to separate memorization from reasoning.
[O] The upside is that the authors' own error analysis modeled exactly the kind of diagnostic rigor eval work should do, they didn't just report a number, they went and found the mechanism behind it with actual doctors annotating actual retrieved evidence. [G] Agreed, and if there's one habit from this paper worth exporting to every benchmark that follows, it's exactly that: don't just report the score, go find out what's actually driving it.
