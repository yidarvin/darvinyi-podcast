[S] Set the scene for us. By twenty nineteen, when this was first posted, what did a hard example for an ImageNet classifier even mean?
[G] Mostly it meant an ell-p adversarial example. You take a real photo and nudge every pixel by some tiny, carefully computed amount, small enough that a person can't see it but large enough to flip the model's prediction. The whole field of adversarial robustness grew up around that threat model.
[O] And that always felt a little unsatisfying to me, honestly. An attacker who can perturb every pixel by a precise, optimized amount isn't exactly the failure mode I worry about when a model is deployed in the wild.
[G] The paper leans on exactly that critique. It cites Su and colleagues, who found that ell-p adversarial examples crafted against one model mostly don't transfer outside that model's own family. So the threat is narrow, and it's also somewhat artificial. Nobody's camera perturbs pixels by a worst-case budget.
[S] What about the corruption benchmarks, the same authors' own earlier IMAGENET-C work?
[G] Right, that's a second, more heterogeneous line, seventy-five algorithmically generated corruptions, blur, weather, noise, applied to clean images. Useful, but still synthetic. A blur filter is not a photograph. There's also IMAGENET-R, real images, but of art and cartoons and sculptures, real, but stylized, not what a camera captures walking down the street.
[O] And underneath all of that sits a nastier problem: is the ImageNet test set itself even hard enough to tell these methods apart?
[G] That's the Recht and colleagues line of work, the ImageNetV2 replication paper. They observed the original test images tend to be simple, clear, close-up shots, and when they tried to build a matched replacement set, getting comparable difficulty required Mechanical Turk annotators to hand-pick the easiest, most obvious candidates. Geirhos and colleagues' shortcut-learning argument compounds this. A model can hit high accuracy by keying on a spurious cue, a cow's green pasture background instead of the cow, and a too-easy test set will never catch that.
[S] So the gap this paper is aiming at is that nobody had shown a set of real, unmodified photographs that are hard specifically because they expose a shared classifier blind spot, not because someone edited the pixels, not because the test set is too easy in some diffuse way.
[G] Exactly. And it wasn't obvious that category even existed at scale, or that it would generalize past whichever model you used to find it.
[O] So how do you actually go find a naturally occurring adversarial example?
[G] The recipe is what the paper calls adversarial filtration, and it's borrowed, loosely, from natural language processing. Datasets like HellaSwag and Winogrande use a similar sieve: download a large candidate pool, run a fixed model over it, and throw out anything the model solves. The twist here is that the NLP version keeps the easiest examples and discards the rest, while this paper's version keeps the hardest and discards the middle. And the authors note nobody had applied that idea to curating a straight image dataset before.
[S] Walk me through IMAGENET-A specifically.
[G] They restrict to a two-hundred-class subset of the full one-thousand ImageNet classes, chosen so a mistake between any two of them is what they call egregious, confusing a Persian cat for a candle, not a Norwich terrier for a Norfolk terrier. They exclude rare classes, classes that have drifted since the original ImageNet was built, and classes that overlap each other. Then they pull huge pools of candidate images from iNaturalist, Flickr, and DuckDuckGo, deliberately not just the existing ImageNet test set, which turned out too small to survive filtering.
[O] And the filter itself?
[G] Two ResNet-50 classifiers, one fine-tuned on the two-hundred-class subset, one just using two hundred of its original thousand logits. Both run ten-crop classification on every candidate image. If any crop gets classified correctly, the image is thrown out. If either model puts more than fifteen percent confidence on the correct class, it's also thrown out. Then there's a second filtering pass that caps any single confusion pair, Persian cat mistaken for lynx, say, at fifteen occurrences, so you don't end up with a shortlist that's really just one confusion repeated a thousand times.
[S] And then, after all that automated filtering, a human still has to look at every image?
[G] Graduate students, who memorized the entire two-hundred-class list so they could catch multi-label ambiguity, an image that could plausibly belong to more than one class. The numbers on one class make the funnel vivid. They download roughly eighty-one thousand four hundred dragonfly images from iNaturalist. The ResNet-50 filter cuts that to under nine thousand. The diversified shortlist knocks it down to about one thousand four hundred fifty. And the humans manually keep eighty of those for the final dataset.
[O] Eighty out of eighty-one thousand.
[G] Roughly a tenth of a percent survival rate, yes. The final IMAGENET-A set is seven thousand five hundred images across those two hundred classes. And they run a sanity check on the whole thing: the Fréchet Inception Distance, a standard measure of how different two image distributions are, is about point nine nine between the ordinary ImageNet validation and test sets, essentially identical, but fifty point four between IMAGENET-A and that same validation set. So this is a real, substantial distribution shift, not a labeling artifact. And critically, they estimate their own human curators still get around ninety percent accuracy on these images. The images are hard for the model, not ambiguous for a person.
[S] What about the companion set, IMAGENET-O?
[G] Same idea, aimed at out-of-distribution detection instead of classification. They start from ImageNet-22K, a much larger label space, strip out anything that's one of the original thousand ImageNet-1K classes, and keep only the images a ResNet-50 confidently, wrongly, classifies as one of those thousand in-distribution classes. They picked ImageNet-22K on purpose, because it's collected the same way as ImageNet-1K, so the distribution shift is purely semantic, new object categories, rather than some confound like different image resolution or compression artifacting. The result is two thousand curated anomalies, scored by area under the precision-recall curve against random chance, which sits around sixteen and two-thirds percent.
[O] So IMAGENET-A asks can you still classify correctly, and IMAGENET-O asks do you at least know that you don't know?
[G] That's the cleanest way to put it. One tests classification under input shift, the other tests calibrated uncertainty under label shift.
[S] Give me the headline numbers.
[G] On the two hundred IMAGENET-A classes, ordinary classifiers score ninety percent or better on standard ImageNet images. A DenseNet-121, one of five architectures that had nothing to do with building the dataset, falls to about two percent. And it's not just DenseNet: across AlexNet, SqueezeNet, VGG-19, DenseNet-121, and ResNet-50, none of them clear roughly three percent.
[O] Compare that to the ImageNetV2 story we mentioned earlier.
[G] Engstrom and colleagues' later re-analysis puts the ImageNet-to-ImageNetV2 accuracy drop at under three point six percent. The paper's own words are that accuracy, quote, can decrease by over fifty percent with IMAGENET-A, and in absolute terms we're talking a roughly ninety-point collapse, not a few points.
[S] And the out-of-distribution side?
[G] Every model they test lands within a few points of that sixteen and two-thirds percent random-chance line on IMAGENET-O. The maximum softmax probability detector, a genuinely long-standing, well-regarded baseline, is basically defeated.
[O] So then the natural question: can you fix it? What actually moves the number?
[G] Data augmentation, mostly, does very little. Starting from a from-scratch ResNet-50 baseline at two point one seven percent: adversarial training actually makes it worse, one point six eight percent. Style transfer training, which substantially helps on the synthetic IMAGENET-C corruptions, adds just point one three of a percentage point over baseline. AugMix gets to three point eight percent. Cutout, four point four. Moment Exchange, five point five. Mixup, six point six. And the best of the bunch, CutMix, reaches seven point three percent, roughly a five-point gain, tops, over the untouched baseline.
[S] That's a real result, and I want to flag it: methods that are genuinely celebrated for improving robustness on other benchmarks are basically inert here.
[G] Exactly the paper's point. More labeled data doesn't fare much better at ordinary scale. Pretraining on Places365 actually drops accuracy to one point five six percent. Fine-tuning a baseline model on eighty percent of IMAGENET-A itself and testing on the held-out twenty percent shows no real improvement either. Only a full order-of-magnitude jump in pretraining data, ImageNet-21K, roughly fourteen million images across twenty-one thousand classes, buys a real gain, up to eleven point four one percent, a nine point two four percentage point increase. The IMAGENET-O number moves too, from sixteen point two to twenty-one point eight six percent AUPR, though the authors themselves flag that gain as less trustworthy, since ImageNet-21K overlaps with the images IMAGENET-O was built from.
[O] So augmentation, a few points. An order of magnitude more data, about nine points. What's left?
[G] Architecture, and this is where the paper's numbers actually get exciting. Just scaling ResNet-50 up to ResNet-152 lifts accuracy from two point one seven to six point oh five percent. [O] Setting the specific numbers aside, what's the lasting contribution here for how people build benchmarks generally?
[G] I'd point to two things. Second, and more as a cautionary lesson: any benchmark built by filtering against a specific model inherits that model's blind spots as its definition of hard, so it needs exactly the kind of scrutiny we just walked through.
