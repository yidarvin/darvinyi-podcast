Today's paper is How NOT To Evaluate Your Dialogue System, An Empirical Study of Unsupervised Evaluation Metrics for Dialogue Response Generation, by Chia-Wei Liu, Ryan Lowe, Iulian Serban, Michael Noseworthy, Laurent Charlin, and Joelle Pineau, out of McGill University and the Université de Montréal, published at EMNLP 2016.

[G] Thanks for having me. This is a short paper, almost entirely negative in its conclusions, and it is still being cited a decade later, including by the team that eventually built BLEURT.
[O] Which tells you something about how badly the field needed someone to check its homework.
[S] Let's set the scene, Fergus. Why was anyone using BLEU for chatbots in the first place?
[G] Because by 2016, end-to-end neural dialogue models had no task-completion signal to grade against. There is no did-the-user-book-the-flight checkbox for open-domain chit chat, so paper after paper reached for whatever automatic metric was sitting on the shelf. Ritter and colleagues used BLEU for a statistical translation style system on Twitter data. Sordoni and colleagues used it for a context-sensitive recurrent model. Li and colleagues used it to score a diversity-promoting objective. Wen and colleagues used it too. None of them first checked whether BLEU actually tracks what a human thinks of a generated reply.
[O] And the reasoning was not crazy on its face. BLEU works for machine translation, METEOR and ROUGE work for summarization, so why not borrow the closest available yardstick?
[S] Because the assumption underneath all three is that a good output looks like the reference output, word for word. That is defensible in translation, where there is a comparatively narrow band of correct target sentences.
[G] And it falls apart in open-domain dialogue, where the space of valid replies to a single context is enormous. What do you want to do tonight has hundreds of reasonable answers that share almost no vocabulary. That is the premise this paper tests empirically instead of just asserting.
[O] Walk us through what they actually measured. Ten different metrics is a lot to hold in your head.
[G] It splits cleanly into two families. The word-overlap family is BLEU at four different n-gram lengths, BLEU-1 through BLEU-4, plus METEOR and ROUGE-L. All three compare a generated response against a single ground-truth response by counting shared words or aligned tokens. BLEU counts overlapping n-grams and applies a brevity penalty so short outputs cannot game it. METEOR builds an explicit alignment, first exact matches, then WordNet synonyms, then stems, then paraphrases, and reports the harmonic mean of precision and recall over that alignment. ROUGE-L instead scores the longest common subsequence, so the matched words do not need to be contiguous.
[S] And the second family drops the word-matching requirement entirely.
[G] Right, the embedding family: greedy matching, embedding average, and vector extrema. All three represent words as Word2Vec vectors and compare responses by cosine similarity of those vectors instead of counting shared tokens, so in principle a response can score well with zero word overlap, as long as its words are semantically close to the reference's. One deliberate design choice worth naming: the embeddings are trained on a corpus that does not overlap with the task data, specifically to keep the metric statistically independent of what it is scoring.
[O] So on paper, the embedding metrics should have fixed the problem from our cold open. Oh sure, heard the film about Turing is out should score fine on meaning even with no shared words.
[G] That is the hypothesis. Whether it holds is the whole second half of this paper.
[S] Before results, who generated the responses being judged, and who judged them?
[G] Five systems per domain, deliberately spanning a wide quality range. Two TF-IDF retrieval baselines, one matching on the context and one matching on the response, a neural Dual Encoder retrieval model, a plain LSTM language-model generator, and HRED, the hierarchical recurrent encoder-decoder. They ran all five on two contrasting corpora, a casual, non-technical Twitter dataset, and the technical, troubleshooting-oriented Ubuntu Dialogue Corpus. Those two were chosen deliberately, they sit at opposite ends of the diversity and specificity spectrum.
[O] And the human side of the comparison?
[G] Twenty-five volunteers, all from the authors' own computer science department, each rated a hundred responses per domain, twenty contexts times five candidate responses per context: one random utterance, one from each of TF-IDF, the Dual Encoder, and HRED, and one written by a human annotator. Each was rated on a one-to-five adequacy scale. Two raters got dropped for weak agreement, a Cohen's kappa under point two, leaving twenty-three. The remaining raters had a median kappa around point five five, and just under eighty-nine percent of all rater pairs cleared point four, a reasonable bar for moderate agreement.
[S] So a real, if modest-sized, human instrument. What is the actual test?
[G] For every metric, they compute both Pearson correlation, which captures a linear relationship, and Spearman correlation, which captures any monotonic relationship, between that metric's score and the averaged human rating, response by response. Critically, they also compute the same correlation between two randomly split halves of the human raters, against each other. That gives an empirical ceiling: how well could any metric possibly agree with, quote, the human judgment, given that even two random groups of humans do not agree perfectly with each other.
[O] Give us the headline number.
[G] Table three is the paper's core result. On Twitter, BLEU-2 is the best-performing word-overlap metric, Spearman point three six, Pearson point three nine, both comfortably significant. On Ubuntu, every single word-overlap metric is statistically indistinguishable from zero, every p-value there is point one six or higher.
[S] Compare that to the human ceiling.
[G] Two independently sampled halves of the human raters agree with each other at a Spearman of roughly point nine five on Twitter and point nine six on Ubuntu. So the best word-overlap metric, on its best domain, captures well under half of the correlation two humans get with each other. On the harder domain it captures essentially none of it.
[O] What about the embedding metrics, the ones built specifically to fix this?
[G] They do not fix it. Best case on Twitter, embedding average and greedy matching land around point two two, actually a bit below BLEU-2. On Ubuntu, embedding average goes negative, Pearson negative point one six, Spearman negative point one four. A metric built to reward semantic closeness is anti-correlated with human judgment on the technical domain.
[S] Negative is a striking failure mode. That is not no signal, that is actively misleading.
[G] It is. There is also a curiosity buried in the BLEU numbers worth flagging. BLEU-3 and BLEU-4 track BLEU-2 fairly closely on Twitter, even though the authors found only four response pairs, out of a hundred, scored above essentially zero on BLEU-4. The correlation survives because of the smoothing constant, which quietly assigns weight to unigram and bigram overlap regardless of whether any four-gram ever matches. The authors' own recommendation, if you use BLEU at all here, is BLEU-2, not the more commonly reported BLEU-4.
[O] Here is a result that should make us pause before writing these metrics off entirely. Do the embedding metrics still separate good systems from bad ones, in aggregate?
[G] They do, and this is the paper's sharpest methodological point. Averaged over many responses, the Dual Encoder significantly beats both TF-IDF baselines on every embedding metric in both domains, and HRED significantly beats the plain LSTM. On Ubuntu specifically, the LSTM's embedding-average score craters to about point one three while HRED holds at point five eight. Looked at that way, you would conclude the metric is doing real work.
[S] But that is exactly the trap the authors name, is it not? A metric that ranks systems correctly on average can still be almost uncorrelated with human judgment at the level of an individual response.
[G] Precisely, and they title that section Conclusions from an Incomplete Analysis for a reason. Most papers' example tables and per-response claims operate at exactly the level this correlation study says these metrics get wrong.
[S] Two more results worth naming before we argue about what it all means. First, they strip stopwords and punctuation from BLEU, and it gets worse, not better, Spearman drops from point three six to point two oh on Twitter. That implies BLEU's raw correlation was partly riding on non-semantic surface features, not meaning.
[G] Second, they test sensitivity to response length. BLEU-1, BLEU-2, and METEOR all show a large, significant score gap between response pairs with a small word-count difference and pairs with a large one. Mean human ratings barely move between those same groups, two point six six versus two point five seven, nowhere near significant. Worth noting the embedding average metric is not sensitive to length in the same way, so this particular failure is specific to the overlap family. A meaningful chunk of what BLEU and METEOR are measuring here is just how similar in length, which has nothing to do with dialogue quality.
[O] Let me make the strongest optimist case, because I do not think this paper is actually a downer. A field publishing a rigorous negative result about its own tooling, with real correlation numbers instead of vibes, is a field doing science correctly. This is exactly the kind of paper that should get cited for a decade, because it changed what people trusted.
[S] I will take the deflationary side. Every headline BLEU improvement reported by Ritter, Sordoni, Li, and Wen before this paper, and plenty after it, was potentially measuring something correlated with response length and stopword overlap, not dialogue quality. That is not a footnote, that is the evidentiary basis of a multi-year subfield being called into question by one hundred-response survey.
[O] I would push back on called into question, though. The aggregate result held up, the Dual Encoder beat TF-IDF, HRED beat the LSTM, on every metric, in both domains. If a team is doing rapid iteration and just needs to know did my new model get better than my old one, an imperfect but directionally consistent signal is still useful, even if it is the wrong tool for claiming victory in a published paper.
[S] That is a real distinction, directional utility during iteration versus evidentiary claims in a publication, but the paper does not license it. The five systems here span an enormous quality range, from a random utterance and TF-IDF up to a purpose-built retrieval model. That is the easiest possible ranking task. Nothing here tells you the metric would still separate two closely matched checkpoints of the same architecture, which is the comparison most iteration actually involves. And I want to name the instrument itself. Twenty-three raters, all from the authors' own computer science department, on just a hundred responses per domain. Several of the significant findings sit right at the edge, METEOR on Twitter lands at a p-value of point zero six, just above the conventional cutoff, Vector Extrema's Pearson at point zero six seven. With a rater pool this small and this homogeneous, graduate students, not a broad population of chat users, both the point estimates and which comparisons even clear significance are shakier than one clean table suggests.
[G] You are both partly right, and the paper itself draws the line here. On the negative result, I am fully with the skeptic. The authors' own words are that a metric not specifically validated for a new task should not be used to evaluate that task, full stop, and that lands squarely on the whole 2015 and 2016 dialogue literature. On the optimist's iteration argument, the paper does not test it either way. It only measures correlation against human adequacy across five systems chosen to span the full quality range, so extrapolating to fine for close comparisons during development is a reasonable inference, but it is one beyond what is actually in the tables.
[O] Fair. I will also grant the skeptic this: the honest headline is not BLEU is somewhat useful, it is the field had no idea whether its metric agreed with humans, and now we know it mostly did not.
[S] And I will grant the optimist's framing point back. Naming the problem this clearly, with numbers, is what made room for what came after. BLEURT specifically opens by citing exactly this kind of correlation failure as its own motivation.
[G] That is the legacy worth sitting with. This paper does not propose a fix. It earns the right to say we do not have a good metric yet with actual evidence, and the field spent years afterward building learned, context-aware metrics partly because of this exact correlation study.
[O] Zoom out for a second, what does this mean for evaluation practice today, a decade later?
[G] The methodological move generalizes cleanly: before trusting an automatic metric on a new task, measure its correlation with human judgment on that task, not on the task it was originally validated for. That is exactly the question people now ask about LLM-as-judge scoring, or reward models used for reinforcement learning from human feedback. A judge validated on one distribution of outputs is not automatically valid on another.
[S] The response-level versus aggregate-level distinction still bites too. Plenty of current benchmark papers report an average score across many examples and treat a few points of improvement as meaningful, without ever checking whether the metric is sensitive to what actually matters at the level of one example.
[O] The flip side is genuinely optimistic. This paper is one clear ancestor of every learned, human-calibrated evaluation metric that came after it, up through BLEURT and beyond. Naming a failure this precisely is often the fastest route to fixing it.
[G] One honest limit on that lesson: this paper does not test whether context-aware or learned metrics would fare better, it explicitly leaves that as future work. It diagnoses the disease with real rigor, without prescribing the cure.
[G] If there is one sentence to take from this paper, it is that a word-overlap or embedding-average score, borrowed wholesale from another task, correlates with human judgment on dialogue at best weakly, and on a technical domain not at all, so treat our BLEU score went up as close to no evidence on its own.
[O] Mine is that this is what a healthy field's course correction looks like, a short, sharply designed human study that changed what an entire subfield trusted.
[S] Mine is a standing warning: any time a paper reports an automatic score without first showing it correlates with what a human actually cares about, ask this paper's question before believing the number.
