[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take one paper apart. Today we are covering the DeepSeek V three Technical Report, and our guest is Owen, who has spent real time inside this one.
[S] The paper comes from DeepSeek AI, released in December twenty twenty-four, no individual bylines up front, just an extensive contributor list tucked into an appendix and a research email address.
[O] Owen, welcome. Six hundred seventy-one billion total parameters, only thirty-seven billion active per token, trained for under six million dollars — walk us into why anyone even believed this was possible.
[G] Happy to. By late twenty twenty-four, open-weight models had basically closed the quality gap with the closed labs, but they had done it the expensive way, dense models, every parameter touched on every token. Llama three point one, four hundred five billion, is the extreme case of that approach.
[S] So the fix is Mixture-of-Experts, activate a slice of the network instead of the whole thing. That is not new — why does this paper get treated as a big deal instead of a routine scaling exercise?
[G] Because Mixture-of-Experts at real scale breaks three things at once, and most papers only fix one. Load balancing usually needs an auxiliary loss that fights the language-modeling objective and costs you quality. Routing tokens to experts scattered across hundreds of G P Us floods the network with cross-node traffic and leaves accelerators idle. And nobody had shown that F-P-eight, eight-bit floating point training, actually holds up at this scale without the run quietly diverging.
[O] So the pitch is not "we made a bigger model," it is "we solved three separate systems problems that were each individually blocking anyone from doing this cheaply."
[G] Exactly — the paper frames it as all three solved together, not any one alone.
[S] Give me the shape of the model first. Sixty-one Transformer layers, hidden dimension seven thousand one hundred sixty-eight, is that right?
[G] Right. Two architectural pieces carry over from the earlier DeepSeek V two, and two new training ideas sit on top of them.
[O] Let's take them one at a time, starting with attention. What is Multi-head Latent Attention actually doing?
[G] Multi-head Latent Attention, or M L A, compresses the keys and values into a single low-rank latent vector. Ordinary multi-head attention caches a full key and value per head, and at one hundred twenty-eight heads with a per-head dimension of one hundred twenty-eight, that cache is what eats your inference memory on long contexts. M L A instead caches the compressed latent, plus a small decoupled piece carrying the rotary position embedding, and reconstructs the full keys and values on the fly.
[S] So it is not a new attention mechanism, it is a compression trick bolted onto ordinary attention.
[G] Fair on the mechanics, though DeepSeek's point is not novelty, it is that V two already validated this compression keeps performance comparable to standard multi-head attention. This report just carries it forward unchanged.
[O] And the payoff is inference cost, not training cost.
[G] Right, it is specifically the K-V cache at serving time. The query side gets a similar compression too, mainly to cut activation memory during training.
[S] Now the part I actually want to argue about. Load balancing without a loss function — how does that even work?
[G] Each expert layer has one shared expert that always fires, plus two hundred fifty-six routed experts, of which the top eight get selected per token, capped at four nodes so communication does not explode. The standard fix for keeping them evenly loaded is an auxiliary loss added to the training objective, nudging tokens toward under-used experts. The problem is that loss fights the language-modeling loss the entire time.
[O] So DeepSeek's fix is to move the balancing outside the loss function entirely.
[G] Exactly. Each expert carries a bias term, updated after every training step, nudged up for under-loaded experts and down for over-loaded ones. That bias only affects which experts get selected into the top eight. It never touches the gating value that scales the expert's output — that still uses the original, unbiased routing score. So you steer traffic without distorting the signal the model is trained on.
[S] Does it actually work better, or is it just cleaner in theory?
[G] They ran a controlled ablation at two scales, holding data and architecture fixed and only removing the auxiliary loss. At the larger scale, GSM eight K moved from seventy point seven to seventy-four point five, HumanEval from forty point two to forty-six point three, MATH from thirty-seven point two to thirty-nine point six. Consistent gains, and essentially free, no extra compute for the model that skips the loss.
[O] Give me the other new piece, multi-token prediction.
[G] Multi-Token Prediction, or M T P, trains the model to predict more than just the immediate next token. Prediction depth here is set to one, so besides the actual next token, the model also predicts the token after that, through a small sequential module sharing its embedding and output head with the main model. It densifies the training signal, each position now supervises two future tokens instead of one.
[S] Does that show up as an evaluation gain, or is it mostly an inference trick?
[G] Both, and they measured the two separately. On training, an ablation at the larger scale showed HumanEval jump from forty-four point five to fifty-three point seven, GSM eight K from seventy-two point three to seventy-four. On inference, they repurpose those same modules for speculative decoding — the second-token prediction gets accepted eighty-five to ninety percent of the time, which is why the deployed model gets one point eight times the tokens-per-second of a version without it.
[O] So genuinely two separate wins, not one number double-counted.
[G] That is a fair way to put it, yes.
[S] Now the systems side, where I get skeptical fastest. F-P-eight training on a model this size — has not that always been the thing that quietly diverges halfway through a run?
[G] That is exactly the risk the paper claims to have solved: this is the first validation of F-P-eight mixed-precision training at extremely large scale. The trick is fine-grained quantization instead of one scale factor for a whole tensor — activations scaled per token, in tiles of one hundred twenty-eight channels, weights scaled in blocks of one hundred twenty-eight by one hundred twenty-eight, so outliers do not blow out the low-precision range.
[S] And did it actually match higher precision, or just "not explode"?
[G] They ran a direct comparison against B-F-sixteen training, and the relative loss error of the F-P-eight run stayed consistently below zero point two five percent, characterized as within normal training randomness. Strong result, and the first time anyone has shown that number at this parameter count.
[O] And DualPipe is the piece that keeps all those G P Us actually busy while the experts are scattered across nodes.
[G] Right. Routing tokens to experts on other nodes means constant cross-node communication, which normally stalls the pipeline. DualPipe overlaps the forward and backward computation of one micro-batch with the communication of another, moving both directions through the pipeline at once, which the paper shows cuts idle bubbles further than the methods it compares against.
[S] All of that lands in one line I keep coming back to: two point seven eight eight million H eight hundred G P U hours, five point five seven six million dollars, at an assumed two dollars per G P U hour. Pre-training alone is two point six six four million hours, context extension out to one hundred twenty-eight thousand tokens is another one hundred nineteen thousand, and post-training is basically free at five thousand hours.
[G] Per trillion tokens, that pre-training works out to one hundred eighty thousand G P U hours, about three point seven days on their two thousand forty-eight G P U cluster. Fourteen point eight trillion tokens, in well under two months, with no irrecoverable loss spikes and no rollbacks.
[O] Let's get to whether it actually plays like a frontier model. Give me the headline numbers.
[G] On MATH-500 it scores ninety point two, against Claude three point five Sonnet's seventy-eight point three and G P T four oh's seventy-four point six. On AIME twenty twenty-four, thirty-nine point two against sixteen and nine point three. On the Codeforces percentile, fifty-one point six, against Claude's twenty point three and G P T four oh's twenty-three point six. It tops LiveCodeBench too, and is the first open model to clear eighty-five percent on Arena-Hard, landing at eighty-five point five.
[S] Those are all math and competitive code. What happens on the benchmarks that are not math and code?
[G] It stops winning. GPQA-Diamond, graduate-level science, DeepSeek-V3 gets fifty-nine point one against Claude's sixty-five. MMLU-Pro, seventy-five point nine against Claude's seventy-eight. SWE-bench Verified, agentic software engineering, forty-two against Claude's fifty point eight. On SimpleQA, English factual recall, twenty-four point nine against G P T four oh's thirty-eight point two — a real gap, not a rounding error.
[O] But it flips hard on Chinese benchmarks, C-Eval at eighty-six point five, Chinese SimpleQA at sixty-four point eight, both ahead of the closed models.
[G] Yes, and MMLU itself sits at eighty-eight point five, roughly tied with the field, so general knowledge is a wash while the real wins concentrate in math, competitive code, and Chinese-language tasks.
[S] Where does the R one distillation come in, because the paper is upfront that some of this is inherited, not trained from scratch.
[G] During post-training they distill long chain-of-thought reasoning from DeepSeek R one into V three. They isolated that contribution with an ablation on the earlier V two point five model — feeding it R one's reasoning data lifted LiveCodeBench Pass at one from thirty-one point one to thirty-seven point four, and MATH-500 from seventy-four point six to eighty-three point two. So a real chunk of the math and code strength is inherited from a reasoning-specialized teacher, not discovered independently by V three's own pre-training.
[O] Worth saying plainly, distillation is a legitimate, common technique, and it is not hidden anywhere in the paper.
