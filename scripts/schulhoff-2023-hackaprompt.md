[S] By late twenty twenty-three, prompt injection was one of those things everybody in the field already believed was dangerous, but almost nobody had actually measured.
[O] Which is a strange place for a security problem to sit — acknowledged everywhere, quantified almost nowhere.
[S] So a team at the University of Maryland decided to stop debating it and just run the experiment: open up three production language models to the entire internet and see how fast a global crowd could break their instructions.
[O] The answer was uncomfortable. Nine of ten defenses, each one built by researchers who thought they had covered the angles, fell within days.
[S] Only one held, and even that might just mean nobody found the trick yet, not that the trick does not exist.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the litsearch dot darvinyi dot com atlas.
[S] Today's paper is "Ignore This Title and HackAPrompt: Exposing Systemic Vulnerabilities of LLMs through a Global Scale Prompt Hacking Competition," by Sander Schulhoff, Jeremy Pinto, Jordan Boyd-Graber, and colleagues at the University of Maryland, published at EMNLP twenty twenty-three, where it won the Best Theme Paper award.
[O] And joining us is Sebastian, a visiting scholar who has spent real time with this dataset and its taxonomy. Sebastian, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. I would frame this one up front as a measurement paper wearing a hacking-competition costume — the actual contribution is a dataset and a way of cataloguing failure, not a new attack.
[S] Sebastian, walk us through why this needed a competition instead of just another lab study.
[G] The problem framing is straightforward. By twenty twenty-three, LLMs were controlled through prompts almost everywhere — chatbots, writing assistants, even, as the paper puts it, command and control systems — and that flexibility is also the attack surface. A malicious user can hand the model text that overrides its original instructions, and prior work on this had stayed small. Perez and Ribeiro tested a handful of template prompts against InstructGPT. Liu and colleagues hand-collected seventy-eight jailbreak prompts and built a manual taxonomy from them. Shen and colleagues scraped around six thousand three hundred prompts from four online platforms.
[O] That is still a real body of work, though.
[G] It is, but none of it answers the basic quantitative question: against a production model, defended in a realistic application setting, how often does a human attacker actually win? There is a second gap too — automated red-teaming, where you use one language model to attack another, substitutes a model's own creativity for what a large, motivated, diverse population of human adversaries will actually try. Nobody had crowdsourced this at scale.
[S] So the design choice is: do not simulate the adversary, recruit thousands of them.
[G] Exactly, and pay them, which matters. Real prizes simulate the incentive a real attacker has, not just curiosity.
[O] That is the part I like structurally, before we even get to results. Measuring a security claim by actually incentivizing skilled people to try to break it, at scale, is a more honest test than anything a lab can construct internally.
[S] Okay, mechanics. What did competitors actually see?
[G] Ten challenges, arranged roughly from easy to brutal, each modeled on a real deployment pattern — translation, question answering, story generation, moral judgment, an emoji-only response task, and others. Each challenge fixes an application-side prompt template with a slot for user input, and competitors submit text to fill that slot. Success means forcing the model to emit one specific, benign target output the researchers chose precisely because it is unambiguous and automatically checkable, rather than anything genuinely harmful.
[O] That is a deliberate research-ethics choice, using a harmless marker phrase instead of asking people to generate actual toxic content.
[G] Right, and it is worth sitting with, because it shapes everything downstream. The challenges also vary in difficulty — some block certain words outright, some use few-shot templates instead of zero-shot, some place the user's input at the start, middle, or end of the prompt, which changes how exploitable it is.
[S] And which models were on the receiving end?
[G] Three: GPT-3, specifically the text-davinci-003 variant, ChatGPT, meaning gpt-3.5-turbo, and Flan-T5-XXL. Competitors picked a target model per attempt, could submit up to five hundred attempts a day, and worked solo or in teams of up to four. Every submission was re-run server-side at a fixed, deterministic setting before it counted, so a lucky one-off completion could not be gamed onto the leaderboard.
[O] And the scoring rewarded elegance, not just brute force.
[G] It did. Shorter, more efficient submissions scored higher than sprawling ones, and a successful attempt against ChatGPT was worth double, because ChatGPT's defenses were empirically the hardest to beat in pre-competition testing. That is a nice signal on its own — the organizers already knew, before launch, which model would be the harder target.
[S] Over two thousand eight hundred participants is the headline number. Where does the data actually come from?
[G] Two datasets. A Submissions Dataset, the refined set of prompts people actually pushed to the leaderboard, around forty-one thousand six hundred of them. And a much larger Playground Dataset, every single attempt anyone made in the live testing interface, successful or not, which runs to about five hundred sixty thousand. Together, over two thousand eight hundred participants across more than fifty countries produced more than six hundred thousand adversarial prompts. That scale, against production APIs, is the paper's core empirical contribution before you even get to what they found in it.
[O] And then there is the taxonomy, which I think is actually the more interesting artifact long-term.
[G] Agreed. The authors reviewed every prior taxonomy of this kind of attack, merged overlapping definitions, folded in techniques they observed for the first time during the competition, and landed on twenty-nine named categories, organized hierarchically — things like instruction-override, context-switching, obfuscation, and payload-splitting, where some categories are specific instances of a broader one and some are built by combining simpler ones.
[S] How do you even validate a taxonomy like that is not just twenty-nine ad hoc labels?
[G] They used GPT-4 to automatically classify a sample of one thousand prompts against the taxonomy, and checked that against the authors' own hand labels, landing at roughly seventy-five percent agreement. That is good enough to trust the shape of the distribution, not good enough to trust every single classification, especially in the long tail of rarer categories.
[O] Let's get to what actually happened when the competition opened.
[G] Nine of the ten challenges fell within the first few days, despite each one being built, in the authors' words, with what they believed were current best practices for defense. The tenth, the emoji-only response challenge, was never solved, and the authors are not even sure it has a solution.
[S] That is a striking split. Should we read the tenth as "here is what a real defense looks like," or as "we got lucky with the input format"?
[G] The paper does not fully resolve that, and it is worth flagging as an open question rather than a triumph. What it does report cleanly is the aggregate numbers. On the refined Submissions Dataset, eighty-three point two percent of prompts succeeded, thirty-four thousand six hundred forty-one out of forty-one thousand five hundred ninety-six. On the raw Playground Dataset, only seven point seven percent succeeded, forty-three thousand two hundred ninety-five out of five hundred sixty thousand one hundred sixty-one.
[O] That gap makes sense once you think about what each dataset actually is.
[G] Right. The Submissions Dataset is heavily pre-selected — people only pushed prompts to the leaderboard once they already worked. The Playground Dataset captures all the failed guessing along the way, so it is a much more honest picture of how hard this actually is in the moment, rather than in hindsight.
[S] Break it down by model, Sebastian. Did ChatGPT's extra defenses actually buy it anything?
[G] A little, not a lot. Against Flan-T5-XXL, roughly two hundred twenty-eight thousand attempts, eight percent successful. Against ChatGPT, about two hundred seventy-seven thousand attempts, seven percent successful. Against GPT-3, around fifty-six thousand attempts, also seven percent. So despite very different usage volumes, and despite ChatGPT being the harder target in pre-competition testing, the success rate barely moved.
[O] Which tells you the extra defensive layer raised the cost slightly, but a motivated crowd just paid it.
[S] And the paper's actual conclusion about defenses?
[G] Blunt, and I would say this is the headline finding more than the percentages: prompt-based defenses do not hold up. That covers both flavors tested — instructing the model itself to resist manipulation, and using a second model to check the first model's output. Every challenge built around one of those strategies fell.
[O] I want to flag one more result, at the level of what it implies rather than how it was done. Competitors found that a shockingly short submission, just a couple of tokens, was enough to make one of the models leak a hidden key it was supposed to protect on a later challenge.
[G] Right, and the point of an example like that is not the trick itself, it is what it says about the floor: the minimum effort a successful attack requires turned out to be much lower than the challenge designers expected.
[S] Let's argue about what this actually proves. I will go first, because I think the headline numbers get overclaimed.
[G] Go ahead.
[S] Every one of those percentages is measured against a benign proxy target, getting the model to output a fixed harmless phrase or leak a planted key. That is not the same as getting it to produce genuinely harmful content, evade a real content filter, or survive whatever parsing happens downstream in an actual application. The authors argue by analogy in their own framing, that if you can force this phrase, you can probably force something worse, but that is a hypothesis, not something this paper tests.
[O] That is fair as a limit on what the number means, but I do not think it undercuts the finding. If the target had to be genuinely harmful, this study could not ethically exist at all — you cannot crowdsource six hundred thousand attempts at making a model produce real toxic content. The proxy is the price of being able to run this at scale and release it responsibly.
[S] Sure, but then there is staleness. The paper's own limitations section says it outright: this is a snapshot against text-davinci-003, gpt-3.5-turbo, and Flan-T5-XXL, models that were current in mid twenty twenty-three and have since been iterated on repeatedly. They even found at least six thousand of their own successful prompts already only work some of the time, something they call prompt drift. Whatever the true susceptibility of today's frontier models is, this paper does not measure it.
[G] That criticism is accurate, and the authors do not hide from it — they name prompt drift as a limitation themselves rather than letting a reviewer catch it. Where I would push back a little is on which part of the paper that undermines. The specific success-rate numbers age out, sure. The taxonomy, the twenty-nine categories and how they relate to each other, does not depend on which model generation you are testing. That is the piece built to outlast the snapshot.
[O] Which is exactly my case for why this is a genuinely useful paper and not just a scary headline. Before this, "prompt injection is dangerous" was a widely shared belief with almost no shared vocabulary or shared dataset behind it. Now there are six hundred thousand real examples, a public taxonomy, and a clean negative result, that prompt-only defenses do not work, telling the field to stop patching and start looking at architectural fixes instead.
[S] I will grant that is real progress on vocabulary and on negative results. My last objection is about validation, though. The taxonomy chart is built on GPT-4 automatically labeling a thousand prompts, checked against the authors' own labels at about seventy-five percent agreement. A quarter mismatch is a lot of room for the long tail of rarer categories to be noise dressed up as signal.
[G] Also fair, and it is specifically a long-tail problem. The dominant categories in that distribution are common enough that seventy-five percent agreement barely moves the picture, but for a category that shows up twenty times out of a thousand, a quarter of those could be mislabeled, and you would want to treat any claim built on the rarer categories cautiously.
[O] There is one more piece of this I think is underrated. The authors clearly agonized over whether to release six hundred thousand adversarial prompts at all, and they published their reasoning rather than just doing it quietly.
[G] They did. Their argument is that comparable jailbreaks were already circulating publicly before this competition, that the released material does not itself contain harmful content, and that the value to people building defenses outweighs whatever marginal edge it hands an attacker who could already find this online. Whether you find that fully convincing is a judgment call, but it is a real dual-use tension they weighed on the page, not one they ignored.
[S] I will take documented and defensible over silent any day.
[O] Zooming out, where does this sit relative to the rest of the safety and red-teaming literature on the map?
[G] It is the empirical anchor a lot of later work cites when it needs to say prompt injection is real and here is how big the problem is, rather than gesturing at anecdotes. It also set a methodological template, application-grounded challenges, a verifiable target, human incentives at scale, that later red-teaming and jailbreak benchmarks borrow from, including ones that build automated judges specifically because a study this size cannot be graded by hand.
[S] The methodological lesson I would take from it generalizes past security, honestly. Any time a benchmark's success criterion is a convenient proxy instead of the real thing you care about, you should ask what that proxy is quietly failing to measure. Here it is real-world harm, but the same question applies to plenty of non-adversarial evals too.
[O] That is a good general rule, actually, not just a security one.
[G] If there is one sentence to take from the paper itself, it is this: at the scale of a real, motivated, incentivized crowd, prompt-based defenses on their own do not hold, and the resulting dataset and taxonomy are what let the field study that rigorously instead of anecdotally.
[O] My one sentence: this is what responsible, at-scale security research looks like, measure the failure precisely, publish the taxonomy, and let defenders build on it.
[S] Mine is a caution: treat the eighty-three percent and the seven percent as a snapshot of models that no longer exist in that form, not a verdict on what you are deploying today, and go read the full write-up on litsearch dot darvinyi dot com for the figures and the taxonomy tree.
