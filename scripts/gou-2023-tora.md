[G] Format one is rationale-only, plain chain of thought, no tools. Format two is program-only, like an earlier method called PAL, program-aided language models, the model just writes code, no natural language allowed. Format three is To-RA's own tool-integrated format, interleaving the two. On LLaMA-2, it beats rationale-only by twenty nine points and program-only by six point seven. With GPT-4 as the base model, it's nineteen point one over rationale-only and nine point eight over program-only.

[S] Good. Now walk me through what "interleaving" actually looks like mechanically, because "reason in words, then write code" sounds simple but I assume the devil is in the details.
[G] It's a genuinely simple loop once you see it. Given a problem, the model generates a chunk of natural language reasoning. If that already ends in a boxed final answer, generation stops. If not, the model writes a short program, guided by what it just reasoned, and a real Python interpreter executes it, not a hallucinated one. The output gets pasted back into context, and the model keeps reasoning from there, rationale, program, output, repeat, until an answer is boxed or a maximum round count is hit.
[O] And the paper has a nice example of exactly why this beats both extremes. There's a problem about two complex numbers, where you're given the sum of their squares and the sum of their cubes and asked for every possible value of their sum.
[G] Right, Figure two walks all three formats through that exact problem. Pure rationale reasoning derives a plausible algebraic identity, then makes an arithmetic slip at the last step and lands on the wrong answer. Pure program reasoning tries to set up the equations as direct Python assignments, a category error, and just throws a syntax error and dies. The tool-integrated version reasons in words about which solver to reach for, writes a few lines of SymPy to actually solve the system, reads back the roots, notices duplicates to remove, and boxes the correct set of values.
[S] That's a cherry-picked example though. One clean anecdote doesn't tell me the mechanism reliably helps.
[G] Agreed, the controlled ablation we just discussed is the evidence that matters, not the figure. But it's a good intuition pump for "complementary": language is good at planning and noticing errors, code is good at not making arithmetic errors in the first place.
[O] Let's talk training, because I assume nobody had a dataset lying around that pairs natural language reasoning with interactive tool calls.
[G] Correct, so the authors built one. They prompted GPT-4 with few-shot examples of the interleaved format and ran it over the training splits of GSM eight K and MATH, allowing up to three tool-interaction rounds, greedy decoding first, falling back to sampling when that failed. That successfully annotated ninety eight point two percent of GSM eight K questions and eighty three point one percent of MATH questions. After filtering out tool errors and wrong answers, they were left with sixteen thousand trajectories, To-RA-Corpus.
[S] Sixteen thousand examples sounds small for training a family of models from seven billion up to seventy billion parameters.
[G] It's small, deliberately so. The paper tables it against contemporaneous math datasets: RFT uses over one hundred thousand annotations, WizardMath over ninety six thousand, MathInstruct two hundred sixty thousand. To-RA-Corpus is roughly an order of magnitude smaller, and the pitch is it's the only one combining genuine interactive tool use with rationale in the same trajectory.
[O] I like that framing. Smaller but structurally richer data beating brute-force scale is a pattern worth rooting for.
[S] I like it too, in principle, but "quality over quantity" is also the thing every paper with a small dataset says about itself. What's the actual training recipe on top of that corpus?
[G] Two stages. Stage one is plain imitation learning, fine-tuning LLaMA-2 and Code-LLaMA, seven billion up to seventy billion parameters, directly on those sixteen thousand trajectories. That alone already beats every open model they compare against. Stage two has a name: output space shaping.
[O] That's a phrase that could mean almost anything. What is it concretely?
[G] Most training questions in To-RA-Corpus have exactly one recorded valid trajectory. Fine-tune only on that and the model's output distribution gets narrow and brittle, it's learned one path per problem instead of a family of paths. So the authors sample sixty four candidate trajectories per question from the imitation-learned model. Correct, error-free ones get kept as-is, netting two hundred thirty three thousand distinct valid samples. For trajectories that go wrong, they lean on an observation from earlier work: a wrong trajectory is usually correct for a while and only derails partway through. So they truncate it at an arbitrary line and hand the remainder to a teacher model, Code-LLaMA at thirty four billion parameters, which greedily completes it. That yields sixty nine thousand corrected trajectories.
[S] So the "teacher" here isn't GPT-4 again, it's a smaller open model correcting its own family's mistakes.
[G] Exactly, a nice bit of efficiency, no extra proprietary-model calls needed. They merge up to four sampled-or-corrected trajectories per problem with the original corpus, landing on a final sixty nine thousand annotation training set, and every To-RA model gets retrained on that.
[O] What does that second stage actually buy you, in points?
