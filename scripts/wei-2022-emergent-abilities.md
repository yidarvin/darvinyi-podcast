[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a guest scholar take one paper apart. Today it's "Emergent Abilities of Large Language Models," by Jason Wei and colleagues at Google, DeepMind, and Stanford, published in Transactions on Machine Learning Research in twenty twenty-two.
[S] It's on the docket because this is the paper that gave the field a name for something everyone had half-noticed and nobody had systematized. It's also racked up something like thirty-seven hundred citations already, which for a survey paper with no new model and no new dataset is enormous.
[O] Joining us to walk through it is Elena, a researcher who's spent a lot of time with this one. Elena, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. This paper earns the attention because it took a vague, everybody's-noticed-it intuition and turned it into a catalogued, falsifiable pattern across five different model families.
[S] Set the table. What was the conventional wisdom before this, and what broke it?
[G] The conventional wisdom was predictability. Scaling laws work coming out of Kaplan and colleagues in twenty twenty, and later the Chinchilla paper from Hoffmann and colleagues, showed that a language model's cross-entropy loss falls in a smooth power law as you add compute, parameters, and data. That trend holds over more than seven orders of magnitude. If you can forecast the trend line, you can in principle forecast future behavior just by training small models and extrapolating.
[O] Which is a genuinely useful promise. Plan your training run, know roughly what you'll get.
[G] Right, except plenty of downstream tasks don't cooperate with that story. A model can sit at flat, near-random accuracy across many orders of magnitude of compute, and then, past some threshold, jump to well above random. Nothing in the small-model data hints that the jump is coming.
[S] So the loss curve is smooth, but performance on the actual task is not.
[G] Exactly, and that's the gap the paper names. They borrow the word "emergence" from a nineteen seventy-two essay by the physicist Philip Anderson, called "More Is Different" — the idea that quantitative changes in a system can produce a qualitative change in behavior. Their operational definition: an ability is emergent if it's absent in smaller models but present in larger ones, meaning you cannot predict it by extrapolating the smaller models' trend.
[O] That's a strong claim built on a fairly simple test — plot performance against scale, and look for the elbow.
[G] That is the entire method, honestly. It's a survey, not a new experiment. They gather scaling curves from prior published work — five model families, GPT-3, LaMDA, Gopher, Chinchilla, and PaLM — and read one recurring shape off dozens of them.
[S] Walk through what actually counts as emergent, because I think the definition is doing a lot of work.
[G] Two regimes. First, few-shot prompting — you show a frozen, already-trained model a handful of input-output examples in its context window, no gradient updates, and ask it to complete a new one. An ability is emergent here if accuracy is flat at random chance until some scale, then jumps well above it.
[O] And the second regime?
[G] Augmented prompting and fine-tuning strategies — chain of thought prompting, instruction tuning, giving a model a scratchpad for arithmetic. Here the bar is different: a technique counts as emergent if it gives no benefit, or actively hurts, below some scale, and only starts helping once the model is big enough.
[S] So in the second case you're not measuring raw capability, you're measuring whether an intervention pays off at all.
[G] Right, and that distinction matters later. One thing worth flagging, because the authors are careful about it themselves: scale is not one clean number. Training compute, parameter count, and dataset size move together but not identically. Chinchilla has about a quarter of Gopher's parameters at similar training compute, because it was trained on far more data per parameter.
[O] So when the x-axis says "training FLOPs," that's a proxy, not one physical quantity.
[G] Correct, and they say plainly that the scale at which an ability emerges is not some immutable constant. Better data or a better architecture can pull the threshold earlier. That caveat is in the paper's own words, and it turns out to matter enormously for how the finding aged.
[S] Before results, I want to push on the framing itself. "Phase transition" is a loaded term. Water genuinely does freeze at zero degrees because of the underlying physics. Is there an equivalent mechanism here, or is it just a name borrowed for the shape of a curve?
[G] Fair challenge, and the honest answer from the paper's own discussion is that they don't know. They offer intuitions — a multi-step reasoning task might need a model deep enough to run that many sequential computation steps, so there could be a real capacity threshold — but they say outright there are few compelling explanations for why abilities emerge in the way they do. That admission does a lot of quiet work, and we'll come back to it.
[O] Let's get concrete, then. What are the actual numbers?
[G] Start with arithmetic. On three-digit addition and subtraction, GPT-3 sits at essentially zero accuracy until two point three times ten to the twenty-second FLOPs, about thirteen billion parameters, then jumps sharply above random. Four and five digit arithmetic holds out longer, only emerging around three point one times ten to the twenty-third FLOPs, at a hundred seventy-five billion parameters.
[S] That's roughly a thirteen-fold jump in compute just to go from three digits to five.
[G] Right. Next, MMLU, a fifty-seven subject knowledge test spanning math, law, history, and more. Models under about ten billion parameters do no better than guessing, averaged across all fifty-seven subjects. GPT-3 crosses the threshold at a hundred seventy-five billion parameters; Gopher and Chinchilla cross it somewhere between seventy and two hundred eighty billion, depending on the model.
[O] And TruthfulQA is the interesting outlier, right? That one was adversarial by design.
[G] It's the sharpest case in the paper. TruthfulQA was deliberately built to trip up GPT-3, and GPT-3 never clears random chance, even at its largest size. Gopher does clear it, but only at its largest size too, two hundred eighty billion parameters, and once it clears the threshold it jumps to more than twenty percent above random.
[S] So for GPT-3 the story isn't "not yet emergent," it's "possibly never, on this architecture."
[G] That's a fair reading, and it's echoed by the Word in Context benchmark, a semantic understanding task. GPT-3 and Chinchilla both fail to beat random chance even at their largest sizes, roughly five times ten to the twenty-third FLOPs. It only emerges once PaLM is scaled to two point five times ten to the twenty-fourth FLOPs, five hundred forty billion parameters, an order of magnitude past where the other two families topped out.
[O] What about the technique side, chain of thought and the rest?
[G] Chain of thought prompting on grade-school math word problems only beats plain prompting once a model passes roughly ten to the twenty-third FLOPs, around a hundred billion parameters; below that it's a wash. Instruction tuning is the sharpest case here: fine-tuning a model to follow instructions actively hurts performance for models of eight billion parameters or smaller, and only starts helping once you're past roughly a hundred billion. A scratchpad for eight-digit addition, by contrast, pays off at a much smaller scale, around nine times ten to the nineteenth FLOPs, only about forty million parameters.
[S] That's a huge range, forty million parameters for one technique, a hundred billion for another. "Emergent" is covering a lot of ground.
[G] It is, and that range is itself informative, it tells you the threshold is task-specific, not one knob on the model. One more result that complicates the whole scale story: PaLM at sixty-two billion parameters beats both LaMDA at a hundred thirty-seven billion and GPT-3 at a hundred seventy-five billion, on fourteen separate BIG-Bench tasks, despite far fewer parameters and less training compute.
[O] Which means it's not simply "bigger wins."
[G] The authors attribute it to higher-quality training data, more multilingual and code data than LaMDA had, and architectural choices like how PaLM encodes digits. And the instruction-tuning threshold itself later collapsed: this paper needed sixty-eight billion parameters, Sanh and colleagues induced similar behavior in an eleven billion parameter encoder-decoder model, and Ouyang and colleagues' InstructGPT got human raters to prefer a one point three billion parameter model over much larger ones. Whatever's emerging, it isn't pinned to a fixed scale.
[O] Okay, time for the actual argument. I'll take the optimist side first, because I think it's stronger than people give it credit for. This paper took a phenomenon everyone had informally noticed and gave it a definition precise enough to catalogue. Table one lists around twenty distinct emergent abilities across five model families. That's a documented pattern, and it directly warns the field that small-model evaluation cannot tell you everything about what the next model will do. That's a real safety and planning point.
[S] I'll grant the pattern is real in the sense that usefulness does jump, a model that can't do three-digit addition at all is a different product than one that can. But "emergent" smuggles in a much stronger claim, that there's a discontinuity inside the model, a phase transition in the physics sense. I don't think that claim survives contact with how the tasks are scored. Elena, walk us through the mirage argument.
[G] This is Rylan Schaeffer and colleagues, in a paper called "Are Emergent Abilities of Large Language Models a Mirage?", published at NeurIPS in twenty twenty-three. Their core argument is about the scoring function, not the model. Take a task where you must get every token of a multi-step answer right to be scored correct, that's exact match. If the model's per-token error rate is improving smoothly and gradually as you scale, but you need, say, twenty tokens all correct to get any credit, the probability of getting all twenty right is that per-token accuracy raised to the twentieth power. That relationship is wildly nonlinear.
[O] So a smooth, gradual improvement in the underlying quantity can produce what looks like a sudden cliff in the pass-or-fail metric.
[G] That's exactly Schaeffer and colleagues' claim, and it isn't just theoretical for this paper. Wei and colleagues test it themselves, in their own discussion section. They measure cross-entropy loss, the smooth training objective, on six of the emergent BIG-Bench tasks, and find that loss improves steadily even while the downstream metric is still flat at random. The model's likelihood of the right answer is quietly rising the whole time the accuracy plot looks flat.
[S] Which is the authors essentially handing the mirage argument its central piece of evidence, a year before the rebuttal paper existed.
[G] It's a remarkably self-aware admission. To be fair to the original authors, they say plainly that the smooth-loss finding does not fully explain why the downstream metric is emergent, or let you predict where the threshold falls. It's evidence for the metric-artifact reading, not a complete account of it.
[O] But I want to push back on treating this as a full debunking. Schaeffer and colleagues' argument works cleanly for metrics like exact match, on multi-step generation tasks. It's much less clear it explains TruthfulQA or Word in Context, which the paper scores as ordinary classification, right or wrong on a single question, with no chain of intermediate tokens to multiply together.
[G] That's a real limit on how far the mirage argument reaches, and it's a fair point, the two papers don't fully agree on where "metric artifact" ends and something else begins. There's also a quieter confound in the original paper: every curve strings together different model families, different data, different tokenizers, different architectures, so scale itself is never cleanly isolated. The PaLM-sixty-two-billion result is the proof; something other than parameter count is clearly doing work there too.
[S] So: real usefulness jump, contested cause. I'll call that a point to each side.
[O] I can live with that scorecard.
[S] Zoom out, then. If we take the metric-artifact reading seriously, what does that actually change about how anyone should evaluate a model?
[G] It's a direct warning about metric validity, which is really the throughline of any "does it hold up" question for a benchmark. If your evaluation metric is discontinuous, pass or fail, exact match, no partial credit, you cannot tell the difference between "the model isn't improving" and "the model is improving smoothly but your ruler only has one tick mark." The fix that both this paper and Schaeffer and colleagues point toward is reporting a continuous signal alongside the discrete one, token-level likelihood, edit distance, a Brier score, so a flat accuracy curve can't hide real progress or fake a cliff that isn't there.
[O] And on the other side, this paper's real, lasting effect: it gave people a reason to keep scaling past the point where a naive extrapolation says diminishing returns. If new abilities can show up in the flat region of a small-model trend line, small-model evaluation genuinely cannot rule out big surprises at the next size up. That's still true whether or not you buy the physics framing.
[S] Which is exactly why "it might partly be a metric artifact" isn't the same as "ignore this paper." It's a large part of the reason serious benchmark design work exists at all now.
[G] Right, and that's arguably the most durable legacy here, less the specific phase-transition claim, more that it forced the field to take metric choice as seriously as model choice.
[G] If there's one sentence, it's this: some abilities in language models are not predictable by extrapolating smaller models, whether that's a real discontinuity in the model or a discontinuity in how we score it, and the paper never fully settles which.
[O] My takeaway: this is the paper that made "just keep scaling" a genuinely open empirical question again, in a way that scaling laws alone had made feel closed.
[S] Mine: never trust a sharp jump in a metric until you've checked whether the jump is in the model or in your ruler. Go read the full writeup on the litsearch site for the figures and the citation graph.
