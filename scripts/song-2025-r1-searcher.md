[O] Picture a seven billion parameter model that was never shown a single example of how to search the web, no demonstrations, no distillation from a bigger teacher, and by the end of training it beats GPT-four-oh-mini-based pipelines at multi-hop question answering.
[S] Picture the flip side: that same paper sits at two hundred fifty seven citations, while a nearly identical idea posted five days later has racked up twelve hundred thirty two. Being first isn't the same as being remembered.
[O] That tension, a genuinely elegant training recipe against a possible priority accident, is exactly why we're doing this one.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an AI optimist, an AI skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from litsearch dot darvinyi dot com.
[S] Today's paper is R-one-Searcher, Incentivizing the Search Capability in L-L-Ms via Reinforcement Learning, by Huatong Song, Jinhao Jiang, and colleagues at Renmin University of China, posted to arXiv in March of twenty twenty five.
[O] And joining us to walk through it is Julian, who's read this one closely, including its later, more famous cousin. Julian, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is one of those papers that's more influential than its citation count suggests, because it's really the moment a training recipe for teaching a model to search got worked out in public, almost simultaneously, by two different groups.
[S] Set the stage for us. Why did anyone need to incentivize search in early twenty twenty five, when retrieval-augmented generation has existed for years?
[G] Because the reasoning models just before this, OpenAI's o-one, DeepSeek-R-one, Kimi k-one point five, had shown that reinforcement learning with a pure outcome reward, no supervision on the reasoning trace itself, could teach genuinely strong step by step reasoning. But all of that reasoning draws on the model's own internal knowledge. Ask it something time-sensitive, or about private information sitting in a local database, and it hallucinates instead of admitting it doesn't know.
[O] So the obvious fix is retrieval-augmented generation, R-A-G. What was wrong with the existing approaches?
[G] Three families, each with a real cost, and the paper is explicit about this. Prompt-engineered pipelines that do iterative question decomposition and sub-question answering work, but they lean on closed-source models to get their best results. Distilling that behavior into a smaller model through supervised fine-tuning tends to make the model memorize solution paths rather than learn the underlying skill, which hurts it on novel questions. And test-time scaling methods, ReARTeR and CR-Planner among them, use Monte Carlo Tree Search to explore retrieval strategies fresh, at inference time, for every single query. That buys accuracy, but at real latency and compute cost, since none of that search skill ever gets baked into the model's weights.
[S] So the pitch is, bake the skill into the weights during training with reinforcement learning, and skip the supervised fine-tuning step entirely.
[G] Exactly. Incentivize the search capability itself, purely through outcome-based R-L, no distillation, no supervised cold start, no process reward for the intermediate reasoning steps. That's the whole thesis of the title.
[O] Walk us through how you actually reward a model for learning to search, when you have no labels for what a good search even looks like.
[G] This is the paper's central design choice, and it's clever precisely because it's staged. Stage one doesn't care at all whether the model answers correctly. It only rewards the act of retrieving, point five if the model makes at least one retrieval call during its rollout, plus another point five format reward for correctly wrapping its query in the proper tags and its final answer in think and answer tags. No answer reward at all in stage one.
[S] That sounds almost too simple. You're rewarding a model for querying anything, regardless of whether the query is useful?
[G] Right, and that's deliberate. It's a warm-up, just three hundred fifty examples, mixing HotpotQA and two-Wiki-Multi-Hop-Q-A, whose only job is to get the retrieval-invocation format to stick, so the model discovers it even has this tool available. Then stage two drops the retrieval reward entirely, flips the format penalty harsh, minus two for anything malformed, and introduces an F-one based answer reward, two times the intersection word count, divided by the sum of the predicted and reference word counts. Roughly eight thousand one hundred fifty examples drive that stage, sixteen rollouts per sample, and now the model actually has to use what it retrieves to get rewarded.
[O] So stage one teaches, you're allowed to search, and stage two teaches, here's when and what to search for.
[G] Precisely, separating the mechanic from the skill. Underneath this, there are two changes to the R-L algorithm itself, a modified Reinforce plus plus. First, R-A-G-based rollout, generation literally pauses at the end of a query tag, the query gets extracted and sent to a retriever, the results come back wrapped in document tags, and reasoning resumes with that evidence sitting in context. Second, retrieval mask-based loss calculation, those retrieved-document tokens get marked as special tokens and excluded from the policy gradient loss, so the model is never trained to generate the retrieved text itself, only to reason over it and respond to it.
[S] That masking trick, is that novel to this paper?
[G] It converges independently with Search-R-one, which showed up on arXiv five days later, working essentially the same problem. Different groups, different papers, same conclusion, if you don't mask out the retrieved tokens, you're training the policy gradient on text the model didn't generate, and that destabilizes training. Search-R-one's own ablation actually quantifies how much this matters, removing the mask drops their seven-dataset average from point four three one down to point three four three. This wasn't a minor implementation detail, it's close to load-bearing.
[O] What's actually happening on the retrieval side, is this hitting a live search engine during training?
[G] No, and this matters for what comes later. During training, retrieval is a local dense-embedding index over a twenty nineteen Wikipedia snapshot, twenty nine million passages, the same corpus the baselines use. That choice is purely about training speed and cost. The interesting question the paper asks afterward is whether a policy trained only against that static local index can generalize to something it's never seen, live web search.
[S] Let's get to numbers. What's the headline comparison?
[G] Four multi-hop benchmarks, HotpotQA and two-Wiki, which are partially in-domain since some of their training data was used for R-L, and Musique and Bamboogle, which are fully out-of-domain. Scored two ways, a strict word-overlap metric called Cover Exact Match, and an L-L-M-as-judge score using GPT-four-oh-mini. Against the strongest baseline, ReARTeR, which is primarily a GPT-four-oh-mini Monte Carlo Tree Search method, R-one-Searcher on a Llama three point one, eight B backbone beats it by forty eight point two percent on HotpotQA and twenty one point seven percent on two-Wiki, under the judge metric.
[O] And this is where the no-cold-start story gets more interesting, right? You mentioned a base-model variant.
[G] That's the more surprising result. They run the same two-stage recipe starting from Qwen two point five, seven B Base, no instruction tuning at all going in, and it does even better, best or near-best on essentially every dataset, and it surpasses the GPT-four-oh-mini pipelines despite being a much smaller, fully open model. That's the clearest evidence the capability really is coming from the R-L recipe, not from whatever instruction-following the backbone already had.
[S] Before I get excited about beating GPT-four-oh-mini, I want to know if that's actually a fair fight. Same retriever, same corpus, same harness?
[G] No, and this is worth being precise about, because it's the paper's real methodological soft spot. Every baseline runs through the FlashRAG toolkit against that shared twenty nineteen Wikipedia corpus with a fixed retriever. R-one-Searcher is trained and evaluated with its own separate retrieval pipeline. The paper never reports re-running R-one-Searcher inside that exact same harness the baselines use. So some slice of that forty eight percent gap could be retriever or corpus differences, not purely the R-L recipe.
[O] Okay, but here's the result I actually find most convincing, and it dodges that whole harness problem, the online search generalization test.
[G] Agreed, that's the strongest evidence in the paper. They take the Qwen R-L policy, trained exclusively against that static local Wikipedia index, and at test time, only on Bamboogle, let it call the real Google Search A-P-I instead. Switching from local to live search, same backbone, same weights, gets an eighteen point two percent relative improvement in Cover Exact Match. And the online variant beats Search-o-one, a thirty two B parameter model built specifically for online-search reasoning, by eleven point four percent, despite R-one-Searcher never once training against live search.
[S] That is a real out-of-distribution test, I'll give them that. It's not just a held-out split of the same distribution, it's a completely different retrieval environment at inference time.
[G] Right, and there's a case study that explains why. They compare an R-L-trained model against an S-F-T-trained model on the same backbone. Ask both when the last king from Britain's House of Hanover died, and the S-F-T model confidently answers with George the Third's death date, wrong, pulled straight from unreliable internal knowledge. The R-L model instead searches twice, first for the name, then for the date, and lands on the right answer, William the Fourth, June twentieth, eighteen thirty seven. Across the full ablation, R-L beats S-F-T on both in-domain and out-of-domain sets by a wide margin, sixty point six average versus fifty point one on the Qwen backbone.
[O] Okay, let me make the strongest case for why this paper matters more than its citation count suggests. This is a training recipe that gets a model to reliably know when it doesn't know something, and go find out, without a single labeled example of what a good search trajectory looks like. That capability transfers to a completely different retrieval environment at test time, and it does it with a seven billion parameter open model beating closed frontier-model pipelines. If this generalizes past multi-hop Wikipedia Q-A, it's a template for how you'd train any tool-using agent.
[S] I'll push back on the if it generalizes part, because that's doing a lot of work. Everything here, training and evaluation both, lives inside HotpotQA and two-Wiki-style multi-hop question answering. Four datasets total, two of them partly in-distribution by construction. There's no contamination screen anywhere in this paper, nothing checking whether Qwen Base could already answer some training questions from parametric memory before it ever learned to call retrieval. Julian, does the paper address that at all?
[G] No, it doesn't, and it's a real gap. Neither does Search-R-one, for what it's worth, this is a blind spot the two contemporaneous papers actually share. The closest thing to a fix in this line of work comes a bit later, from DeepResearcher, which adds a memorization filter specifically to screen this out. Neither of these two March papers has that.
[O] Fair, but I'd say the online-search result is exactly the kind of evidence that should make you less worried about contamination, not more. If the model had just memorized the shape of HotpotQA and two-Wiki, why would switching to live Google search on a dataset it's never seen improve performance at all? Memorization doesn't transfer across retrieval environments. A learned skill does.
[S] That's a genuinely good point, and I'll concede it moves me. But I still want to flag the judge. The headline L-L-M-as-judge metric is GPT-four-oh-mini scoring GPT-four-oh-mini-based baselines. That's the same model family grading itself and its relatives. And the paper never checks that judge against human labels, or reconciles it with its own stricter Cover Exact Match numbers, even though it reports both in every table.
[G] Fair criticism, and I'd add one more wrinkle, the baseline harness mismatch we already discussed. Between an unvalidated same-family judge and baselines run through a retrieval pipeline that isn't matched to R-one-Searcher's own, I'd trust the ranking here, that R-one-Searcher clearly beats these baselines, more than I'd trust the exact percentage gaps. Forty eight point two percent might really be twenty five percent under a tightly controlled rerun. It's probably still winning. I wouldn't bet on the specific number.
[O] And I think that's actually consistent with taking the paper seriously, you can believe the mechanism is sound and still discount the precise margin.
[S] One more thing worth putting on the record, because it's about the field, not the paper. This is the sibling story. R-one-Searcher posts five days before Search-R-one, works essentially the same problem, arrives independently at the same retrieval-masking trick, and ends up with a fifth of the citations. Search-R-one published at C-O-L-M, the Conference on Language Modeling. This one is still an arXiv preprint, under review. Julian, is that just a publication-venue accident, or did Search-R-one actually do something better?
[G] Some of both, honestly. Search-R-one's baseline comparison is more tightly controlled, same retriever, same corpus, same training data across every single baseline, which is genuinely cleaner science than what we just described here. But Search-R-one never tests live search generalization at all, it stays inside one static Wikipedia snapshot throughout. And Search-R-one uses a plain exact-match reward with no format reward, where this paper's own reward ablation shows the F-one reward beats exact-match-based rewards by up to fifty two point six percent on average. So each paper has something the other one doesn't. I don't think the citation count here is tracking which one is better, I think it's tracking which one got the cleaner peer-reviewed publication, which is a different thing.
[O] Zooming out, if you're building any agent that needs to call an external tool, not just search, what does this paper actually hand you?
[G] The template of the two-stage reward, mostly. Separate learn the mechanic exists from learn to use it well, and you avoid needing labeled tool-use trajectories at all. That generalizes past search, anything with a well-defined invocation format and a checkable outcome could in principle use this recipe.
[S] From an evaluation standpoint, the part I'd want other people in this space to copy isn't the reward design, it's the online generalization test. Testing on a retrieval environment the model never trained against is a much sharper stress test of did you learn a skill than another held-out split of the same benchmark family, and it's cheap to run. More papers in this space should be forced to run that test before they get to claim generalization.
[G] The paper's own future-work section actually agrees with a version of that concern. The authors flag wanting more sophisticated training-data curricula, since so far it's simple mixing, and they flag wanting to scale past seven billion parameters, up toward thirty two B, to see if the same recipe holds at scale.
[O] My takeaway is that this deserves more attention than its citation count gets it. The recipe is genuinely elegant, and the live-search transfer result is the kind of evidence that should make you update toward this generalizing.
[G] If there's one sentence, it's this: a two-stage, purely outcome-based reward, first teach the model that it's allowed to search, then teach it to search well, produces a search skill that survives being dropped into a completely different retrieval environment at test time. That's the paper's best evidence it's a real capability, not a memorized shape.
