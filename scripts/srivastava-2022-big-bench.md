[G] Because GLUE already told them what was coming. SuperGLUE, the harder benchmark built specifically to be sticky and hard to game, hit its own human baseline within about eighteen months. Once a benchmark rewards whichever model is already good at it, it has a short shelf life almost by construction.
[O] That's the challenge, solve, and replace cycle. Something looks hard, a bigger model or a smarter fine-tune eats it, and the field publishes a slightly harder successor. Rinse, repeat.
[G] Right, and the authors point at something sharper than saturation speed alone. Take GPT's accuracy across a wide range of existing benchmarks and plot it against scale, and you get a clean, almost seductive log-linear line climbing toward human performance. It looks like you could just read the future off a ruler.
[S] What's wrong with that? If the trend line's clean, extrapolate it.
[G] Because the trend is an average over benchmarks that were already narrow and already close to solved. A smooth aggregate can climb steadily while hiding the fact that no single capability underneath it is behaving smoothly at all. You don't learn which specific skill switches on at which scale, or whether something is about to jump instead of creep.
[S] Fine, but couldn't scale eventually smooth that problem out on its own? Why build two hundred four hand-curated tasks instead of just waiting for bigger models and better benchmarks to catch up naturally?
[G] Because waiting doesn't tell you what to prepare for. The authors are explicit that the motivation is partly safety-minded: you want to see disruptive new capabilities, and harmful ones like social bias, before they show up unannounced in a deployed system, not read about them after the fact. That only works if the benchmark is already stocked with the failures you're trying to catch.
[O] So the fix isn't a harder single-skill test, it's breadth on purpose, and difficulty on purpose.
[G] That's the pitch. Build something broad enough, and deliberately stocked with problems models are expected to fail at, that it doesn't saturate in eighteen months. Read capability off the shape of the scaling curve rather than off a leaderboard rank.
[S] Okay, Julian, four hundred fifty authors. Tell me how that's not just an author-count stunt.
[G] It's how the benchmark was actually built. BIG-bench was assembled as an open collaboration on GitHub. Anyone could submit a task as a pull request, it went through peer review by discussion on that pull request, and if it was accepted, you earned co-authorship on this paper. That process produced two hundred four tasks, spanning linguistics, childhood development, math, common-sense reasoning, biology, physics, software development, social bias, and plenty of territory well outside conventional NLP.
[O] And the instruction to task authors was explicitly, give us something models can't do yet.
[G] Explicitly. The review criteria call for tasks that are novel, diverse in topic and language, and not fully solvable by current models. That's the opposite instinct from most benchmark construction, where you actually want something solvable enough to produce a clean leaderboard.
[S] How do you even score two hundred four wildly different tasks on one number?
[G] Two task formats, mostly. About eighty percent are JSON tasks, a file of input-target pairs, graded automatically, either by exact or fuzzy string match against generated text, or by comparing the model's assigned log probabilities across multiple-choice options. The remaining roughly twenty percent are programmatic tasks, written in Python, that can query the model over several rounds and score it with a custom metric.
[O] And to pool wildly different metrics into one number?
[G] Every task author sets a preferred metric plus a low and a high anchor score for their own task. The benchmark rescales each task's raw score linearly so zero means poor and one hundred means excellent, then averages across tasks. A model can technically score below zero, if it does worse than random guessing, or above one hundred.
[S] That equal-weighting is going to come up again, I promise you.
[G] It should, hold that thought for the debate. Four more design choices give the benchmark its structure. First, because running all two hundred four tasks is expensive, they curated a twenty-four-task subset called BIG-bench Lite, chosen for keyword coverage and inclusion of things like code, non-English tasks, and bias, a cheap, representative slice for anyone who can't afford the full run.
[O] Second is the model sweep.
[G] They evaluated OpenAI's GPT series, Google's dense BIG-G transformers, sparse Switch-style BIG-G sparse transformers, and later folded in published results from PaLM, sweeping model sizes across six orders of magnitude, from millions of parameters up to hundreds of billions, at zero to three shots.
[S] And the human baseline?
[G] A team of expert raters did the tasks themselves, with any resource they wanted, internet search included, to make the baseline as strong as possible. The authors report both the mean and the max score across raters, and, to their credit, they're upfront that collapsing wildly different rater expertise into one human performance number is, in their own words, a fraught enterprise.
[S] Internet-assisted raters competing against models that were also trained on internet-scale data feels like an odd comparison to hang an entire benchmark's headline gap on. Why should I trust that number at all?
[G] You should trust it as a ceiling estimate, not a precise one, and the authors say as much themselves rather than dressing it up as clean. It tells you experts, given real resources, can do far better than these models, which is the point they need to make. It doesn't tell you the single truest number for human performance on any given task, and they never claim it does.
[O] And the piece I actually love, the contamination guard.
[G] The canary string. Every BIG-bench task file embeds a fixed marker string, and the instruction to anyone writing about BIG-bench is to always include that string in their own publication too, so future web crawls can filter it back out of training data. There's also a dedicated task, training on test set, that probes after the fact whether a given model appears to have trained on the benchmark.
[S] Forward-looking, not retroactive.
[G] Exactly the limitation to flag, and we will.
[O] Give us the headline number, Julian.
[G] Across the full task set, the best model tested scored under twenty on that zero to one hundred normalized scale. On the subset with a human baseline, the best rater sits around seventy-eight, the average rater around forty-four, and the strongest models trail well behind both. On BIG-bench Lite specifically, the best rater reaches about eighty, the average rater about thirty-three, and the strongest model in that comparison, PaLM, gets to roughly forty-two.
[S] So scale helps, but from a very low floor.
[G] Scale helps monotonically is the more precise way to put it. Bigger models and more shots both push the number up, consistently, across every model family tested. It just doesn't push it very far.
[O] What about calibration? Does the model at least know when it's guessing?
[G] Better than smaller models, but still poor in absolute terms. On the hundred nine multiple-choice JSON tasks, Brier scores sit around point two to point three, and expected calibration error around point two five to point four five.
[O] Wait, Julian, is point two five to point four five actually bad? I don't have a great intuition for expected calibration error in absolute terms.
[G] It is. A well-calibrated model's confidence should roughly match its actual accuracy, so an error that size means the model's stated confidence is off by twenty-five to forty-five percentage points from its real accuracy, on average, after binning. That's meaningfully overconfident, even at the largest scale tested.
[S] Here's a number I find more interesting than the headline, honestly: the loss curve.
[G] Cross-entropy loss follows a textbook power law with scale, and the exponent is almost identical across model families, roughly zero point zero six six for GPT, zero point zero six eight for BIG-G, zero point zero six nine for BIG-G sparse. Nearly the same slope, despite different training data, different architectures, different labs.
[O] So the loss is lawful, but the downstream task behavior isn't.
[G] That tension is what the whole paper is organized around. Smooth loss, lumpy capability. They introduce two heuristic scores to categorize that lumpiness, linearity, for tasks that improve gradually and predictably, and breakthroughness, for tasks that jump at some critical scale. High-linearity tasks tend to be knowledge or memorization heavy. High-breakthroughness tasks tend to need multiple reasoning steps, or, and this is the catch, a brittle metric.
[S] Walk through the emoji one, it's the example everyone remembers.

[S] And periodic_elements?
[G] Name the element from its atomic number. Below a billion parameters the outputs are close to gibberish. [O] What about the brittleness result, the cause-and-effect one?
[G] Present the model with two causally linked events and ask directly which one caused the other, and it performs at chance. Ask it instead to just judge which of two full sentences is more probable, where the more probable sentence happens to encode the correct causal order, and it does fine. Functionally the same underlying knowledge, wildly different scores, purely from how the question is phrased.
[S] And the bias finding is the one that should worry people most, honestly.
[G] It's the most sobering result in the paper. In broad or ambiguous contexts, social bias measurably increases with scale. At the largest size tested, the model judges it about twenty-two times more likely that a white boy grows up to be a good doctor than a Native American girl does. In narrow, unambiguous contexts, bias tends to decrease with scale instead, and it can be partially steered down with the right prompting. Bigger is not automatically safer.
[O] There's also a non-English gap worth naming.
[G] A real one. Most of the field's scaling story, in this paper, is really an English-language scaling story.
