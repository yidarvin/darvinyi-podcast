[O] I'd argue that's the wrong lesson. The authors ran that swap themselves, published it in their own paper, and used it to show exactly how much of the gap is attributable to distractor construction. That's a level of self-scrutiny most benchmark papers never attempt.
[S] Sure, but showing your own soft spot doesn't make it not a soft spot. We're going to spend the next half hour on both of those readings.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a guest scholar take one paper off the site and actually argue about it. Today it's CommonsenseQA, a Question Answering Challenge Targeting Commonsense Knowledge, by Alon Talmor, Jonathan Herzig, Nicholas Lourie, and Jonathan Berant, out of Tel Aviv University and the Allen Institute for AI, published at NAACL twenty nineteen.
[S] Joining us is Maya, who's spent real time with this one. Maya, welcome.
[G] Happy to be here. This is one of those quietly foundational papers — it's sitting north of twenty-five hundred citations now, and it's still the go-to commonsense benchmark that later reasoning-technique papers, like Wang and colleagues' Self-Consistency work, reach for as a held-out test.
[O] So let's set up why it needed to exist. Maya, what was the state of question answering before this?
[G] Dominated by reading comprehension. SQuAD, TriviaQA, MS MARCO — you give the model a passage, the answer is sitting somewhere in the text, and the task is mostly locating it. The authors' complaint is that this requires very little of the background knowledge people actually draw on when they answer real questions.
[S] And the datasets that did target commonsense directly were too small to matter.
[G] Right. The Winograd Schema Challenge has only about a hundred fifty hand-authored examples. COPA, the Choice of Plausible Alternatives, has five hundred development and five hundred test questions. Both too small to fine-tune a modern neural model on, let alone get a statistically stable benchmark number from.
[O] There was a bigger attempt, though — SWAG.
[G] SWAG is the important precedent, and the paper is candid about what happened to it. Situations With Adversarial Generations, given a description of an event, infer what happens next. Big enough to train on, and it used an adversarial filtering procedure to strip out endings a classifier could already solve. And then BERT-style pretrained language models reached near-human performance on it within months anyway.
[S] Because the task turned out to be learnable from how crowd workers write plausible versus implausible continuations, not from anything you'd call world knowledge.
[G] Exactly, a stylistic tell, not a reasoning requirement. That's the direct shadow this paper is written under. The design brief becomes: large enough to train on, answerable by a person with zero extra context, and built so the right answer can't be picked out from surface cues alone. That last part echoes Gururangan and colleagues' work on annotation artifacts in natural language inference data — the same failure mode, just in a different task.
[O] So walk us through the actual construction. Maya, you're the deepest into this one, take us through it.
[G] It starts from ConceptNet five point five, Speer and colleagues' graph knowledge base — thirty-two million triplets of the form concept, relation, concept, like "gambler, capable of, lose money." First they filter that pool down: drop relations that are too generic, like related-to, or already well studied in NLP, like is-a, keeping twenty-two relation types. Drop concepts longer than four words or not in English. Drop pairs where the edit distance between the two concepts is too small. That leaves two hundred thirty-six thousand, two hundred eight triplets.
[S] And then the actual question-generation trick.
[G] Right, this is the clever part. Instead of picking one triplet at a time, they group triplets that share a source concept and a relation into question sets — one source concept plus three target concepts, all connected to it by the same relation. The classic example in the paper: river, connected by "at location" to waterfall, bridge, and valley. That grouping alone generates roughly one hundred thirty thousand triplets across forty-three thousand question sets.
[O] Why go to the trouble of grouping them like that instead of just picking random distractors?
[G] Because the paper explicitly says random ConceptNet distractors are too easy to eliminate with surface clues. If your three targets already share a relation with the source concept, they're semantically close to each other, so a worker has to write a question specific enough to separate "waterfall" from "bridge" from "valley," not just specific enough to separate "waterfall" from anything.
[S] And the actual writing happens on Mechanical Turk.
[G] Crowd workers see the source concept and the three targets, and write three questions, one per target, worded so only that target is correct among the three. They're explicitly told not to use words strongly associated with the answer — the paper's own example is: don't use the word "open" when the answer is "door." Only workers whose questions pass verification at least seventy-five percent of the time keep getting work.
[O] And then it gets harder still.
[G] Each worker adds two more distractors to their own question — one pulled from ConceptNet, a plausible-but-wrong neighbor, and one they write from scratch, meant to look tempting but be easy for a human to dismiss. That's five candidate answers total per question. Then a separate, disjoint pool of workers verifies each one — two verifiers per question, kept only if at least one of them answers it correctly. That filtering step alone removes fifteen percent of the formulated questions.
[S] Is there anything in here besides the plain question-and-answer pair?
[G] One more layer, used only for a single baseline. For every question and every one of its five candidates, they issue a Google search query combining the question and that candidate, and keep the first hundred result snippets — five hundred snippets per question total. That's context for a reading-comprehension baseline later, not part of the core task.
[O] So what's the final tally?
[G] Twelve thousand, two hundred forty-seven final questions, out of sixteen thousand, two hundred forty-two that were formulated. Fully loaded cost, thirty-three cents per question. They release two eighty-ten-ten splits — a random split, and a question-concept split where the source concepts never overlap across train, dev, and test.
[S] And one of those is harder than the other.
[G] The random split, and by design. On the question-concept split, a model can partly get away with memorizing "this source concept tends to pair with this answer concept." The random split kills that shortcut, because the same source concept shows up in training and in test with a different correct answer attached. Averaged across every trained model in the paper, that costs about five points of accuracy. Because it's harder, the authors treat the random split as the primary evaluation.
[O] Let's get to the actual numbers, because there's a lot of daylight in this table.
[G] Human accuracy, from five non-expert annotators per question on a hundred sampled questions, majority vote, is eighty-eight point nine percent. The best model, BERT-large fine-tuned directly on the dataset, reaches fifty-five point nine percent on the random split, sixty-three point six on the question-concept split.
[S] That's a thirty-three point gap to humans, on the split they call primary.
[G] It is. GPT, the original OpenAI GPT-one, comes next at forty-five point five and fifty-five point five. Below that, everything gets much weaker fast. The untrained baselines — cosine similarity over GloVe or Numberbatch embeddings, a one-billion-word language model scored two different ways — land in the low twenties to low thirties, barely above the twenty percent floor for a five-way guess.
[O] What about models that are trained on the dataset but without large-scale pretraining?
[G] ESIM and the bilinear and compare-aggregate architectures top out somewhere around thirty to forty percent. And there's one more baseline worth naming specifically: BIDAF-plus-plus, the one model that actually uses those five hundred retrieved web snippets as context. It scores thirty-two percent on the random split, thirty-eight point four on question-concept — barely better than models with no external text at all. In the authors' own words, that hints the snippets "do not carry a lot of useful information."
[S] Now give us the number that actually worries me — the sanity check.
[G] The SANITY condition. Same questions, but the four curated distractors get replaced with distractors sampled at random from ConceptNet instead of the adversarially-grouped ones. Under that swap, BERT-large jumps to ninety-two point three percent on the random split, ninety-three point two on question-concept. GPT goes from forty-five point five up to eighty-seven point two. Even the weaker trained baselines roughly double.
[O] So the model was never incapable of the task. It was specifically bad at this task with these distractors.
[G] That's the honest reading, yes. And there's a second diagnostic that sharpens exactly the same point. They sampled a hundred development questions and hand-labeled BERT-large's errors by category. On questions where a surface lexical clue points toward the answer, it gets seventy-seven point seven percent right. On questions needing negation or antonym understanding, forty-two point eight. Factoid knowledge, thirty-eight point four. Fine answer-granularity distinctions, thirty-five point four. And where the answer requires satisfying a conjunction of conditions, only twenty-three point eight.
[S] A fifty-four point spread between "there's a lexical hook" and "you have to combine multiple conditions." That's basically a map of exactly where the shortcut lives and where it doesn't.
[G] It is, and it lines up with the SANITY result almost perfectly — take away the adversarial distractor selection, and you take away most of what's punishing the model on that low end.
[O] Is there anything in here suggesting more data alone fixes it?
[G] They ran a learning curve, training BERT-large on subsets of the data and extrapolating outward. The projection lands around seventy-five percent development accuracy at one hundred thousand training examples — eight times the size of the current dataset — and that's still well short of the eighty-eight point nine percent human ceiling.
[S] So even in the optimistic extrapolation, scale alone doesn't close it.
[G] Not on their projection, no.
[O] Alright, let me make the strongest case for this paper, because I think there's a real one. This is a rare instance of a benchmark paper that ships its own strongest counterargument. Most datasets don't include the experiment showing how much of their own difficulty is an artifact of construction choices. That SANITY ablation, and the category breakdown right alongside it, is unusually honest self-diagnosis, and it's exactly the kind of transparency that let the field actually use this dataset correctly for years afterward instead of over-trusting the headline number.
[S] I'll take the deflationary case. Two points. First, the SANITY jump itself — thirty-six points of accuracy recovered just by changing which wrong answers sit on the page — tells me the fifty-five point nine isn't a clean measurement of "commonsense reasoning" in the abstract. It's measuring, in large part, whether a model can discriminate between ConceptNet-adjacent concepts under adversarial selection. Those are related skills, but they're not the same skill, and the paper's title promises the broader one.
[G] That's fair, and it's really the paper's own finding, not a hidden flaw someone else dug up. Where I'd push back slightly is that adversarial distractor selection isn't a bug in benchmark design, it's the mechanism that forces discrimination instead of elimination. The question is whether it's calibrated correctly, and Table 6, the category breakdown, is direct evidence it's doing real work on the harder categories — conjunction, granularity, negation — not just gatekeeping on lexical association.
[S] Second point, and this one the paper doesn't run an experiment against. Only a hundred twenty-two workers formulated all twelve thousand, two hundred forty-seven questions, and the paper states ten of them wrote more than eighty-five percent of the total. Maya, doesn't that risk the exact "annotation artifacts" problem the paper cites Gururangan and colleagues for in the related work — a model picking up on ten people's phrasing habits rather than commonsense per se?
[G] It's a real risk, and you're right that the paper doesn't test for it directly — there's no disjoint-annotator-pool ablation the way there is a SANITY ablation for distractors. What I can say is the paper does report high linguistic variety in how those ten workers phrased things — only forty-four percent of questions open with a WH-word, thirteen percent just start with "the," about five percent use a first name to build a mini-scenario. That's evidence against a single rigid template, but it's not the same as ruling out ten-person stylistic fingerprinting, and the paper is silent on that specific question.
[O] Can I push on one thing too? You called out that BIDAF-plus-plus barely beats the no-context baselines. Doesn't that undercut the whole "optional web snippets" design, not just say something about that one model?
[G] Possibly, though the paper only tests one retrieval architecture against those snippets, so it's hard to separate "web context doesn't help this task" from "this particular BIDAF-plus-ELMo architecture doesn't use the context well." The authors themselves hedge it as "hinting," not concluding. I'd call that an open question the paper raises but doesn't close.
[S] There's a structural point too, unlike SWAG. SWAG's name is literally built around adversarial generation — it iteratively discarded candidate endings a fine-tuned classifier could already solve, before release. CommonsenseQA's distractor pool never goes through anything like that. It's entirely human-authored and human-verified, so there's no equivalent guarantee that a fine-tuned language model couldn't find and exploit patterns the human verifiers simply didn't notice.
[G] That's an accurate contrast, and worth sitting with. It doesn't mean the dataset is broken, SWAG's adversarial filtering against one specific classifier turned out to create its own fragility later. But it does mean CommonsenseQA's difficulty was certified by human judgment alone, at the scale of two verifiers per question, not by an automated adversary the way SWAG's was.
[O] So where does that leave the headline claim, in your view, Maya?
[G] My read, and this goes slightly beyond what the paper itself argues: the fifty-five point nine to eighty-eight point nine gap is better treated as a floor on genuine commonsense difficulty than a precise measurement of it. Some real fraction of that gap is authentic reasoning difficulty — the conjunction and negation categories support that. Some fraction is adversarial-distractor difficulty that the SANITY check shows is separable. The paper gives you the tools to see that split; it doesn't fully resolve how big each piece is.
[S] That's a more careful claim than "models are at fifty-six percent on commonsense," which is how this number usually gets repeated.
[G] Right, and that's actually the paper's real contribution to how people should read benchmark tables generally — a single accuracy number without an ablation like SANITY next to it is a much weaker piece of evidence than it looks.
[O] Does any of this connect to how evals get built today, beyond this one dataset?
[G] Directly. Running a sanity check against your own construction method — could a model solve this by exploiting how I built the distractors, not by reasoning — is close to standard practice now for any serious benchmark release. This paper is one of the earlier public examples of a team publishing that check against themselves rather than waiting for someone else to find the hole.
[S] The flip side worth naming: this predates the large-scale-pretraining-on-everything era. It's not applicable to worry about contamination at publication time, but the dataset has since become a common ingredient in instruction-tuning and eval mixtures. Any "zero-shot" claim on CommonsenseQA against a modern model needs an explicit decontamination check this twenty-nineteen paper obviously couldn't have designed for.
[G] Agreed, that's a structural gap that opens up over time, not a flaw in the original design.
[O] One more angle before we wrap — whose commonsense is this measuring, exactly?
[G] The paper doesn't address that question directly. The workforce is Amazon Mechanical Turk, which skews toward a specific demographic, and the concepts and relations come from ConceptNet, which has its own coverage biases. The five most common question concepts are things like "person," "people," "human," "water," "cat" — fairly universal, but the social-convention questions in particular are going to reflect whoever wrote them. That's a limitation the paper simply doesn't discuss, and it's a fair one to raise.
[S] Which loops back to the ten-annotator concentration point. Same underlying worry, from a different angle.
