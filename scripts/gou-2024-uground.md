[O] Picture an AI agent that never reads a single line of the page it's looking at, no HTML, no accessibility tree, just pixels, and it still out-clicks agents that get that extra cheat sheet handed to them.
[S] Or picture the opposite read: it wins mostly on lab metrics that just grade whether it points at the right icon, and the moment you check whether the whole task actually finishes, the number falls into the twenties and thirties percent.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an AI optimist and an AI skeptic argue it out with someone who has read the paper closely. Today's paper is "Navigating the Digital World as Humans Do: Universal Visual Grounding for GUI Agents," from Boyu Gou and colleagues at Ohio State University and Orby AI, published at ICLR twenty twenty-five. Joining us is Julian, who has spent real time with this one.
[G] Glad to be here. One disclaimer up front: I'm not one of the authors, so when I say the authors show something, that's exactly what I mean, not "we."
[S] Julian, set the scene for us. Why did GUI agents lean on HTML and accessibility trees in the first place, instead of just looking at the screen the way a person does?
[G] Mostly convenience. Text gives you an explicit, structured list of clickable things, so instead of learning where to click, a planner just picks item number six off a menu. The paper's argument is that convenience is expensive and unreliable: HTML can run up to ten times more tokens than the equivalent screenshot to encode the same page, and every extra token is latency and cost that compounds across a long task.
[O] And unreliable how, exactly?
[G] Accessibility trees only work if developers annotated their app correctly, and mostly they haven't. The authors cite a survey of the top one million homepages on the web: ninety-five point nine percent had accessibility conformance errors, averaging fifty-six point eight errors per page. So the supposedly clean text-based shortcut is neither clean nor dependable at scale.
[S] Fine, that's a real cost. But dropping it entirely and going pixels-only is a big bet. What's the actual argument for why that's even possible?
[G] The paper frames it as a completeness argument. In principle, a GUI agent only needs two things: eyes that see the rendered screen, and a hand that can click or type anywhere on it. Everything else, HTML, accessibility trees, labeled candidate lists, is scaffolding for a missing capability. That missing capability, they argue, is visual grounding, turning a phrase like "click the search bar at the top of the page" into an actual pixel coordinate.
[O] So the bottleneck was never reasoning about what to do, it was literally not knowing where to put the cursor.
[G] That is the paper's central diagnosis. Prior grounding models like CogAgent and SeeClick existed, but the authors argue none cleared three bars at once: high accuracy, since one bad click derails an entire task; strong generalization across web, desktop, and mobile; and flexibility, plugging into whatever planning model you're already using instead of being welded to one system. Closing that gap is what UGround is built for.
[S] Walk us through the actual system, then. What is UGround, mechanically?
[G] It sits inside a framework the authors call SeeAct-V, adapted from an earlier agent called SeeAct. The job splits into two specialists. A planner, GPT-4 or GPT-4o in their experiments, looks only at the screenshot and the task, and writes a plan in words: which element to act on, what action, click, type, or scroll, and any value to type. UGround, the grounding model, takes that same screenshot plus the description and returns one pixel coordinate, no candidate list, no menu of HTML elements, no numbered boxes overlaid on the image.
[O] So the planner never gets to cheat by picking off a multiple-choice list.
[G] Exactly, and that matters, because those numbered-box approaches, so-called Set-of-Mark labeling, still need HTML or an accessibility tree to generate the boxes in the first place. SeeAct-V removes that dependency completely. The planner describes what it wants in plain language, roughly the way you'd describe it to another person, and UGround finds it.
[S] What's actually inside UGround? Some exotic new architecture?
[G] Deliberately not. It's a seven billion parameter LLaVA-Next model, an existing, fairly ordinary vision-language architecture, prompted with a fixed template: "In the screenshot, what are the pixel element coordinates corresponding to," then the description. It answers with raw numbers, decoded like ordinary text, no bounding-box normalization, no special output head.
[O] That's almost anticlimactic. No clever new modeling trick at all?
[G] Two adaptations, both about resolution. GUI screenshots need roughly a thousand pixels or more to stay legible, so they widen the model's image-tiling scheme to thirty-six slices, swap in a finer image encoder, and push the maximum landscape resolution to thirteen forty-four by thirteen forty-four pixels, with a longer context window to hold the extra visual tokens. Second, they drop the low-resolution global-view image that architecture normally adds, because at two hundred twenty-four pixels it's too blurry to help on a dense interface. Past that, it's the training data doing the real work.
[S] That's the part I actually want to interrogate. "The data does the work" is carrying a lot of weight in that sentence.
[G] Fair, and it's the paper's real contribution. They build the largest GUI-grounding dataset to date, ten million elements across one point three million screenshots. The bulk of it, called Web-Hybrid, is nine million elements over seven hundred seventy-three thousand screenshots, synthesized entirely from Common Crawl webpages.
[O] Why webpages specifically?
[G] Because a webpage hands you both sides for free, the rendered pixels a person sees, and the HTML that produced them. So element-to-bounding-box correspondence comes without any human labeling, and HTML attributes, inner text, alt text, aria labels, give you a rich starting point for describing each element in words.
[S] But real people don't describe interface elements the way alt text does. "Submit button" isn't how I'd tell a friend where to click.
[G] That's exactly the gap the authors identify, and it's the clever part of the pipeline. They categorize how people refer to interface elements into three types: visual, "the red icon labeled Uniqlo"; positional, "the button at the top-left corner"; and functional, "navigate back to the homepage." Prior grounding datasets leaned almost entirely on the first type. UGround's pipeline deliberately covers all three, blending curated rules for positional references with an open multimodal model that looks at each element's rendering and adds visual or functional color, phrases like "hollow heart," or world knowledge like "this blue bird icon represents Twitter." A smaller model then tidies those into concise descriptions.
[O] And this whole pipeline runs with essentially no human labeling.
[G] For Web-Hybrid, yes, rules plus two open models, no humans involved. They supplement it with a smaller GPT-4o-generated set for more open-ended phrasing, plus several existing human-annotated Android datasets. One detail worth flagging: there's no desktop training data anywhere in the mix, zero desktop screenshots.
[S] None at all? And you're about to tell me it does fine on desktop.
[G] I am, and we should sit with how strange that is before we celebrate it.
[O] Let's get to the numbers, because this is where the paper makes its case.
[G] Start with pure grounding, on a benchmark called ScreenSpot, twelve seventy-two single-step instructions across mobile, desktop, and web. In the standard setting, UGround averages seventy-three point three, against SeeClick's fifty-three point four and CogAgent's forty-seven point four. GPT-4o alone, just asked to point at pixels with no specialized training, manages eighteen point three. That's roughly a twenty-point absolute jump over the prior best specialized model.
[S] And the harder setting?
[G] The agent setting, where a planner model writes the referring expressions itself instead of a human annotator. There, UGround hits eighty-one point four against SeeClick's fifty-two point three, closer to a twenty-nine-point gap. A newer version, UGround-V1, built on a stronger backbone called Qwen2-VL instead of LLaVA, pushes the standard-setting average up to eighty-six point three.
[O] Where does it win by the widest margin?
[G] Icons and widgets, consistently, across every benchmark, the categories that need semantic or contextual understanding rather than just reading text off the screen. And it holds up on desktop despite that zero-desktop-training-data fact we just flagged.
[S] Grounding accuracy is one thing. Does any of it translate into agents actually finishing tasks?
[G] That's the agent tiers, offline on cached environment states, online on live systems. Offline, on Multimodal-Mind2Web, over a thousand real web tasks across a hundred-plus sites, SeeAct-V with UGround reaches forty-six point eight in element accuracy, ahead of an HTML-based baseline called Choice at forty-two point three, and the Set-of-Mark approach at twenty-five point six, without ever seeing the HTML those baselines get to read.
[O] So the vision-only agent beats the ones holding the answer key.
[G] On element accuracy, yes. On mobile, a benchmark called AndroidControl, it scores forty-eight point four on high-level tasks and sixty-two point four on low-level tasks, against a text-only baseline's forty-two point one and fifty-five. On desktop, OmniACT, it reaches an action score of thirty-two point eight against a specialized desktop pipeline called DetACT's seventeen.
[S] Those are all offline, cached-state numbers though. What happens live?
[G] Online is more sobering, and it's where I'd push back on any "matches humans" framing. On Mind2Web-Live, real websites, task success sits at twenty-three point one percent. On AndroidWorld, live Android emulators, it's thirty-two point eight percent with GPT-4o as the planner, against a Set-of-Mark baseline's twenty-five point four, rising to forty-four percent with the stronger UGround-V1 backbone. Both beat their baselines, but those are the honest end-to-end ceilings, not the seventy or eighty percent numbers from the grounding-only benchmark.
[O] I'd still take that trade. A task succeeding one time in three, fully autonomously, on a live app it's never seen, is not nothing.
[S] It's not nothing. It's also a long way from navigating the digital world as humans do.
[G] Two more results before we argue. A scaling curve: trained on shrinking slices of Web-Hybrid, UGround at just fifty thousand screenshots, about six hundred thousand elements, already beats SeeClick, which trained on roughly three million elements over four hundred thousand screenshots, by more than ten points. Returns flatten after about a hundred thousand screenshots. And an ablation: on Web-Hybrid alone, UGround scores seventy-six point nine in the agent setting; on the supplementary data alone, seventy-three point four; combined, eighty-one point four. Web-Hybrid is genuinely load-bearing, not a nice-to-have on top of the human data.
[O] Let me make the strong version of the case. This paper closes a wall that's stood since the first LLM-based web agents: grounding. A seven billion parameter model, trained with almost no human labeling, built mostly from Common Crawl, beats specialized grounding models by twenty to twenty-nine points, generalizes to a platform it never trained on, and lets a screenshot-only agent match or beat agents reading the HTML answer key. If grounding is genuinely solved, the assumption that agents need structured web access looks outdated within years.
[S] Now let me take it apart. First, that marquee twenty-percent number bundles together three things that aren't the recipe: a bigger training set, ten million elements versus SeeClick's three million, and, once you look at UGround-V1, a straightforwardly stronger backbone. Swapping LLaVA for Qwen2-VL alone moves the ScreenSpot average from seventy-three point three to eighty-six point three. That's a thirteen-point swing from the base model, not from anything UGround invented.
[G] Fair, and credit to the authors, they anticipated it by adding the Qwen2-VL version as a control. But the honest reading of "twenty percent better than SeeClick" isn't a clean ablation of the data recipe alone, it's data, architecture, and scale, stacked together. There's a related issue: most baseline numbers in the tables are lifted straight from earlier papers rather than re-run under one shared planner and prompt. The Mind2Web Set-of-Mark comparison, for instance, is measured on a thirty-task subset of each split, not the full test set UGround is scored on.
[O] That's a real methodology gap. But even granting all of it, the online, live-environment numbers are new runs, not borrowed tables, and UGround still wins there.
[S] It does win there. My bigger complaint is what the headline conceals. Those live numbers are twenty-three percent and thirty-two to forty-four percent task success. The paper's own error analysis says planning mistakes, not grounding mistakes, now dominate failures on almost every benchmark. So the paper's own data says the bottleneck it set out to solve has moved, not vanished, and it moved somewhere UGround doesn't touch, the planner.
[G] Also true, and it cuts against the vision-only story a little. Desktop is the one place that flips closer to even: forty-six point five percent of ScreenSpot-Desktop failures are still grounding errors, versus twenty-seven point seven percent on mobile, driven by exactly the long-tail icon a training set built entirely from consumer webpages would never see, a stylized dollar sign for the Zelle app, two cartoon figures for an Outlook contact list. So "works on desktop despite zero desktop training data" is true, and desktop is simultaneously where the model breaks most often.
[O] To be fair, the authors say this themselves, their limitations section flags no desktop training data as a known gap.
[S] One more, then I'll let it go. The entire case for going vision-only rests on an efficiency argument, HTML costing up to ten times the tokens of a screenshot. That number is asserted, cited from a different paper, never measured here. For a method whose whole motivation is "the text-based approach is too expensive," that's a strange thing to leave unquantified.
[G] Also correct, and it's on my list of what would raise my confidence: rerun every baseline under one shared planner and prompt, report wall-clock time and cost alongside accuracy, and add a contamination check between Common Crawl and ScreenSpot, since ScreenSpot is small, only twelve seventy-two items, and already close to saturated, UGround-V1 at seventy-two billion parameters reaches eighty-nine point four. None of that erases the core result, it just means "solved" is too strong a word; "no longer the binding constraint" is closer to what the evidence supports.
[O] So where does this leave the field practically, Julian?
[G] If you accept the core result, even the qualified version, it reframes what to build next. Instead of pouring effort into scraping and cleaning HTML or accessibility trees for every new platform, you invest in the planner's reasoning, now the dominant failure mode by the paper's own accounting. And a grounding model that plugs into any planner, rather than being welded to one, is a useful building block regardless of how the backbone-confound argument shakes out.
[S] There's an evaluation-practice angle too, worth flagging for anyone building agent benchmarks. This paper is a clean case study in why element accuracy and end-to-end task success can tell opposite stories, and a paper reporting only the first would be easy to over-trust. Anyone reading an agent paper should ask which of the two they're looking at, and why.
[G] The authors are explicit about that trade-off themselves. On Multimodal-Mind2Web they say outright that they omit operation scores because they're, quote, orthogonal to grounding comparisons. That's defensible for isolating one variable, but a reader has to notice it's being made.
[G] If I leave you with one line from the paper itself, it's this: grounding was the wall, and a plain seven billion parameter model with the right data clears it, without a single desktop screenshot in training.
[O] My takeaway: the fact that pixels-only can match or beat HTML-based agents at all is the real headline, and I think it ages well as backbones keep improving under it.
[S] Mine: don't let a twenty-point grounding jump distract you from twenty-three percent live task success, the interesting number in this paper is the one saying the bottleneck moved to planning, not the one sitting at the top of the leaderboard.
