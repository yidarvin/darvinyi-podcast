[O] Here is the sentence that should stop you mid-scroll. Take one pretrained language model, teach it to follow instructions on tasks it has seen, and it starts following instructions on tasks it has never seen at all.
[S] And here is the sentence right next to it that nobody quotes as often. Do that exact same procedure on a smaller model, and it gets worse at those unseen tasks. Not neutral. Worse.
[O] Which is the more interesting result, honestly. Everyone remembers this paper for beating GPT-3. I think the real headline is buried in an ablation chart.
[S] Today we find out whether "instruction tuning" is a clean, general recipe, or a phenomenon this one paper caught at exactly one scale, with exactly one dataset mixture, and got a little lucky with the framing.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a guest scholar take apart one paper from the litsearch dot darvinyi dot com atlas. I'm your optimist host.
[S] And I'm the skeptic. Today's paper is "Finetuned Language Models Are Zero-Shot Learners," by Jason Wei, Maarten Bosma, Vincent Zhao, and Kelvin Guu as co-first authors, with Adams Wei Yu, Brian Lester, Nan Du, Andrew Dai, and Quoc Le, out of Google Research. Posted in 2021, published at ICLR 2022.
[O] Joining us is Sasha, who knows this paper's ablation section better than most people know their own resume. Sasha, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. This is one of those papers where the title becomes the name of an entire era of language modeling, and it's worth remembering it started as a fairly modest ablation study.
[S] Let's set up why anyone needed this. What was broken before instruction tuning?
[G] GPT-3 had just shown the field that a large enough language model is a genuinely strong few-shot learner. Give it a handful of examples in the prompt and it generalizes well. But its zero-shot numbers, no examples, just an instruction, were noticeably worse on reading comprehension, question answering, and natural language inference. The authors' diagnosis is about format, not knowledge. A raw pretrained model has only ever seen continuous web text. Hand it something like "does the premise entail the hypothesis," which doesn't occur naturally on the internet, and it's confused about what's even being asked.
[O] So the model might know the answer, it just doesn't know it's being asked a question.
[G] That's the core intuition. And the existing playbooks each traded something away. Pretrain-then-finetune, the BERT and T5 approach, gets a strong specialist, but needs labeled data per task and one model per task. Prompting, the GPT-3 approach, keeps one general model, but leans entirely on exemplars or hand-engineered prompts. The authors wanted one general model that generalizes to instructions for tasks it was never explicitly finetuned on.
[S] Which sounds almost too simple to be the whole idea. What did they actually do?
[G] It's deliberately simple, which is part of why it landed. Take a pretrained model called LaMDA-PT, a decoder-only transformer at a hundred thirty-seven billion parameters, pretrained on two point four nine trillion tokens of web text, dialog, and Wikipedia, about ten percent of it non-English. This is the plain language-model checkpoint, not the version later finetuned for dialog as LaMDA proper. Then they finetune it on sixty-two existing NLP datasets, each one rewritten as a natural-language instruction. They call the result FLAN, for Finetuned Language Net.
[O] And "rewritten as an instruction" is doing a lot of work in that sentence.
[G] It is. They sorted the sixty-two datasets into twelve task clusters by type: natural language inference, reading comprehension, closed-book question answering, commonsense reasoning, sentiment, paraphrase, coreference, translation across eight language pairs, structured-data-to-text, summarization, and so on. For every dataset, they manually wrote ten different instruction templates, so the model sees the same task phrased many different ways: "does the premise entail the hypothesis," "based on the paragraph above, can we conclude that," "read the following and determine if." A few of the ten deliberately flip the task around, so for sentiment classification some templates ask the model to generate a positive or negative review instead of classifying one.
[S] Why bother with ten templates instead of just picking the best one?
[G] Because the goal isn't to memorize one phrasing, it's to learn that many phrasings point at the same underlying task. At training time, each example gets wrapped in a randomly chosen template from its dataset's ten. Under the hood it's ordinary supervised finetuning, cross-entropy loss on the target text given the instruction-wrapped input.
[O] What's the compute bill for this?
[G] Small, relative to pretraining. Thirty thousand gradient steps, batch size of eight thousand tokens, the Adafactor optimizer, learning rate of three times ten to the minus five. The whole run takes about sixty hours on a hundred twenty-eight core TPU v3 pod, under two percent of the compute that pretrained the base model.
[S] Okay, so far this is a recipe. Where's the actual scientific claim, the part that could be wrong?
[G] Right here, and it's the most important design decision in the paper. How do you honestly test whether the model generalizes to a task it "hasn't seen"? Some prior work defined unseen loosely, just meaning the exact dataset wasn't in training. This paper is stricter: unseen at the cluster level. To evaluate zero-shot NLI, you don't just exclude the NLI datasets, you exclude every dataset from the entire NLI cluster during instruction tuning, then finetune a fresh checkpoint on everything else, and only then test it. Reporting zero-shot results across ten task types means training ten separate checkpoints, each holding out a different cluster.
[O] That's expensive to do properly, and it's exactly what a lazier paper would have skipped.
[G] It's a real cost they paid for rigor. Worth flagging early: there is no single downloadable "FLAN" model that produces every number in the paper. Each zero-shot result comes from a different held-out checkpoint.
[S] Let's get to results. What's the headline number?
[G] With the best dev-set template selected per dataset, zero-shot FLAN beats zero-shot GPT-3 at a hundred seventy-five billion parameters on twenty of twenty-five evaluated datasets. On ten of those it even beats GPT-3's few-shot performance, not just zero-shot, including large margins on ANLI, RTE, BoolQ, the AI2 reasoning challenge, OpenBookQA, and StoryCloze.
[O] Twenty of twenty-five against a much larger, well-known model. That's the number quoted everywhere.
[S] And I want to flag immediately, it's not a clean comparison. GPT-3 is a different model, different size, different pretraining data. If FLAN beats it, is that instruction tuning, or just a different model being different?
[G] Exactly the right question, and to the authors' credit, they build the clean comparison themselves and foreground it rather than bury it. They evaluate LaMDA-PT, the identical hundred thirty-seven billion parameter checkpoint, before instruction tuning, prompted the exact same way as GPT-3 with no instructions. Same weights, same pretraining, the only variable is whether instruction tuning happened. On three representative clusters — NLI, reading comprehension, closed-book QA — GPT-3 zero-shot scores forty-two point nine, sixty-three point seven, and forty-nine point eight. GPT-3 few-shot: fifty-three point two, seventy-two point six, fifty-five point seven. Zero-shot FLAN: fifty-six point two, seventy-seven point four, fifty-six point six, ahead of GPT-3's few-shot numbers on all three, with zero exemplars.
[O] Beating few-shot GPT-3 with zero shots is the part that made people sit up.
[S] Does it hold everywhere, or is that cherry-picked terrain?
[G] Not universal, and the paper says so plainly. Against the sparse mixture-of-experts model GLaM, at sixty-four billion parameters, zero-shot FLAN wins on thirteen of nineteen comparable datasets. On tasks already close to plain language modeling, commonsense reasoning and coreference posed as finishing a sentence, instructions are largely redundant, and FLAN only beats untuned LaMDA-PT on three of seven such tasks. Translation is asymmetric: FLAN beats zero-shot GPT-3 on all six translation evaluations and is competitive with supervised systems translating into English, but noticeably weaker translating out of English, tracking with an English-only tokenizer and overwhelmingly English pretraining data.
[O] So the pattern is: instructions help exactly where the task doesn't already look like ordinary text continuation.
[G] That's the authors' own framing, and the ablations back it up directly. This is where I think the paper is actually strongest, because they don't stop at the headline, they go interrogate their own mechanism.
[S] Let's do these one at a time. Start with how many task clusters you need.
[G] They hold out three clusters for evaluation, NLI, closed-book QA, and commonsense, and sweep from one to seven instruction-tuning clusters used in training, adding clusters in decreasing order of dataset count. Average accuracy on the three held-out clusters climbs from forty-nine point nine with one cluster to sixty-three point five with seven, basically monotonically, with no sign of flattening. The authors' implication is that more task diversity would keep helping past seven clusters, they just didn't test further.
[O] That's a genuinely optimistic result. The ceiling isn't nearby.
[S] Or they haven't found the ceiling yet, which isn't quite the same claim. Go on.
[G] Second ablation, and I think this is the one that actually proves the mechanism rather than describing it. Is the gain from instructions specifically, or just from training on more tasks in general? They test two stripped-down setups: finetuning with no template at all, just raw input and output pairs, and finetuning with the input prepended by a bare dataset name instead of a natural instruction, something like "Translation: WMT-14 to French." Compare those to FLAN's real instructions, averaged over four held-out clusters. No template: thirty-seven point three. Dataset name only: forty-seven point zero. Natural instructions, FLAN's actual recipe: fifty-five point two.
[O] So more than half the gap between "no signal at all" and FLAN comes specifically from the instructions being natural language, not from the multitask training itself.
[G] Right, that's the authors' conclusion: training with actual instructions is what's doing the work, not just exposure to more datasets. It's the cleanest piece of evidence in the paper for what instruction tuning actually is.
[S] Now the one you flagged at the top. The scaling result.
[G] The big one. Same held-out cluster setup, but they sweep model size: four hundred twenty-two million, two billion, eight billion, sixty-eight billion, and a hundred thirty-seven billion parameters, each compared against its own untuned baseline on thirteen held-out tasks. For the two large models, instruction tuning substantially improves held-out performance. But at eight billion parameters and below, instruction tuning actively hurts held-out performance, relative to the untuned model of the same size.
[O] Actively hurts. Not "doesn't help as much."
[G] Actively hurts, "thought-provoking" is the paper's own word for it. Their hypothesized explanation is capacity: a small model finetuned on roughly forty instruction-tuning tasks may spend its entire capacity memorizing those tasks, leaving nothing to generalize with. A large model has spare capacity to both learn the training tasks and pick up the general skill of following instructions.
[S] "Hypothesized" is doing a lot of quiet work there. Did they test the capacity story, or just propose it?
[G] Propose it, and the paper is honest that it's a plausible mechanism, not a demonstrated one. It also turned out to be recipe-specific rather than a fundamental law. Concurrent work on a model called T0, at eleven billion parameters, and the later FLAN-T5 line, both showed real instruction-following gains at far smaller scale with a better task mixture. So "you need on the order of a hundred billion parameters" was true of this recipe, not a hard floor on the idea itself.
[O] Which vindicates the optimist case a little. The floor moved down as soon as people iterated on the mixture.
[S] It also means the paper's most-quoted caveat was less fundamental than it looked. I'll call that a point for both of us.
[G] Two more results, fast. Adding few-shot exemplars on top of instruction tuning helps every cluster further, especially ones with complex outputs like translation and struct-to-text, and shrinks variance across templates. And instruction-tuned FLAN is a much better starting point for prompt tuning than the untuned model, with over ten percent improvement in the low-resource, thirty-two-example setting.
[O] Alright, the actual debate. My case: this paper demonstrates a real, reproducible phenomenon. A model can be taught the general behavior of "read an instruction, do the task," and that skill transfers to task types it was never finetuned on, verified with a genuinely conservative cluster-holdout protocol most papers wouldn't bother building. The instructions-versus-dataset-name ablation is airtight evidence for the mechanism. This is the direct ancestor of every "instruct" model shipped since. That's a foundational contribution by any standard.
[S] My deflationary case. The headline, twenty of twenty-five against GPT-3, is a cross-model comparison confounded by size and pretraining data, and the paper itself supplies the cleaner within-model number, so that headline does more marketing than science. Second, twenty-of-twenty-five uses the best of ten templates, selected on a dev set. The mean-over-templates number, the "typical instruction" proxy, is lower, and template variance isn't small. A practitioner writing one prompt gets the mean, not the cherry-picked max. Third, "unseen" means an unseen academic task cluster, all sixty-two datasets are standard NLP benchmarks. Real and useful, but not yet the open-ended, arbitrary-user-request behavior "instruction-following" came to mean once InstructGPT arrived a year later.
[G] Let me adjudicate those, because they don't all land the same. On the cross-model confound, point to the skeptic, and the authors would agree, they built the LaMDA-PT control precisely because they knew the GPT-3 comparison alone wasn't sufficient, and they foreground it rather than hide it. On template selection, also fair, the paper reports both numbers, so a careful reader isn't misled, but a headline reader skimming the abstract is. On "unseen means unseen academic task type," I'd call that accurate description, not a flaw. The paper never claims open-ended instruction-following, it explicitly cites InstructGPT as a parallel, later effort aimed at that broader target.
[O] Where does that leave contamination, usually the first thing a skeptic reaches for?
[G] Addressed directly, replicating GPT-3's n-gram overlap analysis. DROP, SQuAD v2, and ANLI rounds one and two showed heavy overlap in passage context, but almost none in actual answers, five out of eleven thousand one hundred fifty-three SQuAD v2 examples with answer overlap. For 2021, that's an honest, reasonably careful check, caveats about conservative n-gram matching acknowledged rather than omitted.
[S] What would have raised your confidence further?
[G] Testing the capacity-saturation story directly rather than proposing it. Surfacing template variance in the main results, not the appendix. And evaluating on instruction types beyond academic benchmark phrasing, ahead of the question InstructGPT ended up answering instead.
[O] If I had to state the optimist's bottom line: this is the paper that proved a large enough model can learn the general skill of following an instruction, not just imitating one, and that single idea reshaped how every subsequent large model gets trained.
[S] Mine: trust the within-model ablation and the instructions-versus-dataset-name result, they're rock solid. Be more skeptical of the twenty-of-twenty-five GPT-3 headline than most citations of this paper are, and remember the hundred-billion-parameter floor was a property of this recipe, not a law of nature.
[G] And the line I'd leave a listener with, going a small step past what the paper itself claims: the most durable lesson here isn't the score against GPT-3, it's that the recipe was simple enough, sixty existing datasets, ten hand-written templates each, an ordinary finetune, that within a year, better versions of the same idea at much smaller scale made "instruction tuning" a standard step rather than a research finding.
