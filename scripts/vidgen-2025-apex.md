[S] The paper is "The AI Productivity Index: APEX-v1-extended," from Bertie Vidgen and colleagues at Mercor, with contributions from Harvard Law's Cass Sunstein and Scripps Research's Eric Topol.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is the extended technical report on APEX, a benchmark Mercor first released in September twenty twenty-five, and it is one of the more deliberate attempts I have seen to grade frontier models on the kind of work that actually pays a salary.
[O] So what is the actual gap here? We already have a hundred benchmarks.
[G] We do, but the authors' complaint is that most of them are what they call scholastic: multiple choice exams, math competitions, contest coding. Clean to grade, easy to put on a leaderboard, and almost entirely disconnected from what a professional is paid to produce.
[S] That is a fair complaint. A model acing a bar exam question and a model drafting a bar-quality contract redline are not the same skill.
[G] Exactly the distinction APEX is built around. The authors do credit a couple of honest predecessors, OpenAI's GDPval and Harvey's BigLaw Bench, but say the field is still mostly missing benchmarks that test whether a model can deliver real economic value, the kind that shows up as productivity for an actual human worker.
[O] So instead of a question and an answer key, you get a job.
[G] Four jobs, specifically: investment banking associate, management consultant, big law associate, and primary care physician. Each one produces a discrete artifact, a valuation, a market-sizing memo, a contract redline, a clinical plan, where a wrong number or a missed clause has consequences a multiple-choice score never captures.
[S] And this report specifically is the second generation of that idea.
[G] Right, APEX-v1-extended. The original APEX-v1 launched with fifty held-out cases per job, two hundred total. This version doubles that to a hundred per job, four hundred total, and rebuilds the grading pipeline underneath it. It is not a new benchmark, it is a hardened one.
[O] Hardened is the right word, because the interesting story here is not just the score, it is how paranoid they got about making the score trustworthy.
[S] Let's get into that, because "expert-graded" is a phrase that gets abused constantly. Who actually wrote these tasks?
[G] Real practitioners, sourced through Mercor's own talent platform, not crowdworkers. Candidates go through a thirty to forty-five minute interview, and the ones who pass get paid to complete a one to two hour assessment that specifically tests whether they can write good prompts and good grading rubrics. Only then are they contracted onto the project.
[O] That is a real bar. What is the pool size?
[G] It grew from seventy-six contributors on the original APEX to a hundred and thirty-seven for this extended version, with a mean of over seven years of industry experience. These are working bankers, lawyers, consultants, and physicians building tasks out of their own day-to-day work.
[S] So what does one task actually look like?
[G] Three parts. A realistic prompt drawn from the expert's real work, a set of source documents the model has to reason over, PDFs, spreadsheets, Word documents, plain text, CSVs, up to a combined one hundred thousand tokens, parsed through a tool called Reducto, and a prompt-specific grading rubric. Nothing paywalled or license-restricted is allowed as a source.
[O] The time estimate on these tasks tells you they are not toy problems.
[G] The authors estimate the mean real-world time to actually complete one at two point seven hours, ranging as high as twenty hours for the heaviest cases. Both the prompt and its rubric go through multiple rounds of human review before a case is accepted into the set.
[S] Now the part I actually want to interrogate: the rubric. How do you grade something as fuzzy as quality without it turning into vibes?
[G] Their answer is to decompose quality into discrete, testable claims, the paper's own analogy is unit tests for code. Each criterion is an objective, self-contained statement about the response that can be marked pass or fail. A model's score on a task is just the fraction of criteria it satisfies. In this extended version the mean is fourteen point eight one criteria per case, actually down from twenty-nine point oh nine in the original version, because they trimmed redundant criteria and added leaner new cases.
[O] And who is doing the actual grading? Please tell me it is not the same models being tested.
[G] It is a language-model judge, and this is one of three deliberate changes in this technical report. In the original APEX, a panel of judge models graded each response. Here they have switched to a single judge, Gemini two point five Flash, which they say is faster, cheaper, and more transparent. Each criterion gets fed to the judge along with the model's response, and the judge returns a pass or fail with a short justification.
[S] A single judge. Hold that thought, I have opinions.
[G] I imagine you do. The other two changes: every prompt now goes to each model eight times instead of three, so they can report a mean score with ninety-five percent confidence intervals instead of a three-sample median. And third, every prompt has an appended instruction forcing a single-turn response, telling the model not to ask follow-up questions, just to produce a complete deliverable from the brief and the attached files alone.
[O] That last one is interesting, because plenty of frontier models are specifically trained to ask clarifying questions when a brief is ambiguous.
[G] Which the authors acknowledge, it is why they had to force it. Without that instruction, a chunk of responses would come back as questions instead of deliverables, and you cannot grade a question against a rubric built for a finished answer.
[S] File that away, I am coming back to it.
[G] One more piece worth noting: the four hundred graded cases stay private, a closed held-out set. But Mercor also open-sourced a separate hundred-case devset, twenty-five per job, under a CC-BY license, along with the full evaluation harness, so outsiders can actually inspect the format.
[O] Okay, numbers. Who won?
[G] Ten frontier models, from Anthropic, Google DeepMind, OpenAI, and xAI, all tested with thinking turned on and set to high where configurable, temperature at zero point seven, responses collected at the end of November twenty twenty-five. GPT-5 with thinking set to high comes out on top overall, at sixty-seven percent of criteria met.
[S] Sixty-seven percent. So the best model in the world, tested on real work from real professionals, fails a third of the bar.
[G] That is the paper's own framing too, their conclusion is that frontier models still have substantial limitations on everyday professional tasks. Behind GPT-5, Gemini 3 Pro with thinking high scores sixty-four point three percent, and then a tight cluster: o3 and Grok 4 both at sixty-three point five percent, Opus four point five right behind at sixty-three point one percent.
[O] And it is not uniform across the four jobs.
[G] Far from it. Law is the most tractable job, the top score there is seventy-seven point nine percent, from GPT-5. Medicine tops out at sixty-five point five percent, also GPT-5. Management consulting tops out at sixty-four percent, from Gemini 3 Pro. And investment banking is the hardest of the four, even the best model there, Gemini 3 Pro again, only reaches sixty-three percent.
[S] Investment banking being hardest is notable, given it is probably the most quantitative of the four jobs.
[G] It is, and I will flag that it connects to something worth coming back to. Despite that spread in difficulty, model rank order holds up fairly consistently across all four jobs, GPT-5 and Gemini 3 Pro are near the top almost everywhere, Opus four point one is near the bottom almost everywhere.
[O] Is the gap between the top models real, or is that noise?
[G] Genuinely real at the top, and this is where the statistics get careful. A Friedman omnibus test puts the overall difference between models at a p-value under one in a million. Running paired t-tests across all forty-five model pairs, with a Bonferroni correction for multiple comparisons, thirty-five of those forty-five comparisons, seventy-eight percent, stay significant.
[S] Which leaves twenty-two percent of model pairs statistically indistinguishable from each other.
[G] Correct, and that matters because the mean standard deviation across the eight runs per model is nine point oh five percentage points, ranging from eight point two five for Opus four point five up to nine point five for Grok 4. That is wider than the gap between several adjacent models on the leaderboard.
[O] There is also a re-ranking trick in here I found genuinely interesting, the z-score analysis.
[G] Right, because raw score does not tell you how a model does on the hardest tasks specifically. Z-scores measure how many standard deviations above the field a model sits, task by task. By that measure GPT-5 scores zero point five zero, clearly ahead of the field. But Opus four point five jumps from fifth place on the raw leaderboard to second place on z-score, at zero point two eight, meaning it is disproportionately strong specifically on the hardest cases. Opus four point one, at the other end, sits at negative zero point seven seven.
[S] And the public devset, does it reproduce the private leaderboard, or is this whole thing built on a set nobody outside Mercor can check?
[G] They ran the same ten models, same eight-run methodology, against the hundred-case open devset. GPT-5 stays in first on both, sixty-seven percent on the private set, sixty-five point seven percent on the public one, and most ranks barely move. But three models, GPT-5.1, Gemini two point five Flash, and Sonnet four point five, each score more than ten percentage points lower on the public set than on the private one.

This is a benchmark built by domain experts, source-grounded in real documents, graded against explicit criteria instead of vague notions of helpfulness, with confidence intervals and a real significance test instead of a bare leaderboard. [S] Second issue: forced single-turn. The instruction explicitly bars models from asking clarifying questions, and investment banking, the hardest job on the leaderboard by a wide margin, is exactly where a real associate would push back on an ambiguous brief before building a model. The harness structurally cannot reward that behavior, on the one job where it may matter most.
[G] The authors are open that they added the instruction specifically because models were producing questions instead of deliverables, which the rubric format cannot grade. It is a real trade-off, not an oversight, but it does mean the benchmark cannot currently tell "the model does not know how" apart from "the model was not allowed to ask."
[O] Last point in my favor: the private held-out set. Four hundred cases nobody outside Mercor can see is exactly what stops these tasks from leaking into the next round of pretraining data.
[S] Sure, but it cuts both ways, it also means nobody outside Mercor can audit whether the grading is fair. That could be sampling noise, or it could be exposure effects on the public data. The paper does not have the evidence to tell us which.
[G] I would score that one as open. Flagged honestly, but unresolved.
[O] Stepping back, if this holds up, what actually changes?
[G] For evaluation practice specifically, I think the honest takeaway is that rubric decomposition, turning quality into a checklist of pass-fail claims, is a genuinely useful pattern, and it is spreading. The paper itself cites related work doing similar rubric-style grading, like HealthBench. The open question APEX raises for the whole category is whether a single, unvalidated judge model is an acceptable cost for speed.
[S] For anyone actually deploying these models in banking, law, consulting, or medicine, the practical read is: strong drafting assistant, not an autonomous professional. Two-thirds of the bar with no critical-error accounting is not a number you would staff against.
