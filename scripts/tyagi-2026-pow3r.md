Every baseline in this paper confidently names a station off in the corner instead, and even invents train-line connections that aren't on the map.
[S] And the tempting story is that the new method here taught the model to see better. It didn't, not directly. It taught the training signal to stop wasting itself on rubric checkboxes the model had already nailed, or could never possibly pass, so more of the reward gradient landed on the checkboxes still actually in play, like naming the right station.
[O] Which sounds like a technicality until you see the scoreboard. This reweighting wins twenty-four of thirty head-to-head comparisons against every baseline the authors tested, and hits the same training plateau two and a half to four times faster.

[S] I'm the skeptic, she's the optimist, and today we're covering Not Every Rubric Teaches Equally, Policy-Aware Rubric Rewards for RLVR, out of Scale AI, led by Utkarsh Tyagi and colleagues.
[O] And to help us get the mechanics right, and referee where the evidence actually lands, we've brought in Roman, who's spent real time inside this paper's rubrics and its training curves. Roman, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. This is a paper that's easy to summarize in one sentence, and much harder to fully believe in one sentence, which is exactly the kind of paper worth taking apart carefully.
[S] Roman, start with the setup. Why do we even need rubrics here — isn't reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards already the recipe that made DeepSeek's R1 and everything after it work?
[G] It is, when the target collapses onto one checkable outcome, a math answer, a passing unit test. But the authors point out most valuable behaviors don't collapse that way. Long-form medical advice, scientific writing, coding help, visually grounded reasoning — a good answer there has to be factually correct, complete, faithful to the evidence, well formatted, and on-instruction, all at the same time. That's vector-valued quality, not a single score.
[O] So a rubric is how you turn that vector into something gradable.
[G] Right. A rubric decomposes one response into a list of prompt-specific criteria, and an LLM judge scores each one independently, pass or fail. The problem the authors zero in on is what happens next. GRPO, the reinforcement learning algorithm actually doing the training, still needs one number per response. Every rubric criterion eventually has to get folded into a single scalar reward.
[S] And the obvious way to fold a list of weighted criteria into one number is to just add them up, weighted by how important a human said each one is.
[G] That's exactly the standard approach, and the authors' claim is it smuggles in an assumption nobody examines: that a criterion's human-assigned weight tells you both how much it should matter in the final answer, and how useful it currently is for teaching the model. Those are not the same question.
[O] Walk us through why that gap actually breaks training, not just the philosophy of it.
[G] It comes down to how GRPO turns reward into a gradient. For a given prompt, GRPO samples a batch of responses, scores each one, and then standardizes across the batch, subtracting the group's mean reward and dividing by the group's standard deviation. That standardized number is what actually moves the model.
[S] So if a rubric criterion behaves the same way on every response in the batch, everybody passes it, or nobody does—
[G] —then it adds the exact same constant to every response's reward, and a constant washes out completely once you subtract the mean. Zero gradient, no matter how many points a human assigned that criterion. The authors call a criterion like that saturated if everyone passes, dead if no one does. Only a criterion where verdicts differ across the batch, what they call mixed, can actually move the needle.
[O] How big a slice of a rubric is stuck in that dead zone in practice?
[G] They measured it directly, on two frozen base models across both datasets, before any training happens. Seventeen to twenty-six percent of criteria are already saturated for a fresh policy, and twenty to thirty-three percent are dead. Add those together and static weighting is routing something like forty-five to fifty-one percent of its within-category training pressure onto criteria that literally cannot teach the model anything right now.
[S] And presumably that's concentrated in the low-stakes, nobody-cares criteria, so it's not actually costing you much.
[G] That's the intuitive guess, and it's wrong. Human weight and rollout variance are essentially uncorrelated in their data. Roughly half of the highest-weight criteria, the ones a person flagged as most important, already have zero variance for the current policy. Static weighting tells you what should matter in the final answer. It tells you nothing about what can currently teach the model.
[O] So what's the fix, mechanically?
[G] The framework is called POW3R, for policy-aware rubric reward, and the elegant part is what it doesn't touch. The rubric itself, the judge, and the human weights all stay exactly as authored. Nothing about the evaluation target changes. What changes is purely how credit gets divided up before it goes into GRPO's standardization step.
[S] Two things I want before we go further: what actually gets computed, and does it need any change to the optimizer.
[G] No optimizer change, and that's a real practical selling point, it's a drop-in swap of the reward function. Mechanically it's two layers. First, a category-normalized baseline. Criteria are grouped into categories, for the multimodal dataset that's things like visual perception, visual reasoning, content completeness, and each category's contribution gets divided by its own total weight, then averaged across the categories present for that prompt. That alone fixes a smaller, more boring bug: a category with a dozen criteria shouldn't drown out a category with two, just because it has more line items.
[O] That sounds like a fix you'd want regardless of the fancier idea.
[G] It is, and it's worth remembering, because the paper reports it as its own separate baseline, category-balanced, and we'll come back to why that matters for reading the results honestly. The second layer is the actual policy-aware part. After each training epoch, for every criterion, the framework looks at the pass rate across that epoch's rollouts and computes its variance, pass rate times one minus pass rate. That variance is exactly the mixed-versus-saturated-versus-dead signal from the diagnostic, turned into a live number.
[S] And that number becomes a multiplier on the human weight?
[G] Right, with safety features built in. The raw variance gets smoothed, normalized against the category's own weighted average variance, blended partway toward one so it never swings wildly on one epoch's noise, then clipped into a fixed band, roughly two-thirds on the low end, one and a half on the high end, and smoothed again across epochs with an exponential moving average. The clipping is the design choice I'd flag as the smart part. A dead or saturated criterion never gets driven all the way to zero weight. It keeps a floor.
[O] Why does that floor matter so much?
[G] Because otherwise you'd be building a method that could permanently write off a criterion the moment the current policy happens to nail it or fail it, and policies aren't static. A criterion that's saturated at epoch ten could become genuinely contested again after ten more epochs of the model improving elsewhere. The floor keeps the door open for that.
[S] Does any of this actually show up as an effect on the reward before you even get to full training runs?
[G] Yes, and this is the part I find most convincing methodologically, because it's checked independently of the downstream benchmark numbers. They ran an offline replay and found the reweighting moves eight to twelve percentage points of training pressure off zero-signal criteria and onto contrastive ones, and it widens the spread of raw rollout rewards, before standardization, by twenty-five to thirty-seven percent. Fewer prompts land in that tied, zero-standard-deviation regime where the whole batch produces no gradient at all. And it isn't just an aggregate artifact, the same relationship holds prompt by prompt, across more than a thousand individual diagnostic runs they checked.
[O] One thing I'm curious about. Rubrics don't just reward good behavior, some of them dock points for bad behavior. Does the math still work if a weight is negative?
[G] Good catch, and HealthBench actually has exactly that, physician-authored criteria with signed point values, positive for a desired behavior, negative for something unsafe. POW3R's normalization assumes non-negative weights throughout, so for training only, they rewrite every negative-weight criterion into its positive mirror image, flip what the judge checks for so passing means avoiding the bad behavior, and use the absolute value of the weight. Evaluation never sees that rewrite, the reported HealthBench numbers always run through HealthBench's own original scoring script, signed weights and all. It's a small piece of engineering, but it's the kind of detail that tells you they actually tried to break their own method on a real, messy dataset rather than only the one they built to fit it.
[S] Okay, let's get to the scoreboard. What's the actual experimental setup?
[G] Five reward constructions, compared apples to apples under the identical GRPO recipe. The untrained base model, a sparse binary reward that only pays out if every required criterion passes, the static weighted sum, the category-balanced version, and POW3R. Six base policies total, three vision-language models on the multimodal dataset, three text-only models on HealthBench, each setting averaged over three separate training runs.
[O] And the headline?
[G] POW3R wins the best score on twenty-four of thirty base-policy-by-metric comparisons. It sweeps every rubric-reward and strict-completion column on the multimodal test set, and every overall-reward column on HealthBench. Take one concrete row: the four billion parameter Qwen vision-language model. The base model scores forty-two point six percent mean rubric reward. The static weighted sum gets it to forty-seven point one. Category-balancing alone gets to forty-seven point nine. POW3R reaches forty-eight point eight. On strict completion, meaning every required criterion satisfied with no partial credit, it's fourteen point seven percent for the base model, climbing to twenty point two for POW3R, against seventeen point nine for static and eighteen point seven for category-balanced.
[S] Where does it actually lose?
[G] The six misses split two ways. Four sit on external vision-language benchmarks the model isn't directly trained against, and across those, POW3R still takes fourteen of eighteen individual comparisons outright, sweeping columns like the instruction-following and spatial-reasoning benchmarks across all three base policies. The four it loses are one hallucination benchmark and one object-perception benchmark on the smallest model, and a math-reasoning benchmark on the two larger ones, each by a few tenths of a point. The other two misses are on HealthBench's strict perfect-score column, on two of the three base policies, trailing the static or category-balanced reward by roughly a tenth or two of a percentage point.
[O] Is the ordering at least consistent, or does it flip around depending on which model you use?
[G] Remarkably consistent. Across all six base-policy-and-dataset combinations, the ranking is always POW3R ahead of category-balanced, ahead of static scalar, ahead of the untrained base, never once out of order, and the smallest POW3R gain over base, in the worst case, is still three point seven percentage points. There's also a Pareto-dominance result worth flagging. Plot mean rubric reward against strict completion, and POW3R sits at the best point on both axes at once, for every base policy, ahead of all four alternatives. A method can raise the average score just by getting a little better everywhere while still leaving plenty of prompts with one failed required criterion. POW3R's gains are actually converting into fully satisfied rubrics, not just marginal partial credit.
[S] And the category breakdown, is it winning everywhere equally, or is one category doing all the work?
[G] It leads on every rubric category from essentially the first training checkpoint onward. The biggest gaps are on categories with the most rollout variance to exploit, visual perception, visual reasoning, truthfulness, content completeness, instruction following. On writing style, the three trained methods stay within about a point of each other, because the base policy already passes most style criteria before training even starts, so there's very little contrast left for POW3R to redirect pressure toward. That's not a weakness, that's the mechanism working exactly as designed, where there's no disagreement to exploit, it collapses back to static baseline behavior.
[O] You mentioned training two and a half to four times faster earlier. Where does that number come from?
[G] They tracked the first validation checkpoint where each method crosses a fixed reward threshold, on the four billion parameter Qwen vision-language model. To hit a validation reward of forty-six percent, POW3R gets there at step eighty-three. The static weighted sum needs step two hundred forty-nine. Category-balancing needs step three hundred thirty-two, worse than static at that particular threshold, interestingly. That's a four times speed-up over static. Across different thresholds the speed-up ranges from two and a half to four times, and POW3R is the only one of the three that ever crosses a fifty percent validation reward at all within their six hundred sixty-four step training budget. The other two never get there.
[S] Same compute budget throughout, nothing about a different learning rate hiding in there?
[G] Same GRPO recipe, same prompt budget, same checkpoint schedule for every method. The only thing that changes between runs is how the reward gets aggregated before it hits the group standardization step.

[G] The reframing generalizes past rubrics specifically.
