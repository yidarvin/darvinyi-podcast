[O] There are two of them mentioned in this report, and the comparison quietly swaps between them table to table.
[S] Which decides whether "matches a model five times its size" is a real result or a sentence written for a press release.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the litsearch site. Today's paper is the Qwen2.5 Technical Report, from the Qwen Team at Alibaba — An Yang, Baosong Yang, Beichen Zhang, Binyuan Hui, and colleagues — posted in December of twenty twenty-four.
[S] It's on the docket because Qwen2.5 became the default open base for a huge wave of reasoning and reinforcement-learning research that followed it. If you've read a reasoning paper in the last year that started from an open checkpoint instead of training from scratch, there's a decent chance it started from this one.
[O] Joining us is Marcus, who's spent real time with this report. Marcus, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. This is a genuinely dense technical report — seven open dense models, two proprietary mixture-of-experts models, a new pre-training recipe, and a post-training stack with more moving parts than most labs disclose. There's a lot to get through.
[S] Set the scene first. Why did Alibaba need a Qwen2.5 at all — what was actually wrong with Qwen2?
[G] By late twenty twenty-four the open-weight race had a pretty clear shape. Meta's Llama series and Mistral led the field in the West, and the live question was whether an open model could close the gap to the best proprietary systems without needing a four-hundred-billion-parameter budget to get there. The report is candid about where Qwen2 fell short. On data, Qwen2 only saw seven trillion pre-training tokens, leaving knowledge, math, and code under-fed. On coverage, it skipped the three billion, fourteen billion, and thirty-two billion sizes, the sweet spot for anyone running models on limited hardware. And on usability, generation was capped around two thousand tokens, structured output like tables and JSON was weak, tool use was clumsy, and context windows were short.
[O] So it's not one gap, it's three, and none of them are exotic. Bigger corpus, fill in the missing sizes, make the thing more usable day to day.
[G] Right, and the deeper agenda isn't really "ship a bigger model," it's "ship a recipe." Scale and rebalance the pre-training corpus, then layer a much heavier post-training stack on top, so the same base can be specialized downstream into Qwen2.5-Math, Qwen2.5-Coder, the QwQ reasoning model, and multimodal variants. That reusability is a big part of why this particular checkpoint ended up everywhere.
[S] Worth flagging early, because "everywhere" is doing a lot of work in why we're covering it. This isn't just a strong benchmark run, it's an ecosystem decision a lot of other labs effectively made for them.
[O] Let's get into the architecture. Is there anything unconventional here, or is this "more of the same, but bigger and better fed"?
[G] Mostly the latter, and that's deliberate. It's a standard transformer decoder — grouped query attention for a cheaper key-value cache, SwiGLU activations, rotary position embeddings, and pre-normalization with R-M-S-Norm. Dense models span seven sizes, half a billion up through seventy-two billion parameters. The two proprietary models, Turbo and Plus, swap the feed-forward layers for a fine-grained mixture-of-experts design with shared expert routing instead.
[S] And the tokenizer?
[G] Byte-level byte-pair encoding, roughly a hundred fifty-two thousand vocabulary tokens, with the control-token set expanded from three tokens to twenty-two, including two new ones specifically for tool use. Small detail, but it matters if you're building agents on top of this.
[O] The headline pre-training number is the jump from seven trillion to eighteen trillion tokens. Is that just more of the same data, or did the mixture actually change?
[G] The report's real thesis is that mixture matters as much as raw scale, and they lean on four levers. First, Qwen2-Instruct models act as quality filters, scoring candidate training samples before anything is kept. Second, math and code data get seeded directly from the Qwen2.5-Math and Qwen2.5-Coder projects, so the general model inherits domain-specific work already done elsewhere. Third, synthetic data, generated by a seventy-two billion parameter Qwen2 Instruct model and a specialized math variant, gets re-filtered through a reward model before it's allowed into the mix. And fourth, they actively rebalance the mixture, down-sampling over-represented, repetitive domains like e-commerce and social media, and up-sampling higher-quality but scarcer domains like technology and science.
[S] So a meaningful chunk of "eighteen trillion tokens" is the previous model generation teaching itself what to keep.
[G] Fair way to put it. Hyperparameters aren't hand-tuned either, they fit scaling laws across dense models from forty-four million up to fourteen billion parameters and mixture-of-experts models up to a billion active parameters, to predict good batch size and learning rate before committing to a full run.
[O] What about context length? That was one of the three original gaps.
[G] Two-phase pre-training for most of the family, four thousand ninety-six tokens first, then extended to thirty-two thousand seven hundred sixty-eight, with the rotary embedding's base frequency raised from ten thousand to one million to keep positions well separated at that length. Turbo climbs progressively instead, through four stages up to two hundred sixty-two thousand tokens, mixing forty percent full-length sequences with sixty percent shorter ones at each stage. At inference time, two more tricks, YARN and Dual Chunk Attention, roughly quadruple the usable length beyond training alone, which is what gets Turbo to a one-million-token window and the rest of the family to about a hundred twenty-eight thousand.
[S] That's the pre-training side. What changed in post-training, because that's usually where a model's real personality gets set.
[G] This is arguably the more interesting half of the report. Supervised fine-tuning, SFT, uses over one million examples targeted at specific weak spots: long-form generation out to eight thousand output tokens, math chain-of-thought, code across roughly forty programming languages validated in a sandbox with real unit tests, instruction-following checked by generated verification code rather than graded by eye, structured-data reasoning, seventy thousand new logical-reasoning queries, cross-lingual transfer, and more robust system prompts. All of it is fine-tuned for two epochs at a thirty-two-thousand-token sequence length.
[O] And then reinforcement learning on top of that.
[G] Two stages. Offline RL uses Direct Preference Optimization, DPO, on about a hundred fifty thousand verifiable preference pairs, one epoch, aimed at tasks where you can check the final answer but a reward model struggles to score the reasoning, math and factuality. Online RL uses Group Relative Policy Optimization, GRPO, against a reward model scoring six axes — truthfulness, helpfulness, conciseness, relevance, harmlessness, and debiasing. They sample eight responses per query and prioritize queries where those scores disagree most, on the logic that high-variance queries teach the model more per step.
[S] Before results, decontamination. Did they actually check whether test sets leaked into training data?
[G] They did, and this is one of the more credible parts of the report. The rule: drop a training sequence if it shares a long common subsequence with any evaluation sequence, at least thirteen tokens of overlap covering sixty percent of the shorter sequence. Applied to both pre-training and post-training data, and disclosed rather than just asserted.
[S] Disclosed, but self-administered. Nobody outside Alibaba ran that check. Hold that thought, it's going to come back.
[O] Let's get to the headline claim. Seventy-two billion matching four hundred five billion.
[G] On the instruct models, Qwen2.5 seventy-two billion Instruct actually surpasses Llama-3.1 four hundred five billion Instruct on several benchmarks. MATH: eighty-three point one versus seventy-three point eight. LiveCodeBench: fifty-five point five versus forty-one point six. Arena-Hard: eighty-one point two versus sixty-nine point three. It trades a few others narrowly, GSM8K ninety-five point eight versus ninety-six point eight, HumanEval eighty-six point six versus eighty-nine point oh.
[S] So it wins big on math and code, loses small on a couple of narrower benchmarks. That's a real pattern, not one cherry-picked number.
[G] Agreed, and the base models, before any instruction tuning, tell the same story, comparable to the Llama-3 four hundred five billion base "while using only one-fifth the parameters," in the report's own phrase. This isn't just an instruction-tuning trick sitting on a mediocre base.
[O] Does the story hold below seventy-two billion?
[G] Surprisingly well. The seven billion Instruct model hits seventy-five point five on MATH and eighty-four point eight on HumanEval, beating both Gemma2 nine billion Instruct and Llama-3.1 eight billion Instruct on nearly every benchmark. The whole size ladder punches above its weight, not just the flagship.
[S] Now the part I find most interesting, long context. A million-token window is easy to claim and hard to make real.
[G] This is where the paper is genuinely strong. On RULER, the standard long-context benchmark, seventy-two billion Instruct posts the best average of anything compared, ninety-five point one, ahead of GPT-4 at ninety-one point six and GPT-4o-mini at eighty-seven point three. And it isn't propped up by short-context scores alone, at the full hundred twenty-eight thousand token length it scores eighty-eight point four with the YARN and Dual Chunk Attention extrapolation on, versus sixty-seven with it off.
[O] And Turbo, the one-million-token model?
[G] Turbo hits one hundred percent accuracy on one-million-token passkey retrieval, recovering a planted number from anywhere inside a million tokens of irrelevant text, at every depth tested. And they made it fast, too, a sparse attention scheme based on Minference cuts attention compute at one million tokens by roughly twelve and a half times, translating to a three point two to four point three times speedup in time to first token. A million-token context that takes minutes to start generating from isn't actually usable, this is the part making sure it is.
[S] That's the strongest empirical claim in the whole report, in my read. It sits on a benchmark nobody can easily game, and the ablation, extrapolation on versus off, isolates exactly what's causing the gain.

[O] Zoom out, why does this paper matter beyond its own benchmark table?
[G] Because of what happened after it shipped. Qwen2.5 became the default open base for the wave of reinforcement-learning-with-verifiable-rewards research that followed DeepSeek-R1 — work like DAPO and General-Reasoner mostly starts from a Qwen2.5 checkpoint rather than training from scratch. The size ladder and the decontamination discipline are a big part of why, researchers could trust the base enough to build on it.
