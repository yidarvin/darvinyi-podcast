Today's paper is Scaling Instruction-Finetuned Language Models, from Hyung Won Chung, Le Hou, Shayne Longpre, and colleagues at Google, posted in October of twenty twenty-two, with Quoc Le and Jason Wei among the senior authors.
[S] Joining us is Daniel, who has spent a lot of time with this exact paper. Daniel, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. Worth saying up front, since the title invites confusion: this is not the original FLAN paper. That was Wei and colleagues in twenty twenty-one, a single one hundred thirty-seven billion parameter model finetuned on sixty-two tasks. This is the follow-up a year later that pushes that same recipe hard, along three separate axes at once, rather than tweaking it at the edges.
[O] Which is exactly why it earns a slot on the docket. It's arguably the paper that turned instruction finetuning from an interesting idea into a mandatory, unglamorous step in basically every serious language model shipped since.
[S] Let's set up the gap first. Why did a model like PaLM, five hundred forty billion parameters, trained on trillions of tokens, need this at all?
[G] Because a model trained purely to predict the next token is a strange conversational partner. Ask PaLM a plain question, zero-shot, and it will often just continue your sentence, repeat your question back with small edits, or never stop generating. It was never taught an end-of-sequence signal, never taught that your text was a request rather than something to keep writing.
[O] Which people mostly worked around with prompt engineering, hand-crafting few-shot examples until the model fell into the right behavior.
[G] Right, and instruction finetuning was the known fix: finetune on datasets rewritten as natural-language instructions, and the model learns the general shape of following an instruction rather than completing text. By late twenty twenty-two that idea already existed in FLAN, in T0, in InstructGPT.
[S] So what was actually missing, if the fix was already known?
[G] Scale, in a specific sense. Prior work scaled one axis at a time: Wei and colleagues to one hundred thirty-seven billion parameters but only sixty-two tasks, the T0 paper to more tasks but just eleven billion parameters, another effort to sixteen hundred tasks but only a three billion parameter model. Nobody pushed model size and task count together, and nobody checked what any of this does to multi-step reasoning, which chain of thought prompting had just shown large models were capable of.
[O] That last part sounds like the real gap. Everyone evaluated instruction finetuning on the same kind of task it trained on, answer directly, and nobody asked whether it helps or hurts step-by-step reasoning.
[G] Exactly, and that turns out to be the paper's most important finding, not a footnote. The bet is that pushing three axes at once, task count, model size, and chain of thought data, isn't three incremental tweaks. It's a single, cheap, general recipe.
[S] Walk us through the recipe itself. What actually is Flan, as a procedure?
[G] Flan is a finetuning procedure, not a model — you take a pretrained checkpoint and prepend "Flan-" to it: Flan-PaLM, Flan-T5, Flan-U-PaLM. It has three moving parts. First, an enormous task mixture: four existing collections combined into one thousand eight hundred thirty-six finetuning tasks, spanning four hundred seventy-three datasets and one hundred forty-six task categories. Muffin contributes eighty tasks, including new dialog and code-generation data the authors added. T0-SF contributes one hundred ninety-three. Natural Instructions version two contributes the bulk, fifteen fifty-four. And a fourth, much smaller mixture ends up mattering more than its size suggests: just nine datasets with hand-written chain of thought annotations.
[O] Nine, out of eighteen hundred. That's a tiny slice to be doing this much work.
[G] It is, and we'll get to exactly how much in a minute. One more detail worth flagging: the authors scrubbed forty-four MMLU-related tasks out of the mixture, specifically so their main held-out benchmark stays genuinely held out.
[S] What's the second moving part?
[G] Prompting-format diversity. Every task gets rendered both with and without few-shot exemplars, and for the chain of thought data, both with and without a written-out reasoning chain — the same task in four outfits, which is what lets one checkpoint handle zero-shot, few-shot, and chain of thought prompting well, instead of only the format it saw in training.
[O] And the third part is just, apply it everywhere?
[G] Broadly, and cheaply. They run the identical procedure across T5, an encoder-decoder model from eighty million parameters up to eleven billion, and across PaLM, decoder-only, at eight billion, sixty-two billion, and five hundred forty billion, plus U-PaLM, a UL2-continued-pretraining variant of PaLM. Same recipe, same mixture, only a few hyperparameters change per model.
[S] And the compute cost, since that's the number that gets quoted most.
[G] For Flan-PaLM at five hundred forty billion, instruction finetuning uses point two percent of the pretraining compute — about five hundred twelve version-four TPU chips for thirty-seven hours, one point four billion finetuning tokens against seven hundred eighty billion pretraining tokens. It doesn't touch inference cost at all, because you're not changing the architecture or serving a bigger model.
[O] That's the part that made this a template rather than a one-off result. You're not paying for a second pretraining run, you're paying for what's basically a long weekend of finetuning.
[G] One more piece of machinery worth naming, because every results table uses it: the normalized average. Instead of averaging raw accuracy, they rescale each score against its random-guess floor, so a multiple-choice task at chance reads as zero, not twenty-five percent, which keeps benchmarks with different baselines from distorting the aggregate.
[O] Give us the headline scaling result.
[G] Both knobs help, monotonically. Flan-PaLM at five hundred forty billion beats plain PaLM by nine point four percent on the normalized average across held-out benchmarks, and smaller models gain even more in absolute terms: ten point four percent at sixty-two billion, fifteen point five percent at eight billion.
[S] What about the task-scaling curve, the number of finetuning tasks itself?
[G] That's the surprising part — it flattens fast. Going from zero to two hundred eighty-two tasks captures most of the gain; going all the way up to eighteen hundred thirty-six adds comparatively little on top. The authors' read is that instruction finetuning mostly teaches the model to express knowledge it already picked up in pretraining, not install new knowledge, which tracks with finetuning touching only point two percent as many tokens as pretraining.
[O] So the paper's own title, "scaling," is doing more work for model size than for task count.
[G] Fair reading, and the authors more or less say so themselves in the discussion.
[S] Now the chain of thought result, because you called it the most consequential finding.
[G] Here's the shape of it. Finetune on the non-chain-of-thought data alone, and the model gets worse at chain of thought reasoning than the untouched base model — actually worse, not flat. Add back just those nine chain of thought datasets, and it doesn't just fix the degradation, it lifts performance on every evaluation, chain of thought and non-chain-of-thought alike.
[O] Why would finetuning on more data make a model worse at anything?
[G] Because instruction finetuning, the authors argue, mostly transfers within the same prompting paradigm it trained on. Finetune only on direct-answer data and you get better at direct answers, worse at reasoning chains, because the model is being pushed toward short, terse output. You need both formats in the mixture to keep one checkpoint strong everywhere.
[S] And it unlocks something new too, zero-shot chain of thought?
[G] Right. After chain of thought finetuning, Flan-PaLM will actually produce a working reasoning chain from a bare "let's think step by step" prompt, on twenty-three hard BIG-Bench tasks. Plain PaLM, without finetuning, mostly doesn't — it repeats the prompt, drifts into unrelated text, or never stops generating.
[O] Now the state-of-the-art numbers, because those are what got quoted everywhere.
[G] Combine chain of thought prompting with self-consistency — sampling several reasoning chains and majority-voting — and Flan-PaLM at five hundred forty billion hits seventy-five point two percent on five-shot MMLU. The matched comparison, PaLM with the same self-consistency trick, scores sixty-nine point five, so the real gap is five point seven points, not the forty-three point nine percent GPT-three number people sometimes drag in for contrast. On the multilingual math benchmark MGSM, Flan-PaLM reaches seventy-two percent, including sixty-nine point six on Bengali, a low-resource language, well ahead of PaLM's numbers on even high-resource languages like French or German.
[S] And the multilingual question-answering result is a good one too.
[G] TyDiQA, yes — Flan-PaLM improves fourteen point nine percentage points over PaLM one-shot, and at just sixty-two billion parameters it beats plain PaLM at five hundred forty billion on that benchmark, fifty-eight point seven versus fifty-two point nine exact match.
[O] What about the open checkpoints, Flan-T5? That's the part most people outside a big lab actually got to touch.
[G] Genuinely strong. Flan-T5-XL is only three billion parameters and scores fifty-two point four percent on MMLU, ahead of GPT-3 at one hundred seventy-five billion, which scores forty-three point nine. The T5 family gains the most of any architecture from this recipe, plus twenty-six point six normalized-average points at the eleven billion size. Stacking instruction finetuning with UL2 continued pretraining produces Flan-U-PaLM, the single strongest model anywhere in the paper.
[S] There's also a human-preference result worth putting on the record, since it's the most direct evidence this improves usability, not just benchmark scores.
[G] One hundred ninety open-ended prompts, PaLM versus Flan-PaLM, human raters choosing which response they preferred. Flan-PaLM wins seventy-nine percent of the time overall, with the margin widening by roughly another ten points on prompts using a chain of thought trigger, and no regression on the few-shot prompts.

[S] So where does that leave us on the headline claims?
[G] Split them apart. The scaling story — model size and task count both help, task count with diminishing returns past around two hundred eighty-two — holds up cleanly. The chain of thought finding, that non-chain-of-thought finetuning alone degrades reasoning and nine datasets fix it, is the most durable, least hedged result in the paper. The state-of-the-art headlines, seventy-five point two on MMLU and especially eighty-three point nine on GSM8K, need their inference budget and held-in caveat attached every time they're repeated.

[O] What actually changed downstream because of this paper?
[G] It became the standard first stage of what people now think of as the finetune-then-align pipeline. Nearly every instruction-following model shipped after this one runs some version of large-scale, multi-format instruction finetuning before anything like reinforcement learning from human feedback happens on top. And the specific lesson about chain of thought data shows up directly in how later training mixtures got built.
[S] From an evaluation standpoint, one thing for anyone building a benchmark today: the paper's own held-out discipline, physically keeping MMLU and its overlapping tasks out of the finetuning data, is exactly the practice a lot of later benchmark papers skipped — and it's a big part of why this paper's core claims still hold up years later while some others don't.
[G] The Flan-T5 checkpoints specifically had an outsized afterlife. Small, open, and genuinely strong, they became a default base model for a huge amount of downstream research that couldn't afford five hundred forty billion parameters.
[G] If I had to leave you with one line: this paper is proof that instruction finetuning is not a niche trick, it's a cheap, general, load-bearing step, and that a handful of chain of thought examples inside that mixture is what keeps a model able to reason rather than just answer.
