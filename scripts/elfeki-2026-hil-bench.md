[S] And then the authors take away a handful of details, and that same agent falls to 4 to 24 percent, even though it has a tool sitting right there that lets it just ask what's missing.
[O] That's not a capability story to me, that's an untapped upside story. These models can code. Somebody just needs to teach them to raise a hand.
[S] Or it's a much darker story. Every benchmark we've been trusting rewards a confident wrong guess exactly the same as a right answer, and nobody noticed until now.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a guest scholar take apart one paper from litsearch dot darvinyi dot com.
[S] Today's paper is HiL-Bench, the Human-in-Loop Benchmark, subtitled "Do Agents Know When to Ask for Help," from a team at Scale AI led by Tu Trinh and Mohamed Elfeki.
[O] And joining us is Eleanor, who has spent a lot of time with this paper's blocker registries and failure traces. Eleanor, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is one of the more clarifying papers I've read this year, in more ways than one.
[S] Eleanor, start us at the beginning. What's actually broken in how we evaluate coding agents today?
[G] The authors point to something almost embarrassingly simple. Picture an experienced engineer handed a vague ticket. They don't just start typing. They figure out what they can resolve on their own, and when a requirement has a dozen equally plausible readings, they ask before committing, because guessing wrong wastes hours. The authors call that skill selective escalation, and their claim is that no existing benchmark measures it.
[O] Which is strange, because these agents already ship the tooling for it. Claude Code has an ask-user-question tool, Codex and Cursor have interactive modes.
[G] Right, the mechanism exists. The paper's argument is that models rarely fire it at the right moment. Faced with an unclear spec, they fill the gap with a confident assumption and hand back plausible, wrong output. There's a line from Karpathy that the authors quote, that agents don't ask humans, lack the right context, and try to one-shot everything.
[S] And the reason nobody caught this is the benchmarks themselves are the problem. SWE-bench, HumanEval, BIRD-SQL, they all hand the agent a complete, unambiguous spec and score execution correctness only.
[G] Exactly. An agent that silently assumes its way past a missing requirement and gets lucky scores identically to one that would have stopped and asked. The authors call it a misleading loop. High scores drive deployment, deployment surfaces the judgment failures the benchmark never tested, and you end up with the failure rate everyone's been citing for enterprise agent pilots, north of ninety percent, without the evaluation ever pointing at the actual cause.
[O] So the bottleneck they're proposing isn't "agents can't code."
[G] No. Every model in this paper can code, and can technically call the escalation tool. The bottleneck is judgment, knowing when to act alone and when to ask. That's the single skill HiL-Bench tries to isolate.
[S] Walk us through how you actually build a benchmark that isolates judgment instead of capability.
[G] They start from two places where agents are actually deployed today. SWE-Bench Pro for repository-scale software engineering across Python, Go, JavaScript, and TypeScript, and BIRD for text-to-SQL over real databases spanning finance, healthcare, education, and more. Both are well-specified benchmarks with a known answer.
[O] And they deliberately picked Pro over the more famous SWE-Bench Verified.
[G] Because frontier models have memorized a lot of Verified. Pro avoids that. And critically, they only kept tasks where top models already hit around 85 percent pass rate with full information restored. That's the whole trick. If a task is already solvable at 85 percent when nothing is hidden, any collapse under blocked conditions can't be waved away as "the task was just hard."
[S] So then what do they actually remove?
[G] Trained domain-expert annotators inject 3 to 5 blockers per task, and each blocker is one of three types. Missing information is the largest share, 42 percent, a required value just isn't in the spec, like which of seven rating columns counts as "overall." Ambiguous requests, 36 percent, admit multiple valid readings, like handling version epochs "appropriately." And contradictory information, 22 percent, two requirements that can't both be true, like a query asking for northern California schools that then names southern ones.
[O] Those all sound like things I've actually seen in real tickets.
[G] That's the point, and it's enforced. Every blocker has to clear seven quality criteria or it's rejected. Realism, criticality, meaning it actually breaks correctness if unresolved, objectivity, a single unambiguous answer, a vast search space so you can't just guess it, independence between blockers, no contamination, meaning the answer can't be found anywhere else in the repo or schema, and non-contrived. Common engineering defaults like three retries or port eighty eighty don't count, because an experienced developer could just guess those. Each task then goes through five to six rounds of independent human audit.
[S] What stops a model from just front-loading twenty questions before it starts, to cover its bases?
[G] This is the design decision I think matters most, progressive discovery. Blockers only surface as the agent actually explores. It has to touch the schema or read the code before the gap becomes visible. You can't ask about a blocker you haven't encountered yet.
[O] Do we know that's actually true, or is that an assumption baked into the design?
[G] They tested it. They ran Claude Opus 4.6 on the SQL tasks with the task description and the ask human tool, but stripped out every environment tool, no schema inspection, no execution. Blocker recall fell from 61 percent with full access down to 11 percent with none. The gaps genuinely aren't visible from the prompt alone.
[S] So how does asking actually work mechanically? What's on the other end of that tool call?
[G] It's called ask human, and it's backed by a frozen Llama 3.3, 70 billion parameter instruct model acting as a semantic judge. It compares the agent's question against a registry of trigger phrasings for each blocker. If the question targets a real blocker, it returns the exact resolution. Otherwise it returns the literal string "irrelevant question." That judge was validated against human-labeled question pairs at 97 percent precision and 91 percent recall.
[O] And the actual scoring metric?
[G] Ask F1, the harmonic mean of question precision and blocker recall. Precision is the fraction of questions that actually hit a real blocker, recall is the fraction of blockers that get resolved. The harmonic mean matters because it architecturally kills question spam. If you fire fifty questions per task on a five-blocker problem and rack up 80 percent recall with only 8 percent precision, you score fourteen point five percent, nowhere near a passing grade.
[S] That's a genuinely elegant fix for a real gaming vector.
[G] The final dataset is 300 tasks, 150 each from SWE and SQL, with 1,131 blockers total, about three point eight per task, split into 200 public tasks and 100 held-out private ones.
[O] Okay, give me the headline number.
[G] Under full information, models reach 86 to 91 percent pass rate on SQL and 64 to 88 percent on software engineering. Once they have to decide for themselves whether to invoke ask human, the paper's own words are that the best model reaches only 38 percent on SQL and 12 percent on software engineering. Ask F1 averages 41 percent on SQL and 37 percent on software engineering.
[S] And the near-zero "no tool" baseline is there to prove what, exactly?
[G] To rule out the objection that these tasks are just solvable through lucky guessing. With no tool at all, pass rates are close to zero, confirming the tasks genuinely require the missing information. So the collapse from the high eighties down to single digits is judgment, full stop, not a capability ceiling.
[O] Which model handles it best?
[G] Claude Opus 4.6, and only on SQL, where its Ask F1 hits 58. But look at software engineering, no model exceeds 50 percent recall there. Not one.
[S] Why the domain split? Is SQL just an easier problem?
[G] The authors think it's structural. SQL blockers can hide in the schema, the business logic store, or the query itself, three separate places, which somehow makes gaps easier to notice as gaps. Software engineering blockers hide in a sprawling codebase full of cross-references, and models default to a plausible "best guess" pattern instead of stopping to ask.
[O] I want the model-by-model breakdown, because I think this is where the paper actually earns its keep.
[G] It classified over 3,600 failure traces, and each model has a genuinely distinct fingerprint that holds across both domains. GPT-5.3-Codex and GPT-5.4 are accuracy-dominant. On SQL, 73 to 93 percent of their tool-use failures are the right tool called with wrong parameters, and 76 to 88 percent of logic failures are just wrong beliefs applied confidently. Giving them ask human barely changes the pattern, because they so rarely detect that anything is missing in the first place.
[S] So the tool just sits there unused.
[G] Largely, yes. Claude Opus 4.6 is the opposite failure mode, uncertainty detection without resolution. In 45 percent of its alignment failures, the trace shows the model explicitly recognizing it's stuck, and then submitting anyway. It's the only model in the study that ever verbalizes that a task is infeasible. But detecting the gap doesn't translate into asking about it. It's completion-heavy instead, exploring extensively and burning up to five times the tokens of any other model without ever pulling the trigger.
[O] And Gemini?
[G] Gemini 3.1 Pro is domain-sensitive and externally correctable, at least on SQL. Give it ask human, and its tool-use completion failures drop 38 percentage points, it goes from stalling to actually acting, though a lot of those new actions are still wrong. On software engineering, that correctability disappears entirely.
[S] I'll push on this. How many questions is Claude actually asking per task, because "willing to ask" and "asking well" aren't the same thing.
[G] Fair, and the paper reports this directly. Claude asks roughly four and a half questions per task on average, more than any other model. Gemini asks around two point seven on software engineering but six point six on SQL. GPT asks the fewest, around one to two. So Claude is the most willing to escalate, but its precision on software engineering is only 26 percent, its worst score in the paper. Willingness without targeting doesn't save you.
[O] Alright, let me make the strongest case for optimism here, Eleanor, and then you tell me where I'm wrong. This paper doesn't just diagnose a problem, it shows a fix works. They took a reward shaped around Ask F1, fine-tuned a 32 billion parameter Qwen3 model with LoRA, and in-domain SQL Ask F1 goes from 18 percent to 46 percent, up 28 percentage points, with pass rate climbing from 11 to 24 percent in lockstep. That's not a benchmark paper complaining about a gap, that's a benchmark paper closing part of it.
[S] Here's my problem with that framing. It's one 32 billion parameter open model. We have zero evidence a frontier-scale model, the ones actually shipping in production, responds the same way to this reward. "Judgment is trainable" is a much bigger claim than what was actually shown.
[G] That's fair, and the authors are honest about the scope. What I'd add is the transfer result is the more interesting piece, independent of scale. Train the reward only on SQL tasks, and Ask F1 still improves on held-out software engineering tasks, and vice versa. The model isn't learning which SQL columns tend to be ambiguous, it's learning to detect unresolvable uncertainty as a general skill. That's evidence for a mechanism, even if the demonstration is small.
[O] Right, and that's the point I'd make back to Sam. The reward design itself is clever engineering, not just a headline metric. A dense per-step term, plus point three for a question that hits a real blocker, minus point one for noise, plus a terminal coverage term gated to zero if the agent never asks at all, so you can't game it by staying silent. That's real infrastructure other labs can just reuse.
[S] I'll grant that's a genuine contribution. My bigger issue is with the oracle itself, not the RL result. Ask human never lies, never stalls, never gets annoyed, and always answers in one round. Real humans on a real ticket are slow, sometimes wrong, and get irritated by the fortieth clarifying question. Every number in this paper, the 38 percent, the 12 percent, all of it, is measured against a collaborator that doesn't exist in production.
[G] The authors actually say this themselves, that the reported rates are a floor. And I think that cuts in a direction you'd both agree with. It makes the 4 to 24 percent collapse more alarming, not less, because a real, noisy human collaborator can only make selective escalation harder, not easier. But it does mean you shouldn't read any of these pass rates as what you'd get in deployment.
[O] Okay, second skeptic point, go.
[S] Contamination. Two hundred of the three hundred tasks are public. The moment those SWE-Bench Pro-derived problems and their blocker registries are sitting in a public split, they start leaking into the next round of pretraining data, the same way SWE-Bench Verified apparently got memorized enough that this paper explicitly avoided using it.
[G] Also fair, though they do defend against it structurally, not just by trusting people not to peek. Commit histories are squashed so an agent can't dig up the answer from git log, no contamination is a hard rejection criterion for every blocker, and the vast search space requirement rules out anything guessable. But you're right that only the 100 private tasks stay clean over time, and the paper doesn't report a plan for refreshing that private split.
[S] One more, and this one's about methodology, not the benchmark design. The whole model-fingerprint story, GPT versus Claude versus Gemini, comes from an LLM judge, Sonnet-4, grading those 3,600 traces. What's that judge's reliability?
[G] They report a kappa of about point nine three for inter-run self-agreement, meaning the judge agrees with itself across repeated runs. That's a real number, but it's not the same as human agreement. The paper says calibration against human judgment was maintained through spot-checks during development, which is softer than the 97 and 91 percent precision and recall they validated the ask human judge against directly.
[O] So the headline numbers, the pass rates and Ask F1, are tightly validated, but the "why" story underneath them is on somewhat softer footing.
[G] That's a fair way to put it.
[O] So where do you land, adjudicating both of us?
[G] Both cases have real teeth. The core finding, that judgment collapses independent of coding capability, is well controlled, I don't think either of you should doubt that part. The trainability claim is promising but genuinely thin at one small model, so treat "judgment is trainable" as shown, not "judgment is trainable at frontier scale." The oracle critique is the sharpest methodological gap in the paper, not fatal, but it means read every headline percentage as best case. And the contamination and taxonomy-judge points are both real, manageable risks the authors already flagged themselves, not blind spots they missed.
[S] Eleanor, does this actually change anything for how we build evals more broadly, or is this a one-off metric for one narrow skill?
[G] I think it's bigger than one skill. Ask F1 is presented as the first optimization target for help-seeking specifically, separate from pass rate and separate from RLHF-style preference reward, neither of which currently penalizes an agent for confidently solving the wrong problem. If you're building any agentic eval, and contamination and judge reliability are already things this show talks about a lot, this paper is a template. Hold capability constant, then measure the one behavior you actually care about in isolation.
[O] And practically, if you're deploying agents on ambiguous tickets today, the message is blunt. Don't trust a high pass rate as evidence the agent will tell you when it's confused. Those are different skills, and this paper just showed they're nowhere near correlated.
[G] The paper's own framing at the end is a matrix. An agent either asks or it doesn't, and it either succeeds or fails. The dangerous quadrant, and the one most frontier agents sit in today, is the one where it never asks, forms a wrong belief, and hands back something plausible. Current benchmarks can't tell that agent apart from a good one. This one can.
[O] That's a good place to start wrapping up.
[G] If there's one line from this paper worth keeping, it's that no matter how capable these models get, there's always context locked in a human's head that no model can infer on its own, and the real question is whether the agent knows that.
