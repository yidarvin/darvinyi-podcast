[S] Here's an evaluator with no training run behind it at all. Ask a language model how likely it is to generate your candidate text, given the right instruction, and that likelihood becomes the quality score.
[O] And on the hardest test in the paper, dialogue evaluation, that untrained likelihood score beats every specialized metric they compare against, including some that actively score negative correlation with human judgment.
[S] Except once they switch on the RLHF-tuned version of the same 175 billion parameter model, the trick gets noticeably worse at judging dialogue quality than the non-aligned version. So whatever made the model nicer to talk to may have made it a worse judge.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the frontier of evaluation research. Today's paper is GPTScore: Evaluate as You Desire, by Jinlan Fu, See-Kiong Ng, Zhengbao Jiang, and Pengfei Liu, out of the National University of Singapore and Carnegie Mellon.
[S] Joining us is Roman, who's spent real time with this paper. Roman, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. This one's worth an hour because it's less a new metric and more a bet that evaluation itself doesn't need training data anymore, just the right sentence.
[O] That's the hook for me. Before we get to whether it holds up, let's set up why this was even a problem.
[G] For years, automatic text evaluation split into two camps. Reference-based metrics like ROUGE just measure word overlap with a gold reference, so they capture one narrow notion of similarity and nothing about fluency or factuality on their own.
[S] And the more sophisticated learned metrics, like BERTScore or BARTScore, fix that by training a scorer, but then you're locked into whatever single aspect you trained it on.
[G] Right, and when researchers tried building multi-aspect evaluators, ones that score fluency, relevance, and informativeness all at once, the paper argues those still under-specify what each aspect even means and how the aspects relate to each other. BARTScore ties each aspect to a specific fine-tuned metric variant, and UniEval learns each aspect from supervised labels.
[O] So every time a team wants a new evaluation criterion, say, "is this response engaging," someone has to write labels and retrain a model.
[G] Exactly, and that's the friction the authors are targeting. Their bet is that generative pre-trained models, by early 2023, had already developed the two abilities needed to skip all of that: zero-shot instruction following and in-context learning.
[S] That's a reasonable bet, but it's also just restating "large language models are good at following instructions." What's actually new here?
[G] The reframing of evaluation as a generation-probability question. If a model can be instructed to generate a fluent response, that same instruction should let you read off, with no extra training, how likely a candidate text is to be a high-quality generation under it. That's the whole paper in one sentence.
[O] Walk us through the mechanics, Roman. What number does GPTScore actually produce?
[G] It's the model's own conditional generation probability of the text being evaluated, given a prompt that packages three things: a task description, an aspect definition, and optionally the source context. Formally it's the summed, per-token log probability of the hypothesis text, conditioned on that combined prompt, with every token weighted equally in this work.
[S] So there's no separate scoring head, no classifier bolted on top. You're running the frozen model forward and reading off how surprised it is by the text it's being asked to grade.
[G] Correct, and that's the entire mechanism. No gradient step, no labeled training set. The "training" is entirely prompt engineering: you write a task specification like "generate a summary for the following text," pair it with a plain-English aspect definition, such as "the details provided by the generated text are consistent with the details in the source text" for relevance, and optionally append a handful of demonstrated input-output pairs before the evaluated sample.
[O] And that's what makes it multi-aspect for free. Want to check informativeness instead of relevance? Change the sentence, not the model.
[G] Right, and the paper tests three settings for every backbone: vanilla, meaning no instruction and no demonstration; instruction, meaning the task-and-aspect prompt with no examples; and instruction-plus-demonstration, which adds a handful of in-context examples on top.
[S] How many backbones are we talking about, and does this only work at frontier scale?
[G] Nineteen model variants across four families, spanning nearly three orders of magnitude: GPT2 from three hundred fifty-five million up to one and a half billion parameters, plus GPT-J at six billion; OPT from three hundred fifty million to sixty-six billion; FLAN-T5 from eighty million to eleven billion; and five GPT-3 variants, from text-ada-001 at three hundred fifty million up through the two 175 billion parameter models, text-davinci-001 and text-davinci-003.
[O] So it's not "just throw the biggest model at it." They're checking whether the trick generalizes down to eighty million parameters too.
[G] It does generalize for the instruction effect specifically, though absolute correlation still tracks model quality. All of this gets tested across four generation tasks: dialogue response generation on the FED dataset, summarization on SummEval, RealSumm, Newsroom, and QAGS-XSUM, data-to-text generation on BAGEL and SFRES, and machine translation on the Chinese-to-English portion of the Multidimensional Quality Metrics set. Altogether that's thirty-seven datasets and twenty-two evaluation aspects, scored against human judgments with Spearman and Pearson correlation.
[S] Let's get to numbers, then. Does the instruction actually move the needle, or is this a rounding-error improvement dressed up as a finding?
[G] It's a consistent, if modest, lift. On SummEval, averaged across all nineteen backbone variants, instruction improves Spearman correlation on every aspect reported: coherence goes from thirty-eight point oh to forty point two, consistency from forty point four to forty-one point four, fluency from thirty-five point eight to thirty-seven point two, and relevance from thirty-one point three to thirty-two point two. On RealSumm's coverage aspect it moves from thirty-seven point four to thirty-nine point eight.
[O] Those are single-digit gains, but they land on every aspect tested, with significance testing behind most of them. That's not nothing for a change that costs zero training.
[S] Fair, but single digits on summarization isn't the headline. What's the strong result?
[G] Dialogue. On FED, the best GPTScore variant, GPT3-d01 with instruction, reaches an average Spearman correlation of fifty-four point three at the dialogue level. The strongest prior baseline tested, a purpose-built dialogue metric called DynaEval, gets thirty-nine point eight. Reference-based BARTScore variants do worse still, and some score negative correlation with humans on specific aspects, like negative eighteen point nine on coherence for the fine-tuned BARTScore-plus-CNN-plus-Para variant.
[O] Negative correlation means that metric is actively pointing you the wrong direction. That's the real contrast: a training-free likelihood score beating a purpose-built trained metric that sometimes gets the sign wrong.
[G] On individual aspects the gap is larger still. GPT3-d01 hits fifty-six point nine on coherence and sixty-six point nine on depth, against DynaEval's forty-three point seven and forty-nine point eight.
[S] Now the RLHF result you both teased. Give me the actual table, not the summary sentence.
[G] Table six is unambiguous. On the FED dialogue-level set, text-davinci-001, which is not RLHF-tuned, scores fifty-four point three; text-davinci-003, which is RLHF-tuned, scores thirteen point five. That's a forty point eight point gap on the same 175 billion parameter model. On the turn-level set the gap shrinks to five point five points: thirty-eight point three versus thirty-two point eight.
[S] Worth flagging on air: the paper's own prose actually swaps which gap it assigns to which level when it states the finding. The table itself is clean, though — dialogue-level is the forty point eight gap, turn-level is the five point five gap.
[G] Correct, and that's a real inconsistency worth catching, though it doesn't change the direction of the result. The RLHF model is a measurably worse dialogue evaluator here.
[O] And it flips on summarization, where d03 sometimes edges out d01.
[G] It does, on coherence for instance, forty-three point seven versus forty point oh in the vanilla setting. The authors surface the inconsistency but don't explain it. Their own words are that it needs, quote, deep explorations on the working mechanism of human feedback-based instruction learning.
[S] That's a large unresolved thread to leave hanging in a paper whose whole selling point is that these models make reliable judges.
[O] There's also a nice aspect-composition ablation, right? Combining correlated aspect definitions into one prompt?
[G] Yes. On FED's "interesting" aspect, scoring with that definition alone gets thirty point eight Spearman. Combine it with "engaging" and "specific" and it jumps to forty-eight point six. Combine five correlated aspects and it reaches fifty-one point four, ahead of DynaEval's turn-level average of twenty-five point six on that same dataset.
[S] So which aspects you bundle into one prompt is itself a hyperparameter someone has to hand-tune. That's a form of prompt sensitivity hiding inside a method that's sold as training-free.
[G] That's a fair characterization. The authors present it as evidence the aspects aren't independent, but yes, getting the best number required curating which aspects to combine.
[O] Let me make the strongest case for this paper. It reframes an entire subfield: instead of collecting labels and training a scorer for every new criterion, it's write a new sentence. The dialogue numbers, fifty-four point three against DynaEval's thirty-nine point eight, are a real, reproducible win with significance testing behind them. And it degrades gracefully — even an eighty million parameter FLAN-T5-small benefits from instruction, so this isn't a "you need a huge model" story.
[S] Now let me push back. The dialogue win is real, but the implicit claim of general superiority doesn't hold on summarization. Look at Table three: BARTScore-plus-CNN-plus-Para, a fine-tuned baseline that needs labeled training data and is explicitly not training-free, still scores forty-two point five on coherence, matching or beating most GPTScore variants at the instruction setting, which cluster in the high thirties to mid forties, with GPT3-d01 at forty point one. On RealSumm's coverage aspect, that same fine-tuned baseline hits forty point nine against GPT3-d01's thirty-three point nine.
[G] That's accurate, and it's the paper's own Table three showing it. The abstract's framing implies a broad win; the summarization numbers look more like a trade than a rout.
[O] Sure, but competitive with a fine-tuned model using zero training data is still a strong result. That's the trade I'd take.
[S] I'd take it too, on cost grounds, if the paper actually discussed cost. It doesn't. They admit outright that they, quote, randomly extract and construct sub-datasets for meta-evaluation specifically because of the, quote, expensive API cost of GPT-3. So the headline GPT-3 numbers aren't even computed on the full evaluation sets, and nowhere do they quantify what running 175 billion parameter API calls as a routine evaluation metric would cost in production.
[G] That's an accurate reading. There's no cost accounting anywhere in the paper.
[O] Fair miss, I'll concede that one.
[S] Second miss: contamination. The score is literally the model's log-probability of seeing that exact text. If any of these reference summaries, or the CNN and Daily Mail articles behind them, leaked into GPT-3, GPT-2, or OPT's pretraining data, which is plausible for a widely mirrored corpus like that, part of the "likelihood" signal is memorization, not a genuine quality judgment. The paper never checks this.
[G] Confirmed, there's no contamination analysis anywhere in the paper, and it's a real hole given the method's mechanism is literally reading pretraining-shaped likelihood.
[O] I'll grant that one too. That's a legitimate blind spot, not a nitpick.
[S] One more thing bothers me structurally, not just empirically. The score is defined as a sum of per-token log probabilities, not an average, with every token weighted equally. Nothing in the paper discusses whether that biases the metric toward shorter candidates, or whether more common, higher-frequency phrasing simply scores higher regardless of whether it's actually better text.
[G] That's a fair mechanical critique. The paper never raises length normalization or frequency effects anywhere, not in the method section, not in the appendix. It's an omission, not something they tested and set aside.
[O] Which would matter more in some tasks than others. Summarization outputs vary a lot in length; a metric that quietly rewards brevity would show up right there.
[S] Exactly, and there's no ablation that would tell us either way.
[G] I'd add one more structural limit that doesn't get emphasized enough: this needs direct access to token-level probabilities, so it runs cleanly on open models and the older completion-style GPT-3 API, but it's awkward or impossible to apply directly to a closed chat model that only ever returns finished text, not per-token log-probabilities.
[O] That access constraint matters for where this goes next. A lot of the field moved toward chat-style models as judges after this paper came out.
[G] It did. This is a training-free scorer that needs likelihoods, and the industry mostly standardized on instruction-tuned chat APIs that don't expose that number cleanly. That's part of why later "LLM-as-a-judge" approaches tend to ask the model to output a verdict in text rather than read its probability directly.
[S] Which sidesteps the likelihood-versus-quality gap this paper has, but reintroduces the problems GPTScore was trying to avoid in a new form: prompt sensitivity, plus self-consistency and format sensitivity in whatever text the judge outputs.
[O] So the honest legacy here isn't "run GPTScore in production." It's the cleanest demonstration that pretraining likelihood tracks human quality judgment better than most people expected, and the field then built judge methods that trade that clean signal for broader model compatibility.
[G] That's a reasonable read, a bit beyond what the paper itself claims, but it matches how the evaluation literature moved after this.
[G] If there's one sentence: the paper shows that a frozen, untrained language model's own generation probability, asked the right way, tracks human judgment of text quality about as well as, and on dialogue clearly better than, metrics built specifically for the job.
[O] My takeaway is that this turned training-free, multi-aspect evaluation into a real working baseline instead of a hope, and that's worth more than the summarization numbers alone suggest.
[S] Mine is that likelihood still isn't quality, cost and contamination are unexamined, and the RLHF-versus-non-RLHF flip is a warning sign for anyone about to trust an aligned model's raw probabilities as ground truth.
