[G] Thanks for having me. It's an unusual paper to talk about, because its whole job was organizing other people's work, not reporting a new result of its own.
[S] Start with why it needed to exist. Fairness in machine learning wasn't a new topic in twenty nineteen.
[G] It wasn't, but it was scattered. The authors open with the real-world cases that had already made headlines by then: COMPAS, the recidivism-risk tool used in US courts, flagging Black defendants as high-risk at nearly twice the false-positive rate of white defendants. An AI judge in a beauty contest that scored darker-skinned contestants lower. Digital cameras that misread Asian faces as blinking.
[O] Those are the greatest hits. What made the paper worth writing wasn't finding new examples, it was noticing that classification researchers, NLP researchers, and recommender-system researchers were all rediscovering the same problems under different names.
[G] Exactly that. The paper's own framing is that fairness work had happened independently across classification, causal inference, community detection, and language processing, each field inventing its own terminology with almost no cross-referencing. A method satisfying one fairness property in a classification paper might not even be recognized as related work by someone doing the same thing in word embeddings.
[S] So the contribution isn't a new algorithm. It's librarianship.
[G] The authors probably wouldn't object to that description. They read through a hundred and eighty-eight references and built one shared structure: where bias enters a system, what fairness means once you've found it, and what's been tried to fix it.
[O] And that structure is the part that's held up. Let's get into it. Walk us through the organizing idea, Priya, because it's not just a list.
[G] It's a loop. Data trains an algorithm, the algorithm's outputs shape what users see and click, and user behavior becomes the next round of training data. The authors place every bias definition they catalog on one of the three arrows of that loop: data to algorithm, algorithm to user, or user back to data.
[S] Give me a concrete one from each arrow, because "loop" is abstract until you have an example.
[G] On the data-to-algorithm arrow, take aggregation bias. The paper's example is a diabetes blood marker that's known to behave differently across ethnicities and genders. A model that pools everyone into one regression line will misdiagnose fairly even on a dataset that's perfectly balanced by group, because the true relationship isn't the same shape in every subgroup. On the algorithm-to-user arrow, there's evaluation bias: using a benchmark like Adience or I J B A to certify a face-recognition system as accurate, when those benchmarks are themselves close to eighty percent light-skinned faces in one case, over eighty-six percent in the other, so passing the benchmark doesn't mean the system works on everyone it'll actually see. And on the user-to-data arrow, historical bias: search for women CEOs in twenty eighteen and you'd get mostly men back, because only about five percent of Fortune five hundred CEOs were women. The search results were accurately reflecting a biased world, which is its own separate problem from a sampling error.
[O] That third one is the one that always gets people. The algorithm isn't wrong, the world is.
[S] Which is exactly the case that should worry you most, because "the algorithm is technically correct" is not a defense anyone wants to be making in front of a judge.
[G] The paper doesn't resolve that tension, it just insists you notice which kind of bias you're looking at before you reach for a fix. Counting through all three arrows, there are twenty-three separately named bias types in total, seven on data-to-algorithm, five on algorithm-to-user, seven on user-to-data, plus a couple of named subtypes, like Simpson's paradox, nested inside those.
[S] Simpson's paradox, that's the UC Berkeley admissions case, right? Where the aggregate numbers looked biased against women, but broken out by department, women were doing fine or better?
[G] That's the paper's own example. Women applied more to departments with lower admission rates for everyone, so the aggregate hid what was actually happening at the department level. It's a decades-old statistics result, and the paper's contribution is putting it in the same taxonomy as much newer, ML-specific biases, so a practitioner sees them as instances of the same family of mistake.
[O] Once you've mapped where bias comes from, the next question is what "fair" even means once you've found it.
[G] Right, and this is section four, probably the part of the paper with the most downstream influence. It catalogs ten named fairness definitions and sorts them into three families: group fairness, treat protected groups equally in aggregate; individual fairness, give similar predictions to similar individuals; and subgroup fairness, which checks a group-fairness criterion across many overlapping subgroups instead of one binary split.
[S] Give me one definition precisely, because "treat groups equally" can mean a dozen different things depending on which number you're equalizing.
[G] Take equalized odds, one of the more cited ones. It says the protected and unprotected groups need equal true-positive rates and equal false-positive rates, not equal outcomes overall, equal error rates in both directions. Equal opportunity is a narrower cousin, same idea, but only the true-positive rate has to match.
[O] And demographic parity is the blunter one, just equal probability of a positive outcome regardless of group, full stop, no reference to whether the outcome was actually correct.
[G] Right, and those two things, equalized odds and demographic parity, can point in completely different directions on the same dataset. That's most of why this catalog matters, once you can name the definition you're using, you can also name which one you're not using, and ask whether that was a choice or an accident.
[S] This is where I want to push, because "definitions" makes it sound like picking a variable name. Demographic parity versus equalized odds isn't a technical choice, it's a values choice about what a just outcome looks like.
[G] The paper agrees with you, actually, more than you might expect from a computer science venue. It cites a result from Kleinberg and colleagues, published in twenty sixteen, showing that calibration, a risk score meaning the same thing regardless of group, and balancing error rates across groups cannot both hold at once, except in narrow special cases. That's not an engineering limitation you can code around. It's a mathematical incompatibility between two things that both sound like "fairness."
[O] Okay, so that's the map of the problem and the map of what fairness could mean. What did they do with the third piece, fixing it?
[G] Section five sorts every mitigation method into three families, regardless of which subdomain they came from. Pre-processing changes the training data before a model ever touches it. In-processing changes the learning objective itself, during training. Post-processing treats a trained model as a black box and recalibrates its outputs afterward using a held-out set.
[S] Give me the one that's actually clever, not just "clean the data first."
[G] Adversarial debiasing, from work by Zhang, Lemoine, and Mitchell that the paper walks through in detail. You train two networks at once: a predictor trying to get the label right, and an adversary trying to recover the protected attribute, race, gender, whatever it is, purely from the predictor's output. Every time the adversary succeeds, the predictor gets penalized. Accuracy and fairness get put in direct competition during training, instead of being bolted on afterward.
[O] And then there's the part of the survey that isn't classification at all, word embeddings, which is the example everyone actually remembers from this era.
[G] The famous one, from Bolukbasi and colleagues in twenty sixteen: run a word-analogy test on a standard embedding, man is to computer programmer as woman is to what, and the model answers homemaker. The paper walks through the fix, identify the gender subspace inside the embedding, then either zero it out for gender-neutral words or nudge it just enough to reduce the bias while keeping most of the original structure intact. There's a later result the paper cites, on ELMo's contextual embeddings, finding male entities appear in the training corpus nearly three times as often as female entities. The bias isn't a bug the model introduced, it's a direct read-out of the training text.
[S] Numbers I want to hear: how big is this survey, actually? "Comprehensive" gets claimed by every paper.
[G] A hundred and eighty-eight references. Twenty-three bias types. Ten fairness definitions. Sixteen distinct ML subdomains covered, from classification and regression down to causal inference, graph embedding, and five separate NLP tasks. The appendix adds eight benchmark datasets practitioners actually use, the UCI Adult dataset at close to forty-nine thousand records, COMPAS itself at around eighteen thousand six hundred, and Diversity in Faces, which is a full one million images.
