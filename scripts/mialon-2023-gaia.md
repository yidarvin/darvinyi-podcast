[O] Start with the shape of this paper's gap, before you get to the numbers: a set of questions so easy a human could clear most of them on a coffee break.
[S] And a model with plugins, tools, and a full web connection, answering barely one in six of those exact same questions.
[O] Ninety-two percent for people. Roughly fifteen percent for the best AI system tested. And on the hardest tier, every automated method the authors tried scored exactly zero.
[S] Zero, not "struggled." Zero out of seventy-five questions, for every method, including the best one. That's the kind of number that indicts either the models or the benchmark.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist and a skeptic argue about a paper until someone who actually knows it settles the score.
[S] Today's paper is GAIA, A Benchmark for General AI Assistants, from Gregoire Mialon, Clementine Fourrier, Craig Swift, Thomas Wolf, Yann LeCun, and Thomas Scialom — a Meta FAIR, Hugging Face, and AutoGPT collaboration, posted in November twenty twenty-three and later accepted at ICLR twenty twenty-four.
[O] It's on the docket because it's one of the earliest, most cited attempts to ask a different evaluation question: not "how do we make the test harder," but "how do we make it un-gameable." Joining us is Cleo, who's spent real time with this benchmark. Cleo, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. Quick housekeeping first: I'm not one of the six authors on this paper, so when I say "the authors" or "they show," that's exactly what I mean, not "we."
[S] Cleo, set the stage. Why did the field need yet another benchmark in late twenty twenty-three?
[G] Because the existing ones were dying on schedule. GLUE was introduced, and models beat humans within about a year; its successor, SuperGLUE, didn't last much longer. MMLU is the sharper example — fifteen thousand questions across fifty-seven subjects — and by this paper's writing, GPT-4 was already at eighty-six point four percent, past the thirty-four point five percent human baseline.
[O] So the field's answer was: make it harder. Bar-exam questions, olympiad math, graduate coursework.
[G] And the authors argue that has a structural flaw. Something hard for a person isn't necessarily hard for a model trained on most of the internet, especially once test-adjacent material leaks into pretraining. A benchmark built around human difficulty decays exactly when it succeeds, because progress and contamination produce the same score.
[S] There's a second half to the problem too: grading. The moment a benchmark demands open-ended output instead of multiple choice, scoring stops being free.
[G] Human grading doesn't scale, and it gets slower as the task gets harder. Model-based grading swaps that bottleneck for a different one: you need a judge model that's itself trustworthy, usually stronger and pricier than the one you're testing, and judges carry their own biases, like favoring whichever answer is shown first.
[O] What about the other general-assistant benchmarks that already existed?
[G] The authors survey them and find each falls short differently. ToolQA and Gentopia repurpose datasets like MMLU and MATH, risking the same contamination without guaranteeing tool use happened. Gorilla's APIBench and API-Bank test API syntax fluency, not task completion. AgentBench is broader, but its closed-box environments reward learning a sandbox's quirks over real-world competence. The closest relative, OpenAGI, anchors its tasks to what current models can already do, not a fixed target.
[S] So GAIA's pitch is: flip the axis entirely.
[G] Keep the questions conceptually simple, even tedious, for a human. Require an AI to autonomously plan and execute a real sequence of actions, browsing, reading files, running code, to reach one unambiguous, hard-to-fake answer. The paper compares this to a cryptographic proof of work: expensive to solve honestly, cheap to verify.
[O] Let's get concrete. What's actually in GAIA?
[G] Four hundred sixty-six questions, each with a single correct, short answer — a number, a short string, or a comma-separated list of either. Some arrive with an attached file: an image, a spreadsheet, a PDF, an audio clip. Answering one usually means combining information from several sources, a web page here, a file there, into one precise value, not an essay.
[S] Give me the three worked examples, they do a lot of the explaining.
[G] Level 1: find an enrollment count on a clinical-trials page, essentially one lookup. Level 2: a photographed nutrition label plus a remembered regulatory standard, computing a percentage. Level 3, the one people quote, chains an old NASA photo caption, astronaut-group membership, and current biographical data into one astronaut's name and the exact minutes he spent in space.
[O] That Level 3 example is almost comically specific.
[G] It has to be. The specificity is what makes the answer unambiguous and keeps it out of plain text anywhere online, that's the whole non-gameability argument.
[S] Walk through the four design principles, they're doing real structural work.
[G] Real-world and hard for AI, not humans, that's the inversion. Interpretability: the set is small and hand-curated, and the human pass rate is near perfect, so you can read a model's reasoning trace. Non-gameability: the answer is engineered not to exist in plain text anywhere. And simplicity of use: every ground truth is a short factoid, so scoring is deterministic, zero-shot, quasi-exact-match, no judge, no rubric.
[O] How does the scoring actually work?
[G] Type-aware normalization. Numbers drop commas and units unless asked for. Strings drop articles and abbreviations, digits spelled out. No partial credit, one correct answer per question.
[S] And the difficulty tiers, how are those actually defined? "Conceptually simple but hard to automate" is a philosophy, not a rubric.
[G] The proxy is the number of steps and distinct tools annotators used while crafting each question. Level 1 needs no tool, or at most one, and no more than five steps — one hundred forty-six questions. Level 2 needs multiple tools across roughly five to ten steps — two hundred forty-five questions. Level 3, a near-perfect general assistant, arbitrarily long sequences, any number of tools — seventy-five questions. The authors admit the boundary is fuzzy.
[O] What capabilities actually show up across the four hundred sixty-six questions?
[G] Web browsing dominates, three hundred fifty-five questions need it, then coding at one hundred fifty-four, multi-modality at one hundred thirty-eight, diverse filetype reading at one hundred twenty-nine. Thirty-two need no augmentation at all. Spreadsheets are the largest attached-file category, then images, PDFs, text, and audio.
[S] How were these questions built and checked? "Unambiguous, single correct answer" is a strong claim for four hundred sixty-six hand-written questions.
[G] The authors wrote seed questions, then handed them, with a written rubric, to compensated annotators through Surge AI: ground every question in a cited source, Wikipedia, arXiv, an attached document; make sure the answer isn't already online and won't drift over time; and, added after the fact, check the source's robots-dot-text so an AI assistant is permitted to fetch it.
[O] And then a second pass to catch mistakes.
[G] Two fresh annotators independently answer each new question, blind. Across six hundred twenty-three newly crafted questions, one thousand two hundred forty-six re-answers, they agreed with the original fifty-five percent of the time, split twenty-seven percent, and both disagreed eighteen percent.
[S] So less than a third of newly written questions were unanimous.
[G] Correct, and that's before repairs. A question only counts as valid if it survives the cross-check, sixty-eight percent did in aggregate, falling with difficulty: seventy-five percent at Level 1, sixty-eight at Level 2, only forty-seven at Level 3. Everything that failed was repaired or discarded — about two hours of annotator time per question, including repairs.
[O] And the human ninety-two percent score comes out of that same process.
[G] It does — ninety-two percent aggregate, ninety-three point nine, ninety-one point eight, eighty-seven point three by level. One hundred sixty-six questions ship with full annotations as a developer set; the other three hundred were held back to power a hidden leaderboard.
[S] Let's get to the numbers that made this paper famous.
[G] Humans at ninety-two percent aggregate, the strongest configuration, GPT-4 with plugins, at roughly fifteen. By level, no automated method reaches above thirty point three at Level 1, and every one, oracle included, scores exactly zero at Level 3.
[O] Walk me through the full table, the spread between methods is almost as interesting as the human gap.
[G] A plain search engine, a human typing the question and skimming results, gets seven point four percent at Level 1, zero after that. Bare GPT-4: nine point one, two point six, zero. GPT-4 Turbo: thirteen, five point five, zero. AutoGPT: fourteen point four, point four, zero. GPT-4 with hand-picked plugins: thirty point three, nine point seven, zero. Humans: ninety-three point nine, ninety-one point eight, eighty-seven point three.
[S] That AutoGPT number is strange, fourteen point four at Level 1, collapsing to point four at Level 2, worse than bare GPT-4.
[G] The authors flag it without fully explaining it, tentatively blaming how AutoGPT calls the API. It's also slow, roughly seven and a half minutes per question, for a fraction of the accuracy.
[O] Speaking of time, that's a second axis the accuracy table hides.
[G] Humans average six point eight minutes on Level 1, up to seventeen point seven on Level 3. Bare GPT-4 answers almost instantly, ten to fifteen seconds, but it's usually wrong. The authors call a human working alongside GPT-4 plus plugins the best score-per-time combination observed, since the human does the tool selection AutoGPT largely fails to automate.
[S] What does the capability breakdown at Level 1 show?
[G] Tool access explains most, not all, of the gap. Bare GPT-4 is essentially unable to touch "diverse filetype reading" or "multi-modality," it structurally can't read the file. Plugins narrow every bucket but close none, even the best configuration never lifts a single capability much past twenty percentage points at this, the easiest tier.
[S] Any qualitative failures worth flagging?
[G] GPT-4 without browsing simply refuses questions past its knowledge cutoff, while the browsing version resolves the identical question with one well-targeted search. But tool access isn't the whole story: GPT-4 also fails a self-contained Rubik's-cube puzzle needing zero tools at all. Some of GAIA's difficulty is just multi-step logical bookkeeping.
[O] Let me make the strongest case for why this paper matters. GAIA diagnosed something the field kept getting wrong, confusing "harder for people" with "harder for models." The proof-of-work framing is genuinely elegant, and the gap it exposed was a real, legible signal about how far tool-using assistants were from general competence.
[S] I'll grant the diagnosis. My problem is the headline number. The strongest score, thirty point three percent, is explicitly labeled by the authors as an "oracle" — the plugins were hand-picked by the researchers, and the paper says outright that configuration "cannot be reproduced exactly." The one baseline that does select tools on its own, AutoGPT, is both slower than a human and worse than bare GPT-4 at Level 2. The paper's best number is a human-curated ceiling on GPT-4's potential, not proof any autonomous system reaches fifteen percent.
[G] Fair, and it's a distinction the paper itself flags in the text, just easy to miss if you only read the abstract's headline framing.
[O] Fine, but even granting that, the zero percent floor at Level 3 across every method is still striking.
[S] It's a hard fact about a very specific, now dated stack, measured before frontier reasoning models or dedicated agentic tool-use systems existed as products. A zero from that stack is a much weaker claim than a zero against today's best agents.
[G] Worth adding: the paper's ambition was forward-looking, not a permanent verdict. It frames a system that solves GAIA as one level below a "competent" general AI under Morris and colleagues' "Levels of AGI" framework, a bet about where the field was heading.
[S] Two more things sit uneasily: scale and web dependency. Four hundred sixty-six questions is thin next to MMLU's fifteen thousand, and the hardest tier is only seventy-five, not much room to separate progress from noise. Since so many answers are anchored to web pages, the paper's own limitations admit evidence can be edited or vanish between writing and evaluation — link-rot for any web-grounded benchmark. Layer contamination on top: no ground truth exists in plain text on the web, but that only blocks the final string, not leakage of the constituent facts, and it misses something specific to a browsing benchmark — GAIA's own developer set and leaderboard are themselves now crawlable pages.
[G] The paper does name benchmark decay and "catastrophic contamination" in its abstract. But it doesn't work through this self-referential case, a leaderboard becoming its own leak vector. Real gap, not a rhetorical one.
[O] Let me push the other way, then: the scoring design. Quasi-exact-match on a short factoid is about as clean as automated evaluation gets. No judge model, no rubric drift.
[S] Clean, but blind. Section six says it outright, GAIA "does not evaluate the trace leading to the answer." A lucky shortcut scores identically to sound reasoning, and unsafe behavior along the way, ignoring the robots-dot-text rule the authors set themselves, is invisible. No partial credit either, so a system ninety percent through a hard chain scores the same zero as one that never engages.
[G] The authors name that as their biggest open limitation. Defensible under their "full automation" framing, but a coarse instrument if you want to know where a modern agent's reasoning breaks down.
[O] One more in the paper's favor, Cleo: the human baseline is a real measurement, not an assumption.
[S] Sure, but it's the same compensated annotators who wrote the questions, not a separately recruited, blinded panel. Only fifty-five percent unanimous agreement on fresh questions, falling to forty-seven percent valid at Level 3 before repair. The reported eighty-seven point three percent Level 3 score is measured on the subset that survived that loop, not on attempts in general.
[G] Reasonable choice, but it means the human ceiling and the benchmark's construction are more entangled than "ninety-two percent" suggests. The authors are candid in their datacard too: GAIA is English-only, built mostly from English pages, its annotator pool US-based. In their words, this "will not validate the usefulness of assistants for non-English speakers," eighty percent of the world's population.
[O] Alright, Cleo, adjudicate.
[G] On diagnosing a real problem with benchmark culture, optimist, clearly. On whether the headline number is trustworthy, skeptic wins outright — the best score is a non-reproducible oracle, and the real autonomous baseline underperforms bare GPT-4. On grading only the final answer, that's barely a debate, the authors concede it themselves. Scale and contamination I'd call a draw leaning skeptic: small size is a defensible tradeoff, but the self-referential leaderboard risk is a genuine hole. And on whether these questions predict open-ended real assistant use, that's the softest point for the optimist — real questions, real files, real web, but still a narrow slice of the job.
[O] So what should someone building or picking benchmarks today take from this?
[G] Design for verification cost, not just difficulty, and a benchmark stays meaningful longer. Publishing a developer set or leaderboard builds its own future leak vector.
[S] And the trace-versus-answer gap is the one I'd want every listener to sit with. A final-answer-only score can't tell you whether a system reasoned well or got lucky, exactly the question that matters more as agents get more autonomous.
[G] My read, going a little beyond the paper: GAIA's legacy by now is less "here's the leaderboard to chase" and more "here's the design pattern," trivial for humans, hard to fake for machines, that later agentic benchmarks borrowed while trying to harden against the problems we just walked through.
[O] Let's close it out. Cleo, the paper's own takeaway.
[G] GAIA inverted the usual benchmark strategy: four hundred sixty-six questions conceptually easy for people, ninety-two percent human accuracy, demanding genuine multi-step, multi-tool execution from a machine, scored by exact string match rather than a judge. The gap it measured, ninety-two against roughly fifteen, zero at the hardest tier, was real and influential, and the authors are candid that grading only the final answer is the benchmark's biggest limitation.
[O] Mine: a genuinely well-designed diagnostic instrument, and "cheap to verify, expensive to fake" deserves to outlive this specific leaderboard.
[S] And mine: read the headline number as a human-curated ceiling on a dated agent stack, not a durable measure of autonomous capability, and remember a benchmark's own developer set and leaderboard can quietly become the leak vector it was designed to prevent.
