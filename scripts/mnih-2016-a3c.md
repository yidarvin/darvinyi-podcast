[O] Half the training time. No GPU at all. Just sixteen ordinary CPU cores, and this method walks straight past the entire Deep Q-Network family's published state of the art on Atari.
[S] Walks past it on a table where every other row is copied from a different paper, run on different hardware, over a different number of days. I'm not saying the win isn't real. I'm saying "half the time" is doing a lot of unexamined work in that one sentence.
[O] Fair, but even you have to admit: matching Dueling Double DQN's score after just one day of training, on a machine that costs a fraction of a GPU rig, is not nothing.
[S] It's not nothing. It's also not quite what the headline implies, and that gap is worth an entire episode.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a guest scholar take one paper apart. Today's paper is Asynchronous Methods for Deep Reinforcement Learning, by Volodymyr Mnih and colleagues at Google DeepMind, published at the International Conference on Machine Learning in twenty sixteen.
[S] It's on the docket because it's quietly one of the most influential reinforcement learning papers of the last decade — the litsearch site has it past ten thousand citations — and because a whole lineage of later baselines defines itself relative to what this paper built.
[O] To actually open it up for us, we've got Miles with us, who knows this one down to the pseudocode. Miles, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. It's a paper that solves a narrow-sounding engineering problem — how do you stabilize online reinforcement learning without a replay buffer — and the solution turns out to generalize far beyond what the authors even needed it to.
[S] Start with the actual gap, Miles. Why did anyone need an alternative to DQN's replay buffer in the first place?
[G] Because naive online reinforcement learning with a neural network was thought to be fundamentally unstable. An online agent's stream of observations is non-stationary and strongly correlated — consecutive frames of the same episode look almost identical, so consecutive gradient steps push the network in correlated directions. DQN's fix was experience replay: store transitions, then train on randomly sampled batches, which decorrelates the updates and lets the same data get reused.
[O] And that worked extremely well. So what's the complaint?
[G] Two structural costs, and the paper is direct about both. Replay uses more memory and computation per real interaction, since every transition has to be stored and later replayed. And it locks you into off-policy algorithms only, because the data being replayed came from an older version of the policy. On-policy methods like Sarsa and actor-critic were effectively ruled out for stable deep reinforcement learning.
[S] There was already a brute-force answer to instability, though. Gorila.
[G] Right, DeepMind's own earlier system. Gorila ran one hundred actor-learner processes plus thirty parameter-server instances, one hundred thirty machines total, and it significantly outperformed DQN, reaching DQN's own scores up to twenty times faster on some games. It proves parallelism helps. It just costs a small data center to get there.
[O] So the actual question this paper asks is whether you can get replay's stabilizing effect, plus access to on-policy methods, without a hundred thirty machines.
[G] That's the question, almost word for word from the introduction.
[O] So what's the actual trick? What replaces the buffer?
[G] Decorrelation in space instead of time. Instead of one agent replaying old memories, you run many actor-learner threads in parallel on a single multi-core machine, each with its own copy of the environment. At any instant those threads are almost certainly in different parts of state space, so the combined stream of gradients hitting one shared set of parameters is naturally decorrelated — the same job replay used to do, without storing a single transition.
[S] And you're applying all those threads' updates directly to one set of weights, with no coordination between them?
[G] Lock-free, yes — Hogwild!-style updates, a technique from Recht and colleagues. Because every thread lives on one machine, there's no parameter-server communication cost, which is exactly the overhead Gorila's distributed design was stuck paying. There's one more trick worth naming: each thread can run its own exploration policy, with epsilon for epsilon-greedy resampled periodically from a distribution, per thread. That diversity does real work — it's what keeps the combined gradient stream from re-correlating across threads.
[O] Walk me through the actual algorithms. There are four of them.
[G] Asynchronous one-step Q-learning, one-step Sarsa, n-step Q-learning, and advantage actor-critic. The value-based ones keep DQN's own trick of a slowly-updated target network for the bootstrap target, and they accumulate gradients over several timesteps before applying one asynchronous update — both choices cut the odds that concurrent threads overwrite each other's progress. The n-step variant is interesting because it uses the forward view: it explicitly rolls out up to some maximum number of steps and computes an n-step return for every state along the way, rather than the backward-view eligibility traces classically used for that job. The authors found the forward view composes more naturally with backpropagation through time and momentum-based optimizers.
[S] And the best one, A3C, is the actor-critic variant.
[G] Right. It maintains a policy and a separate value estimate, sharing every layer except the two output heads — one softmax for the policy, one linear unit for the value. Both get updated together every fixed number of actions, or at a terminal state, using an n-step estimate of the advantage: the discounted sum of rewards over that window, plus a bootstrapped value at the end, minus the value estimate at the start.
[O] What actually stops the policy from collapsing onto one action, if it stumbles onto something that works early?
[G] An entropy bonus, added directly to the objective, to discourage premature convergence onto a deterministic, suboptimal policy. It's an older idea, from Williams and Peng, but it earns its keep here. On the optimizer side, the paper compares plain stochastic gradient descent with momentum against RMSProp, and finds that RMSProp with its second-moment statistic shared across all the asynchronous threads, rather than kept separately per thread, is considerably more robust than either alternative.
[S] Let's get to the actual numbers before I decide how skeptical to be. What happens on Atari?
[G] First a smaller test: five games — Beamrider, Breakout, Pong, Q*bert, Space Invaders — comparing DQN trained on a single Nvidia K40 GPU against all four asynchronous methods trained on sixteen CPU cores. Every asynchronous method trains successfully, and they tend to learn faster than DQN despite the hardware gap. Among the four, A3C clearly outperforms the three value-based variants.
[O] Then they scale it up to the full benchmark.
[G] Fifty-seven Atari games, following the human-starts evaluation protocol from Van Hasselt and colleagues. A3C's own hyperparameters — learning rate and gradient-norm clipping — were tuned on a search over six games: Beamrider, Breakout, Pong, Q*bert, Seaquest, and Space Invaders. Everything else was then fixed for all fifty-seven. The headline: A3C with an LSTM, trained four days on sixteen CPU cores, reaches a six hundred twenty-three percent mean and a one hundred twelve point six percent median human-normalized score. Every GPU baseline that trained eight to ten days — Double DQN, Dueling Double DQN, Prioritized DQN — tops out lower on the mean.
[S] And after just one day of that four-day run?
[G] The feedforward version already matches Dueling Double DQN's mean, and nearly reaches Gorila's median, despite Gorila's one hundred thirty machines.
[O] That's the number that gets me. One day, sixteen cores, no GPU.
[G] It's a real result, and it isn't only Atari. On TORCS, the three-D car-racing simulator, A3C reached roughly seventy-five to ninety percent of a human tester's score across four car-and-opponent configurations, in about twelve hours. On MuJoCo's continuous-control manipulation and locomotion tasks — the first time one of these async methods was shown working on continuous actions at all — A3C found good solutions in under twenty-four hours, typically within a few. And on a brand-new three-D maze task the paper calls Labyrinth, an A3C agent with an LSTM, using nothing but raw eighty-four by eighty-four pixel images, learned to repeatedly find reward objects — apples worth one point, and portals worth ten that respawn the agent and regenerate every apple already collected — for a final average score around fifty.
[S] What about the scaling claim itself — going from one thread to sixteen?
[G] Averaged over seven Atari games, sixteen threads gives a twenty-four point one times speedup for one-step Q, twenty-two point one times for one-step Sarsa, seventeen point two times for n-step Q, and twelve point five times for A3C — every method at least an order of magnitude. The one-step methods are actually super-linear, which can't come from parallel compute alone; the paper's read is that more threads reduce the bias in one-step bootstrap targets, so those methods need less data, not just less wall-clock time, as thread count grows.
[O] And robustness. Is this fragile to hyperparameters, the way a lot of reinforcement learning is?
[G] That's the fifty-random-learning-rate sweep, on the same five games, for A3C. There's a wide plateau of learning rates where every run reaches a good score, with virtually no collapsed runs inside that band. That's the paper's central evidence the method is robust rather than fragile.
[O] Let me make the strongest version of the case, because I think it's underrated. This isn't just a speed trick. Parallel actor-learners genuinely replace what a replay buffer does — decorrelation — without its memory cost and without the off-policy restriction. And the same method, unmodified in spirit, works on discrete Atari, continuous MuJoCo control, three-D car racing, and a visual maze task. That generality is why later baselines still measure themselves against this one a decade on.
[S] I don't dispute the mechanism. I dispute the specific "beats state of the art in half the time" framing sitting right in that fifty-seven-game table. Every baseline row — DQN, Gorila, Double DQN, Dueling DQN, Prioritized DQN — is copied straight from that method's own original paper. None of it was reproduced under one shared harness. And "eight days on a K40 GPU" versus "four days on sixteen CPU cores" is a wall-clock comparison across two completely different pieces of hardware, not a FLOP-normalized or cost-normalized one. "Half the time" is a real practical win. It's not evidence A3C is more compute-efficient in any hardware-neutral sense.
[G] That's fair, and worth being precise about. The paper never claims a controlled, re-run comparison — it's explicit that it's reporting each method's own published numbers. Whether that reads as a real problem or just how the field did comparisons in twenty sixteen is a judgment call, but it's not the apples-to-apples test the headline framing implies.
[O] What would it actually take to trust the "half the time" framing more?
[G] A few concrete things the paper doesn't do. Multi-seed variance on the full fifty-seven-game table, not just the five-game speed plots. A hardware- or compute-normalized comparison instead of wall-clock days. Per-game scores in the main text rather than pushed into a supplementary table. And a learning-rate robustness check run on games outside the six used for tuning. None of that was standard practice in twenty sixteen, which is itself part of the story.
[S] Here's the one that bothers me more. Look at which games got used where. The six games used to tune A3C's only two real hyperparameters — Beamrider, Breakout, Pong, Q*bert, Seaquest, Space Invaders — are almost exactly the five games used for the learning-speed figure, and exactly the five games used for the robustness sweep. Only Seaquest is the odd one out. So the games where A3C is shown to be fast, stable, and insensitive to learning rate are largely the same games its hyperparameters were fit on. The other fifty-one of fifty-seven games, where the real headline claim actually lives, only appear as an aggregate score.
[O] That's a sharper point than the hardware one, honestly. Is it fatal, though?
[G] Not fatal, but it's the paper's real methodological soft spot. There's a second one sitting inside the headline number itself. Six hundred twenty-three percent mean versus Dueling DQN's three hundred forty-three point eight percent looks like a landslide. The medians — one hundred twelve point six percent against one hundred seventeen point one percent — are essentially tied. On a typical game in the suite the two methods are close, and the mean's enormous gap comes from a handful of outlier games that aren't broken out in the main text, only in a supplementary table. Every one of those fifty-seven-game numbers is also a single run, with no seed variance reported — exactly the kind of gap that "Deep Reinforcement Learning that Matters," two years later, would show can swing results substantially.
[O] So where does that leave the headline claim?
[G] Genuinely well-supported on the mechanism: parallel asynchronous actor-learners really do decorrelate updates the way replay does, across four algorithms and four very different domains. Much shakier on the specific "state of the art in half the time" framing, which is a wall-clock, cross-hardware, single-run, mean-dominated comparison that reads more decisively than the underlying evidence actually supports.
[S] This is also a preview of a fight the whole field would have a few years later, isn't it?
[G] Exactly. This paper predates "Deep RL that Matters" by two years, and single-run tables with no reported variance are precisely the practice that later paper indicts across the entire field, not just here. The method also directly seeded the next generation of baselines: synchronous variants strip the asynchrony back out and run the same update in lockstep across parallel workers, and later scaled-up systems push the same parallel-actor idea from sixteen cores to hundreds of machines. None of that lineage exists without this paper first proving the actor-critic update was stable outside a single-threaded setting at all.
[O] So even the eval-practice critique doesn't really damage the paper's legacy. It's a limitation the whole field inherited, and only noticed later.
[G] That's the honest way to put it.
[G] If there's one sentence, it's this: parallel exploration across many actor-learners can do a replay buffer's decorrelating job without a replay buffer, and that idea alone was general enough to outlive every specific number in this paper's own tables.
[O] My takeaway is that sixteen ordinary CPU cores beating a hundred-thirty-machine cluster's median score is the kind of result that changes what a lab without a huge budget thinks is possible.
[S] And mine is: read the fine print on that fifty-seven-game table before you quote it. "Half the time" is true and worth celebrating, and it's still a wall-clock number across mismatched hardware with no error bars.
