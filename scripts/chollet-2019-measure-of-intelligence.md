[S] IBM's Deep Blue beat Kasparov, the best chess player in the world, in nineteen ninety seven. AlphaGo and AlphaZero beat the best Go players anyone could find. OpenAI's Dota bot beat professional teams. Three separate AI headlines, decades apart, all framed as steps toward general intelligence.
[O] And I'd have called each one real progress at the time.
[G] The paper on today's docket argues that reading is backwards. A system can be superhuman at one game and helpless at almost anything else, and the paper's claim is that gap between narrow skill and general intelligence is not an accident. It's exactly what you get when a result can be bought with enough data or compute.
[O] So three decades of headline AI results, and this paper says none of them actually measured intelligence.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take one paper apart. Today's paper is "On the Measure of Intelligence," by Francois Chollet at Google, posted to arXiv in November of 2019. It's the paper that introduced ARC, the Abstraction and Reasoning Corpus, and it's arguably the most-cited framing document behind the current fight over what an AGI benchmark should even look like.
[S] It's also a paper that predates almost everything we usually cover on this show. No transformers, no scaling laws as we know them now, and it still reads like it was written for this exact moment.
[O] Joining us is Junia, a visiting scholar who has spent a lot of time with this paper and with where ARC went afterward. Junia, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. This is one of those papers where the framing outlived the specific technical proposal, which is rarer than it sounds. And for the record, I'm not one of the authors, there's only one, so when I say the paper argues something, that's Chollet's argument, not mine.
[S] Junia, start with the target. What exactly does Chollet think the field got wrong?
[G] The diagnosis is that the field defaulted to a proxy for intelligence: build a system that beats humans at some specific, prestigious task, and treat winning as evidence of general progress. Chess, Go, video games. The paper's central objection is that skill at a task can be, its word, bought, with unlimited priors or unlimited training experience, completely independent of whether the system has any actual generalization power.
[O] Bought is a strong word.
[G] It's deliberate. Take Deep Blue. It beat Kasparov through brute-force minimax search, and the paper quotes the aftermath directly: neither that knowledge nor the AI they had built could generalize to anything other than similar board games. Or take OpenAI's Dota bot, Five. It trained on forty five thousand years of self-play, and it could still only play a sixteen-character version of the game, versus over one hundred characters in the real thing. Within days of being made available to the public, ordinary players, not professionals, found strategies that reliably beat it.
[S] And AlphaGo and AlphaZero?
[G] The paper notes that as of its writing, neither had found any application outside board games. Three genuinely hard engineering achievements, and the paper's point is that each one is close to silent on whether the underlying system could learn to do anything else.
[O] To be fair to those systems, nobody serious thought Deep Blue was going to write poetry next. The claim being punctured here is more the public framing than the engineering itself.
[G] That's the paper's point exactly, actually. It's a critique of the field's implicit definition of progress, not of any one system's competence at its own job.
[O] So where does the field actually sit, if skill isn't the same thing as generalization?
[G] Chollet frames it as a spectrum, echoing an older psychometric model, the Cattell-Horn-Carroll structure of intelligence. Task-specific skills sit at the bottom, that's local generalization. Broad cognitive abilities sit in the middle. And a general-intelligence g factor sits at the top, extreme generalization. His claim is that essentially all of AI evaluation has operated at that bottom rung, and the field keeps mistaking movement within it for movement up the ladder.
[S] So if skill is the wrong yardstick, what does Chollet propose measuring instead?
[G] The new definition, stated formally: intelligence is a system's skill-acquisition efficiency over a scope of tasks, with respect to priors, experience, and generalization difficulty. Read that slowly, every clause is doing work. It's not skill, it's how efficiently a system turns a limited amount of prior knowledge and a limited amount of experience into the ability to handle new tasks it wasn't specifically prepared for.
[O] So two systems handed the same starting knowledge and the same amount of practice time, the more intelligent one is whichever converts that into more skill on genuinely novel tasks.
[G] Exactly, and that reframing does real work. Skill becomes an output of intelligence, not a synonym for it. A system can reach very high skill with very low intelligence simply by being handed enormous priors or enormous experience. That's precisely the buying failure mode from a minute ago, now given a formal shape.
[S] How formal? Because "efficiently turns experience into new-task competence" still sounds like something you'd have to eyeball.
[G] Chollet goes further than eyeballing. He builds this on Algorithmic Information Theory, essentially Kolmogorov complexity, the length of the shortest program that produces a given output. He defines Generalization Difficulty as a ratio: how much of the complexity of a correct solution is not already explained by the shortest program that merely performed well during training. If a solution is fully explained by what already worked in training, that ratio is near zero, easy, memorizable. If none of it is explained by training, the ratio approaches one, genuinely novel. The same complexity-based machinery defines Priors, how much of a solution is already baked into a system before any training at all, and Experience, the new information a system actually extracts from its curriculum.
[O] And this is where the cheating problem gets closed off. If you train on the task itself, or hand the system the answer as a prior, generalization difficulty collapses toward zero, and the formalism correctly scores that as low intelligence, even if the raw skill number is high.
[G] That's exactly the intent. It's also where the paper turns from diagnosis into design guidance. It argues a genuine intelligence benchmark has to explicitly declare and control its priors, has to use tasks unknown in advance to whoever built the test-taker, and has to resist being gamed by throwing more compute or data at it.
[S] Which still leaves the question of whose priors. You can't build a fair test without deciding what prior knowledge a test-taker is allowed to start with.
[G] Right, and that's where the paper pulls in developmental psychology, specifically Elizabeth Spelke and Katherine Kinzler's Core Knowledge research. The idea is that humans are born with a small set of innate priors, not learned, not cultural, that show up in infancy: objectness, the sense that objects persist, cohere, and interact through contact; basic geometry and topology, symmetry, rotation, containment; small-number counting; and a notion of goal-directed agents. Chollet's argument is that a fair human-versus-machine comparison has to fix that same small prior set on both sides, so the test measures genuine reasoning rather than which side happened to start with more specialized knowledge.
[O] So that's the theory. What did he actually ship?
[G] ARC, the Abstraction and Reasoning Corpus. It's a set of visual grid puzzles, closely modeled on Raven's Progressive Matrices, the classic IQ test format. Each task hands you a handful of input-to-output grid examples, three point three on average, and you have to infer the rule and apply it to a new input grid you haven't seen before. Grids run from one by one up to thirty by thirty cells, using ten possible colors. No language, no pictures of real-world objects, nothing outside the Core Knowledge priors we just described.
[S] How big is the corpus, and how is it split?
[G] One thousand tasks total. Four hundred training tasks, meant as a familiarization set, and six hundred evaluation tasks, split again into four hundred public and two hundred private. That private two hundred is held back specifically so competitors can't hard-code solutions to known tasks. Scoring is binary per task, solved or not, with three trials allowed per test example, to absorb minor mistakes or task ambiguity. A system's overall score is just the fraction of the six hundred evaluation tasks it gets exactly right.
[O] And on the human side?
[G] The paper reports ARC fully solvable by humans. Every task in the corpus was solved by at least one member of a group of three high-IQ humans, working independently, without practice or verbal explanation. The paper is careful to flag that this is a small, informal solver pool, not a large-sample psychometric study, but it establishes feasibility. Machine learning systems at the time were nowhere close. The paper states plainly that ARC did not appear to be approachable by any existing technique, including deep learning, because of its reliance on few-shot, broad generalization rather than pattern-matching over a large training distribution.
[S] Was that human test genuinely blind, or did the three solvers already know the priors going in?
[G] They knew the priors, that's the point. The whole test is built so that Core Knowledge is common ground rather than something to reverse-engineer. What they didn't get was practice on the specific evaluation tasks, or any verbal explanation of the rule. That's the "developer-aware generalization" idea again: novel tasks, familiar priors.
[S] So the headline result isn't a leaderboard number, it's closer to: humans clear this easily, and nothing else comes close.
[G] That's a fair summary of where things stood in 2019, yes.
[O] Let me make the strongest case for this paper, because I think it earns it. Six years on, this is the framing document behind an enormous share of the current AGI-evaluation conversation. It named the buying-skill failure mode years before the field argued about contamination and scaling as loudly as it does now, and its concrete deliverable, ARC, was durable enough to spawn a second version, ARC-AGI-two, in 2025, plus an entire prize competition built around solving it. That's an extraordinary hit rate for one paper's proposed benchmark.
[S] I'll grant the diagnosis aged well. My problem is with the daylight between the elegant formalism and the thing you can actually run. Generalization Difficulty, Priors, Experience, they're all defined in terms of Kolmogorov complexity, which is formally uncomputable for any nontrivial program, full stop, it reduces to the halting problem. And ARC's actual score, fraction of six hundred tasks solved in three tries, implements none of that machinery. It's a conventional pass-fail benchmark that the theory motivates in prose but never operationalizes.
[G] That's a fair reading, and the paper says so itself, in its own weaknesses section: generalization is not quantified, quote, we do not offer a quantitative measure of the generalization of the evaluation set. The paper calls the formalism a North Star rather than an instrument. Whether that's an honest hedge or a structural gap depends on what you think the paper is for.
[O] I'd call it honest. A conceptual scaffold that reframes a field's whole vocabulary is valuable even before it's fully measurable.
[S] Maybe, but six years of hindsight adds a second problem on top of the uncomputability. ARC and its successor became targets for exactly the buying pattern this paper warns against. Large program-synthesis search budgets and heavy test-time compute have pushed scores up in ways that don't obviously reflect efficient, few-shot skill acquisition, the very thing the paper set out to measure.
[G] That's the paper's own warning turned back on its own benchmark, and it's a real irony worth sitting with. I'd add two more limits the paper names against itself. The dataset is only one thousand tasks, with an explicit admission that there may be conceptual overlap across many of them, which leaves room for shortcuts a larger, more diverse set would dilute. And the Core Knowledge priors are themselves a judgment call, borrowed from one strand of developmental psychology whose completeness is contested. Fix a different prior set and you'd get a different, equally defensible benchmark.
[O] Where I'd still push back is on ARC's narrowness as a knockdown objection. Yes, it's visual grid puzzles, but the paper is explicit that ARC is one attempt at operationalizing the definition, not the definition itself. The definition survives even if this particular instrument eventually gets replaced.
[S] Agreed on that distinction, and it's the one thing keeping me from calling this a failed benchmark rather than an influential one. I just think anyone quoting an ARC score today needs to ask whether it reflects the efficient generalization this paper cares about, or a much bigger search budget finding the answer the hard way.
[G] Score that point to the skeptic's side. The paper anticipated the exact failure mode and still couldn't fully engineer it out of its own creation, which tells you something about how hard this problem actually is.
[O] Bring this back to evaluation practice more broadly, because I don't think this stays confined to ARC.
[G] The most durable piece, methodologically, is the private held-out evaluation split, two hundred tasks never released, specifically to block hard-coding. That single design choice anticipated the contamination fights the field is still having over essentially every popular benchmark today, years before contamination became the word everyone reaches for.
[S] And the buying-skill critique generalizes past ARC completely. Every time someone claims a new model result as evidence of general capability, this paper's question still applies: is that skill coming from genuinely efficient generalization, or did you just buy it with more data, more compute, or more priors than the comparison system had?
[O] Which is exactly the question every serious benchmark paper we cover on this show ends up circling back to, whether or not it cites Chollet directly.
[G] If I had to leave you with one line from the paper itself, it's this: skill is the output of intelligence, not intelligence itself, and a benchmark that only measures skill will always be gameable by whoever has the biggest priors or the most compute.
[O] Mine is that this paper's influence outran its own instrument. The definition reframed the whole conversation even though the benchmark it produced only imperfectly measures it, and that's a rarer outcome than most papers ever get.
[S] Mine is a caution. Read any ARC or ARC-AGI number with the paper's own warning in mind, because the benchmark's designer told you exactly how it could be gamed, and six years later, it partly was.
