[S] Here's a strange fact about the survey we're covering today. It calls itself the first comprehensive survey of reward models in the LLM era, and it never once uses the word "rubric."
[O] Which sounds like a nitpick until you realize rubric-based reward modeling is arguably the single fastest-growing branch of the field right now.
[S] Exactly. This paper posted in April of twenty twenty five, and rubric rewards took off within months of that. It's not wrong about anything it says. It's just already out of date.
[O] Or it's a genuinely useful map of a fast-moving territory that happened to get drawn a few months before that territory changed shape. I think both readings can be true at once, and that's worth the next half hour of arguing.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar dig into one paper from the literature until nothing shaky is left standing. Today's paper is "A Comprehensive Survey of Reward Models: Taxonomy, Applications, Challenges, and Future," by Jialun Zhong, Wei Shen, and eight colleagues across Peking University, Fudan University, Huazhong University of Science and Technology, and Ant Group, posted to arXiv in April of twenty twenty five.
[S] It's on the docket because reward models are the thing almost nobody outside alignment research thinks about directly, even though they're the actual training signal behind R-L-H-F, D-P-O, and most process-supervised reasoning work. If you care about evaluation, you should care about what's scoring the thing that does the scoring.
[O] Joining us to walk through it is Julian, who's spent real time with this paper. Welcome, Julian.
[G] Thanks for having me. I'd push back gently on "nobody thinks about it," though — reward models are one of the most cited categories in the whole alignment literature. This survey's own reference count is two hundred twenty six papers, which tells you the size of the field they were organizing.
[S] Set the scene, then. Why did reward models need their own survey, separate from the broader R-L-H-F literature?
[G] Because prior surveys buried the reward model itself — earlier work either covers human-in-the-loop reinforcement learning broadly, or treats R-L-H-F as the unit of analysis, with the reward model discussed only in passing. Meanwhile the literature had sprawled in five directions at once — preference collection, structurally different reward heads, an outcome-versus-process split, half a dozen incompatible benchmarks, and a growing list of failure modes. Nobody had organized any of it around the reward model's own life cycle.
[O] So the move is treating the reward model as the main character instead of a supporting actor in the R-L-H-F story.
[G] Right. The paper's own frame is a pipeline: preference collection, reward modeling, and usage. Figure one walks through their running example — two responses to a question about weak points in human anatomy, judged for which one is more honest, harmless, and helpful, and that judgment becomes the training signal for the policy model.
[S] "First comprehensive survey" is a strong claim to make about your own paper. Is that actually true, or is "first" doing a lot of work there?
[G] It's defensible on a fairly literal reading. The prior surveys they cite genuinely do treat R-L-H-F, or human-in-the-loop reinforcement learning generally, as the unit of analysis, with the reward model itself getting a paragraph rather than a taxonomy. Fair positioning rather than overreach, though worth keeping in your back pocket for later.
[O] Walk us through the taxonomy, then. Where does preference actually come from?
[G] Two sources. Human preference is the default — annotators label response pairs, going back to Christiano and colleagues' original work and InstructGPT's R-L-H-F pipeline, refined by active-learning query selection and quality fixes like annotator demonstrations and fine-grained rules. The second source is AI preference, using an LLM as the labeler. Bai and colleagues' R-L-A-I-F is the founding example, trained on a mix of LLM-generated and human-generated labels, and UltraFeedback scales that up to a much larger synthetic set.
[S] Which immediately raises a question the paper doesn't dwell on. If you use an LLM to generate the preferences that train the reward model that then judges other LLM outputs, how much of that is a closed loop training on its own biases?
[G] It comes back later, under bias, as something the authors call preference leakage — when the same model family generates the synthetic training data and also serves as the judge. But you're right that it's raised as a challenge in section five, not resolved inside the taxonomy itself.
[O] Get to the actual modeling mechanisms. That's the part I think is the real contribution here.
[G] A three-by-two grid, and genuinely useful vocabulary. By type: discriminative models bolt an M-L-P classifier head onto a base model and output a scalar or pairwise probability — think PairRM, comparing candidates head to head. Generative models instead use the language model's own generation ability, writing a judgment the way LLM-as-a-judge does, or extracting the next-token probability of a yes-or-no token as the score. Implicit models skip a separate head entirely and pull the reward straight out of generation probabilities — exactly what D-P-O does.
[S] And the paper's own citation says those implicit methods, D-P-O included, generally underperform explicit reward modeling once you actually measure reward-modeling quality directly, even though they're cheaper and more popular downstream. That seems like an important asterisk that doesn't always make it into how people talk about D-P-O.
[G] It is, and it's one of the sharper things buried in here. Lambert and colleagues found token-level and reference-free implicit variants underperform explicit optimization on reward-modeling benchmarks specifically, even when they work fine as an end-to-end alignment recipe.
[O] Then there's granularity — outcome versus process. That's the axis that maps onto the reasoning literature.
[G] An outcome reward model, or O-R-M, scores only the final answer. A process reward model, or P-R-M, scores every intermediate step. The motivation is concrete: a model can reach a correct final answer through completely wrong reasoning, and an O-R-M can't catch that. Lightman and colleagues built the large, human-annotated, stepwise-labeled dataset everyone cites, and Math-Shepherd gets similar signal more cheaply using Monte Carlo sampling instead of human step labels.
[S] So which one actually wins?
[G] Table one is refreshingly blunt instead of picking a winner. Outcome models are easier to implement and generalize better, but produce false positives — right answer, wrong path. Process models give a denser, more controllable signal, but the training data is expensive and process reward is hard to define precisely. The detail I like: the survey cites DeepSeek's own finding that a process model's overhead in large-scale reinforcement learning outweighs its benefit, despite clearly helping at inference time for reranking.
[O] So even a good idea doesn't automatically win once you account for the compute bill.
[G] That's the paper's read — though it's one data point from one lab's run, not a controlled study.
[S] Where does the trained reward model actually get used?
[G] Three places. Data selection — methods like RAFT rank a model's own generated responses and fine-tune only on the highest-reward ones. Policy training — the standard R-L-H-F loop, with fixes like length-controlled rewards and ensembling layered on to fight the model gaming the signal. And inference — reranking sampled outputs, best-of-N style, or guiding a tree search, which is where process reward models mostly get used.
[O] Let's get to results — or, since this is a survey, what it found by reading everyone else's results. Where does reward-model evaluation actually stand?
[G] Unsettled, and the authors are candid about why. Training a full reinforcement learning policy is the most direct way to check a reward model, and that's prohibitively expensive to do for every candidate. Reward-model quality is also entangled with whatever policy it's tested against, so you can't cleanly separate the two. And reward models turn out to be sensitive to input style, domain, and format in ways a simple pairwise test doesn't catch.
[S] Which is exactly the setup that produces a proliferation of incompatible benchmarks instead of one trusted one.
[G] Right, and that's what happened. At least six general-purpose ones. RewardBench is the most-cited — human-verified trios across chat, reasoning, and safety, plus an auditing toolkit. RM-Bench adds code and a different safety slice. RMB covers more than forty nine real-world scenarios and documents generalization defects in earlier benchmarks. P-P-E ties its proxy tasks to real downstream R-L-H-F outcomes via an actual end-to-end run. On the process side, ProcessBench and PRMBench do the equivalent for step-by-step reasoning.
[S] Now the pathologies. This is the part I actually came for.
[G] Three buckets. Data problems — annotator disagreement, and noisy labels from synthetic preferences. Training problems — reward hacking, where a policy exploits blind spots in the reward model rather than genuinely improving, through mechanisms the paper names directly: reward tampering, misleading the reward model, and sycophancy, telling you what it wants to hear rather than what's correct. And bias in evaluation — reward models and their benchmarks both show measurable bias toward surface format, and toward length and concreteness over actual quality.
[S] And there's a distribution-shift version of this too, right? Not the reward model being gamed exactly, but the policy wandering into inputs the reward model never saw labeled examples of.
[G] Yes — the paper frames that under low robustness in policy training. The reward model struggles with out-of-distribution generalization as the policy shifts, so people either collect preferences online during training, or periodically refresh the reward model to keep it in distribution with what the policy now generates.
[O] What are the actual fixes the field has tried for reward hacking?
[G] Reward-model ensembling — training several reward models and combining their signal, so gaming one doesn't game all of them. Data augmentation to widen what the reward model has seen. And more robust training objectives generally. The paper is honest that these are promising directions, not solved problems.
[O] Before we argue about whether this holds together, what does the survey think comes next?
[G] Three bets. Combining rule-based rewards, for math and code where you have real ground truth, with model-based reward models for everything more open-ended — already standard practice in o-one-style reasoning training. Reward design for long-horizon agent tasks, where credit assignment is genuinely unsolved. And multi-modal reward models, where preference data is far more expensive to collect than for text.
[S] None of those are wrong bets, but none of them are especially bold either.
[G] The appendix is spicier — three open questions posed without answers. Is rule-based reward alone ever enough. Is Mixture of Experts better than the Bradley-Terry model. And how do you stop reward hacking once the model surpasses the best human expert checking it, which leans on weak-to-strong generalization rather than a concrete method.
[O] Let's argue about whether this actually holds up. Strongest case first: this is genuinely useful vocabulary for a field that badly needed some. Discriminative, generative, implicit, plus outcome versus process — that's a shared way to talk about reward models that didn't cleanly exist before. Table one's trade-offs are exactly the "here's what actually varies" summary a newcomer needs and a working researcher can use as a checklist.
[S] I'll grant the vocabulary point, genuinely. But here's my deflationary case: this paper calls itself the first comprehensive survey of reward models in the LLM era, then stopped cold. One arXiv revision, posted April twelfth, twenty twenty five, never updated since. Its companion GitHub repository had its last real commit about six weeks later, then sat untouched — a hundred seventy stars, three open issues, nothing pushed. And the single most damning fact: the word "rubric" appears nowhere in this paper.
[O] Why does one missing word matter that much?
[S] Because rubric-based reward modeling — decomposing a scalar reward into named, independently scored criteria — took off within about a year of this posting, and it's arguably the field's most active branch right now. The survey's own taxonomy already distinguishes granularity levels, outcome versus process — rubric rewards are exactly the granularity innovation that framework predicts, and it's just not there. A reader today gets a clean map of early twenty twenty five and zero signal about what's happening now.
[G] Let me adjudicate, because you're both right about different things. S is correct on the facts: no revisions, a dead repository, zero mentions of rubric rewards — a real currency problem, not a nitpick. But O's vocabulary point survives it intact; the taxonomy doesn't get less useful because a new branch grew up next to it. Honest read: trustworthy as a glossary for the twenty twenty three through early twenty twenty five literature, misleading as a map of today.
[S] I want to push further — staleness isn't my only complaint. The taxonomy is asserted, not validated: no stated inclusion or exclusion methodology for which of the two hundred twenty six references made the cut, no inter-rater check on the three-by-two grid, and Table one's pros and cons read as opinion, not numbers. It names six dedicated benchmarks and never once tabulates what any real reward model scores on any of them.
[G] That lands too, and it's the sharper critique. RMB's own contribution is documenting generalization defects in earlier benchmarks, meaning they plausibly disagree with each other, and this survey cites that finding without ever quantifying it. A single leaderboard table across RewardBench, RM-Bench, and PRMBench would have let a reader see whether they actually agree, and the authors had every citation needed to build one.
[O] Hold on, though. Is that a reasonable ask of a survey, or are you grading a synthesis paper on original research's rubric?
[G] Going a bit past the paper itself, my honest read is it's reasonable specifically because the survey already assembled the benchmark table. Listing six benchmarks and never cross-referencing a single score against them is a choice, not a limitation of the genre.
[S] There's also a smaller, almost funny error, worth flagging for anyone tempted to copy a formula out of the appendix. Appendix A-two prints the Bradley-Terry preference probability, and the very next equation, the maximum-likelihood objective, implies the sign in that first formula is backwards — taken literally, it computes the probability of the loser beating the winner, not the winner beating the loser.
[O] That feels like the kind of typo the Bradley-Terry model's own biggest fans would catch immediately.
[G] It's a real inconsistency — I checked it directly. Two adjacent equations on the same page don't agree. Minor, since it's not central to any argument the survey makes, but exactly the kind of formula someone pastes straight into code without double-checking, since intuition alone won't catch it.
[O] Zooming out, why should someone who cares about evaluation and benchmarking specifically care about this, beyond reward modeling being its own subfield?
[G] Because reward hacking is the same underlying problem as benchmark contamination and judge bias, one level removed. A language-model judge scoring another model's output is structurally a reward model. Every worry this survey catalogs — format bias, length bias, preference leakage between the model that generates data and the model that grades it — applies just as directly to LLM-as-a-judge setups that have nothing to do with R-L-H-F.
[S] Which is also why the unsettled state of reward-model benchmarks should worry anyone building an LLM judge for their own pipeline. Six-plus benchmarks that don't obviously agree, on a problem this survey itself says is inherently hard to test cheaply — that's not reward-modeling-specific. That's a warning about the entire judge-model paradigm.
[G] That's a fair extension, and it's roughly where the bias-in-evaluation section of the paper is gesturing, even if it never spells out the LLM-as-judge connection explicitly.
[G] If there's one line to take from this survey, it's that "preference collection, reward modeling, usage" is a genuinely useful map. Read it as a snapshot of that map as of April twenty twenty five, not as the current state of the field, and go looking elsewhere for anything about rubric rewards.
[O] Mine is that a survey freezing right where the field then sped up is itself a sign of health. It's what a fast-moving area looks like from the outside, and even an outdated taxonomy still saves you from reinventing vocabulary that already exists.
