It's the paper that turned "let's think step by step" from a throwaway phrase into a measured research result.
[S] Joining us is Priya, who has spent real time with this paper and the line of work it kicked off.
[G] Glad to be here. One thing up front: I'm not one of the authors. When I say "the authors show" or "they report," that's me describing their work, not claiming it.
[S] Priya, set the table. What did reasoning with large language models look like right before this paper?
[G] A few months earlier, Jason Wei and colleagues had shown chain-of-thought prompting: you give the model a handful of worked examples in the prompt, each one a question followed by a written-out rationale, then the answer. Do that with a large enough model, and multi-step arithmetic and logical reasoning, tasks that had been stubbornly flat as models scaled, suddenly start improving with scale. That was a big deal.
[O] But it needed those worked examples, hand-written, per task.
[G] Exactly. Somebody had to author step-by-step exemplars for grade-school math, different ones for commonsense questions, different ones again for symbolic puzzles. The framing in the field was that reasoning is a few-shot skill, something you demonstrate before the model will do it. And notably, that original chain-of-thought paper didn't even report a zero-shot baseline. Nobody had checked what happens with no examples at all.
[S] Presumably because it was assumed to be bad.
[G] The numbers backed that assumption up. Plain zero-shot prompting on these tasks was near the floor: seventeen point seven percent on MultiArith, ten point four percent on GSM eight K, twelve point eight percent on Coin Flip. The standing belief was that these system-two tasks, the slow, multi-step kind, genuinely required fine-tuning or hand-crafted demonstrations.
[O] So the real question this paper asks is whether that reasoning ability is already latent in the model, just waiting for the right nudge.
[G] That's the framing exactly. One task-agnostic prompt, zero examples. If it worked, it would mean chain-of-thought reasoning isn't something you teach the model in context, it's something you elicit.
[O] So what's the actual mechanism? The headline is "add one sentence," but there's more plumbing than that, right?
[G] There is, and the plumbing is the more interesting engineering contribution. Zero-shot-CoT is a two-stage prompt, the same model queried twice, no gradient updates at all. Stage one is reasoning extraction: take the question, drop it into the template "Q: the question. A: Let's think step by step," and let the model generate. Because that trigger invites a process instead of a one-word guess, the continuation tends to be an actual reasoning chain.
[S] Which gives you a paragraph, not a gradeable answer.
[G] Right, that's stage two, answer extraction. Take the original question, the trigger, the reasoning the model just generated, and append a second cue, something like "therefore, the answer, arabic numerals, is." Feed that whole concatenation back into the same model, and it emits a clean, parseable answer. A simple rule-based parser reads off the first number, or the first multiple-choice letter, it finds.
[O] So the second prompt is self-augmented, built from the model's own output.
[G] That's the authors' own term for it, and it matters, because it's the piece that makes zero-shot chain-of-thought actually gradeable on standard benchmarks. Without it you'd have free text and no clean way to score accuracy.
[S] And the trigger sentence itself, where did that specific wording come from? Did they just guess right on the first try?
[G] No, worth saying clearly: it's the best performer out of sixteen candidate templates, sorted into three buckets, instructive phrasings that encourage step-by-step reasoning, misleading ones that discourage it or point it the wrong way, and irrelevant ones with nothing to do with reasoning at all. "Let's think step by step" won that comparison on MultiArith. Other instructive phrasings did well too, "let's think about this logically" scored seventy-four point five percent, but misleading and irrelevant templates stayed down near the zero-shot floor, mostly in the teens.
[O] So it wasn't magic, it was a small search that happened to land on something quotable.
[G] Fair way to put it. The mechanism is genuinely simple, one template, two forward passes, no exemplars, no fine-tuning, applied identically across arithmetic, symbolic, and commonsense tasks. But the specific wording was chosen, not discovered on the first guess.
[S] Priya, let's get to the numbers, because that's where I start getting suspicious.
[G] Fair. On the large InstructGPT model, called text-davinci-002, a hundred seventy-five billion parameters, Zero-shot-CoT versus plain zero-shot: MultiArith goes from seventeen point seven percent to seventy-eight point seven percent. GSM eight K goes from ten point four percent to forty point seven percent. Coin Flip goes from twelve point eight percent to ninety-one point four percent. Last Letter Concatenation goes from zero point two percent, essentially nothing, to fifty-seven point six percent.
[O] Those are not small effects.
[G] They're not, and it generalizes across most of their twelve datasets: four of six arithmetic tasks improve substantially, both symbolic tasks improve a lot, and the two logic tasks from BIG-bench, date understanding and tracking shuffled objects, both improve too.
[S] You said "most." Where doesn't it work?
[G] Two places worth flagging. Single-step arithmetic barely moves, because those problems don't need a multi-step chain to begin with. And CommonSenseQA actually gets worse, sixty-eight point eight percent down to sixty-four point six percent. The model reasons fluently toward an answer, but the multiple-choice format sometimes penalizes that fluency, it'll hedge across two or three choices instead of committing to one.
[O] How does this stack up against the few-shot version, the one that needs hand-written examples?
[G] On MultiArith, plain few-shot with eight examples gets thirty-three point eight percent, so demonstrations without rationales barely help at all. Few-shot chain-of-thought with the same eight examples gets ninety-three percent. Zero-shot-CoT, at seventy-eight point seven percent, comfortably beats few-shot without reasoning and comfortably trails few-shot with reasoning.
[S] So it's not a new state of the art.
[G] Correct, and the authors say so themselves, they frame it as the strongest zero-shot baseline, not a replacement for careful few-shot engineering. Though on GSM eight K there's a striking comparison: Zero-shot-CoT's forty point seven percent beats a fine-tuned GPT-3 model at thirty-three percent, and comes close to that same fine-tuned model with a trained verifier on top, at fifty-five percent. A prompt with zero training data is competitive with actual gradient-based fine-tuning.
[O] That's the line that gets me. No training run, no labeled data, just a better prompt.
[G] And they reproduce the core pattern on PaLM at five hundred forty billion parameters, a completely different model family. PaLM's GSM eight K goes from twelve point five percent zero-shot to forty-three percent with Zero-shot-CoT, and adding self-consistency, sampling multiple reasoning paths and taking a majority vote, pushes that to seventy point one percent.
[S] Does size matter here? I'd bet the effect isn't uniform across scale.
[G] It very much isn't, and this is one of the most important results in the paper. On the original, non-instruction-tuned GPT-3 at a hundred seventy-five billion parameters, the same trigger only lifts MultiArith from three point three percent to nineteen percent. Compare that to InstructGPT's seventeen point seven percent to seventy-eight point seven percent on the identical benchmark. Below roughly a hundred billion parameters, on any model family, the effect is flat or negligible. This is an emergent capability, not something that scales in smoothly from small models.
[O] Okay, this is where I make my case. I think this paper is a genuinely big deal, independent of the exact percentages. It reframed the question. Before this, the story was "reasoning is something you demonstrate to the model, a few examples at a time." This paper says the reasoning is already latent in a large enough pretrained model, sitting there, and you can pull it out with one short phrase and zero engineering per task. That's a different research program. You can trace a fairly direct line from this paper to self-consistency, to STaR, to the whole test-time-reasoning wave that eventually became today's RLVR-trained reasoning models.
[S] I'll grant the historical importance, that lineage is real. But I want to push on how much of the "reasoning is latent" story survives their own tables. Table one reports every task twice, once with a task-specific answer cue, once with a generic "the answer is." Switching only the answer-extraction cue, changing nothing about the model's reasoning, moves the zero-shot baseline enormously. StrategyQA jumps from twelve point seven percent to fifty-four point three percent. Coin Flip jumps from twelve point eight percent to fifty-three point eight percent. That's not reasoning improving, that's the harness learning to parse an answer the model could already produce.
[G] That's accurate, and it's a real caveat the authors report themselves, though they don't foreground it as loudly as the headline numbers. The fairest reading takes that parsing-robust baseline as the reference point, and several of the eye-popping deltas shrink once you do.
[O] Sure, but MultiArith and GSM eight K, the two headline numbers everyone quotes, aren't explained away by that. GSM eight K's baseline barely moves between answer formats, ten point four to twelve point five. The core arithmetic result holds up.

InstructGPT goes from seventeen point seven to seventy-eight point seven. So the accurate title isn't "large language models are zero-shot reasoners," it's "instruction-tuned language models at huge scale are zero-shot reasoners." Narrower, and much less catchy.
[G] The paper does show that gap in its own scaling figures, so it's not hidden, but I agree the title oversells relative to what's demonstrated. There's a further point worth raising: everything here runs on closed APIs, GPT-3 and PaLM, queried across a few weeks in spring twenty twenty-two, with no public information about pretraining data and no contamination analysis.
[O] Though the authors do have a decent counter to that, don't they? Consistent gains across totally different task types, arithmetic, symbolic letter concatenation, coin flips, logic puzzles, that's hard to explain by memorizing any one benchmark's answer key.

I'd also flag something S hasn't raised yet: the trigger sentence was chosen from sixteen candidates tested on these same evaluation tasks. That's a mild form of hill-climbing on the test set, even if it's prompt wording rather than model weights.
[S] Which is exactly the kind of thing that should make anyone nervous about treating "let's think step by step" as a universal law of language models, rather than a well-tuned incantation for this particular benchmark suite.
[O] Fine, but even granting every one of those caveats, a completely training-free intervention that closes most of the gap between few-shot's floor and few-shot chain-of-thought's ceiling, across two independent model families, is still a landmark zero-shot result.
[G] I'd score that a split decision. S is right that the magnitude is inflated by instruction tuning and by answer-parsing sensitivity, and the title overreaches what the tables support. O is right that the qualitative finding, latent reasoning becoming accessible through prompting alone at sufficient scale, held up under a lot of scrutiny in the years since, well beyond GPT-3 and PaLM specifically.
[O] What actually changes if you take this result at face value?
[G] Practically, it gives you a nearly free baseline. Before fine-tuning anything or hand-writing few-shot exemplars for a new reasoning task, you can just try Zero-shot-CoT and see where you land, no data collection required. It also pulled a lot of research attention toward inference-time tricks generally, chain-of-thought variants, self-consistency, later tree-of-thought style search, all building on the idea that you can spend compute at inference time, not just training time, to get better reasoning.
[S] There's an evaluation-methodology lesson buried in here too, and it's one we keep returning to on this show. The paper's own answer-extraction sensitivity is a small-scale preview of a much bigger problem: change your grading harness and your headline number changes, even if the model's underlying behavior didn't. That's the same failure mode that shows up later in LLM-as-judge setups and rubric-based grading, where small changes to the scoring prompt swing results by double digits.
[G] Fair extension, though I'd flag it as going a little beyond what this specific paper argues. They report the sensitivity, they don't build a general theory of it.
