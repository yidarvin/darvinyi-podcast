[G] Correct, and worth holding onto for later. Meanwhile, machine translation had just solved its own version of this problem. [O] And?
[G] Promising but shaky. A naive port of unmodified BLEU onto DUC 2001 data gets Spearman correlations with human rankings of point six six to point eight two, depending on the task and how many reference summaries you use. Statistically real, but inconsistent enough that the authors go looking for why.
[S] What's actually wrong with just using BLEU as it is?
[G] BLEU is a precision metric. It asks what fraction of the candidate's n-grams show up in the references. DUC's own human protocol asks something different, coverage, how much of the reference's content did the candidate recover. That's a recall question, not a precision question. Lin and Hovy's diagnosis is that you're using a precision-shaped tool to approximate a recall-shaped judgment, and that mismatch is the thing to fix, not the n-gram idea itself.
[O] Walk us through the fix, Derek.
[G] Start with what coverage actually means, because the whole paper is validated against it. DUC assessors used a tool Lin himself built, called SEE, to step through a human model summary unit by unit, and mark which units of a system's peer summary express the same content, rating each match on a five-point scale, all, most, some, hardly any, or none. Those map to numeric credit, one, three quarters, one half, one quarter, or zero. Sum that over the whole model summary and divide by the number of units, and you get the coverage score, C. Set every match to full credit and you've just recovered plain sentence recall.
[S] So coverage is BLEU's precision question turned upside down, before you've written a single formula.
[G] Exactly, and that's the move Lin and Hovy make explicit. BLEU's formula clips candidate n-gram counts against the references and divides by the candidate's own n-gram count, that's precision, scored on the candidate side. Flip the denominator to the reference side instead, and the same n-gram-matching machinery becomes a recall statistic that lines up with coverage. They call the family Ngram of i, j, a brevity bonus, times an exponentiated, weighted sum of log coverage scores from n equals i up to n equals j, with the weights set to one over the number of n-gram lengths you're averaging.
[O] And the brevity bonus is doing what, exactly?
[G] On paper, it's meant to reward a summary that's shorter but says the same thing, the same job BLEU's brevity penalty does for translation. In practice, they fix it to one for every single experiment in this paper. It's motivated in the text and never actually tested.
[S] Filing that one away.
[G] The formula has two useful special cases. Set i equal to one and j equal to four, weight all four n-gram lengths and average them, and you've reconstructed BLEU's own weighted composite, just recall-flavored instead of precision-flavored. Set i equal to j equal to some single k, and you get one n-gram length on its own, no weighting, no averaging. Ngram of k, k, for a single k, is exactly the formula ROUGE-N adopts a year later, word for word. This paper is where it's first written down and tested.
[O] So the whole question becomes, does the BLEU-shaped weighted composite actually help, or does a single, unweighted n-gram length do just as well or better?
[G] That's the entire experiment, run on DUC 2001 data twice, once on eleven single-document systems doing a hundred-word summary, once on twelve multi-document systems across four summary lengths. Correlation with the human coverage ranking is Spearman's rank order coefficient, and there's a second, independent test, does a statistically significant score gap under the automatic metric predict a significant gap under the human judges, checked with z-tests at five different significance thresholds.

[O] Why would averaging in more information make the metric worse?
[G] The authors' own explanation is architectural. BLEU's long n-gram weighting exists because in machine translation, a long matching span is also a fluency signal, get four words in a row right and that's real evidence the output is grammatical, not just topically similar. DUC's 2001 systems were almost all extractive, lifting sentences straight from the source, so there was no fluency problem for a long n-gram to catch. All the weighted composite does in that regime is punish a summary for legitimate compression. Trim "United States of America" down to "United States," and you've probably improved the summary, but you've dropped a trigram and a four-gram the weighted score wanted credit for.
[O] So unigram isn't winning because content matching is simple, it's winning because the thing BLEU's longer n-grams were built to catch isn't a problem summarization has yet.
[O] Give me the strongest version of what this paper is worth. The reframing is the real contribution, and it's a clean one. Precision to recall, matched to what DUC's human protocol was already measuring, is the direct conceptual ancestor of ROUGE's name and its core formula, a full year before either existed. And the finding underneath it, that plain unigram beats a more sophisticated, BLEU-derived formula, is the kind of counterintuitive result that actually earns its citations. It tells the field something true, don't import a metric's internal architecture wholesale, ask what that architecture was built to catch, and whether your domain has that problem.
