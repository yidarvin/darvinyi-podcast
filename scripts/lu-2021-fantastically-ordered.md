[O] Which either means few-shot prompting was a fragile parlor trick all along, or it means there's a fixable bug, and this paper found the fix. I'm rooting for the second one.

Today's paper is "Fantastically Ordered Prompts and Where to Find Them: Overcoming Few-Shot Prompt Order Sensitivity," by Yao Lu, Max Bartolo, Alastair Moore, Sebastian Riedel, and Pontus Stenetorp, out of University College London, published at ACL twenty twenty-two.
[S] It's on the docket because it's one of the earliest papers to actually quantify how shaky those celebrated GPT-3 few-shot numbers really were, and because the fix it proposes is almost embarrassingly simple.
[O] Joining us is Nadia, who's spent a lot of time with this line of work on prompt sensitivity. Nadia, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is one of those papers where the diagnosis is more startling than the cure.
[S] Set the scene for us. This is right after Brown and colleagues' GPT-3 paper, in-context learning, no gradient updates, just a prompt.
[G] Right, and the standard recipe for building that prompt has two already-known-fragile steps. You choose which training examples go in the context, and you choose a template to turn each example into text. Both had already been shown to move accuracy a lot.
[O] So people were tuning example selection, tuning the template.
[G] And treating the order you concatenate the chosen examples in as a free variable. Structurally inert. To the authors' knowledge, nobody had studied it in detail.
[S] Which is exactly the kind of assumption that turns out to be false.
[G] Exactly. The authors fix everything else, same examples, same template, and vary only the order. They test four sizes of GPT-2, from about a tenth of a billion parameters up to one point five billion, plus two sizes of GPT-3, two point seven billion and the full one hundred seventy-five billion. Eleven established text classification datasets, everything from sentiment to entailment.
[O] And the headline number?
[G] GPT2-XL, the one point five billion parameter model, can surpass ninety percent four-shot accuracy on SST-2 with the right permutation of just four examples. That's comparable to a fully supervised model trained on more than sixty thousand labeled samples.
[S] With four examples.
[G] With four examples, correctly ordered. But here's the sharpest single number in the paper. One specific ordering, evaluated on GPT2-XL, scores eighty-eight point seven percent. Take that exact same ordering, same four examples, same template, and run it on GPT2-Large, one size down at point eight billion parameters, and it drops to fifty-one point six percent. Essentially chance.
[O] So it's not just noisy, it isn't even portable one model size down.
[G] Not portable at all. They ranked all twenty-four permutations of one fixed four-example set by accuracy, for every model size, then computed the pairwise Spearman correlation between every pair of sizes. GPT-3 at one hundred seventy-five billion parameters and GPT-3 at two point seven billion correlate at just point oh five. Essentially zero.
[S] Zero correlation between "good order for the small model" and "good order for the big model."
[G] Right, and it isn't only example order. They ran the same analysis on label pattern, positive-negative-positive-negative versus other arrangements, and got the same seemingly random behavior across sizes.
[O] Does scale at least help?
[G] Somewhat, and only somewhat. Variance visibly narrows with size on SST-2. But on Subj, a subjectivity classification task, real variance survives even at one hundred seventy-five billion parameters. They also ran a separate check, adding more training examples to the context rather than holding it at four, and the variance doesn't shrink. On some settings it even grows. Their reading is that order sensitivity is a fundamental property of in-context learning, not something you scale or example your way past.
[S] So if a good ordering doesn't transfer across models, and reaching for a held-out validation set would defeat the whole point of few-shot learning.
[G] Then you need a fix that costs zero labels. That's the core move, and it's motivated directly by earlier work from Perez and colleagues, who argued that a lot of "few-shot" results secretly leaned on a validation set to pick prompts or hyperparameters, which isn't really few-shot at all. This paper takes that constraint seriously.
[O] So where do you get a validation set with zero labels?
[G] You generate one, from the model itself. For a set of n training examples, four in most of their experiments, you take every one of the n-factorial orderings, twenty-four of them at n equals four, and use each as a prompt to sample a fresh continuation from the language model. Temperature of two, to encourage diversity, blocked n-gram repetition so it doesn't just loop, capped at one hundred twenty-eight generated tokens.
[S] And you get back a generated sentence and a generated label.
[G] You get both, but only the sentence half is kept. The generated label is discarded outright. There's no guarantee it's correct, so trusting it would just reintroduce the same supervision problem they're trying to avoid. Pool the kept sentences across all twenty-four orderings, and you have an unlabeled synthetic validation set. They call it the probing set.
[O] Clever. You're using the model's own generative behavior as a proxy for whether a given ordering puts the model in a sane, well-calibrated state.
[G] That's exactly the intuition, and it comes from an error analysis earlier in the paper. They looked at what failing orderings have in common, and the answer is badly skewed predicted-label distributions. A failing prompt collapses almost every prediction into one class.
[S] So you're scoring orderings by whether they avoid that collapse.
[G] Two ways. Global Entropy takes the model's predicted label across the whole probing set, builds a distribution over classes, and computes its entropy. High entropy means it isn't dumping everything into one label. Local Entropy instead looks at the per-example predictive entropy, averaged across the probing set. High entropy there means the model isn't uniformly overconfident on arbitrary inputs, which they treat as a calibration red flag rather than genuine task competence.
[O] And you rank all twenty-four candidate orderings by one of those two scores.
[G] Right, then keep the top four, the "performant" prompts, and that's what you actually deploy at test time. No labels touched anywhere in the pipeline.
[S] I want to push on one thing. The probing-set generations in their appendix are, honestly, pretty degenerate in places. Short fragments, odd syntax. Is the model's own off-topic rambling really a faithful stand-in for the true input distribution?
[G] That's fair, and the paper doesn't defend it theoretically beyond noting it should share a similar distribution to the training samples. It's an empirical bet, not a principled one. What earns it credibility is that it's tested extensively, across model sizes, across all eleven datasets, and across four different templates for SST-2 alone.
[O] So does the bet pay off?
[G] On average, yes. Global Entropy selection gives a thirteen percent relative improvement across all eleven datasets and every model size tested, four orders of magnitude of scale. Local Entropy is close behind, at nine point six percent relative. On the highest-variance settings the gain reaches thirty percent relative, and across the whole sweep there are no cases of a meaningfully negative effect.
[S] Give me one concrete cell, not an average.
[G] GPT-2 at point eight billion parameters, on SST-2. The baseline, averaging over all twenty-four orderings, is seventy-four point five percent, with a standard deviation of about ten points. Global Entropy selection: eighty-four point eight percent, standard deviation down to about four points. Higher mean, roughly half the spread.
[O] Higher accuracy and more reliable. That's the good version of a method paper.
[S] What's the ceiling, if you cheated and used real validation labels to pick the best four orderings?
[G] They compute exactly that as an Oracle upper bound. Same cell: eighty-eight point nine percent, standard deviation under two points. So there's real headroom the entropy statistics don't close. Oracle beats Global Entropy in nearly every cell they report.
[S] So the zero-label method captures most of the gain, but not all of it.
[G] Correct, and it holds at the top of the scale too, which I'd call the most important robustness check. GPT-3 at the full one hundred seventy-five billion parameters, on the CB entailment task, Global Entropy selection improves accuracy by four point nine points. That's in a setting they explicitly flag as budget-constrained: only two random example sets of twelve permutations each, instead of the usual five sets of twenty-four, because the API access wasn't free.
[O] Money literally limited the experiment.
[G] Literally. Two more checks are worth flagging. First, they swept K, the number of top orderings kept, from one to twenty-four, across all eleven datasets, and the accuracy curve slopes downward in every single case. That means the entropy ranking is doing real work, not picking noise. They settled on K equals four because it was the most stable choice in their preliminary runs, even though K equals one sometimes does just as well. Second, the obvious cheap alternative: split your four training examples in half and use the other half as a mini validation set. They test that directly against Global Entropy across every GPT-2 size on SST-2. For GPT-2 at point eight billion, the split-training-set approach gets seventy-five point one percent, Global Entropy gets eighty-four point eight. Entropy-based probing wins every time, despite using strictly less information, no labels at all, versus labels on half the training set.
[O] Okay, time for the actual argument. My case: this is the unglamorous plumbing fix that makes an entire research paradigm trustworthy. In-context learning was being sold as a labeling-free shortcut, but if the number you report depends on which permutation you happened to run, that shortcut was partly an illusion. This paper proves the illusion is real and gives you a fix that costs zero labeled data, and it works from a model one-tenth the size of GPT-3 up to the full one hundred seventy-five billion. That's a genuinely general result.
[S] My deflationary case starts with a paper this one cites approvingly and doesn't fully out-argue. Zhao and colleagues published "Calibrate before use" about two months before this one, diagnosing basically the same symptom, wild few-shot swings, and fixing it with one extra, content-free forward pass that recenters the output distribution. That's essentially free, amortized over every test input afterward. This paper's fix requires generating a probing set across every one of n-factorial candidate orderings, twenty-four sampling passes at n equals four alone, each up to one hundred twenty-eight tokens, then re-scoring the pooled result under every candidate. The paper itself admits this doesn't scale, they write there can be up to sixty-four factorial possible orders for larger example counts, and it's exactly why they had to shrink the GPT-3 one hundred seventy-five billion experiment to two sets of twelve permutations instead of five sets of twenty-four.
[O] But it isn't just a rehash of calibration. It's a genuinely different lever, searching over orderings before inference, rather than correcting the logits after the fact.
[G] That's the crux, and I think you're both right about different halves of it. It is mechanistically distinct. Calibration re-centers a static, order-independent bias in the softmax; entropy-based ordering avoids orderings whose synthetic-data behavior looks pathological, but leaves the model's raw, uncalibrated output completely untouched. And this paper does something rare for a contemporaneous rival-method comparison: they actually ran Zhao's calibration on their own setup, two-shot SST-2, GPT2-XL, all twenty-four orderings, and report the result plainly. Quote, "although calibration leads to much higher performance, the variance remains high." Uncalibrated accuracy spans roughly fifty-one point six to eighty-five point two percent across those orderings. Calibration shifts the whole distribution up, but a real spread survives in their own figure.
[S] So calibration alone doesn't fix it, this paper's method alone doesn't close the oracle gap, and nobody has tested stacking the two.
[G] Correct, that experiment exists in neither paper. And on the cost point, the skeptic case is right that the tradeoff is never priced. No FLOPs count, no dollar figure, even though they're explicit that the API budget forced them to cut corners on their largest model.
[O] I'll concede the cost accounting is missing. But I'd still call this the more fundamental finding, it's the one that explains why calibration alone can't fully fix the failure mode, since two mechanistically different corrections both leave residual variance on the table.
[S] Fair, and I'll concede the internal test against their own rival is more rigorous than most method papers manage. My harder objection is upstream of both fixes: is this even the right problem to be fixing?
[G] That's worth spelling out, because the paper flags it itself. Liu and colleagues, in a separate paper on selecting good in-context examples, found that once training examples are chosen by nearest-neighbor retrieval, rather than sampled at random the way this paper does, quote, "the order in which they are provided in the prompt has little to no effect on performance." This paper's whole phenomenon is demonstrated under random example selection. Whether it survives in the retrieval-based regime that's closer to real practice is untested here.
[S] So the fragility might be partly an artifact of a worst-case experimental choice, not a fundamental property of in-context learning.
[G] That's a fair reading, and the authors don't close that door. Combine it with the fact that every dataset here is a widely circulated academic benchmark with no contamination check run, and that the method is only demonstrated at small shot counts, mainly four-shot, with no generation tasks and no instruction-tuned models since the paper predates them, and I'd call this a sharp, well-documented diagnosis of a real problem under a specific, somewhat worst-case setup. Not a closed case on how general the problem actually is.
[O] Where does this leave a practitioner today, evaluation and benchmarking people specifically?
[G] Two lessons that outlived the specific method. One, if you're reporting a single few-shot number without stating how the example order was chosen, that number is under-specified. Report a distribution over orderings, or at minimum state that the order was fixed and how. Two, the deeper idea, using a model's own generations to build a synthetic probing signal instead of borrowing labeled data, resurfaces again and again in later self-supervised evaluation and calibration work. This paper is an early, clean instance of that pattern.
[S] It's also a good cautionary tale for anyone building an agent-evaluation harness today. If your scaffold has any equivalent of "the order things get fed to the model," don't assume it's a neutral implementation detail until you've actually checked.
[G] If there's one sentence to take from the paper itself, it's that in few-shot prompting, order is not cosmetic. For the same examples and the same template, it can be the entire difference between state-of-the-art and a coin flip, and the fix that most closely respects the few-shot constraint costs zero additional labels.
