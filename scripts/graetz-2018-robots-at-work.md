[S] And the paper that later called this one its own closest precursor finds the opposite sign, just as tightly estimated. Same technology, same rough decade, opposite direction.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio. I'm the optimist, and I already like where this is going.
[S] I'm the skeptic, and I like where this is going for the opposite reason.
[O] Today we're covering Robots at Work, by Georg Graetz of Uppsala University and Guy Michaels of the London School of Economics, published in the Review of Economics and Statistics in twenty eighteen. It's the paper that opened the empirical study of what industrial robots actually do to an economy, using data across seventeen countries. Joining us is Desmond, who knows this paper and its identification strategy closely. Welcome, Desmond.
[G] Thanks for having me. This paper gets cited constantly as a footnote to Acemoglu and Restrepo's later work, but it deserves to be read on its own. It asks the question at a different level of the economy, and on at least one outcome, it gets a genuinely different answer.
[S] Start with the world this was published into, Desmond. What did people actually believe about robots and jobs before this existed?
[G] Almost entirely theory and anecdote. There was a macro literature worrying about the falling labor share and secular stagnation, and popular books like Brynjolfsson and McAfee's The Second Machine Age, and Martin Ford's The Lights in the Tunnel, arguing robots would displace workers en masse. A UK survey at the time found thirty percent of people believed a robot could take their job within twenty years.
[O] That's a lot of anxiety for very little actual measurement.
[G] Exactly the gap the paper identifies. The empirical automation literature that did exist studied conventional information technology, computers, software, communications equipment, usually at the level of a single firm or a single country. Nobody had comparable data on the specific machines everyone was worried about: autonomous, reprogrammable, multipurpose industrial robots, across many countries at once.
[S] So step one is just getting a real measuring stick.
[G] Right, and it already existed, it just hadn't been used this way. The International Federation of Robotics runs an annual survey of robot suppliers with what the paper calls a gold standard definition: an automatically controlled, reprogrammable, multipurpose manipulator, programmable in three or more axes. That excludes single-purpose equipment, and it gives comparable delivery counts back to nineteen ninety-three.
[O] Which still leaves the harder problem. Even with good data, how do you know robots caused the productivity gains, instead of already-productive industries simply being the ones that could afford to buy robots?
[G] That's the second ingredient, and it's really the paper's contribution: two new instruments for robot adoption, built from each industry's occupational structure back in nineteen eighty, decades before robots were common. That's the piece Acemoglu and Restrepo later credited as their own point of departure, when they took the same IFR data down to the level of American commuting zones.
[O] Walk us through the model first, before the data. What's the actual economic logic?
[G] It's a small task-based model of a firm's technology choice. Within an industry, robots and workers compete over a shared set of tasks, but robots can only take over the share of tasks the industry's production process allows, call that share alpha. A firm that adopts robots at all pays a fixed cost, then hires robots and workers up to a cost-minimizing ratio, set by how large alpha is and how expensive robots are to rent relative to labor.
[S] So the mechanism is just relative prices.
[G] Essentially. As the robot rental rate falls, industries with a higher replaceable-task share adopt earlier and more intensively. That single prediction motivates the whole empirical strategy: instrument robot adoption with the pre-existing task structure, not with realized adoption itself.
[O] And the price side of that story is dramatic, if I remember the numbers right.
[G] It is. Quality-adjusted robot prices fell to roughly a fifth of their nineteen ninety level by two thousand five, across the US, France, Germany, Italy, Sweden, and the UK. Over the same stretch, nominal wages in those same six countries rose one hundred and five percent. Robots got dramatically cheaper relative to labor, and that's the shock the model treats as the ultimate driver of adoption.
[S] Okay, now the data. What are we actually working with?
[G] IFR robot-delivery counts merged with the EU-KLEMS industry accounts, value added, hours, capital stocks, total factor productivity, across fourteen industries in seventeen countries, covering nineteen ninety-three through two thousand seven. That's the US, fourteen European countries, South Korea, and Australia. Robot density, the stock of robots per million hours worked, rose from a seventeen-country average of zero point five eight in nineteen ninety-three to one point four eight by two thousand seven, concentrated in transport equipment, chemicals, and metal industries, heaviest in Germany, Sweden, Belgium, and Italy.
[S] Which brings me to the instruments, because that's the part I actually want to interrogate. How do you build something from nineteen eighty that tells you anything about robots?
[G] Two ways. The first, the paper calls "replaceable hours." Take the IFR's own taxonomy of tasks robots could perform by twenty twelve, map it back onto nineteen eighty US occupations, and compute the share of each industry's nineteen eighty hours performed by occupations that would later become replaceable. It's a technology-supply-side measure, fixed decades before robots were common. The second, "reaching and handling," measures how much of an industry's nineteen eighty physical-task hours involved reaching and handling specifically, the kind of manipulation a robotic arm is actually built for, relative to other physical tasks.
[S] Both interacted with the economy-wide price decline, so it's really a shift-share design.
[G] Exactly, in the spirit of a Bartik instrument. Because the underlying industry characteristic is fixed in nineteen eighty, well before robots diffused, it's meant to be orthogonal to whatever industry-specific shocks hit between ninety-three and two thousand seven. One more design choice worth flagging: the preferred specification uses the percentile of the change in robot density, not the raw change, because raw changes are heavily right-skewed. The mean change in the top quartile of adopters was more than ten times that of the third quartile.
[O] Give us the headline result, Desmond.
[G] Country-industry pairs that densified robots faster from ninety-three to two thousand seven saw significantly larger gains in value added per hour worked. The baseline OLS coefficient is zero point three six, standard error zero point two three, rising to zero point six six, standard error zero point two four, once controls and country and industry trends are added.
[S] And the instrumented version?
[G] The two-stage least squares estimates run zero point eight eight to one point zero five using "replaceable hours," and zero point six nine to one point zero two using "reaching and handling." First-stage F-statistics are sixteen to forty-two, comfortably above weak-instrument thresholds, and using both instruments jointly gives nearly the same answer as either alone.
[S] That's a two-and-a-half to three-times jump from OLS to two-stage least squares. Not a small correction. What's the story?
[G] The authors attribute it to classical measurement error in robot density, which attenuates OLS toward zero. Reasonable, robot density surely is measured with noise, but it isn't the only story consistent with the data. Industries that comply with the instrument, the ones whose adoption is actually driven by nineteen-eighty task structure, could simply have a larger effect than the average industry. The paper can't distinguish a noisy-measurement story from a larger-local-effect story using its own data.
[O] Fair, but even taking the conservative end, what does this mean in aggregate? That's the number I actually care about.
[G] Taking the conservative OLS estimate, with country and industry trends, the paper calculates that without ninety-three to two thousand seven robot densification, labor productivity in robot-using industries would have been about sixteen percent lower. Scaled by those industries' roughly one-third share of value added, economy-wide productivity would have been about five point one percent lower. That translates to robots adding roughly zero point three six percentage points a year to labor productivity growth, against a two point four percent mean.
[O] How does that stack up against other general-purpose technologies historically?
[G] The paper places it near steam's estimated zero point three five point contribution to British productivity growth, from Crafts's two thousand four estimate, though steam's effect was sustained over a period four times as long. It's below information technology's estimated zero point six to one point zero point contribution to European and US productivity growth between nineteen ninety-five and two thousand five.
[S] So robots, over this window, look like a real but middling general-purpose technology. Not steam-engine transformative, not internet-scale.
[G] Fair read. And it's not just labor productivity, total factor productivity also rose, zero point two six to zero point four seven in OLS, zero point six two to zero point seven nine in two-stage least squares, and output prices fell. Both consistent with the model's predictions.
[O] Now the part everyone actually wants to know. What happened to jobs?
[G] On aggregate hours worked, the preferred estimates show no significant effect. Small, imprecisely estimated coefficients, mostly negative in the two-stage least squares specifications, but statistically indistinguishable from zero.
[S] That alone is worth sitting with. The robots-will-take-all-the-jobs story predicts a clearly negative number here, and it doesn't show up.
[O] Not in aggregate. But splitting by skill tells a sharper story, doesn't it?
[G] It does. Robot densification raised the share of hours worked by high- and middle-skilled workers, while cutting low-skilled workers' hours share by roughly five point seven percentage points in OLS, and eight point six to eight point nine percentage points in two-stage least squares, across the observed range of robot-density change. It's a skill-biased pattern, but unlike information technology, it doesn't hollow out the middle. Middle-skilled workers gain too.
[S] And wages?
[G] The preferred specification finds robots raised average hourly wages: zero point zero three nine, standard error zero point zero one seven, in OLS, and zero point zero eight four, standard error zero point zero two one, in two-stage least squares. Both statistically significant, both positive.
[O] Which is the number that's going to start our argument.
[S] It is. But before we get there, there's a firm-level check too, something more concrete than a regression coefficient.
[G] Twenty-four case studies of Universal Robots' customers. Payback periods of two to eighteen months, implying annual returns on the order of twenty-five to two hundred percent. Small and self-selected, but a useful sanity check on the aggregate numbers.
[O] Okay. My case. This is the paper that should have calmed the twenty-tens automation panic and mostly didn't. Robots raise productivity, raise total factor productivity, lower prices, don't touch aggregate hours, and here, raise average wages. That is about as close to a free lunch as a general-purpose technology gets, and it isn't a model, it's seventeen countries of actual delivery data.
[S] My case. Start with the instrument. "Replaceable hours" is built from the IFR's twenty-twelve taxonomy of what robots turned out to be good at, projected backward onto nineteen-eighty occupations. That's not a clean, independent supply shock, it's closer to using a late snapshot of the outcome's own technology to construct a supposedly pre-determined regressor. An industry's nineteen-eighty occupational mix could easily correlate with unrelated secular trends, capital deepening, import competition, organizational modernization, that also raised productivity later, with nothing to do with robots specifically.
[G] That's a real concern, and it's the closest thing this paper has to a contamination problem in the machine-learning sense. Not leakage exactly, but a similar shape. The controls for ICT and non-ICT capital changes, and for task routineness and offshorability, narrow it. They don't close it.

[G] That trade-off is real, and it's exactly what produces the sharpest disagreement between the two papers. Acemoglu and Restrepo's commuting-zone estimate is negative zero point seven seven percent, per additional robot per thousand workers. Opposite signs, both estimated with real precision.
[O] But those aren't necessarily contradictory findings about the same thing, are they? They're different margins of the same economy.
[G] That's the most careful way to put it, and here's my honest read, going a bit beyond just restating the paper: a national industry-level design nets out worker reallocation across regions and sectors. A local commuting-zone design would catch a sharp local decline even while the national average rises, if displaced workers or displaced demand move elsewhere. But neither paper's data can actually test that reconciliation. Neither one observes both the geographic and the country-level margin at the same time.
[S] Which means a reader who only reads one of these two papers walks away with the opposite belief about what robots do to pay. That should bother people more than it usually does.
[O] It bothers me a normal amount. I'd call that an open empirical question, not a strike against either paper.
[G] I'd score it that way too. Where I'd give the skeptic's case more weight is the low-skill finding, actually, since that direction is consistent across both papers and across the theoretical model. I trust it more than either paper's single wage point estimate.
[S] One more, then I'll stop piling on. The sample ends in two thousand seven. No two thousand eight financial crisis, and none of the far larger two-thousand-tens robot buildout led by China. Whatever this paper tells us about robots between ninety-three and two thousand seven, it's silent on the scale of adoption since, and it says nothing about generative AI at all.
[G] Also fair, and the paper doesn't claim otherwise. It's explicitly a study of industrial robots, the IFR's own gold-standard definition, in one specific historical window. Extending its logic to language models or the current robotics wave is an extrapolation the authors never make, and the design can't support.
[O] So if I take the paper at face value, what should actually change in how people talk about automation?
[G] At minimum, stop reasoning from feasibility classifiers, could a robot technically do this job, and start looking for a real, measured dose of the technology, with a real date and a real region or industry attached. That's the methodological move this paper makes, and it's why Acemoglu and Restrepo adopted the same underlying data source afterward.
