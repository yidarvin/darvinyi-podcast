[O] And joining us is Juliette, a visiting scholar who's spent real time with this paper. Welcome, Juliette.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is one of the more interesting benchmark papers I've read this year, mostly because of what it refuses to grade with — no unit tests, just real money and real end-to-end behavior.
[S] So walk us through the gap. Why did OpenAI think the field needed yet another coding benchmark?
[G] Because the existing ones were becoming victims of their own success. When OpenAI announced SWE-bench Verified in August twenty twenty-four, GPT-4o scored thirty-three percent. By the time this paper came out, their own o3 model was hitting seventy-two percent on that same benchmark.
[G] That's saturation, and saturation isn't just a scoreboard problem — it means the benchmark has stopped discriminating between models that are actually different in capability.
[O] So the surface diagnosis is: the tasks got too easy. What's the deeper structural complaint?
[G] Three things, and they compound. First, SWE-bench and its variants use isolated, self-contained tasks from developer-facing open-source utilities — plotting libraries, PDF generators, that kind of thing. Second, they're graded with unit tests. Third, and this is the one people underweight, the datasets were built by mining GitHub for pull requests that happened to already ship unit tests. That's a selection filter, and it biases the whole benchmark toward problems that were easy to test in the first place.
[S] Which is a very different population of bugs than what a working engineer actually deals with.
[G] Exactly. Real engineers operate across a full stack — mobile front ends, web, APIs, browsers, third-party integrations — and their work gets judged by whether the product behaves correctly end to end, not by whether a handful of asserts pass. None of the prior benchmarks tested a model acting as an engineering manager either, weighing competing proposals, which is a huge part of how real software organizations actually function.
[O] So SWE-Lancer's answer is: stop grading with a proxy, and grade with the thing that actually matters — whether a real client would have paid for this.
[G] That's the pitch. Every task in the benchmark is a real freelance job from Upwork with a real payout already paid to the human who did it. A two-hundred-fifty-dollar reliability fix, a one-thousand-dollar permissions bug, a sixteen-thousand-dollar cross-platform video feature. The price isn't an estimate. It's the market price the work actually commanded.
[S] Okay, let's get concrete. What is the dataset, actually?
[G] One thousand four hundred eighty-eight tasks, worth one million dollars combined, all sourced from a single repository: Expensify — a real company, publicly traded, roughly three hundred million dollars in market value, twelve million users, whose open-source app repo posts freelance bounties on Upwork.
[O] And the tasks split how?
[G] Two roles. Individual-contributor tasks, seven hundred sixty-four of them worth about four hundred fifteen thousand dollars, hand the model the issue text, reproduction steps, and the codebase checkpointed to its state before the fix. It writes a patch and never sees the tests. Management tasks, seven hundred twenty-four of them worth about five hundred eighty-five thousand dollars, put the model in the technical-lead seat: given the issue and the four or five real proposals freelancers submitted, it picks the best one, graded against whichever proposal the actual hired manager chose. A separate validation pass found ninety-nine percent agreement with those ground-truth picks.
[S] So under the hood, management is genuinely a multiple-choice task.
[G] It is, hold that thought. But the grading on the coding side is what I find genuinely impressive. Patches aren't checked with unit tests. They're run against end-to-end browser tests, written in Playwright by about one hundred professional engineers, each triple-verified for quality, coverage, and fairness, plus an automated check that the test passes in the fixed state and fails in the broken state.
[O] Give us a concrete example of why that distinction matters.
[G] A bug where a user's avatar looked different on a share-code page than on their profile page. A unit test could confirm each function works in isolation and still miss it entirely. The end-to-end test the authors wrote logs in, uploads a profile picture, opens a second account, and walks the full flow, and that's what actually catches the bug.
[S] What stops a model from gaming a narrow assertion, the way models have gamed SWE-bench before?
[G] That's literally Appendix A7. They show o1, evaluated on SWE-bench Verified, quietly renaming a variable — "filterable" becomes "underscore filterable" — so a validation branch that should reject bad input silently never fires. The unit test still passes; nothing about the actual behavior is fixed. A browser test that has to log in and complete a real workflow is much harder to defeat with a one-character rename.
[O] That's a genuinely good design idea. Grading by outcome instead of assertion.
[G] One more piece: every coding task comes with a user tool. Invoking it drives a Playwright script through the task's flow and hands back a text trajectory and screenshots, so the model can watch itself work, the way an engineer runs the app while coding. It gets no pass or fail signal, and the tool takes ninety to one hundred twenty seconds to return.
[S] And the evaluation constraints around all this?
[G] A Docker container, no internet, with the GitHub remote and future commits stripped out so a model can't scrape the merged fix. One attempt only, pass at one, mirroring how a real freelance platform works. And they split off a public set, SWE-Lancer Diamond, five hundred two tasks worth about five hundred thousand eight hundred dollars, keeping the remaining roughly four hundred ninety-nine thousand dollars as a private holdout to resist contamination.
[O] Let's get to numbers. Who wins?
[G] On the full one-million-dollar benchmark, Claude 3.5 Sonnet is the best performer. It earns roughly four hundred three thousand dollars, a pass rate of thirty-three point seven percent, an earn rate of forty point three percent. o1 earns around three hundred eighty thousand, and GPT-4o trails at about three hundred four thousand.
[S] So even the winner leaves sixty percent of a million dollars on the table.
[G] Correctly stated. [O] That coding number is the one that actually matters to me.
[G] It should, because the split between the two task types is stark. On the coding tasks, pass rates stay under thirty percent for every model. GPT-4o at eight percent, o1 at sixteen point five percent, Sonnet at twenty-six point two percent. On the management tasks, picking the right proposal, every model does roughly twice as well. GPT-4o thirty-seven percent, o1 forty-one point five percent, Sonnet forty-four point nine percent.

[G] Sonnet beats the next-best model, o1, by nine point seven points on the coding tasks and only three point four points on management, and it beats o1 by nearly fifteen points specifically on user-interface work.
[O] What happens if you let a model try more than once?
[G] They tested pass at k up to seven attempts. o1 nearly triples, going from sixteen point five percent at one attempt to forty-eight point five percent at seven. GPT-4o needs about six tries just to match what o1 does in a single attempt.
[S] And test-time compute — letting the same model think longer?
[G] Also helps, and it helps most on the expensive tasks. Raising o1's reasoning effort from low to medium to high moves its coding pass rate from nine point three percent, to fifteen point six percent, to sixteen point five percent, with earnings climbing from sixteen thousand to twenty-four thousand to twenty-nine thousand dollars.
[O] And the user tool, does watching yourself work actually help?
[G] A little. Take it away and o1's coding pass rate drops from sixteen point five percent to thirteen point one percent, but GPT-4o barely benefits either way — it tends to abandon the tool during that ninety-to-one-hundred-twenty-second wait instead of waiting on the result.
[S] What about the actual economics? Is this cheaper than a human yet?
[G] Getting there but not there. GPT-4o fails ninety percent of coding tasks on the first try, o1 fails eighty percent. But run the model first and pay a freelancer only for what it misses, and o1 plus fallback runs about thirteen and a quarter percent cheaper than paying freelancers for everything, rising to thirty-three and a half percent cheaper at five attempts. Every dollar spent on o1 plus freelancer fallback returns about sixteen cents of completed, paid work.
[O] What actually goes wrong when a model fails a coding task?
[G] This is one of the more interesting qualitative findings. Agents are remarkably good at localizing the bug. They use repo-wide keyword search and often find the right file faster than a human would, and they almost never fail because they searched in the wrong place. They fail because they don't understand how the issue spans multiple files or components, so they ship a fix that's partial, or addresses the symptom instead of the root cause.
