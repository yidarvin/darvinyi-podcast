And Sylvain Gelly and colleagues showed U-C-T actually worked, with what the survey calls "remarkable success," in their Go program MoGo.

[O] Almost one a week. That alone tells you why somebody needed to sit down and build a map.
[S] Okay, so what's actually in the loop? Because "tree search plus randomness" describes about six different families of algorithms.
[G] It's four steps, repeated until you run out of compute budget. Selection: starting at the root, a tree policy walks down the existing tree, always picking the child node that looks most urgent to explore further. Expansion: once you reach a node that isn't fully explored, you add one or more new children. Simulation, also called a rollout: a default policy, in the simplest version literally uniform random moves, plays the game forward from that new node all the way to a terminal outcome. And backpropagation: that terminal result gets carried back up through every node visited on the way down, updating each one's visit count and total reward.
[O] And because selection always favors whichever branch currently looks most promising, the tree that results isn't balanced at all, it grows deep and narrow along the interesting lines and stays shallow everywhere else.
[G] Right, and that asymmetry is really the whole point. Here's the part that made it matter for a game like Go specifically: none of those four steps ever needs a hand-crafted evaluation function for a mid-game position, you only ever score a fully finished playout. Classical depth-limited search lives or dies on having a good static evaluator, and nobody had one for Go. M-C-T-S just sidesteps that requirement entirely.
[S] Uniform random rollouts sound like a terrible way to estimate anything, though. Coin-flip games all the way to the end and average the results?
[G] It sounds worse than it is, over enough samples, but you're right that it's the weakest link, which is exactly why the survey spends two entire sections cataloguing ways to make each piece smarter. Section three formalizes the base algorithm and, within it, singles out one specific tree policy that ends up dominating everything downstream, U-C-T itself, from Kocsis and Szepesvári. Each child gets scored as its mean simulated reward, plus two times a constant the paper calls C-p, times the square root of two times the natural log of the parent's visit count, divided by that child's own visit count.
[O] Elena, translate that for the people driving right now.
[G] The first term rewards a child that's been winning. The second term, the square-root piece, rewards a child that hasn't been visited much relative to its parent, so it grows if you keep ignoring it. C-p is the dial between those two, exploit what's working, or go check something under-sampled. Kocsis and Szepesvári show that setting C-p to one over the square root of two satisfies a particular statistical bound for rewards between zero and one. And per the survey's own count, U-C-T is, quote, "by far the most widely used M-C-T-S technique," which is a big part of why people now say M-C-T-S and U-C-T almost interchangeably.
[S] So that's the load-bearing formula. What's actually in the catalog on top of it?
[G] Three sections' worth. Section four is "Variations," adapting the base loop to different problem shapes: single-player search for puzzles, coalition reduction for multiplayer games, ways of handling hidden information and randomness for card and dice games, and sample-based planners borrowed from the AI-planning literature outside games entirely. Section five is "Tree Policy Enhancements," alternatives to plain U-C-B-one for the selection step. The biggest family there is R-A-V-E, rapid action value estimation, alongside its close relative all-moves-as-first, sometimes shortened to A-M-A-F. The idea is you share statistics across positions that transpose into each other by a different move order, so your estimates converge faster instead of treating every path through the tree as a fresh, unrelated experiment. There's also progressive bias, which lets domain knowledge nudge the early search before enough simulations accumulate to trust the raw statistics. Section six is everything else, simulation-policy tweaks, backpropagation weighting, and parallelization, since none of this is free computationally. They lay out four ways to split the search across multiple cores: parallelize the playouts from a single leaf, run independent trees on separate cores and merge them at the root, or share one tree across cores with either one global lock or many small per-branch locks.
[O] That's an enormous amount of machinery for one loop.
[G] It is, and that's rather the survey's whole argument, the loop is simple, but by 2011 the space of tuning knobs around it had exploded, and nobody had drawn the map before.
