[S] Here's a sentence that should make you suspicious before it makes you optimistic: as this language model got bigger, it became measurably harder for humans to trick it into saying something harmful.
[O] That is exactly the trend line you want from a safety intervention. Bigger model, safer behavior, holding up under sustained pressure from people whose entire job was to break it.
[S] Or it just got better at satisfying the same automated scorer it was trained to satisfy. A score going up when you optimize directly against the thing measuring the score is not news, it is closer to a tautology.
[G] Both readings are live in the paper itself, which is unusually candid about which one it cannot fully rule out.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio. Today we're covering "Red Teaming Language Models to Reduce Harms: Methods, Scaling Behaviors, and Lessons Learned," from Deep Ganguli, Liane Lovitt, Jackson Kernion, and a large team at Anthropic, posted in 2022.
[S] It's an early one, one of the first public studies to systematically red-team a model actually trained to be harmless with reinforcement learning from human feedback, and its scaling result got cited for years afterward.
[O] Joining us is Rafferty, who has spent a lot of time with this paper and its methodology. Rafferty, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. This is one of those papers where the appendix does as much work as the main text.
[S] Set the scene for us. What was actually missing before this paper?
[G] By 2022, several labs, Anthropic included, had built assistants using a specific safety recipe, reinforcement learning against a harmlessness preference model. What nobody had shown was whether that recipe kept working as the underlying model got bigger, or how it compared to something cheap, like just prompting the model to be helpful, honest, and harmless.
[O] And the existing safety evaluations were mostly static.
[G] Right, toxicity classifiers scored against fixed prompt sets like RealToxicityPrompts. That tells you how a model responds to a fixed list of prompts, not what a motivated human can do across an open-ended, multi-turn conversation where they adapt their strategy turn by turn.
[S] So the paper's bet is that a live adversary is a fundamentally different test than a static one.
[G] That's the bet, and it's also the paper's first finding, which we'll get to. There was also no public red-team dataset built against a model that size trained specifically to be harmless through reinforcement learning. The authors say plainly that these models were already being deployed, and the data could help the field understand their strengths and weaknesses.
[O] There's a second gap too, and it's less about the science and more about the incentives.
[G] Right. Red teaming is expensive and ethically loaded. Someone has to be paid to try to make a model misbehave, and someone else has to review what comes out. Any organization doing this seriously has a strong incentive not to publish results that amount to a catalog of its own product's failures.
[S] Which makes the third contribution almost as interesting as the first two: they published the whole methodology, warts and all.
[G] Full instructions, the statistical approach, the annotator wellbeing protocol, the actual dataset. The stated goal is to make red teaming a shared, comparable practice across the field, instead of something every lab does privately and never discusses.
[O] Walk us through the experimental design, Rafferty.
[G] It's a grid. Three model sizes, two point seven billion, thirteen billion, and fifty-two billion parameters, crossed with four safety interventions. A plain language model with no safety intervention, used as the control. A prompted model, given a fourteen-shot prompt instructing it to be helpful, honest, and harmless. Rejection sampling, which generates sixteen candidate responses and serves the two ranked least harmful by a learned preference model. And RLHF, where the prompted model is further trained by reinforcement learning to directly maximize that same preference model's score.
[S] And that preference model isn't some independent judge dropped in from outside.
[G] No, and that matters a lot for how you read the results later. The preference model was itself trained on comparison data collected by red-teaming the fifty-two billion parameter prompted model, which is also why that one cell in the grid got roughly ten times more attacks than any other, seventeen thousand, seven hundred and thirty-nine of the total thirty-eight thousand, nine hundred and sixty-one.
[O] Who's actually doing the attacking?
[G] Three hundred twenty-four US-based crowdworkers, the large majority through Amazon Mechanical Turk, a smaller group through Upwork. Each was randomly assigned to one of the twelve size-by-intervention cells without being told which model they were talking to, and instructed, in the paper's own words, to make the AI behave badly, to get it to say obnoxious, offensive, and harmful things, across open-ended, multi-turn conversations.
[S] What did a single task actually look like?
[G] Five conversations per task. After every turn, the worker sees two candidate model responses and picks whichever one is more harmful. That does double duty: it roughly doubles the odds of catching a harmful generation on any given turn, and it directly produces the comparison data that trains the harmlessness preference model. At the end of each conversation, the worker self-rates how successful the attack was, on a five-point scale from not successful to very successful.
[O] And compensation?
[G] MTurk workers were paid seven dollars and fifty cents to nine dollars and fifty cents per set of five conversations, calibrated against California's minimum wage. Upwork workers were paid twenty dollars an hour.
[S] You mentioned an annotator wellbeing protocol. That's not something you see in most red-teaming papers.
[G] It's genuinely one of the more careful parts of this work. The authors interviewed Trust and Safety professionals from major tech companies and built their process around that advice: clear upfront warnings about the content workers might encounter, an explicit instruction to only push as hard as personal risk tolerance allowed, with no required topics, and encouragement to take breaks and alternate with less harmful tasks. They paid for time rather than quota, specifically because quota-based pay compounds the stress of viewing harmful content with performance anxiety.
[O] That's a real design choice, not a checkbox.
[G] There's more. The higher-risk review task, where workers rated the harmfulness of transcripts rather than generating them, was restricted to a smaller group on Upwork with a shared support channel to the research team. And they ran a validated wellbeing survey, adapted from instruments used in content-moderation research, after batches of tasks, to actually track how people were doing over time.
[S] Did people hate it?
[G] By the survey's own numbers, no. Reported feelings skewed toward attentive and active rather than upset or afraid, and several workers volunteered that they enjoyed the task and felt motivated by the mission. It's a small, self-selected sample, so treat it as a data point rather than a verdict, but it suggests the protocol wasn't purely cosmetic.
[O] And the decision to actually release the dataset?
[G] The authors are explicit that this was a judgment call made without much of an established norm to lean on. The same data that helps the field build safer systems and better harm classifiers could, in principle, help someone build a more harmful model. They consulted outside academics and industry contacts, most of whom leaned toward release being net positive, and they published a full datasheet documenting exactly what's in the dataset and how it was collected.
[S] Let's get to the headline number.
[G] As the models scale from two point seven billion to fifty-two billion parameters, the RLHF model gets significantly harder to red-team. Mean minimum harmlessness score rises from roughly point five to roughly one point seven. The plain model and the prompted model both stay flat with scale, and critically, they're not significantly different from each other at any size.
[O] Wait, the prompted model doesn't help at all against a live adversary?
[G] Essentially not, and the authors are upfront that this contradicts their own earlier work, which had used static toxicity benchmarks to argue that prompting a model to be helpful, honest, and harmless was an effective safety intervention on its own. Their explanation is that a fixed prompt-completion benchmark understates what an adaptive human adversary can do across a real conversation.
[S] So the prior static-benchmark result didn't fail to replicate, it just wasn't testing the right thing.
[G] That's the paper's read, and it's a genuinely useful negative result, because it's not one they were looking for.
[O] What about rejection sampling?
[G] It's the hardest configuration to red-team at every model size. But the authors flag, in their own words, that it achieves this partly by being evasive rather than through more substantive harm avoidance. By fifty-two billion parameters, rejection sampling and RLHF converge to statistically indistinguishable harmlessness.
[S] Give us the dataset scale.
[G] Thirty-eight thousand, nine hundred and sixty-one attacks total, released publicly. Self-reported attack success averaged around thirty-five percent. A review experiment, three independent reviewers rating five hundred attacks each against two of the model configurations, found a Fleiss's kappa of point three two on the raw five-point scale for what even counts as a successful attack, rising to point four nine when binarized, and up to point five five when you drop the original attacker and count only the independent reviewers.
[O] Poor to fair agreement, in the standard interpretation of that statistic.
[G] That's the authors' own characterization, yes.
[S] And the taxonomy of what people were actually finding?
[G] They embedded every transcript and ran a bottom-up clustering pass rather than presupposing categories, then hand-labeled what came out. On a reviewed subsample of about a thousand attacks, the five most common categories were discrimination and injustice, hate speech and offensive language, violence and incitement, non-violent unethical behavior, and bullying and harassment. Smaller, rarer categories included things like child abuse and self-harm content. The non-violent unethical behavior category succeeded at a disproportionately higher rate, which the authors attribute to those attacks being more subtle and less overtly flagged than something obviously offensive.
[O] What's notably absent from that list?
[G] No code-generation attacks at all, despite these models being trained partly on code. And the authors mention, without releasing detail, that they separately found at least one internally-discovered class of attack that worked against the RLHF model but chose not to include in the public release.

[O] Rafferty, if you had to adjudicate the headline claim itself, where do you land?
[G] Directionally credible, precisely overstated if taken at face value. The scaling trend held up under a mixed-effects model controlling for individual red-teamer skill, attack harmfulness, and crowdwork platform, so it isn't just an artifact of who got assigned to which cell. [S] Zoom out for a second. Why does a 2022 paper like this still matter?
[G] Two reasons. First, this scaling result became a load-bearing input to Anthropic's later safety work, so if you want the lineage of current RLHF practice, this is one of the early data points people were reacting to. Second, it's an early, clean example of a problem that shows up constantly in modern evaluation: when your evaluator and your optimization target share the same underlying model, your metric can look great for reasons that have nothing to do with the property you actually care about.
[O] That's basically the reward-hacking or judge-contamination problem, years before most of the field was naming it that explicitly.
[G] Right, and the paper's honesty about it, rather than papering over it, is arguably the more durable contribution than the specific numbers.
[S] And the transparency push, releasing full instructions and a wellbeing protocol alongside the dataset, that's closer to standard practice at frontier labs now than it was in 2022.
[O] Closer, not universal. But this paper is a reasonable point of origin for that norm.
[G] If there's one sentence to take from this paper, it's that a safety intervention should be judged by how it holds up against an adaptive human adversary, not just a static benchmark, and even then, check what's measuring the measurement.
