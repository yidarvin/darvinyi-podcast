[O] Say you've got a hundred seed math problems and a budget for fifty thousand queries to a strong teacher model. What's the smartest way to spend that budget?
[S] Two options on the table. Re-answer those same hundred questions over and over in different ways, or spend the budget inventing brand new questions nobody's asked yet.
[O] This paper says the answer isn't a matter of taste. It's one number, and getting it right is worth a twenty-one point accuracy swing on the exact same hundred seeds.
[S] Get it wrong and you're burning teacher queries on data that adds nothing. I like a clean decision rule. I trust this one exactly as far as the evidence behind it, and we're going to look hard at that evidence.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio. I'm the optimist, and I think this is one of the more useful, unglamorous papers we've covered — it's not chasing a new state of the art, it's answering a question every practitioner actually has.
[S] I'm the skeptic, and today's guest is Wade, who's spent real time inside this budget math. The paper is "Balancing Cost and Effectiveness of Synthetic Data Generation Strategies for LLMs," out of Scale AI — Yung-Chieh Chan, George Pu, and a team of five co-authors, presented at a NeurIPS twenty twenty-four workshop on fine-tuning.
[G] Thanks for having me. The way I'd frame it: this is a paper about a question everyone building with synthetic data has, but almost nobody answers rigorously — given a fixed budget of queries to a teacher model and a small set of seed instructions, what should you actually spend those queries on?
[O] Wade, start with the gap. Why does this question even need a paper?
[G] Because the field has a lot of recipes and almost no comparison between them. High-quality human-written data is the gold standard, but it's expensive to produce at scale, so people turn to a teacher model to generate synthetic training data instead. There's already a whole zoo of ways to do that — Self-Instruct, Evol-Instruct and WizardLM, MetaMath, OSS-Instruct and Magicoder. Each one was demonstrated in a single domain, usually math or code, at one generous scale.
[S] So nobody's ever run these head to head under the same budget, on the same tasks?
[G] Not really, no. Prior work optimizes for peak performance once you throw a lot of resources at it. It doesn't tell a practitioner with a hundred seed instructions and a capped query budget which recipe to run, or whether the answer changes as that budget grows. The authors reframe the whole problem as resource allocation rather than peak performance, and that reframing is really the paper's contribution.
[O] So walk us through the setup. What are they actually comparing?
[G] It's a knowledge distillation setup for supervised fine-tuning. You have a seed instruction set, an augmenter model, a teacher model, and a student model you're trying to improve. Critically, they never assume access to ground-truth responses for the seed instructions — only the questions. Every generation strategy is a two-step pipeline: transform the seed instructions somehow, then sample responses from the teacher.
[S] And the three strategies differ only in that first step, transforming the instructions?
[G] Exactly. Answer Augmentation leaves the questions untouched and just samples diverse chain-of-thought responses from the teacher at a moderate temperature — new answers, same prompts. Question Rephrasing uses the augmenter model to reword each seed question before the teacher answers it — new phrasing, same underlying problem. New Question Evolution is the ambitious one: it conditions the augmenter on the seed set to invent genuinely new questions with different final answers, with a self-verification step checking the new question is answerable and well-formatted, then the teacher answers those.
[O] There's a nice example in the paper for this, isn't there?
[G] The running example is a seed question — how many times does Earth rotate on its axis in one day. Answer Augmentation just re-answers it. Question Rephrasing turns it into "the number of rotations Earth makes within a twenty-four hour period" — same answer. New Question invents something related but distinct — how many times does the Moon rotate on its axis in the time it takes to orbit Earth twice — a genuinely new question with a different answer.
[S] Okay, but these strategies aren't free in the same way. How do they account for cost?
[G] They model cost purely by query count to the teacher and augmenter, and they set the ratio of Answer Augmentation to Question Rephrasing to New Question at one to two to two. Answer Augmentation only pays for a teacher call. The other two pay for both an augmenter call and a teacher call, so they cost twice as much per example. That gives them a single organizing variable, which they call the budget ratio — total queries divided by the number of seed instructions. A low ratio means you're not sampling your seed set very hard. A high ratio means you're squeezing the same small seed set for a lot of data.
[O] And the models involved — who's the teacher, who's the student?
[G] The student being fine-tuned is Llama 2 7B Chat. The teacher and the augmenter are both Llama 3.1 70B Instruct — so there's a large capability gap to work with. On the three evaluation sets before any fine-tuning, the student starts at fifteen percent on GSM8k, twenty three point seven percent on Spider, and forty seven point six percent on ARC-C. The teacher scores ninety four point six percent, seventy seven point three percent, and ninety three point nine percent respectively. That gap is the whole point — it makes the gains from distillation legible.
[S] What are the actual tasks?
[G] Three, deliberately outside the usual math-only comfort zone. GSM8k for grade-school math, with seven thousand five hundred seed instructions, graded on exact-match accuracy. Spider for text-to-SQL — two hundred databases across a hundred thirty-eight domains — with seven thousand seed instructions, graded on execution accuracy. And ARC-C, grade-school science reasoning questions, with about three thousand seed instructions on the challenge split, exact-match accuracy.
[O] That's a lot of combinations to sweep. How big is the grid?
[G] Each task gets sharded into three seed set sizes — one hundred, one thousand, and the full set — giving nine seed instruction sets total. Each of those is paired with each of the three generation strategies and swept across seven query budgets, from one thousand up to one hundred thousand. That's eighty-one distinct training runs.
[S] With that much noise across eighty-one runs, how do they even read off a clean answer?
[G] They fit a scaling law to each task-and-method curve, adapting a data-constrained scaling formulation from prior work on scaling laws under repeated data. The intuition is that repeatedly querying the same fixed seed set adds less new information each time — the marginal value of another query decays. The fitted curve decomposes accuracy into a ceiling, a term for how much scaling the seed size itself would help, and a term for how much scaling the query budget helps, with that decay built in.
[O] And does the fit actually hold up numerically?
[G] It fits with an R squared over point nine eight across every task and method combination, optimized to minimize squared error. One wrinkle worth flagging now, because it matters later: before fitting, they apply a running-max adjustment to the raw accuracy numbers, so each point reflects the best accuracy seen so far at that budget or lower. That forces the curves to be monotonically increasing.
[S] So give us the headline. Is there actually a single best strategy?
[G] No, and that's the whole finding. There's no strategy that wins everywhere. When the budget ratio is low, Answer Augmentation wins — spend on new responses to the prompts you already have. When the ratio is high, generating new prompts wins, and New Question Evolution generally beats Question Rephrasing in both cost-efficiency and how far it scales.
[O] Where's the actual crossover point?
[G] It depends on how many seed instructions you start with. With only a hundred seeds, the switch from favoring new answers to favoring new questions happens somewhere between a budget ratio of twenty-seven and fifty-one, which works out to roughly three thousand to five thousand generated samples. As the seed set grows, that crossover ratio shrinks — averaging seventeen point six at one thousand seeds, and sixteen point four at the largest seed sizes tested.
[S] Those are pretty tight numbers to hang a rule on. How much should I trust a specific ratio like seventeen point six versus, say, twenty?
[G] Fair question, and I'd hold off fully answering that until we get to the fit itself — there's a real caveat there. But qualitatively, the direction is consistent across all three tasks and all three seed sizes, which is more convincing than any single number.
[O] Give me the most dramatic case in the paper.
[G] GSM8k with only a hundred seed instructions. New Question Evolution keeps improving accuracy as you scale the synthetic dataset past fifty thousand examples — over five hundred times the size of the original seed set — while the other two strategies plateau. At a budget of one hundred thousand queries, New Question reaches fifty three point five percent, Answer Augmentation only reaches thirty two point one percent. That's a twenty-one point gap, from the exact same hundred starting questions.
[S] And when data isn't scarce, does any of this still matter?
[G] Barely. At seven thousand five hundred GSM8k seeds and a hundred-thousand-query budget, all three methods land close together — Answer Augmentation at sixty five point eight percent, Question Rephrasing at sixty seven point six, New Question at sixty eight point two. That's within about two and a half points. The authors' own framing is that these design choices "matter substantially more in low to mid data regimes than in high data regimes."
[O] Let's get into the ablations. What happens if you use a cheaper augmenter model?
[G] It depends entirely on the strategy. Question Rephrasing barely notices — rephrasing turns out to be an easy enough task that even a weak augmenter handles it fine. New Question Evolution is a different story. Swap in Llama 2 7B as the augmenter — a model that itself only scores around fifteen percent on GSM8k — and New Question's accuracy drops by roughly fifteen points at ten thousand samples compared to using a stronger augmenter. You can cheap out on the augmenter for rephrasing. You can't for inventing new questions.
[S] What about verifying the teacher's answers against ground truth? Surely filtering out wrong responses helps.
[G] You'd think so. The teacher gets roughly ten percent of instructions wrong, so there's real noise in the training data. But filtering those wrong answers out gives no measurable improvement over just training on more unfiltered data of the same size. They tested this on Answer Augmentation and Question Rephrasing on Spider — New Question's answers aren't independently verifiable by construction, so it wasn't tested there. Two explanations offered: the student can still extract a useful learning signal even from an incorrect teacher response, echoing an earlier finding that a weaker model improved even when trained on wrong responses from a stronger one, and filtering shrinks the diversity of the dataset, which may cancel out the benefit of correctness.
[O] And they check whether all this holds for a different student model too?
[G] Yes, they swap the student from Llama 2 7B to Mistral 7B, using the GSM8k setup with one thousand seed instructions. Same qualitative pattern — Answer Augmentation wins at low budget ratios, new instructions win as the ratio climbs. The crossover for Mistral lands at a ratio of twelve, which sits inside the eight-to-twenty-six range they found for Llama 2 at that same medium seed size. That's their case for generalization across model families.
[O] Here's my optimist read. This is a genuinely actionable rule, and it's cheap to check for yourself — one number, budget divided by seed count, tells you where to spend. It holds across three very different tasks, math, SQL, and science QA, and it survives a swap to a different student model entirely. That's not nothing for a workshop paper.
[S] I'll grant it's a clean rule. But look at what it's actually validated against. Every comparison in this paper is internal — three in-house recipes measured against each other at matched sample counts, same teacher, same student. There's no head-to-head against real human-written data at the same budget, beyond one small appendix sanity check that synthetic data is roughly comparable to real data at equal size. And there's no comparison to any established synthetic-data pipeline people already use — no Evol-Instruct, no WizardMath, no Magpie, no OSS-Instruct. So what we actually learn is the ranking of three in-house variants, not whether any of them is competitive with what the field already runs.
[G] That's accurate, and it's the paper's clearest limitation. The appendix check you're describing is real but small — it generates synthetic data sized to match the original training set and confirms comparable effectiveness to real data, but it's a spot check, not a systematic baseline across the whole budget grid.
[O] Even so, as an internal decision rule for someone who's already committed to a synthetic pipeline and just needs to choose a strategy, that ranking is still useful information.
[S] Here's my second, sharper concern. Those crossover budget ratios — seventeen point six, sixteen point four — are read off the fitted scaling-law curves, not raw data points. And the fitting procedure applies that running-max adjustment we mentioned earlier, which forces the curves to only ever go up. That adjustment exists specifically because Answer Augmentation and Question Rephrasing actually lose accuracy at high budget ratios from over-sampling the same seed set — and the smoothed, monotonic curves used to report the crossover points hide that degradation. On top of that, every cell in this eighty-one-run grid is a single training run, no repeats, no error bars. So how much should I trust the difference between, say, Spider's crossover at eight point three and GSM8k's at fourteen point four?
[G] You're right to flag both of those, and I'd score this one clearly to you. The running-max adjustment is doing real work to hide a real phenomenon — the degradation at high budget ratios is itself a practically important thing to know, and smoothing it away for a cleaner crossover number trades information for readability. And with single runs and no error bars, precision to the first decimal place on a crossover ratio is more than the evidence supports. The direction of the rule — low ratio favors new answers, high ratio favors new questions — is consistent and, I think, real. The exact threshold numbers should be read as ballpark, not as a lookup table.
[O] The Mistral cross-check at least shows the qualitative pattern isn't an artifact of one specific student model.
[S] Agreed, that's a genuine point in its favor. My last concern is contamination. GSM8k, Spider, and ARC-C, along with their public test splits, are almost certainly somewhere in Llama 3.1's pretraining data — that's a big part of why the teacher scores ninety four point six percent on GSM8k to begin with. If the teacher's competence on these tasks is partly memorized rather than reasoned, distillation could just be transferring memorization, and the "new" questions the augmenter invents might be echoes of things it's already seen rather than genuinely novel problems.
[G] The paper does not run any decontamination analysis, so that's a fair gap, not a resolved question. My own read, going a bit past what's actually in the paper, is that the relative ranking between the three strategies is probably less sensitive to this than the absolute accuracy numbers are — contamination would likely inflate all three methods somewhat similarly, since they share the same teacher. But that's my inference, not something the authors test directly.
[O] So the practical takeaway for a practitioner. If you're building a fine-tuning dataset and you know your seed count and your query budget, you can compute this ratio in about thirty seconds and get a real recommendation, rather than guessing between three recipes.
[S] And the connection to how we should think about evals more broadly — this is a nice small case study in why internal-only comparisons and single-run grids can produce a rule that's directionally right but numerically overconfident. The fix isn't complicated — repeated runs, error bars, an external baseline — it's just expensive, and workshop papers don't always have the budget for it either.
[G] The paper's own bottom line is that the choice of augmentation strategy is governed by the ratio of query budget to seed size, and that this choice matters far less once you already have abundant data.
[O] My takeaway is that this is a rare synthetic-data paper that actually tells you what to do instead of just showing you a new benchmark score, and that's worth more to a working practitioner than another leaderboard entry.
