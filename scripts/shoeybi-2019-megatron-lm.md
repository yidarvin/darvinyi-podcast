[S] Today it's Megatron-LM, Training Multi-Billion Parameter Language Models Using Model Parallelism, out of NVIDIA, by Mohammad Shoeybi, Mostofa Patwary, Raul Puri, and colleagues, posted to arXiv in September of twenty nineteen.
[O] And joining us to walk through it is Sophie, who's spent real time with this paper's math. Sophie, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. This is one of those papers where the trick fits on an index card, but the reasoning behind why that trick works is genuinely elegant, and I think it rewards slowing down on the linear algebra.
[O] Let's set the scene first. Sophie, why couldn't people just keep making transformers bigger in twenty nineteen?
[G] Pure memory arithmetic. To train with standard tooling, the entire model has to live on one accelerator, and a thirty-two gigabyte V100 fills up fast once you count everything training actually stores: the weights themselves, the gradients, and with Adam, two extra optimizer moments per parameter, on top of the activations backprop needs to hold onto. A couple billion parameters and you're already out of room. Data parallelism doesn't rescue you either, because replicating the model across workers means every worker still carries a full copy, so the memory ceiling never moves. The only way past it is splitting the model itself.
[S] There were already frameworks that did that, though. What was wrong with GPipe and Mesh-TensorFlow?
[G] Each came with a real tax. GPipe slices the model into pipeline stages, which have idle bubbles and need optimizer changes to stay consistent. Mesh-TensorFlow can express general distributed tensor computation, but you write your model in its own language and lean on a custom compiler. Both ask you to leave your existing PyTorch code behind entirely.
[O] So what did NVIDIA actually build instead?
[G] Something they call intra-layer model parallelism, what the field now just calls tensor parallelism. Instead of cutting the network into pipeline stages or replicating it wholesale, you shard the weight matrices inside each layer across a small group of GPUs. The entire craft is choosing how to split each matrix so the partial results line up again with almost no talking between GPUs.
[S] Walk me through that, because splitting a matrix multiply usually means you need to resync before the next operation.
[G] Right, and that's exactly the trap they avoid. Take the feed-forward block: a GEMM, then a GeLU nonlinearity, then a second GEMM. If you split the first weight matrix by rows, each GPU only computes a partial sum, and because GeLU is nonlinear, you can't just add those partial sums after the fact, you have to synchronize before applying GeLU at all. Split that same matrix by columns instead, and GeLU applies independently to each shard, no synchronization needed. Then you split the second matrix by rows, so it consumes the sharded GeLU output directly, and you sum the partial results with one all-reduce as the block exits.
[O] And that's the whole trick, column-split then row-split?
[G] That's the geometric heart of it, yes. They formalize the synchronization itself as a pair of conjugate operators, f and g. f is an identity operation on the forward pass and an all-reduce on the backward pass; g is the mirror image, all-reduce forward, identity backward. Each one is maybe five lines of PyTorch autograd code. No custom kernel, no compiler pass, just two small classes.
[S] And self-attention gets the same treatment?
[G] Almost for free, because attention heads are already independent of each other. They partition the query, key, and value projections by columns, so each attention head's matrix multiply lives entirely on one GPU with no communication needed to compute attention itself. Then the output projection gets split by rows, so it consumes that parallel result directly. One all-reduce forward, one backward, same as the MLP block.
[O] So stack both blocks together and you get...
[G] A complete model-parallel transformer layer that needs exactly four collective operations total: two all-reduces forward, two backward. Everything else in the layer, layer normalization, dropout, the residual connections, gets duplicated redundantly on every GPU in the group rather than computed once and broadcast, because recomputing something small is cheaper than moving a tensor across the wire.
[S] What about the vocabulary and the embedding layer? That's usually the expensive part in a big language model.
[G] It is, and they handle it cleverly. The input embedding is sharded along the vocabulary dimension, which needs one all-reduce. But the output embedding shares weights with the input embedding in these models, and naively computing logits and gathering them would mean communicating batch-size times sequence-length times vocabulary-size numbers, which is enormous once your vocabulary is in the tens of thousands. So instead they fuse the output projection directly with the cross-entropy loss, which shrinks that communication down to just batch-size times sequence-length scalars, the losses themselves, instead of the full logit matrix. GPT-2's vocabulary is padded from fifty thousand two hundred fifty-seven up to fifty-one thousand two hundred so it divides cleanly across GPUs.
[O] And how does this compose with ordinary data parallelism?
[G] Because tensor parallelism and data parallelism are orthogonal, you run both at once. They keep each tensor-parallel group confined inside a single server, eight GPUs wired together by NVSwitch at three hundred gigabytes per second, and form data-parallel groups across servers, connected at a slower one hundred gigabytes per second over InfiniBand. The eight point three billion parameter run is eight-way tensor parallel crossed with sixty-four-way data parallel, five hundred twelve GPUs total.
[S] And genuinely none of this needs a custom kernel or compiler? That's the whole pitch?
[G] That's the whole pitch, and it's why it spread as fast as it did. It's a handful of synchronization primitives dropped into an otherwise completely ordinary PyTorch transformer.
[O] Let's get to the numbers, then. What did that buy them in raw throughput?
[G] Their baseline is deliberately tough to beat: a one point two billion parameter model on a single V100 sustains thirty-nine teraflops per second, which is already thirty percent of that GPU's theoretical peak. Scale up to the eight point three billion parameter model across five hundred twelve GPUs, and the whole application sustains fifteen point one petaflops per second, seventy-six percent of what perfect linear scaling from that single-GPU baseline would predict. The efficiency degrades gracefully as you add tensor-parallel GPUs alone, one hundred percent, ninety-five, eighty-two, seventy-seven percent at one, two, four, and eight GPUs, and then ninety-six down to seventy-four percent once you layer on sixty-four-way data parallelism up to the full five hundred twelve.
[S] Is that weak scaling or strong scaling? Because those tell you very different things.
[G] Weak scaling, and that distinction matters. The problem itself grows with the GPU count, roughly one billion parameters added per GPU, so it's answering "can I train a proportionally bigger model efficiently," not "can I train this exact model faster." They do report a strong-scaling number too, in the appendix, holding the one point two billion parameter model fixed and just adding GPUs: two GPUs gets you one point six four times the speed, four gets two point three four times, eight gets two point nine eight times. Diminishing returns show up fast once per-GPU compute shrinks and communication starts to dominate.
[O] What about accuracy, not just raw speed?
[G] It scales monotonically with size, and the headline numbers are real state of the art for the time. Zero-shot on WikiText103, the eight point three billion model hits a perplexity of ten point eight one against a previous best of fifteen point seven nine. On LAMBADA cloze accuracy, sixty-six point five one percent against a previous sixty-three point two four. Both benchmarks improve cleanly as you go from three hundred fifty-five million parameters up through two point five billion to eight point three billion.
[S] Did they check for contamination, training on their own test data?
[G] They ran the standard eight-gram overlap check. WikiText103's test set shows at most ten point eight percent overlap with their training corpus, though they note the WikiText103 test set already overlaps nine point oh nine percent with its own official training set, so that's roughly the expected background level, not something unique to their pipeline. LAMBADA's overlap tops out at one point four percent. The training corpus itself is Wikipedia, CC-Stories, RealNews, and OpenWebText, deduplicated with locality-sensitive hashing down to one hundred seventy-four gigabytes of text.
[O] There's also a BERT result buried in here that sounds like a genuine scientific finding, not just an engineering one.
[G] It is, and it's my favorite part of the paper. ALBERT had already reported that naively growing BERT past the three hundred thirty-six million parameter BERT-Large size makes it worse, not better. This team traced the cause to architecture rather than capacity: rearranging where layer normalization sits relative to the residual connection eliminates the training instability entirely and reaches a lower loss. With that one change, held-out validation perplexity falls monotonically, one point five eight, then one point three oh, then one point one six, across their three hundred thirty-six million, one point three billion, and three point nine billion parameter models. The three point nine billion model sets development-set state of the art across MNLI, QQP, and both SQuAD tasks, and on RACE it hits eighty-nine point five percent as a single model and ninety point nine percent as an ensemble, edging out ALBERT's prior ensemble result of eighty-nine point four.
[S] That's a nice result, but is the rearrangement actually novel, or were other people finding the same thing around the same time?
[G] Fair to flag. The paper is careful to say they're the first to report this specific change for BERT, not the first to touch this idea at all, and related pre-normalization transformer work was emerging concurrently elsewhere. It's also worth saying they don't isolate the layer-norm reordering from a second change they make at the same time, rescaling the residual connections by a factor tied to the number of layers. Both changes ship together, so you can't tell from this paper alone how much of the improvement is which ingredient.
[O] Alright, let's make the case properly. Sophie, hold the adjudication, I want to argue this first. The point of this paper isn't the percentage, it's that they broke the single-GPU memory wall with almost nothing: no new compiler, no rewritten model, just synchronization primitives inserted into code researchers already had. That's exactly the kind of result that gets copied everywhere, and it did. This became the tensor-parallelism axis that GPT-3, LLaMA, BloombergGPT, FlashAttention, and HELM are all built on top of.
[S] I don't dispute the influence. I dispute how the headline number is being read. Seventy-six percent scaling efficiency, applied to a baseline running at thirty percent of peak FLOPs, works out to roughly twenty-three percent absolute hardware utilization at eight point three billion parameters. Only one of those two numbers is the actual fraction of the chip you're using. And "five hundred twelve GPUs" quietly conflates two axes: tensor parallelism itself only ever goes eight-way, confined inside a single NVSwitch-connected server. The paper says outright that anything past sixteen billion parameters needs inter-node model parallelism, meaning the tensor-parallel dimension doesn't scale past one node at all.
[G] Both points are accurate, and worth saying plainly rather than burying in an appendix. The paper is honest about the node-local limit, it says so in its own future-work section, so call that a disclosed constraint rather than a hidden flaw. The seventy-six versus twenty-three framing is fair to flag, though: it's not wrong, it's just the more flattering of two true numbers, and which one leads the abstract is a pattern worth watching for generally.
[O] Even so, "flattering framing" isn't the same complaint as "the engineering doesn't work." The two all-reduces per layer are real, the eight point three billion parameter model genuinely trained and genuinely converged.
[S] Agreed, it converged. My other objection is what memory problem is actually solved. Activations still grow with batch size and sequence length, so they lean on recomputing activations during the backward pass on every single layer rather than storing them. And the redundancy across data-parallel replicas, every replica holding its own full copy of the optimizer state, is left completely untouched here.
[G] That's precisely the gap that ZeRO and fully sharded data parallel later filled, a few years after this paper. So it's fair to say tensor parallelism, as presented here, is necessary but not sufficient on its own for the largest models; the paper itself frames it as one axis meant to combine with pipeline and data parallelism, not a complete solution by itself.
[O] One more thing worth naming: the GPT-2 evaluation is narrow, just zero-shot perplexity and cloze accuracy on two datasets, no broader downstream suite. That's the metric that tracks the training objective most directly, which makes the win almost circular by design.
[S] Which is exactly the caution I'd carry into reading any big-model scaling paper since. Two questions, every time: is this weak scaling or strong scaling, and seventy-six percent relative to what absolute baseline?
[O] Zooming out, then, what actually changed in the field because of this paper?
[G] Tensor parallelism became one leg of what people now call three-dimensional parallelism, tensor times pipeline times data, and it's the default building block underneath essentially every large transformer trained since. NVIDIA's own Megatron codebase got reused directly for follow-on systems like Microsoft's seventeen billion parameter Turing-NLG. The idea that a model-parallel scheme could be a few lines of autograd code instead of a bespoke compiler is the part that actually traveled.
[S] And it's traveled with its blind spots intact. The field mostly quotes "seventy-six percent at five hundred twelve GPUs" without the weak-scaling or node-local caveats, a small distortion that compounds every time someone reuses the headline without reading the appendix.
