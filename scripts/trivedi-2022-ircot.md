[O] Here's the claim that stopped me: a three-billion-parameter model, using nothing but plain old B-M-25 search, can out-retrieve and out-answer a GPT-3 fifty-eight times its size, just by changing how it asks its search queries.
[S] Fifty-eight times bigger and it loses on every dataset they tested — that's the kind of headline number that makes me want to see exactly what "asking better" means before I believe it wasn't luck.
[O] It's not one lucky benchmark either. It holds across multiple datasets and model families, and even when they swap which dataset the demonstrations come from.
[S] We'll see. I've got questions about a leaderboard table later in this paper before I let "state of the art" stand unchallenged.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the litsearch dot darvinyi dot com map. Today's paper is "Interleaving Retrieval with Chain-of-Thought Reasoning for Knowledge-Intensive Multi-Step Questions," the paper that introduced I-R-CoT, by Harsh Trivedi and colleagues at Stony Brook University and the Allen Institute for A-I. It was posted in December twenty twenty-two, published at A-C-L in twenty twenty-three, and has already been cited more than eleven hundred times. Joining us is Priya, who has spent real time with this paper.
[G] Glad to be here. And since I wasn't one of the authors, when I say "they" found something, that means the paper's authors, not me.
[S] Priya, give us the one-sentence version before we dig in.
[G] A training-free method that alternates one sentence of chain-of-thought reasoning with one fresh retrieval call, using each new reasoning sentence as the very next search query, so the system never has to guess up front everything it will eventually need to look up.
[O] Start with the gap. Chain-of-thought prompting already worked for multi-step reasoning — why wasn't that enough on its own?
[G] Because chain-of-thought only works when everything the model needs is already inside its parameters or its prompt. For open-domain questions that need outside facts, the standard fix was retrieve-and-read: use the question once, pull some text, then answer. The paper's central claim is that this one-step approach breaks for genuinely multi-step questions, because what you need to retrieve for the second hop depends on what you've already figured out from the first hop.
[S] Give us the concrete example, because I think it's the clearest way to see the failure.
[G] Their running example is the question, "In what country was Lost Gravity manufactured?" Lost Gravity is a roller coaster. Retrieve using just that question and you get the Wikipedia page for the roller coaster, which never mentions a country. Only after a model infers that Lost Gravity was built by a company called Mack Rides can it form a query that actually surfaces the fact that Mack Rides is based in Germany. Retrieve once on the original question, and that second fact is essentially unreachable.
[O] So the two failure modes reinforce each other. No retrieval means the model is likely to hallucinate the first hop, and no first hop means there's nothing to search on for the second.
[G] Exactly that. Without retrieved facts, the reasoning is liable to just make something up. And without a generated reasoning step, there's no lexical handle, no shared words, to retrieve the next piece of evidence with.
[S] What had people already tried? Multi-step retrieval isn't a brand-new idea.
[G] Three contemporaneous systems, each falling short differently. SelfAsk decomposes a question into sub-questions and answers each with a separate Google Search call, but it isn't built around chain-of-thought and hands retrieval off to a single-hop model. DecomP is a similar decomposition framework that delegates to a B-M-25 retriever. And ReAct interleaves reasoning and action steps, but it needed a five-hundred-forty-billion-parameter PaLM model, fine-tuned, just to beat plain chain-of-thought prompting. None of the three had been shown to work on smaller models with no training at all, which is exactly the gap I-R-CoT goes after.
[O] Priya, walk us through the actual mechanism. What are the pieces?
[G] Three ingredients, none of which require training. A base retriever, they use B-M-25 through Elasticsearch, that returns paragraphs for a text query. A language model with zero or few-shot chain-of-thought ability. And a small set of hand-written demonstrations: twenty annotated question-and-reasoning-chain examples per dataset, from which they sample fifteen for any given run.
[S] So everything is delivered through the prompt. No fine-tuning anywhere.
[G] Correct, that's central to the pitch. The loop starts by retrieving an initial batch of paragraphs using the question alone, then alternates two steps until it stops. The reason step takes the question, every paragraph collected so far, and the chain-of-thought written so far, and generates exactly one new sentence, even if the model produces more text, only the first sentence is kept. The retrieve step then takes that brand-new sentence and uses it, on its own, as the query for another retrieval call, adding whatever comes back to the growing pool.
[O] And it just keeps going until it has an answer?
[G] It terminates when a generated sentence contains the phrase "the answer is," or when it hits a cap of eight reasoning steps, whichever comes first. There's also a hard ceiling of fifteen collected paragraphs total, mostly so demonstrations still fit in context. Once it stops, every paragraph gathered across every step, not just the last batch, gets handed to a separate question-answering reader, prompted either directly for the answer or with its own chain-of-thought, whichever the base model handles better.
[S] Why not just read the answer off the reasoning chain the retrieval loop already produced? Seems redundant to run a second reader.
[G] They tried that, and the paper says explicitly it under-performed a dedicated reader, so they kept the two steps separate. It's a small design choice, but it's evidence they tested the obvious shortcut and it lost.
[O] What models did they actually run this on?
[G] Two families. OpenAI's code-davinci-002 version of GPT-3, and Flan-T5 at four sizes, roughly two hundred million up to eleven billion parameters, so they could see whether the effect needed a huge model or not. All of it tested open-domain, on four datasets: HotpotQA, 2WikiMultiHopQA, MuSiQue, and IIRC, each rebuilt into a genuine retrieval corpus rather than the closed reading-comprehension setup they originally shipped with.
[S] What's actually doing the work here, though? Is it the retrieval, or is chain-of-thought prompting just generally strong and this is riding on that?
[G] That's exactly the question their ablations are built to isolate, and it's worth holding onto until we get to the numbers, because they compare a no-retrieval baseline, a one-step retrieval baseline, and I-R-CoT, all using the identical reader model, so the only thing that changes is the retrieval strategy.
[O] Let's get to those numbers. Retrieval first.
[G] Against one-step retrieval, using the exact same underlying language model, I-R-CoT's recall improves on every single dataset. With Flan-T5 at eleven billion parameters: up seven point nine points on HotpotQA, fourteen point three on 2WikiMultiHopQA, three point five on MuSiQue, ten point two on IIRC. With GPT-3, the gains are bigger still: eleven point three, twenty-two point six, twelve point five, and twenty-one point two points, respectively.
[S] And that recall gain actually turns into better final answers?
[G] Mostly, yes. Downstream answer F-one, I-R-CoT against one-step retrieval with the same reader: for Flan-T5, up nine point four, fifteen point three, five, and two point five points across the four datasets. For GPT-3, up seven point one, thirteen point two, and seven point one, but not on IIRC, where GPT-3 doesn't improve at all despite a twenty-one-point recall gain.
[O] Why would better retrieval not translate into a better answer there?
[G] The paper's explanation is that IIRC's answers are often already inside GPT-3's parametric knowledge. Its no-retrieval baseline scores almost as well as its retrieval-augmented one on that dataset, which is consistent with the model already knowing the answer without needing to look anything up.
[S] What about the scaling claim from the cold open, Priya, the three-billion-parameter model beating the full GPT-3?
[G] That's real, and it's one of the more striking results. Across HotpotQA, 2WikiMultiHopQA, and MuSiQue, a three-billion-parameter Flan-T5 running I-R-CoT beats both no-retrieval and one-step retrieval using the full one-hundred-seventy-five-billion-parameter GPT-3. The gain scales down further still: even a small, sub-billion-parameter Flan-T5 gets a recall boost from I-R-CoT, though at that smallest size the downstream answer score doesn't quite improve.
[O] And the hallucination claim, that's separate from just getting the right final answer?
[G] Right, and I think it's underrated relative to the headline numbers. They manually checked forty GPT-3-generated reasoning chains per dataset for factual errors in the intermediate steps, independent of whether the final answer was correct. No-retrieval makes the most errors, one-step retrieval fewer, and I-R-CoT the fewest, cutting errors by fifty percent on HotpotQA and forty percent on 2WikiMultiHopQA relative to one-step retrieval. That's evidence the retrieved text is actually grounding the reasoning as it happens, not just patching the final answer at the end.
[S] And then there's the leaderboard table against other published systems.
[G] Yes. Against Internet-Augmented QA, RECITE, ReAct, SelfAsk, and DecomP, I-R-CoT QA posts the highest published numbers where a comparison exists. But the paper is explicit, in its own words, that this isn't head-to-head, because every one of those systems differs in knowledge corpus, base language model, or A-P-I access.
[O] Let me make the strongest case for this paper. It's a training-free retrieval strategy that improves both recall and answer accuracy on every dataset they tried, holds up when you swap which dataset the demonstrations come from, works down to models a fraction of GPT-3's size, and — this is the part I find underappreciated — it measurably reduces hallucination in the reasoning trace itself, not just the final answer. That's a real, mechanistic improvement, and it needed zero additional training data to get.
[S] I've got three problems with how far that gets stretched. First, that leaderboard table: the authors themselves flag it as not apples to apples, yet it's still presented as a ranking. Their own follow-up appendix, with numbers current as of May twenty twenty-three, shows a system called D-S-P actually beating I-R-CoT on HotpotQA by two points, and a newer version of DecomP beating it on 2WikiMultiHopQA by two point eight points. So "state of the art" didn't even survive the paper's own updated table.
[O] That's fair, but the internal comparison, same reader, same demonstrations, only retrieval varying, is untouched by that critique.
[S] Granted, that part's clean. My second problem is cost: I-R-CoT makes a separate language model call for every single sentence of reasoning, and the paper never reports the resulting latency or dollar cost against the one-step baseline. Third, the GPT-3 experiments run entirely on code-davinci-002, which OpenAI deprecated right after the paper was submitted. There's no contamination check anywhere on whether that model, or Flan-T5, had already seen the specific test questions, and their own no-retrieval baseline already scores in the high forties and low fifties on F-one purely from parametric memory on some datasets, which is consistent with either real world knowledge or partial memorization, and the paper doesn't try to tell those apart.
[G] Let me take those one at a time, because they land differently. On the leaderboard table, I'd score that point to the skeptic. The caveat is honestly written, but the table format still invites exactly the ranking read the caveat is trying to prevent, and the updated appendix numbers bear that out. On cost, also a real gap, though the Limitations section does name the extra-call tradeoff explicitly, even without attaching numbers to it. On contamination, that's the sharpest miss. There's no analysis anywhere distinguishing genuine parametric knowledge from memorized test data.
[O] So where does the optimist case still stand?
[G] On the internal ablation, fully. No-retrieval, one-step, and I-R-CoT sharing an identical reader, tested across four datasets, two model families, in-distribution and out-of-distribution splits, reported as means with standard deviations across three demonstration sets — that's a genuinely well-built comparison, and it's what the recall and F-one gains actually rest on. The retrieval metric itself is unusual, too: they call it fixed-budget optimal recall, justified because the retrieved paragraphs aren't ranked and the total count varies question to question, which they argue makes standard measures like recall-at-k not applicable here. Defensible, but it also means the headline recall numbers can't be lined up directly against other retrieval papers.
[S] So the mechanism is real, the ablation backs it, and the two places I'd discount the paper's own framing are the SOTA claim and the total absence of a contamination check.
[G] That's a fair summary of where the evidence actually lands.
[O] Does any of this reach beyond retrieval specifically?
[G] I think the metric choice is the most transferable lesson. The paper picks a nonstandard recall definition because the standard ones genuinely don't fit an unranked, variable-size retrieved set, and they say so, in a footnote, rather than silently reporting an incomparable number as if it were the usual one. That's a small methodological habit worth more attention than it gets.
[S] And the deprecated-API problem is going to keep recurring. Anyone building on a commercial model that gets pulled or updated inherits a reproducibility gap they didn't create, and this paper's own Limitations section says as much about code-davinci-002.
[O] My going-beyond-the-paper read is that interleaving retrieval and reasoning, one step at a time, looks like a template a lot of later agentic and tool-using systems ended up converging on. But that's my inference, not something the paper claims about its own influence.
[G] The paper doesn't make any claim about what came after it, so I'll leave that exactly where you put it: a reasonable guess, not a documented result.
[G] If I had to leave you with one line from the paper itself, it's this: for multi-step questions, what you need to retrieve next depends on what you've already reasoned through, and a training-free loop that lets retrieval and reasoning take turns closes that gap better than doing either step once.
