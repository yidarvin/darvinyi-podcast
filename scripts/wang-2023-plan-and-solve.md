[S] It's on the docket because it's a direct, deliberate follow-up to zero-shot chain-of-thought — same two-stage prompting recipe, same no-exemplars premise, but it goes after the specific failure modes that paper's own error analysis left sitting on the table.
[O] Joining us to walk through it is Julian.
[G] Glad to be here. Standard disclaimer up front: I'm not one of the authors, so when I say "the authors report" or "they show," that's me describing their claims, not making them.
[S] Julian, ground us. What's the actual gap this paper is chasing?
[G] Zero-shot chain-of-thought, from Kojima and colleagues, showed you could get a large language model reasoning step by step just by appending "let's think step by step" to the question, no hand-written examples required. It recovered most of the benefit of few-shot chain-of-thought, which does need worked demonstrations. This paper's authors go back and actually diagnose why zero-shot chain-of-thought still gets things wrong. They sample a hundred GSM eight K problems it got wrong on GPT-3, and hand-bucket every error.
[O] And the buckets are the whole setup for the paper.
[G] Right. Seven percent are pure calculation errors, arithmetic slips. Twelve percent are missing-step errors, where the model skips a necessary intermediate step, especially on longer problems. And the biggest bucket, twenty-seven percent, is semantic misunderstanding — the model just reads the problem wrong from the start.
[S] Three different failure modes, and presumably you can't fix all three with one lever.
[G] That's exactly their read, and they target the missing-step failures specifically. Their argument is that "let's think step by step" invites free-form reasoning, but it never tells the model to lay out the subtasks before diving in. Nothing forces it to notice a step is missing until it's already past it. So the question becomes: can a different, still-zero-shot trigger sentence make the model plan before it solves?
[O] Which is nicely falsifiable — no new architecture, no fine-tuning recipe, just a test of whether wording alone closes a gap they'd already measured.
[G] That's the whole bet of the paper.
[O] Walk us through the method itself. What actually changed?
[G] Structurally, almost nothing. Zero-shot chain-of-thought is a two-stage prompt: stage one generates a reasoning trace from the question plus a trigger sentence, stage two feeds that reasoning back in with an extraction cue and pulls out a clean number. Plan-and-Solve keeps that exact two-stage recipe. The only thing that changes is the trigger sentence in stage one.
[S] So what's the new sentence?
[G] The full quote: "Let's first understand the problem and devise a plan to solve the problem. Then, let's carry out the plan and solve the problem step by step." That's the entire method. No exemplars, no fine-tuning, no external tools. They call that version P-S prompting.
[O] And then there's a plus version.
[G] Right, P-S plus. To go after the calculation errors specifically, they extend the trigger with two more explicit instructions: "extract relevant variables and their corresponding numerals," and "calculate intermediate results, pay attention to calculation and commonsense." The logic is that if the model never writes down the numbers it's working with, it's more likely to lose track of one, and more likely to skip a later step because it never explicitly extracted what mattered.
[S] That's testable. Did they test it?
[G] They did, with a correlation analysis on those same hundred sampled GSM eight K problems. Whether the generated text contains an explicit "variables" section correlates negatively with calculation errors, at roughly negative zero point four one, and with missing-step errors, at negative zero point five six. Whether it contains an explicit "plan" section correlates even more strongly with fewer missing-step errors, at negative zero point eight three.
[O] So the plan and the variable extraction are each doing something mechanistically, not just correlated by accident.
[S] A hundred hand-labeled examples isn't nothing, but correlation isn't causal proof. What I actually want is the ablation — strip pieces out and see what breaks.
[G] They ran exactly that, and it's the most interesting table in the paper. They test six trigger sentences on GSM eight K and SVAMP. Prompt one is the plain "let's think step by step" baseline, fifty-six point four percent on GSM eight K. Prompt three drops the plan language entirely and only asks the model to extract variables and their numerals before solving, no "devise a plan," no "carry out the plan." That prompt scores fifty point five percent. Below the plain chain-of-thought baseline.
[S] Wait. Asking the model to be more careful about extracting variables makes it worse than just saying "think step by step"?
[G] On that specific benchmark, yes. Prompt four adds "calculate intermediate variables" on top, still without plan language, and only recovers to fifty-four point eight, still below the baseline. It's prompt five, restoring "devise a plan" and "carry out the plan" with none of the variable or calculation instructions, that jumps to fifty-eight point two. Prompt six, the full P-S plus trigger with everything combined, reaches fifty-nine point three.
[O] So the planning language specifically is carrying the result, not "more detailed instructions" in general.
[G] That's the honest reading. The variable-extraction and calculation instructions do help on top of the plan, they push P-S plus a full point above plain P-S. But on their own, without the plan framing, they underperform the original zero-shot baseline. Whatever's happening here is specifically about being told to plan before you solve.
[S] That's the first genuinely persuasive piece of evidence I've heard so far. Keep going.
[O] Let's get to the headline numbers. How does P-S plus actually perform?
[G] Across six arithmetic-word-problem datasets, average accuracy: zero-shot chain-of-thought at seventy point four percent, zero-shot Program-of-Thought, where the model writes and executes a Python program instead of prose, at seventy-three point five, plain P-S at seventy-two point nine, and P-S plus at seventy-six point seven. For reference, eight-shot Manual chain-of-thought, the hand-written-demonstrations baseline, gets seventy-seven point six, and Auto-CoT, which selects its own demonstrations automatically, gets seventy-five point nine.
[S] So zero-shot P-S plus, with no examples at all, lands within a point of an eight-shot method.
[G] On the average, yes, and that's the paper's own headline framing, "comparable performance with eight-shot chain-of-thought." Per dataset it's more mixed. P-S plus actually beats Manual-CoT outright on three of the six: GSM eight K, fifty-nine point three against fifty-eight point four; Add Sub, ninety-two point two against ninety-one point six; and Single Eq, ninety-four point seven against ninety-three point five. It trails Manual-CoT on Multi Arith, AQuA, and SVAMP.
[O] The comparison I actually care about is versus plain zero-shot chain-of-thought, since that's the baseline this paper set out to beat.
[G] There it's a large, consistent margin, at least five points on every one of the six datasets except GSM eight K, which only gains two point nine points, from fifty-six point four to fifty-nine point three. The authors attribute that smaller gain to GSM eight K's greater linguistic complexity.
[S] What about outside arithmetic?
[G] On commonsense reasoning, P-S plus beats zero-shot chain-of-thought on both sets, CommonsenseQA, seventy-one point nine against sixty-five point two, and StrategyQA, sixty-five point four against sixty-three point eight, but trails few-shot Manual chain-of-thought by a wider margin there, seventy-eight point three and seventy-one point two respectively. On symbolic reasoning, P-S plus actually beats Manual-CoT on Last Letter Concatenation, seventy-five point two against seventy point six, and comes close to Manual-CoT's perfect one hundred percent on Coin Flip, at ninety-nine point six against zero-shot chain-of-thought's ninety-six point eight.
[O] And self-consistency layered on top?
[G] They sample ten reasoning chains at a higher temperature and majority-vote the answer, on GSM eight K and SVAMP specifically. It lifts both methods substantially, and P-S plus keeps its lead: on GSM eight K, P-S plus with self-consistency reaches seventy-three point seven percent against chain-of-thought's seventy point seven; on SVAMP, eighty-four point four against eighty-one point seven.
[S] Now the part I care about most. Did the plan trigger actually fix the failure modes it was built to fix?
[G] That's the cleanest result in the paper. Same hundred sampled GSM eight K problems from the original error analysis. Zero-shot chain-of-thought gets forty-six wrong. Plain P-S gets forty-three wrong. P-S plus gets thirty-nine wrong. And the error-type breakdown moves exactly where you'd predict: calculation errors drop from seven percent to five percent, missing-step errors drop from twelve percent to seven percent. Semantic misunderstanding errors barely move, twenty-seven percent for both.
[O] Which makes sense, since that third bucket was never the target.
[G] Right, and the authors say so directly, they're explicit that semantic misunderstanding is a comprehension failure a differently worded trigger isn't well positioned to fix. One more number worth flagging: they manually checked whether P-S's own outputs actually contained an explicit plan, and found ninety of a hundred sampled predictions did. They also note, almost in passing, that this points to stronger planning ability emerging in newer models like GPT-3.5 and GPT-4, but that's an aside, not something run through the actual accuracy tables.

[S] So what actually changes if this holds up?
[G] Practically, it's a nearly free swap. If you're already using a "think step by step"-style trigger anywhere, there's no real cost to trying the plan-then-solve version instead, especially the full P-S plus wording, since it needs no extra examples and no extra calls beyond the same two-stage pipeline zero-shot chain-of-thought already used.
[O] And it seeds a whole family of successor work. Later planning-and-execution methods for agents lean on exactly this idea, decompose before you act, well past pure math word problems.
