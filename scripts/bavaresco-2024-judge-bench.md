[O] Start with the plain version of the claim everyone in eval circles has been leaning on for two years: an LLM judge is basically as good as a human rater, so just use the model.
[S] Here's the sentence that should make anyone nervous about that claim. Across twenty separate human-annotated tasks, the best judge in this study, GPT-4o, ties or loses to an open Mixtral model on several of them, and on toxicity judgments every single model scores worse than random chance.
[G] Both of those are true readings of the same paper, and reconciling them is basically the whole episode.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where we pull one paper off the citation map and argue about it. Today's paper is "LLMs instead of Human Judges? A Large Scale Empirical Study across 20 NLP Evaluation Tasks," by Anna Bavaresco and a twenty-author, twelve-institution team, published at ACL twenty twenty five. It's often called Judge-Bench, and it's not to be confused with a separate, similarly-named paper by Tan and colleagues, a different benchmark asking a different question. Joining us is Coralie, who has spent real time in this paper's tables. Welcome, Coralie.
[G] Glad to be here. Worth saying up front, I'm not one of the twenty authors, just someone who has read this closely, so you'll hear "Bavaresco and colleagues," never "we."
[S] And I'll be doing my usual job, reading the fine print under the headline number.
[O] Coralie, why did this paper need to exist? LLM-as-judge was already everywhere by twenty twenty four.
[G] Exactly the problem. By the time Bavaresco and colleagues wrote this, using an LLM to grade another model's output had become the field's default substitute for human evaluation. It underpins leaderboards, reward signals for reinforcement learning, most rubric-based evals, largely because it's so much cheaper than paying human raters. But the evidence that it's trustworthy was genuinely mixed. Some prior work reported LLM judges correlating well with humans, other work found substantial bias, both against human judgments directly and through purely internal analyses of judge behavior.
[S] Mixed evidence is normal in a young literature. What made this worth a new paper instead of another single-dataset study?
[G] The authors' own diagnosis is that those contradictions are an artifact of scale, not a real disagreement. Their words are that the discrepancies likely stem from prior work relying on a few datasets and models, often restricted to closed-source proprietary systems. Two more problems sit on top of that: reproducibility, since a closed judge can be retrained or retired at any time and quietly invalidate a comparison, and data leakage, since a lot of that prior validation ran on datasets an evaluated model may already have seen.
[O] So the fix is just, do it at scale properly.
[G] That is genuinely the whole design. Judge-Bench evaluates eleven current LLMs, open-weight and proprietary, across twenty datasets that already carry human annotations, deliberately spanning task type, judged property, categorical versus graded judgment format, and expert versus non-expert annotators. Any conclusion about whether LLM judges are reliable has to survive that variation, not just one favorable setup.
[S] Walk me through what "twenty datasets" actually means, because that phrase can hide a lot of cherry-picking.
[G] It's a real haul, over seventy thousand test instances with human judgments, unified under one schema. The list includes CoLA and a fine-grained CoLA-grammar variant for sentence acceptability, Switchboard and DailyDialog for dialogue acceptability, an inferential-strategies set for deductive-reasoning validity, ToxicChat and two DICES splits for safety, LLMBar, Persona Chat, and Topical Chat, ROSCOE reasoning-trace quality across four source datasets including GSM8K and e-SNLI, a recipe-generation planning set, Medical-safety, WMT twenty twenty and twenty twenty three machine-translation evaluations, and SummEval, QAGS, and NewsRoom for summarization.
[O] That's a genuinely broad net. What's the one design choice you'd flag as the smartest part of the setup?
[G] Mixing human-generated and machine-generated content on purpose. Some items being judged are model outputs, translations, dialogue turns, generated plans, where the goal is evaluating an NLP system. Others are human-written text being assessed for grammaticality or toxicity. That split lets the authors test something specific: do LLM judges carry a positive bias toward machine-generated text, a bias reported in prior work.
[S] And the eleven models?
[G] GPT-4o, Llama-3.1 at eight billion and seventy billion parameters, Gemini-1.5, two sizes of Mixtral, Command R and Command R+, OLMo-7B, Starling-7B, and Mistral-7B, chosen for strong performance on the Open LLM and Chatbot Arena leaderboards. Wherever a dataset's original human-annotation instructions exist, those instructions get reused verbatim as the model prompt, with one added constraint to keep answers parseable: answer with one of the given options, do not explain your answer.
[O] Did they also try coaxing the models with better prompting, Chain-of-Thought and so on?
[G] They did, across all twenty datasets for nine of the eleven models, and more narrowly tested few-shot and paraphrased prompts on three datasets the models struggled with. None of it produces a systematic improvement. That negative result matters as much as anything positive in this paper.
[S] How do you score two very different judgment types, a one-to-five Likert scale and a binary grammatical-or-not label, on the same footing?
[G] They split scoring along exactly that line. Graded judgments, continuous or Likert-style scores, get Spearman's correlation between model and human scores. Categorical judgments, binary or discrete labels, get Cohen's kappa. And because models sometimes refuse to answer or produce something unparseable, especially on sensitive prompts, there's a specific evaluation-protocol choice worth sitting with: to keep sample sizes equal across models, invalid responses get replaced with judgments randomly sampled from the relevant set of human annotations.
[O] That sounds like it could quietly punish or reward a model depending on how often it refuses.
[G] Hold that thought, it's central to the critique later. One more piece of the method: wherever a dataset has multiple human raters, typically three, the authors bootstrap an upper-bound estimate, the average agreement between one held-out rater and the aggregate of the rest. That's the ceiling any judge, human or machine, could realistically be expected to hit.
[O] Give us the headline numbers.
[G] The paper reports detailed results for the six models with the highest valid-response rates: GPT-4o, Llama-3.1-70B, Mixtral-8x22B, Gemini-1.5, Mixtral-8x7B, and Command R+. Averaged across the categorical datasets, GPT-4o and Llama-3.1-70B tie for the best mean Cohen's kappa at zero point two eight. On the graded datasets, GPT-4o leads outright with a mean Spearman's rho of zero point five zero, ahead of Mixtral-8x22B at zero point four four.
[S] Zero point five zero doesn't sound like "matches human judgment" to me.
[G] It isn't, and the paper doesn't claim it is. That's an average across wildly different tasks, and the average hides real reversals. On CoLA, sentence acceptability, Mixtral-8x7B scores zero point five five and Mixtral-8x22B scores zero point five four, against GPT-4o's zero point three four. On SummEval, graded summary quality, Mixtral-8x22B reaches zero point five four versus GPT-4o's zero point three five. The open models beat the closed leader outright on specific properties.
[O] So "GPT-4o leads on average" is true and also somewhat misleading if you're picking a judge for one particular task.
[G] Exactly the paper's own point. No single model dominates across the board.
[S] Where does it break down hardest?
[G] Toxicity and safety, no contest. On DICES-990, all six headline models post a negative Cohen's kappa, GPT-4o at negative zero point two four down to Command R+ at negative zero point zero nine. Negative kappa means worse than chance agreement. Part of that traces to safety guardrails, models facing a medical or safety-sensitive prompt often generate an explanation or a refusal instead of a judgment, and on DICES specifically they lean toward calling things "unsure" or "unsafe" rather than ever saying "safe."
[O] Any properties where models come close to the human ceiling?
[G] A handful. On QAGS, Recipe-generation, and NewsRoom, model scores land close to the bootstrapped upper bound instead of notably below it. Recipe-generation is the extreme case, GPT-4o posts zero point seven eight, actually higher than that dataset's upper-bound estimate of zero point six five. The paper doesn't dwell on that specific gap, but it's a useful reminder that the upper bound itself is a bootstrapped estimate from a small number of raters, not a fixed ceiling.
[S] Contrast that with reasoning validity.
[G] Stark contrast. On the inferential-strategies dataset, testing deductive-reasoning validity, the human upper bound is a perfect one point zero, and GPT-4o, the best model there, only reaches zero point four two. Humans agree with each other completely on that task, models are nowhere close.
[O] What about the expert-versus-non-expert split you mentioned earlier?
[G] A clean, consistent pattern, on every graded dataset every model correlates more strongly with non-expert annotators than with domain experts. The authors offer a speculative explanation, that non-experts may lean on surface-level features that happen to align with what LLMs are most sensitive to, and they're explicit that it's speculative and needs further investigation. The human-versus-machine-generated-text bias is just as consistent, every model aligns better with human-written text than with machine-generated text, across both categorical and graded judgments.
[S] And the elicitation tricks, Chain-of-Thought and the rest?
[G] Genuinely mixed, case by case. Llama-3.1-8B's rho on WMT twenty twenty three English-to-German rises from zero point zero eight to zero point one eight with Chain-of-Thought. The same model's kappa on the expert-annotated DICES split falls from zero point zero one to negative zero point zero seven with the same technique. The paper's own conclusion is that Chain-of-Thought does not reliably improve agreement.
[O] Give me the strongest optimist case, because I think there's a real one buried in the caution. This is the most honest, best-controlled study of this question that exists. Eleven models, twenty datasets, both judgment formats, both annotator expertise levels, both text sources, and the conclusion isn't "LLM judges are broken," it's "reliability is task- and property-dependent." On instruction-following-adjacent tasks and on mathematical reasoning traces, models come close to the human ceiling. That's a real, usable positive result, not hype.
[S] I'll take the other side. Two design choices in the evaluation protocol deserve more scrutiny than the paper gives them. First, the invalid-response substitution. Valid-response rates range from zero point seven zero for Starling-7B and zero point eight four for OLMo-7B up to roughly one point zero zero for the strongest models. Every refusal or unparseable answer from a low-compliance model gets swapped for a randomly sampled human judgment. That keeps sample sizes equal, but it also means roughly thirty percent of Starling-7B's reported score is mechanically agreement with a random label, not the model's own judgment. The paper never reports what that score would look like scored as a straightforward failure instead.
[G] That's a fair and precise read of the paper's own text. It states the substitution rule plainly but doesn't run the comparison you're asking for.
[O] Counterpoint, the six headline models the paper actually foregrounds all have valid-response rates near ninety-eight percent or higher. The substitution problem is real, but it mostly hits the weaker models the paper isn't leaning its main claims on.
[S] Granted, that softens it for the headline six. It doesn't fully explain it away though, because Command R+, one of those headline six, still posts the lowest scores in the group, and we don't know how much of that gap is genuine judgment quality versus a lower valid-response rate dragging the average down.
[S] Second issue, contamination. The paper raises data leakage as a field-wide concern in its own introduction, then doesn't audit its own benchmark for it. Judge-Bench's own dataset table flags CoLA and CoLA-grammar as leaked, citing Balloccu and colleagues, meaning those items are known or suspected to already sit in the pretraining data of the very models being tested. No decontamination pass, no leakage-excluded re-analysis, CoLA's numbers go straight into the headline averages.
[G] Also accurate. The Limitations section concedes that using existing datasets could carry exactly that risk, but the paper stops at acknowledging it.
[O] To be fair to them, decontaminating twenty datasets against eleven models' training data is a genuinely hard, maybe impossible, engineering problem given most of these are closed models with undisclosed training sets.
[S] Sure, but then don't fold the flagged datasets into an unqualified leaderboard number without a footnote pointing back at your own table.
[G] There's a third thing worth putting on the table, and it comes straight from the paper's own Limitations section rather than from either of you. Correlation with human judgment may not be the right validation target at all. The authors' own words are that there may be domains where human annotators and LLM evaluators appear aligned simply because they're affected by similar biases. High correlation could be measuring shared blind spots, not correctness.
[O] That's a genuinely uncomfortable point, because it means even the good scores in this paper aren't necessarily proof the judge is right, just proof it agrees with the humans, who might also be wrong.
[S] Which is exactly why I'd weight the negative results in this paper, the toxicity collapse, the reasoning-validity gap, more heavily than the positive ones. A negative correlation is unambiguous evidence of disagreement. A positive one is ambiguous between "the judge understands the task" and "the judge shares the humans' shortcut."
[G] Adjudicating the whole exchange, the paper's central claim, that reliability varies enormously by task, property, and annotator expertise, is well supported and is the actual contribution here. The specific per-model leaderboard numbers deserve more caution, they bake in a random-substitution rule for low-compliance models and include at least two self-flagged, potentially contaminated datasets without adjustment.
[O] So what should a team actually do differently on Monday morning?
[G] The paper's own recommendation is blunt, validate and calibrate any LLM judge against task-specific human judgments before deploying it, rather than trusting a single leaderboard-style number. That feeds directly back into evaluation and benchmarking practice more broadly, contamination checks, judge reliability, whether a correlation coefficient is even the right target, are the same questions the field is fighting about for benchmarks generally.
[S] The other implication is about judge selection. If different models win on different properties, coherence and consistency to the Mixtral models, acceptability and verbosity to GPT-4o and Gemini, then "pick the strongest general-purpose model as your judge" is the wrong default. You need a per-property comparison, not one leaderboard number.
[O] Worth noting how much has moved since this ran, too. Only one frontier proprietary judge, GPT-4o, appears here, no Claude, no reasoning-tuned model. Given how fast judge capability has moved, I'd want this exact protocol rerun with today's frontier models before trusting the specific rankings for a new project.
[G] The paper doesn't speak to that, since it's over a year old at this point, but it's a reasonable extrapolation, not a claim the paper itself makes. Worth adding one scope note from the Limitations section too, pairwise preference judging, the "which of these two is better" style used by benchmarks like PairEval, is explicitly out of scope here, and the study is almost entirely English-language.
[G] Sticking close to the text, Bavaresco and colleagues' bottom line is that LLMs should be carefully validated against human judgments before being used as evaluators, because the paper's own numbers show reliability depends on the task, the property, and who the human on the other side of the comparison is.
