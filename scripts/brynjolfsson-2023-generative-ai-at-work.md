So the prior going in was genuinely uncertain.
[O] There's a second question buried in there too, about who benefits.
[G] Right, and this is the part I find more interesting than the topline number. Every earlier wave of computing was skill-biased. PCs, robots, enterprise software, they all complemented workers who were already skilled, because someone had to sit down and codify the task into explicit rules before a computer could do it. Generative AI doesn't need that. It learns tacit knowledge directly from examples of people doing a job well and badly.
[S] So the theory is it can bottle up what your best employees do without anyone ever writing that down as a rule.
[G] Exactly, and if that's true, it predicts the opposite of what every previous computing wave did. Instead of stretching the gap between high and low performers, it could compress it. That was a theory going in. This paper is the first real field test of it.
[O] Set the scene for us. Who are we actually talking about here?
[G] A Fortune five hundred software firm selling business software to small companies in the US. Their support arm has five thousand one hundred seventy-two agents, eighty-nine percent of them outside the US, mostly in the Philippines. The authors get three million real chat transcripts. The tool itself is built on, in the paper's own words, a recent version of GPT, fine-tuned on past agent conversations labeled with whether the chat was resolved and whether the agent was a designated top performer, and further tuned in a process the authors describe as similar in spirit to the RLHF approach OpenAI used for ChatGPT, to push the tone toward empathetic and professional. It watches the live chat and suggests a reply and documentation links, and the agent can accept it, edit it, or ignore it entirely.
[S] A recent version of GPT is doing a lot of work in that sentence. Which model? How good is it standalone?
[G] The paper doesn't say, and that's a real limitation we should come back to. The vendor is a third party and stays anonymous throughout.
[O] How do you turn a corporate tool rollout into something you can draw a causal arrow from?
[G] The rollout itself is the lucky break. Because of training bottlenecks and a capped license budget, agents got access at different times across the fall of twenty twenty into the winter of twenty twenty-one, some agents waiting months longer than their own deskmates on the same team. Team-level access went from about five percent in October twenty twenty to about seventy percent by January. That staggered timing is what lets them run a difference-in-differences design, with agent fixed effects, year-month fixed effects, and tenure controls.
[S] Staggered rollouts have a known trap, though. If treatment effects change over time, the classic two-way fixed effects estimator can be biased.
[G] Right, and they're careful about it. They use the Sun and Abraham interaction-weighted estimator, built to avoid that bias, and the result holds up across the other modern robust difference-in-differences estimators too, plus a small randomized pilot and an instrumental-variable specification.
[S] Which is the part I actually want to press on, because managers chose who got access first, explicitly, in their own words, to minimize customer-service disruptions. That's not random.
[G] It isn't, and the authors say so directly. Their defense is a team-level instrument, using the date the first worker on a team got access to instrument an individual's own adoption date, less connected to any one agent's own performance than the manager's direct choice would be. On top of that, the event-study coefficients sit completely flat before treatment. No pre-trend. If the strongest agents were selected first, you'd expect productivity to already be diverging before AI access turns on, and it isn't.
[O] And the tiny randomized trial backs that up?
[G] Directionally, yes, though I wouldn't lean on it too hard. About fifty workers, only twenty-two identifiably treated, and the paper never observes the control group directly. A supporting data point, not the load-bearing one.

That gain decomposes into agents handling each chat faster, average handle time down three point seven minutes, eight point five percent, off a forty-three-minute baseline, and handling more chats at once, up point three seven, roughly fifteen percent. [S] So they're getting faster without getting worse. That's the surprising part, because faster usually means sloppier.
[G] It's the first thing that made me trust the paper. Quality holding steady while speed climbs isn't what you'd expect from agents just rubber-stamping AI suggestions to hit a number.
[O] Now the signature finding. Break down who's actually getting that fifteen percent.
[G] Sort agents into skill quintiles based on pre-AI performance, and it's a clean monotonic pattern. The lowest-skill quintile gains half a resolution per hour, thirty-six percent. The highest-skill quintile gains essentially zero, and shows small but significant declines in resolution rate and customer satisfaction. Sort by tenure instead and you get the same shape. Agents with less than a month on the job gain point seven resolutions per hour. Agents with more than a year gain nothing.
[S] Couldn't that just be mean reversion? Your worst agents in month one are always going to look better in month two, AI or not.
[G] That's the obvious objection, and the authors test for it directly. If it were mean reversion, you'd expect a break in the pattern, low performers catching up and then flattening out. Instead they find a consistent linear increase in productivity across every skill level after AI access turns on, with no evidence of the ceiling effect mean reversion would produce. It reads as a genuine treatment effect, not a statistical artifact.
[O] There's a figure in the paper that makes this concrete, the experience curves.
[G] Figure four. Every new agent starts around one point eight resolutions per hour. Agents who never get AI access slowly climb to about two point five, over eight to ten months. Agents who have AI from day one hit that same two point five in about two months, and keep climbing past three by month five. Agents who start without AI and then get it in month five immediately bend onto the fast trajectory. The authors' own framing is that a treated agent with two months of tenure performs like an untreated agent with six-plus months.
[S] Is that real learning, or are they just leaning on a crutch that happens to work?
[G] That's the question the paper takes most seriously, and the cleverest evidence comes from software outages, moments when the AI silently stops making suggestions. Agents stay faster than their own pre-AI baseline even during an outage, and that residual speed grows the longer they'd had AI access beforehand, and it's concentrated entirely among high-adherence agents. Agents who mostly ignored the AI's suggestions show no such residual gain during outages at all. Overall adherence sits around thirty-eight percent, comparable to the twenty-seven to forty-six percent range reported for GitHub Copilot acceptance rates.
[O] So the ones who actually engage with it are the ones who internalize something durable.
[G] That's the authors' read, and I think it's fair. There's a second piece worth flagging too. The benefit is concentrated on moderately rare problems, roughly the seventy-fifth to ninetieth percentile of how often an agent has personally seen that issue before, with handle time down fourteen percent there. It helps least on the routine stuff agents already know cold, which is exactly where you'd expect a system trained on other people's experience to add the most value.
[S] Here's my other worry, and it's in the paper's own conclusion, not something I'm inventing. High-skill agents increase their adherence to AI suggestions just as fast as low-skill agents do, even though their own gains are smaller and sometimes negative. That sounds like your best people quietly getting worse at their job while trusting the tool more.
[G] The authors flag exactly that risk. If star performers lean on AI and stop producing the kind of high-quality, original transcripts the model was trained on in the first place, future versions of the tool may have less to learn from. It's a genuine feedback-loop concern, and the paper is honest that it can't resolve it with only about five months of post-treatment data.
