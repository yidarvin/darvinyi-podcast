[G] A map. By the middle of twenty twenty-four, contamination had gone from a footnote in an L-L-M paper to its own research subfield. Model developers were describing their own overlap checks in technical reports, GPT-3, LLaMA-2, Gemini. Academic groups were countering with membership-inference attacks, masking probes, order-sensitivity tests. Every paper proposed one method, ran it on a handful of models, and moved on. Nobody had put the whole literature side by side until this survey did.
[O] So it is an atlas of everyone else's work, organized around three questions.
[G] Right. What does contamination actually do to a model's reported performance, how do you detect it, and once you have found it, what do you do about it. The framing that makes the whole thing usable is access level. The authors split models into white-box, where you can see the weights and the training data, like BERT or OLMo. Gray-box, where you get the weights but not the training data, like Mistral or Qwen. And black-box, A-P-I only, like ChatGPT, Gemini, or Claude.
[S] Which matters because most of the interesting targets, the actual deployed commercial models, are black-box. You cannot run a training-corpus search against something you cannot see.
[G] Exactly, and that is the organizing constraint for the entire detection section. If you are auditing a black-box model, most of the retrieval family is simply off the table for you. The paper's real usefulness is telling you, given what you are allowed to touch, which of the twenty-odd methods you are even eligible to run.
[O] That is a genuinely practical framing. I have seen plenty of taxonomies that sort methods by what they output. Sorting by what access they require is sorting by what a reader can actually do on Monday morning.
[S] Walk me through the families, then. Fast.
[G] Seven of them. Retrieval is the most direct: search the training corpus for benchmark text. GPT-3 searched for thirteen-gram matches between its training data and the benchmark sets, with the minimum overlap threshold running between eight and thirteen words depending on how long the example was. LLaMA-2 went further: any n-gram match longer than ten tokens gets flagged, and examples get bucketed as clean under twenty percent overlap, "not clean" between twenty and eighty percent, and "dirty" above eighty percent. Gemini's approach is simpler still: retrieve any evaluation data found in the training set and remove it.
[O] And on the academic side, without that kind of internal access?
[G] Academic groups built the search infrastructure developers would not share. Tools like the ROOTS search engine, a system called Gaia, and a platform called WIMBD index corpora like C4 and the Pile so outside researchers can run their own overlap checks. Dodge and colleagues used exactly that kind of search on C4 and found a meaningful volume of text from unexpected sources, patents, U-S military websites. And a separate analysis of the SuperGLUE benchmark against several popular pretraining corpora found contamination reaching as high as one hundred percent on some individual datasets.
[S] One hundred percent. Meaning the entire test set for that dataset was sitting in the training corpus.
[G] For some datasets, per that analysis, yes. Second family is temporal cutoff: compare performance on benchmarks released before versus after a model's training cutoff date. Roberts and colleagues ran the sharpest version of this, using two code and math problem-solving datasets, and found a statistically significant relationship between a model's pass rate, a benchmark's popularity on GitHub, and its release date, exactly the pattern you would expect if leakage were driving the score.
[O] What is third?
[G] Masking. Hide part of a benchmark item and see if the model fills it back in verbatim. This is where the correction from earlier matters most. T-S-Guessing, mask an incorrect multiple-choice option, or an unlikely word, and prompt the model to reconstruct it, is real, and it is genuinely striking. But it belongs to a twenty twenty-three paper with the title we were mistakenly handed for this episode, written by an overlapping set of authors that includes this survey's own lead author. This survey catalogs it as one entry in the masking family. It did not run it.
[S] So the actual headline finding, that several proprietary models could precisely recall a masked wrong answer, that is a citation in this paper, not this paper's own result.
[G] Correct, and the survey is careful to carry over the original caveat too: T-S-Guessing's own reported weakness is that it depends on a model's instruction-following ability. Weaker models tend to just repeat one of the visible choices, or blurt out the correct answer instead of following the fill-in-the-blank instruction, which muddies the signal.
[O] Fourth family?
[G] Perturbation. Rephrase or translate the test item and see whether performance drops. One cited study showed a thirteen-billion-parameter model matching GPT-4-level scores on MMLU, GSM8K, and HumanEval purely by overfitting to the exact original phrasing, with the gap reappearing the moment the items were paraphrased. Fifth is canonical order: does the model prefer a benchmark's original example ordering over a random shuffle. That method worked on models as small as one point four billion parameters using only one thousand test examples, though it breaks entirely if the developer shuffled the data during pretraining. Sixth is behavior manipulation, prompting a model to continue a partial benchmark item and checking whether the completion matches verbatim, or measuring how unnaturally peaked the output distribution is. Seventh is membership inference, borrowed from classical privacy research: perplexity, loss calibrated against compression size, or the lowest-probability tokens in a sequence, used to guess whether a specific example was in training.
[S] Membership inference needs the model's output probabilities, though. That rules out most black-box chat interfaces right there.
[G] It does, which loops back to the access framing. Retrieval needs the corpus. Membership inference needs logits. The genuinely black-box-friendly families, masking, canonical order, behavior manipulation, are exactly the ones that trade that accessibility for weaker guarantees.
[O] Let's get to what all this actually found, not just how it is organized.
[G] Start with impact, because it is the most counterintuitive section. Magar and Schwartz pretrained a BERT-based model on a mix of Wikipedia and labeled downstream data, and found something people do not expect: models can memorize contaminated examples without consistently exploiting them at test time. Whether memorization turns into an inflated score depends on how many times the data was duplicated and on model size, not just on whether it was seen at all.
[S] That already complicates the simple "if it saw it, it aced it" story most people tell about contamination.
[G] It does, and it gets stranger. Jiang and colleagues deliberately pretrained GPT-2 on a contaminated slice of the Pile and found two things: standard n-gram detection methods had limited power to catch the contamination they had built in on purpose, and increasing how many times that contaminated data was repeated during training actually hurt downstream performance, rather than inflating it.
[O] Wait, more contamination made the model worse?
[G] On that specific setup, yes, and the authors of that study do not fully explain why, they just report it. It is the kind of finding that should make anyone confident about "more leaked data equals a bigger score boost" pump the brakes.
[S] What about the cross-lingual result? That one gets cited constantly.
[G] Blevins and Zettlemoyer. Non-English text is under one percent of a typical pretraining corpus, but at the scale these corpora run, under one percent is still hundreds of millions of tokens. That sliver is enough to measurably inflate scores on benchmarks that are supposed to be testing pure cross-lingual generalization, meaning the model was never meant to have seen that language before evaluation. There is a direct relationship between how much of a target language sits in the training data and how well the model appears to transfer into it.
[O] So "cross-lingual capability" scores are, in part, contamination wearing a different label.
[G] That is the survey's read, at least for corpora built the way most current ones are.
[S] And on mitigation, the actual fix side. This is where I want the receipts on that five-to-one number from the cold open.
[G] Four documented strategies, against roughly twenty detection methods spread across seven families. Construct benchmarks from the most recent possible text, so there is simply less time for it to leak into a crawl. Dynamically refresh benchmarks, either by generating fresh variable-complexity items on the fly, or the Clean-Eval approach: paraphrase and back-translate the test data, filter out low-quality rewrites, then pick the best candidate using a similarity score against the original. Encrypt released test data behind a password-protected archive. And withhold the ground-truth labels from public release entirely.
[O] Those all sound reasonable on paper.
[S] Reasonable and rare. Four strategies covering an entire field's remediation needs, while detection research keeps stacking up new methods every few months. That imbalance is the paper's most important number, and it is not even a number the authors computed on purpose, it is just what falls out of counting citations in their own bibliography.
