[S] Sixty-nine percent. That was GPT-4o's score on MMMU, the flagship college-exam benchmark for multimodal reasoning, and it was treated as a milestone for expert-level AI.
[O] And it should have been exciting — a model reading charts, chemical structures, medical scans, and getting college-level questions right across thirty different subjects.
[S] Except a chunk of those "multimodal" questions turned out to be answerable without ever looking at the image. Text-only models, guessing from the options alone, could get plenty of them right.
[O] Which is exactly the kind of finding that should make you angrier at the benchmark than at the model, and today's paper does something about it instead of just complaining.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the research literature. Today it's "MMMU-Pro: A More Robust Multi-discipline Multimodal Understanding Benchmark," from Xiang Yue, Tianyu Zheng, Yuansheng Ni, and colleagues on the MMMU team, published at ACL 2025. Joining us is Alden, a researcher who has spent a lot of time in this benchmark's internals. We picked this one because it's the rare benchmark paper that turns its own critique into a working rebuild.
[S] Alden, welcome. Before we get to the fix, how bad was the leak, really?
[G] Bad enough that the paper opens with two worked examples instead of just an abstract claim. Llama-3-70B-Instruct, a text-only model with zero access to the image, correctly answers an MMMU question asking which group most strongly supported the Grange, a nineteenth-century farmers' movement — it just recalls US history and picks western farmers.
[G] The second example is sharper. The question asks the model to label five stages of a bacteriophage infection shown in a diagram it cannot see. The model admits outright that it doesn't see the image, then still guesses the correct option anyway, purely from a plausible-sounding ordering of the words in the choices.
[O] That second one is the damning one. It's not even relying on trivia, it's exploiting the phrasing of the answer choices themselves.
[S] And the paper names both failure modes precisely. Text-only dependency: some questions just don't need the image at all. Shortcut exploitation: even questions that do require the image for a person can be cracked by a model correlating pre-training knowledge with the candidate options.
[G] There's a structural reason both are so exploitable. MMMU's questions are four-way multiple choice, so a purely random guess already clears twenty-five percent, and every added shortcut on top of that compounds the inflation.
[O] So MMMU wasn't lying about model capability exactly, it just had more leakage in its guessing floor than anyone had stress-tested.
[S] That's the generous read. The less generous one is that a benchmark cited on nearly every frontier model card had a soft spot nobody closed for almost a year.
[O] So walk us through the fix, Alden. What did the team actually build?
[G] A three-step reconstruction, and each step targets one specific leak. Step one is filtering. Four strong open text-only models, Llama-3-70B-Instruct, Qwen2-72B-Instruct, Yi-1.5-34B-Chat, and Mixtral-8x22B-Instruct, are forced to answer every MMMU question with no image at all, ten separate times each, even when a model protests that it needs to see the picture.
[S] Forced, meaning the prompt doesn't let them say they can't tell without the image?
[G] Correct, they have to commit to an option regardless. A question counts as "answerable" by a given model if it's correct on more than five of the ten tries. Any question where at least three of the four models clear that bar gets dropped as text-solvable.
[O] And what's left after that cut?
[G] The authors sample eighteen hundred questions from the surviving pool, spread evenly across all thirty subjects, sixty questions per subject.
[S] That's step one. What's step two?
[G] Widening the options. Even after filtering, a four-way multiple choice still leaves guessable structure, since a model can improve its odds just by eliminating implausible options. So the team expands each question from four candidate options up to ten. GPT-4o generates the extra distractors, Claude 3.5 Sonnet filters them for quality, and two separate rounds of human review refine and verify the final set.
[O] Ten options is a real change to the guessing floor, from twenty-five percent down toward ten.
[G] It also does something filtering alone couldn't. The human review pass, while checking option quality, also catches and removes seventy questions with weak image-question coherence. That leaves seventeen hundred thirty questions.
[S] So two separate mechanisms are pruning the same pool, text-solvability up front, coherence during the option pass.
[G] Right, and both cuts show up in the paper's own diagnostic. Llama-3-70B-Instruct's text-only accuracy on this material falls from around forty-one percent on the original items, to about thirty-four percent after filtering, to roughly twenty percent after the option augmentation, and the same downward staircase holds for the other three sieve models.
[O] That's a strong signal the pipeline is doing what it claims, squeezing out exactly the shortcuts it targeted, not just shrinking the dataset.
[S] Fine, but the interesting one to me is step three.
[G] The vision-only setting, and it's the design choice I'd call the paper's real contribution. For every one of the seventeen hundred thirty questions, human annotators manually photograph or screenshot the question with its options embedded directly inside the image, varying the background, font style, and font size to mimic real screenshot habits.
[O] So the model doesn't get a clean text channel at all, no separately typed-out question, just a picture it has to both read and reason over.
[G] Exactly, and that doubles the corpus. Seventeen hundred thirty standard-format questions plus seventeen hundred thirty vision versions, thirty-four hundred sixty questions total. The paper's motivation is explicit: real users share mixed screenshots far more often than clean separated text, and human cognition handles that mixture effortlessly.
[S] And scoring, how do the three settings combine into one number?
[G] MMMU-Pro's official score is the average of the ten-option standard result and the vision result. The original four-option standard setting is reported too, but only as a bridge back to MMMU for comparison, it isn't part of the headline score.
[O] Let's get to the number that matters. How far did scores actually fall?
[G] Substantially, and consistently. GPT-4o, the 0513 version, goes from sixty-nine point one percent on MMMU down to fifty-four percent on the ten-option standard setting, and forty-nine point seven percent in the vision setting, an overall MMMU-Pro score of about fifty-two percent.
[S] That ten-option number alone is worth pausing on. Sixty-four point seven to fifty-four percent, just from expanding the options, that's a ten point seven point drop on the same underlying questions and the same image.
[G] Which the paper reads exactly as you'd expect, a meaningful share of GPT-4o's original score came from guessing structure in the four-way format, not from the image.
[O] And Claude 3.5 Sonnet and Gemini both land in a similar range?
[G] Claude 3.5 Sonnet falls from sixty-eight point three percent to an overall fifty-one point five percent, a sixteen point eight point decline. Gemini 1.5 Pro, the 0801 release, falls from sixty-five point eight percent to forty-six point nine percent, down eighteen point nine points.
[S] Those two numbers, sixteen point eight and twenty-six point nine, are literally the range the abstract advertises.
[G] Right, twenty-six point nine belongs to VILA-1.5-40B, an open model that collapses from fifty-one point nine percent on the original MMMU all the way to twenty-five percent overall on MMMU-Pro. Its vision-setting score alone drops to fourteen point one percent.
[O] That's the steepest fall in the whole table, and it tracks what I'd expect: weaker vision-language integration breaks down hardest where the benchmark is hardest.
[S] What about the human baseline, does it hold steady the way you'd want a sanity check to?
[G] It does, and this is one of the paper's stronger pieces of evidence. The approximated "high" tier of human experts scores eighty-eight point six percent on the original format, drops only to eighty-five point four percent on both the ten-option standard and the vision settings, and stays there, a three point two point dip, nothing like the model collapses.
[O] So whatever's breaking models isn't breaking the questions themselves.
[S] Assuming the human number is trustworthy, and we should come back to that, because it's not a fresh measurement.
[G] Agreed, we'll get there. On chain-of-thought prompting, it generally helps, sometimes a lot. Claude 3.5 Sonnet's standard-setting score jumps from forty-two point seven percent with a direct prompt to fifty-five percent with chain-of-thought.
[O] That's a huge swing from one prompting choice.
[G] But it isn't universal. VILA-1.5-40B and a couple of other open models barely move or actually regress under chain-of-thought, which the authors attribute to weaker instruction-following in those models.
[S] And the domain breakdown for chain-of-thought is uneven too, isn't it?
[G] Very. For GPT-4o, chain-of-thought adds about fourteen and a half points in Tech and Engineering questions and roughly eight points in Science, but only about a point and a half in Art and Design. For LLaVA-OneVision-72B, chain-of-thought actually costs about seventeen points in Art and Design, it hurts more than it helps there.
[O] Now the part I find most interesting, the OCR analysis. Is the vision-only drop just because models can't read the screenshot?
[G] No, and the paper tests this directly. They measure each model's OCR accuracy, essentially how faithfully it can transcribe the embedded question, and compare vision-setting accuracy with and without an explicit transcribe-first prompt. GPT-4o's OCR accuracy is ninety-two point three percent, and its vision score is forty-nine point seven percent with the OCR prompt versus forty-nine point four without, basically no difference.
[S] So reading the text isn't the bottleneck for the strong models.
[G] Precisely, and there's a sharper comparison that makes the point even better. LLaVA-OneVision-72B has an OCR accuracy of eighty-seven point eight percent, close to GPT-4o mini's eighty-nine point six and InternVL2-Llama3-76B's eighty-eight point one, but its vision-setting accuracy is only twenty-four percent, far below both of those models' thirty-five and thirty-eight percent. Reading the words correctly and reasoning over them in a picture are clearly two different skills.
[O] Which is exactly what the vision-only setting was designed to expose.
[S] So where does the error actually come from, if not OCR?
[G] The team hand-annotated sixty of GPT-4o's vision-setting errors. Forty-six percent are reasoning errors, twenty-seven percent perceptual errors, twenty-five percent attributed to lacking the relevant knowledge, and essentially zero percent OCR errors. That reasoning-error share is nearly double MMMU's original twenty-six percent.
[O] So the failure mode genuinely shifted, from the model not knowing the answer, toward the model seeing and reading it fine, and still reasoning its way to the wrong choice.
[O] Okay, let's do the debate round. I'll make the strongest case for what this means, then it's your turn to push back, and Alden calls it.
[S] Go ahead.
[O] Here's my case. This is how a field should self-correct. Someone builds a benchmark, the community starts trusting a number, and instead of just complaining, the same team finds precisely where the number was lying and rebuilds it with a documented, testable methodology. The ten point seven point drop from options alone is hard evidence, not vibes, a controlled ablation on the same questions and the same images. And the vision-only setting isn't a gimmick, it's testing something real: reading and seeing at once is how people actually use these systems.
[S] I'll grant the diagnosis. The two worked examples and the ten point seven point option-only drop are genuinely convincing, MMMU's four-option format really was leaking. My problem is how much weight "robust" is asked to carry in the title. The text-only filter is calibrated against exactly four specific open models from 2024, a question survives if fewer than three of those four get it right. That's a defensible proxy, but it's frozen in time. A stronger future text-only model, one none of these four resemble, could plausibly re-solve a real slice of those seventeen hundred thirty "filtered" questions from priors alone, and nothing here would catch that, since the sieve was never adversarial against future models.
[G] That's fair — the paper's own limitations section states plainly that the dataset "may still contain subtle statistical shortcuts that models can exploit." The filtering step reduces the leak relative to four particular sieve models; it doesn't close it for all time.
[O] Sure, but that's true of every benchmark ever built, you calibrate against the strongest available models at construction time. That's not a flaw specific to MMMU-Pro, that's the condition of doing this work at all.
[S] Fair, but it does mean I'd discount "robust" in the title to something closer to "robust against the shortcuts we knew about in 2024." My second concern is the human baseline. It isn't freshly measured, it's the original MMMU human-expert numbers, reused on the argument that question content is unchanged and the original evaluators had to show written work, which limits guessing.
[G] The paper is transparent about that too. It says explicitly that leaning on approximated rather than freshly measured human performance introduces its own bias into that ceiling number, rather than hiding the shortcut.
[O] Even so, an eighty-five point four percent human ceiling that barely moves across three completely different presentations of the same questions is a pretty strong signal the questions didn't get harder for a person, only for the models.
[S] Suggestive, not proof. My last one is contamination and the vision setting itself. Seventeen hundred thirty of the original MMMU question stems survive into MMMU-Pro, just re-optioned and, for half of them, re-photographed. Anything a model already memorized about those specific stems from MMMU's public release could still leak through, especially in the standard-ten setting where the text sits there in plain view again.
[G] True, and the paper doesn't claim to have solved contamination, filtering targets text-only solvability, not prior exposure to the exact stem. On the vision setting, the OCR ablation is a genuinely good-faith attempt to isolate reading from reasoning, and it succeeds for the strong models. But the manually photographed images introduce their own uncontrolled variables, lighting, screenshot chrome, phone-camera artifacts, that the paper doesn't ablate against. Some of the vision-only drop for weaker models could be generic robustness to visual noise rather than a genuine text-image integration failure.
[O] Point taken. So where does that leave the scorecard?
[G] Score it this way. The option-widening evidence and the two worked examples are strong, the optimist wins that exchange, the diagnosis of the original leak is close to airtight. The "robust" framing and the human baseline are where the skeptic's caution earns its keep, both are approximations the paper names itself rather than hides, but the title still promises more permanence than the construction can fully guarantee.
[S] That's a fair split. What's the actual takeaway for people building or trusting benchmarks generally?
[G] The clearest lesson is about guessing floors and shortcut auditing as standard practice, not an afterthought. A four-option multiple choice with no text-only sieve behind it is a benchmark waiting for exactly this critique. Filter, widen, and add a channel the shortcut can't cross, that playbook generalizes well beyond this one benchmark.
[O] It also reframes what "multimodal" should mean going forward, a model that only handles text and image as two pre-separated channels is missing something people do constantly, like reading a screenshot or a scanned form.
[S] And it's a caution for every leaderboard number cited off a single benchmark version. GPT-4o's sixty-nine percent wasn't fabricated, but it also wasn't quite what it looked like, and nobody caught that until someone built the adversarial version.
[G] The paper's own framing for future work matters here too, better vision-and-text integration, not just bigger backbones, closes this gap; scale alone predictably helps but doesn't erase it.
[G] If there's one sentence: MMMU-Pro didn't just make a benchmark harder, it located exactly where the old number was inflated and by roughly how much, and said plainly where its own new number still has to be taken on faith.
[O] Mine is that this is what a mature field's benchmarking should look like, an adversarial self-audit that actually shipped a fix, not just a blog post pointing at the flaw.
[S] And mine: "robust" is doing a lot of work in that title, and the honest reading is robust against the shortcuts they could think to test for in 2024, which is real progress, just not a permanent guarantee.
