[S] And I'm the skeptic. Today's paper is "The Pile: An eight hundred gigabyte Dataset of Diverse Text for Language Modeling," by Leo Gao, Stella Biderman, Sid Black, and colleagues at EleutherAI, posted right at the very end of 2020.
[O] Joining us is Julian, who's spent a lot of time inside this corpus. Julian, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is the paper that basically wrote the playbook for open pretraining data, so there's a lot of ground to cover.
[S] We picked it because you can trace an awful lot of what came after — how people talk about data mixtures, how they document what they scraped — straight back to this one.
[G] The setup is worth dwelling on. By the end of 2020 the recipe for a strong language model was basically settled: bigger transformer, more text. GPT-3, T5, Megatron — all of them leaned on Common Crawl for the bulk of their tokens, because it's huge and it's free.
[O] But huge and free isn't the same as good.
[G] Right, and the authors lean on two prior findings to make their case. First, that dataset diversity drives downstream generalization. Second, that a large language model can pick up a whole new domain from a surprisingly small amount of in-domain text. Put those together, and the implication is that a corpus built from many smaller, distinct, high-quality sources should teach a model more than the same number of bytes of filtered web text.
[S] That's a hypothesis, not a fact yet.
[G] Agreed, and the paper treats it as one worth testing rather than asserting. They also point at two concrete gaps in the field. There was no large, openly available, diverse training corpus — GPT-3's own mixture was proprietary, and the public alternatives were either pure Common Crawl derivatives like CC-100, or single-domain. And second, no dataset built to train a massive language model had ever been seriously documented by the people who built it.
[O] Which is the more interesting gap, honestly. Everyone was shipping a giant opaque blob and letting downstream users discover the biases the hard way.
[S] So the pitch is really two products bundled into one paper: a training set, and a documentation norm.
[G] That's a fair read of the contributions list. An eight hundred twenty-five gigabyte English corpus from twenty-two sources, fourteen of them newly built for this project, a set of benchmark results proving the diversity thesis, and an extended documentation exercise the authors hope other people copy.
[O] Let's get into what's actually in the box, Julian.
[G] The new sources are the interesting half, because they deliberately skew away from the open web. Full-text biomedical papers from PubMed Central, arXiv in its raw LaTeX form, GitHub code, U S court opinions through the FreeLaw Project, Stack Exchange questions and answers, patent background sections from the US Patent and Trademark Office, PubMed abstracts, Ubuntu IRC logs, Hacker News, PhilPapers, YouTube subtitles, and NIH grant abstracts.
[S] That's a very academic-and-professional list.
[G] Deliberately so. Then they layer in curated existing sets: Books3, Project Gutenberg through the PG-19 collection, OpenSubtitles, English Wikipedia, DeepMind Mathematics, EuroParl, and the Enron email corpus. And they rebuild their own version of a web scrape, called Pile-CC, using a proper HTML extraction tool on the raw Common Crawl archive files instead of the lossy pre-extracted text files most people use. Plus OpenWebText2, a Reddit-upvote-filtered general web set.
[O] So even the "web" component isn't raw Common Crawl, it's a cleaner cut of it.
[G] Right, that's one of the quieter contributions. Now, the part that actually matters for the diversity story: the twenty-two sub-datasets are not mixed in proportion to their raw size. Following what GPT-3 did, they assign each component an epoch count — how many times a training run sees it per full pass over the Pile.
[S] Give me the actual weights, because "up-weighted" is doing a lot of work in that sentence.
[G] Wikipedia gets seen up to three times. The Gutenberg collection gets two and a half epochs. Several academic sets get two epochs. Big, noisy sources like GitHub and Pile-CC get one epoch — no up-weighting at all. Run that through, and the eight hundred twenty-five gigabyte raw corpus becomes an effective one point two five terabytes of training signal.
[O] So the raw byte count actually understates how much academic voice a model trained on this sees.
[G] Considerably. By final weight, Pile-CC leads at just over eighteen percent, then PubMed Central at just over fourteen, Books3 at just over twelve, OpenWebText2 at about ten, and arXiv at just under nine. Roughly a third web, a third academic, a third books, code, and dialogue.
[S] And how do they even measure quality across sources that different — a court opinion and a Reddit thread don't share a metric.
[G] That's the tokenization problem, and it's why they measure in bits per byte instead of perplexity. Perplexity depends on how a model chops text into tokens, so different tokenizers and different scripts make it incomparable across datasets. Bits per U T F-8 byte sidesteps that: you take the model's log-loss, rescale it by the corpus's tokens-to-bytes ratio, and get a number that means the same thing everywhere. For the Pile as a whole, that ratio works out to about point two nine GPT-2 tokens per byte.
[O] Okay, and what do they actually do with that metric?
[G] Two separate experiments. First, benchmarking: score every off-the-shelf GPT-2 and GPT-3 size, document by document, on each Pile component, to see where existing models are weak. Second — and this is the real test of the diversity hypothesis — train architecturally identical one point three billion parameter models, GPT-3's own design, from scratch on three different corpora held to the same size: the Pile, CC-100, and raw Common Crawl. Each gets downsampled to about forty gigabytes and decontaminated with the same thirteen-gram overlap filter GPT-3 used against its own evaluation sets.
[S] Same size, same architecture, same decontamination. That's a genuinely controlled comparison.
[G] It is, within the box they drew. On top of both experiments, they spend roughly a third of the paper just documenting the corpus — language identification, document length distributions, topic modeling, profanity rates, bias co-occurrence by gender, religion, and race, and a dataset-by-dataset accounting of licensing and consent.
[O] Let's get to what they found. Start with the free result — the one where nothing was trained on the Pile at all.
[G] Right, this is the benchmarking half. Zero-shot, meaning none of these GPT-2 or GPT-3 models ever saw a byte of Pile data, and yet performance follows a clean scaling law against parameter count. For the GPT-3 family, the line of best fit has a slope of negative point one six seven four and an intercept around two point five five, with no sign of diminishing returns.
[S] Hold on — how do they even know the parameter counts for the GPT-3 API models? OpenAI never published those.
[G] They don't know them. They assume ada, babbage, curie, and davinci correspond to two point seven billion, six point seven billion, thirteen billion, and one hundred seventy-five billion parameters, and they say so explicitly, in a footnote. So that slope is a real measurement plotted against a guessed x-axis.
[O] Noted, but the underlying claim — that the Pile behaves as a coherent, broad-coverage benchmark even for models that never trained on it — doesn't depend on the exact parameter counts.
[S] Fair, the ranking's still informative even if the axis is fuzzy.
[G] The second finding is more useful for actually deciding what to train on. They build a proxy metric for how different each Pile component is from GPT-3's own training distribution, normalized against OpenWebText2. The components GPT-3 struggles with most, relative to a Pile-trained GPT-2, are DM Mathematics, Enron Emails, PubMed Central, GitHub, and arXiv — math, email, biomedical writing, code, and academic papers. Sets that overlap GPT-3's actual training data, like Books3, Wikipedia, Pile-CC, and the Gutenberg set, score close to zero.
[O] Which is exactly the sanity check you'd want — it's not measuring noise, it's measuring genuine domain distance.
[S] Except the authors themselves flag it as, quote, "confounded by dataset specific scaling effects," and they admit some components show GPT-3 mysteriously outperforming the Pile-trained model. That's not a small caveat for a metric doing this much argumentative work.
[G] They do say that, and they leave it as an open question rather than explaining it away. Now, the headline experiment — the one point three billion parameter models trained from scratch. At matched size, around forty gigabytes each, the Pile-trained model beats both CC-100 and raw Common Crawl on every single Pile test component, by huge margins on the sources farthest from web text and more modestly on web-like text.
[O] Numbers, please.
[G] Overall Pile test bits per byte: point nine four for the Pile model, one point three three for CC-100, one point one three for raw Common Crawl. On WikiText perplexity: five point six for the Pile, versus eight point three for CC-100 and eleven point eight for raw Common Crawl. That's a clean, large win.
[S] And LAMBADA?
[G] LAMBADA is the flat spot. Twelve point eight perplexity for the Pile versus eleven point eight for CC-100, so the Pile is actually slightly worse there, and fifty point one percent accuracy versus forty-nine point seven — basically a tie. Raw Common Crawl trails badly on LAMBADA, at forty-three point eight percent accuracy.
[O] So the diversity story wins almost everywhere, but it's not a monotonic win on every downstream task.
[S] Which matters, because the paper's own abstract claims improvement on downstream evaluations generally, and LAMBADA doesn't really deliver that.
[G] There's also a genuinely surprising result buried in that same table: raw, unfiltered Common Crawl beats the more heavily filtered CC-100 on Pile bits per byte, even though CC-100 wins comfortably on WikiText and LAMBADA. Their explanation is that CC-100 filters by training a language model on Wikipedia and throwing out anything with perplexity too high or too low, which quietly discards diversity along with noise.
[O] That's a great cautionary result on its own — aggressive filtering can just be a diversity tax you didn't notice you were paying.
[S] Agreed, I'll give them that one for free.
[O] Okay, let's argue about what this actually means. My case: this paper proved, under a controlled, matched-size comparison, that a deliberately diverse mixture beats filtered web crawl, and it did so while being far more honest about its own contents than anything that came before it. Datasheets, per-component bias tables, an explicit consent accounting — none of that was standard practice for training corpora in 2020, and this paper helped make it standard.
[S] My case against: every training result in this paper runs at one point three billion parameters on forty-gigabyte slices. The authors say outright that this comparison is, quote, "generous to CC-100," because CC-100 is only about a third the size of the real Pile. So what's actually demonstrated is "better mixture wins at a small, fixed budget," not "more diverse data wins at the scale anyone training a frontier model actually cares about." That gap never gets closed in this paper.
[G] Both of those are accurate reads, and the paper hands you that exact caveat itself, in that exact phrase. I'd add two more cracks. First, contamination: the authors admit their within-Pile deduplication is imperfect, so some train, validation, and test documents may still overlap. Second, several Pile components — Books3, Wikipedia, Pile-CC, the Gutenberg set — already overlap what GPT-3 trained on, which is precisely why those components score near zero on the domain-difference metric. That means the Pile isn't a clean, fully held-out benchmark for any model that already trained on the open web and books.

Their own table lists five components in exactly that state — public availability checked, nothing else. The authors do offer a fair-use argument for why this is legal, but they put a footnote on their own footnote saying, quote, "this discussion does not, and is not intended to, constitute legal advice."
[O] They're being unusually candid about a real legal risk, which I'd still rather have than silence.
[G] Candor is the right word, and it cuts both ways. It's genuinely more transparent than any comparable release at the time. The paper's own legal defense didn't hold up.
[S] So the diversity thesis survives. The legal foundation under a chunk of it didn't.

[G] That's a fair summary. My own read, going a little beyond what's actually written in the paper, is that the diversity result is well-supported at the scale they tested, and the documentation norm is the part that aged best. The scale question and the licensing question are the two things this paper genuinely leaves open.
[S] For anyone doing evaluation work specifically, what's the actual carryover here?
[G] Two things. Bits per byte, and reporting it per component rather than as one blended number, becomes a template — if you want to know where a model is weak, break the evaluation down by domain instead of hiding behind an aggregate score. And the decontamination discipline, the thirteen-gram overlap filter borrowed from GPT-3, is a norm this paper helped cement: if you're claiming a held-out win, you'd better show your filtering, not just assert it.
[O] And the honest-documentation habit. A dataset paper that ships a bias table and a consent table alongside its benchmark numbers set an expectation a lot of later releases had to live up to, even the ones that didn't love living up to it.
[S] Or that quietly skipped the parts about consent.
[G] If I had to leave the listener with one sentence, it's this: the paper's own case is that a curated, diverse mixture beats filtered web crawl at matched budget, backed by real documentation — and both halves of that claim came with limits the authors stated plainly.
