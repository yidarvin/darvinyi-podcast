[G] Because reward models are load bearing for basically every Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback pipeline, RLHF from here on, and until this paper almost nobody had actually measured what happens to one when the world it sees at test time stops looking like its training data.
[O] Naomi, set the stage for us. What is a reward model doing, mechanically?
[G] It takes a prompt and a candidate response and outputs a single number, a logit, meant to capture how well that response adheres to some rewarded behavior, helpfulness, harmlessness, whatever the preference data encodes. It's trained on pairs. Given a prompt, one response was preferred by a human labeler over another, and the model learns to score the preferred one higher. Then that trained model gets used two ways, to optimize the policy model during training, and increasingly at inference time, to flag whether a fresh response is any good.
[S] And the problem is that every piece of evidence we have that a reward model works comes from a held out test set drawn from that exact same training distribution.
[G] Exactly. Real deployments aren't stationary. Users send prompts unlike anything in the preference set, and the policy model's own outputs drift as it's optimized against the reward model. In ordinary classification this is a well studied failure mode, models lose accuracy under distribution shift, and worse, they get miscalibrated, confident exactly when they shouldn't be. That's the Ovadia and colleagues finding from twenty nineteen. Whether reward models inherit that same fragility was essentially unmeasured before this paper.
[O] There was some prior signal though, right? People had noticed reward models wobble.
[G] Concurrent work had started to circle it. Lightman and colleagues, in the Let's Verify Step by Step paper, saw reward model performance drop across different STEM question types. And a paper called Generalization Analogies, from Clymer and colleagues, found degradation under several kinds of shift. But both of those studies shifted the prompt and the response together, as one bundled change.
[S] So you can't tell which half of the shift is actually doing the damage.
[G] Right, and that is this paper's real methodological contribution. They decompose the shift. Hold one side in distribution, push only the other side out of distribution, or O O D, and watch accuracy, calibration, and detectability separately for prompt shift versus response shift.
[O] Walk us through the actual setup. What model, what data?
[G] It's deliberately minimal, and the authors call it a baseline analysis on purpose. One reward model, OpenAssistant's deberta-v3-large-v2, roughly four hundred million parameters. One dataset, Summarize From Feedback, from Stiennon and colleagues, where each response is a candidate summary of some source text, and the human label says which of two candidate summaries is better.
[S] So this is the older discriminative style reward model, not a modern generative or LLM as judge scorer.
[G] Correct, and that's worth flagging up front, I'll come back to it.
[O] How do they actually score it?
[G] Two axes. First, reward accuracy, does the model score the human preferred response higher than the other one. That's one if the preferred response gets the higher logit, zero otherwise, averaged over the test set. Second, calibration, does the model's confidence match its actual accuracy. Confidence here is the max softmax over the two response logits, since it's a binary forced choice between two candidates. They measure miscalibration with Expected Calibration Error, E C E, using ten confidence bins, the standard recipe from Guo and colleagues' two thousand seventeen calibration paper. A perfectly calibrated model that says it's eighty percent sure should be right eighty percent of the time.
[S] And the actual manipulation, how do they create out of distribution prompts and responses?
[G] Two knobs, and this is the clever part. First, natural or lingual shift. English is in distribution, since that's what Summarize From Feedback is in. They translate the English prompts and responses into French, Spanish, and German using OPUS-MT, and, this is the key move, they cross in distribution and out of distribution independently across prompt and response. Four cells, both English, prompt translated only, response translated only, or both translated. That independence is what lets them isolate which side actually matters.
[O] And the second knob?
[G] Artificial shift, word level perturbation. Each word gets inserted, deleted, or replaced with a random same language word, with some probability p, swept continuously from zero to one across ten trials per setting. That turns out of distribution-ness into a dial instead of a binary flag. They're upfront that this isn't representative of any real world shift, it's a synthetic stress test to trace the shape of the degradation curve.
[S] Do they flag when the label itself might stop meaning anything?
[G] Yes, explicitly. At high perturbation probabilities they say it's unclear whether the human preference ranking even survives the mangling. They keep that regime in the paper anyway, to show the pattern taken to an extreme, but they don't pretend it's meaningful data on its own.
[O] And then there's the detection piece. How do you know, at inference time, that a reward model has wandered off its home turf?
[G] They reuse something called the Energy Score, originally from classification out of distribution detection, Liu and colleagues, two thousand twenty. In classification it's the negative log of the sum of exponentiated logits across classes. Here they swap in the two reward logits for the two candidate responses. Low energy roughly means in distribution, high energy means out of distribution.
[S] What makes that particular choice worth a whole section?
[G] The constraint is the whole point. This score needs nothing but the two output logits, no internal activations, no inference time backpropagation, no access to the reward model's training set. That matters a lot in practice, because reward model training data is often unreleased, and a deployed reward model may expose nothing but its score. They also justify Energy Score over the simpler Maximum Softmax Probability baseline, citing the broader O O D literature where energy reliably beats max softmax.
[O] Okay, numbers. What happened under the lingual shift?
[G] Baseline, English prompt and English response, seventy two point three percent accuracy, fourteen point five three percent E C E. Translate just the prompt, keep the response English, accuracy barely moves, down to seventy point two nine percent, and E C E actually improves slightly, down to ten point eight percent. Translate just the response, keep the prompt English, accuracy drops to sixty five point six nine percent, and E C E jumps to twenty point oh three percent. Translate both, accuracy sixty four point four four percent, E C E nineteen point eight percent.
[S] So response shift costs about six and a half points of accuracy, prompt shift costs about two.
[G] That's the headline asymmetry, and the standard deviations across the three languages are small, under a point on accuracy, under six tenths of a point on E C E, so it's a consistent pattern, not language specific noise.
[O] And the artificial sweep sharpens that story?
[G] It does, and this is where the paper's most interesting finding shows up. As you crank the perturbation probability up, accuracy falls for every condition, fastest when both prompt and response are perturbed together. But the calibration curve for response shift does something genuinely novel. Near the training distribution, so called near O O D, the model stays overconfident even as its accuracy is already dropping. E C E spikes to roughly twenty five percent around a perturbation probability of thirty to forty percent. Then, far out of distribution, confidence collapses and catches up with the falling accuracy, so E C E actually shrinks back down to around five percent, even better than the in distribution baseline.
[S] Which sounds great until you think hard about what catching up actually means there.
[G] I'll get into that shortly. The prompt only curve, meanwhile, is almost flat the whole way. Even with completely randomized prompts, accuracy and confidence barely move.
[O] So reward models are basically deaf to what's happening on the prompt side.
[G] Relative to the response side, yes. There's a nice explanatory footnote here too. The lingual numbers from the first table line up almost exactly with the artificial curve at around fifteen percent perturbation, near O O D, not the far end. The authors' conjecture is that the DeBERTa backbone was pretrained on a multilingual corpus, so cross lingual correlations already fold translated text into a shared representation space, non English just doesn't register as very O O D to this particular model.
[S] And the detection results?
[G] Using the Energy Score on the lingual data, catching an O O D response alone gets an AUROC, area under the ROC curve, of seventy two point eight four percent. Catching an O O D prompt alone, sixty three point one one percent. Catching both shifted together, seventy seven point one two percent. F P R at ninety five, the false positive rate once you fix recall at ninety five percent, stays high across the board, roughly sixty to seventy two percent.
[O] That's a usable signal. Not sharp, but usable.
[S] Usable for responses. For prompts it's barely above a coin flip.
[G] The artificial sweep confirms that shape. Detection AUROC climbs toward essentially one as perturbation grows, with the response and prompt plus response curves rising fast, and the prompt only curve lagging badly, staying close to chance until the shift gets quite large. There's also an appendix result confirming Energy Score beats the simpler Max Softmax Probability baseline, consistent with what the broader O O D literature predicts.
