[O] Picture this — a lab's own report shows its flagship model beating the original GPT-4 on five out of six major benchmarks: math, reasoning, coding, all of it.
[S] Two pages later, that exact same model loses to the newer GPT-4 Turbo on every single one of those same six benchmarks. Same report, same table format, completely different verdict depending which GPT-4 you happen to pick.
[O] Which is exactly why I picked this one — it's the ChatGLM report, tracing four generations from G-L-M-130B all the way to G-L-M-4 All Tools, and to its credit it shows both comparisons side by side instead of hiding one.
[S] Or it just couldn't hide it, because the newer Turbo model shipped while they were still finishing the report. Let's find out which read is fair.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an AI optimist, an AI skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the litsearch site. Today's paper is "ChatGLM: A Family of Large Language Models from G-L-M-130B to G-L-M-4 All Tools," out of Zhipu AI and Tsinghua University, posted in June of twenty twenty-four.
[S] It's on the docket because it documents an entire model lineage across a year and a half rather than one snapshot, and because it makes an unusually specific claim — leading GPT-4 on Chinese-language alignment, a dimension Western labs almost never report on directly.
[O] Joining us to walk through it is Simon, who's studied this report closely. Simon, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. There's a lot packed into this one — four open generations plus a closed flagship — so let's get into it.
[S] Start with the gap, Simon. Why does this report need to exist — haven't we already got GPT-4's own report, the Llama papers, plenty of "here's how we built our LLM" documents?
[G] Sure, but almost all of those come from Western labs, trained and aligned mostly in English. By mid twenty twenty-four there wasn't a comparably detailed public record of a lab building a competitive bilingual Chinese-and-English model line end to end — architecture, pretraining data, a multi-stage alignment recipe, and an agentic tool-use layer on top, the kind of thing GPT-4's plugin ecosystem did but behind a closed API instead of in public.
[O] And the G-L-M line itself goes back further than most people probably realize.
[G] Right, back to twenty twenty-one, when Zhipu and Tsinghua introduced the G-L-M architecture — an autoregressive blank-infilling pretraining objective, a different framing of the pretraining task than GPT-style left-to-right prediction. They scaled that up to G-L-M-130B in twenty twenty-two, one hundred thirty billion parameters, built to match or beat GPT-3, and by that November, the HELM evaluation found it matched GPT-3 across dimensions.
[S] But each of those releases had its own separate paper — nothing tied the arc together, and nothing showed how much of the improvement came from where.
[G] Exactly the gap this report fills. It consolidates G-L-M-130B through ChatGLM-6B, ChatGLM2-6B, ChatGLM3-6B, and finally G-L-M-4 and G-L-M-4 All Tools into one account, so you can actually see what changed release to release.
[O] What made ChatGLM-6B itself land so hard when it shipped in March twenty twenty-three?
[G] Partly size, partly access. They designed it at six point two billion parameters specifically so it could run on a single consumer graphics card using four-bit quantization, at a time when most labs were shipping only much larger models locked behind an API. That accessibility is a big part of why the open-source community picked it up the way it did — the same lineage later crossed over ten million downloads on Hugging Face in twenty twenty-three alone.
[S] Let's get into the actual model, then. What does the G-L-M-4 architecture look like under the hood?
[G] It's a fairly conventional decoder-only Transformer with four specific departures from the standard recipe. No bias terms anywhere except in the query, key, and value projections of attention, mostly a training-speed win that also slightly improved length extrapolation. RMSNorm and SwiGLU replace LayerNorm and ReLU, which the team found simply performs better. Rotary Position Embeddings extended into a two-dimensional form, to fit G-L-M's existing two-dimensional positional scheme. And Group Query Attention instead of full multi-head attention, which shrinks the key-value cache at inference time.
[O] That last one usually costs you some capacity, doesn't it, since you're sharing parameters across more heads?
[G] It would, except they reinvest the savings — with fewer attention parameters, they widen the feedforward network, setting its hidden size to ten-thirds of the model's overall hidden dimension, so total parameter count stays roughly fixed. It's a rebalancing, not a straightforward cut.
[S] Context length is the number I actually want to interrogate, because "up to one million tokens" is the kind of claim that usually comes with an asterisk.
[G] Fair to be skeptical. It moved in three jumps — two thousand tokens for the original ChatGLM, thirty-two thousand for ChatGLM2 and ChatGLM3 using FlashAttention plus position-encoding extension, then a hundred twenty-eight thousand tokens, and an experimental one million tokens, for G-L-M-4. That last jump isn't architecture alone — it leans on their own long-context alignment recipe, called LongAlign, which specifically trains the model to use context that long instead of just accepting it as input and forgetting the middle.
[O] And the pretraining data underneath all of this?
[G] Roughly ten trillion tokens, mostly Chinese and English with a smaller slice across twenty-four other languages, cleaned through exact and fuzzy deduplication plus quality filtering, then tokenized with a byte-level vocabulary of one hundred fifty thousand tokens that merges separately-learned Chinese tokens with OpenAI's own cl100k tokenizer. Post-training runs supervised fine-tuning followed by reinforcement learning from human feedback — R-L-H-F — and the authors are specific that authentic, human-written prompt-and-response pairs mattered more to alignment quality than template- or model-generated ones. Annotators score every response on safety, factuality, relevance, helpfulness, and preference.
[S] And then on top of that aligned checkpoint sits the "All Tools" layer.
[G] Right — a separate alignment stage that trains the model to decide, on its own, when a request needs an external tool: a web browser, a Python interpreter, their CogView text-to-image model, or a user-defined function. It plans a sequence of calls, executes them, and folds the results back into its answer, recursively if the first pass isn't enough. The same mechanism underlies "GLMs," a user-facing platform for building custom agents on that same substrate.
[O] That plan-decide-call-and-fold-back-in loop is basically the template a lot of agent frameworks converged on afterward.
[S] We'll get to whether it actually performs as well as the paper claims it does.
[O] Okay, numbers. Start with the open lineage, since that's the part anyone can actually go verify for themselves.
[G] It compounds nicely. From ChatGLM-6B in March twenty twenty-three, to ChatGLM2-6B in June, to ChatGLM3-6B-Base in October, to the fully open G-L-M-4-9B in June twenty twenty-four — GSM8K climbs one point five, to twenty-five point nine, to seventy-two point three, to eighty-four. MMLU climbs twenty-five point two up to seventy-four point seven. HumanEval goes from zero all the way to seventy point one. And the Chinese C-Eval benchmark climbs from twenty-three point seven to seventy-seven point one.
[S] That's real, and it's checkable — those are open weights anyone can download and rerun themselves.
[G] Exactly, and it's worth sitting with that before we get to the closed model, because the two halves of this story have very different evidentiary status.
[O] Which brings us to the headline — G-L-M-4, the oh-five-twenty checkpoint, against the original GPT-4. Five out of six academic benchmarks in G-L-M's favor: GSM8K, MATH, BBH, GPQA, HumanEval. It trails only on MMLU, where the paper itself says it reaches ninety-six point three percent of GPT-4's accuracy.
[S] But against the newer GPT-4 Turbo, that flips completely. G-L-M trails on all six of the same benchmarks, and on some, like MATH, not narrowly — sixty-one point three versus seventy-three point four.
[G] Both true, and the paper reports both tables plainly, which I'll give them credit for — they don't bury the Turbo comparison. Where it gets more interesting is the Chinese-specific evaluations. On AlignBench, an eight-category Chinese alignment benchmark, G-L-M-4 posts an overall eight point zero, tying GPT-4 Turbo for the top score among every baseline tested, and clearly leading on the logic dimension specifically. On instruction-following, measured by IFEval, G-L-M-4 reaches ninety-nine percent and ninety-eight point six percent of GPT-4 Turbo's strict-mode accuracy in English and Chinese respectively. And on long-context handling, LongBench-Chat, its English score of eighty-seven point three sits right between GPT-4 Turbo and Claude 3 Opus — genuinely matching, not just close.
[O] And the agent numbers are the ones I find most interesting, because that's where the All Tools story actually has to pay off.
[G] On AgentBench, a seven-environment agent benchmark, G-L-M-4 posts the highest overall score of any model tested — three point seven nine, ahead of the original GPT-4's three point six nine and Claude 3 Opus's three point six two. On the Berkeley Function Call Leaderboard it's essentially tied with the newer GPT-4 Turbo, eighty-one point seven six versus eighty-one point two four. And in the actual All Tools setting, it roughly matches GPT-4's tool-using variant on Python-interpreter math tasks, and clearly beats it on browser-based information seeking — seventy-eight point zero eight versus sixty-seven point one two.
[S] Now give me the number that doesn't flatter them, because there's always one.
[G] There is — NaturalCodeBench, a coding benchmark built from real user prompts specifically because HumanEval is known to have contamination problems. There, G-L-M-4 posts forty-seven point one overall, behind Claude 3 Opus's forty-eight point three, and well behind both GPT-4 variants, which land in the low fifties.
[O] Alright, let's actually argue this one. My case: this is one of the clearest agentic-LLM templates I've seen from this era outside the very top labs — plan, decide which tool, call it, fold the result back in, recursively — shipped alongside a real user-facing platform for building custom agents on top of it. And the open half of the story, ChatGLM-6B through G-L-M-4-9B, is a genuinely reproducible improvement curve that any outside group can verify with the released weights.
[S] I'll take the open lineage claim — that one's earned. But "G-L-M-4 matches or beats GPT-4" is doing something different, and it's the part I don't trust yet. The exact checkpoint behind every one of those comparison tables, G-L-M-4 oh-five-twenty, isn't open-weight — it's API or web-app only. Nobody outside Zhipu can download it, rerun the exact prompts, or audit the decoding settings behind a single one of those numbers.
[G] That's the report's most consequential fact, and it's stated almost in passing. The open G-L-M-4-9B is meaningfully weaker than the flagship — seventy-four point seven MMLU versus eighty-three point three — so its openness doesn't transfer credibility to the closed model's head-to-head claims.
[S] And the baseline sourcing compounds it. The paper says competitor numbers were, quote, extracted from the corresponding technical reports or tested through their public APIs — with no indication, per number, which method produced which score, and no confirmation of a shared prompting and decoding harness across the two sources.
[O] Okay, but pooling self-reported numbers with your own API tests is close to standard practice for this whole genre of report — that's not some unique sin of this one.
[S] Standard doesn't mean trustworthy, it just means the whole genre shares the same blind spot. And AlignBench has a sharper version of the same problem — it's graded by GPT-4 itself. The model G-L-M-4 is claimed to tie, GPT-4 Turbo, and the model doing the judging are effectively the same family, and the paper never discusses that or brings in a second judge to check it.
[G] That's fair, and I'd add one more layer to it. G-L-M-4's single clearest win, AgentBench, is a benchmark the G-L-M team designed and published themselves. Its clearest loss, NaturalCodeBench, is a benchmark a competing team built specifically to resist the contamination that HumanEval is known to suffer from — and the report cites that exact contamination problem as its own motivation for building NaturalCodeBench, but never turns the same lens on its own ten-trillion-token training corpus. There's no decontamination table anywhere in the document for G-L-M-4's own scores.
[O] What about the safety numbers — is that section any more careful?
[G] Not particularly. The one safety evaluation they run is scoped to a curated Chinese subset of SafetyBench, with the most sensitive questions already filtered out before any model is tested. Same filter applied identically to every model, so it isn't an unfair advantage — but it does mean the published safety score describes a narrower test than the full official benchmark.
[S] Which is exactly the distinction that matters across this whole report. Vendor technical report, not independently reproducible benchmark study — those are different categories of evidence, and this paper is squarely the first one for every closed-model claim it makes.
[O] That contamination point actually moves me. I still think the open lineage and the All Tools template are real, durable contributions — but "beats GPT-4" as a flagship headline, I'd downgrade that from a result to a claim worth checking independently.
[G] Zoom out, and the evaluation-methodology thread here is actually the more durable one. A benchmark authored by the same team whose model tops it, a judge model that's also one of the models being judged, a contamination motivation that only ever points outward at other people's training data — none of that is unique to G-L-M, it's structural across almost every "beats GPT-4" report from this period.
[O] Which is exactly why the agentic template matters beyond this one paper. Plan, decide, call the tool, fold the result back in — that pattern shows up in essentially every serious agent framework that came after it, independent of whether you trust G-L-M-4's specific head-to-head numbers.
[S] And it's a reminder to treat any closed-model comparison table the way you'd treat a press release with footnotes — useful signal about direction, not a substitute for something you could rerun yourself.
[G] My one-line summary of the paper's own story: four generations of increasingly capable bilingual models, where the open half is fully verifiable and the closed flagship's GPT-4 comparison rests on trust rather than reproducibility.
