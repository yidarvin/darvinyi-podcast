[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the frontier of AI research. Today it's "The Leaderboard Illusion," from Shivalika Singh and colleagues at Cohere Labs with researchers from Princeton, Stanford, MIT, and the Allen Institute for AI — a preprint from April twenty twenty-five, later presented at NeurIPS's Datasets and Benchmarks track.
[S] It's an audit of Chatbot Arena — now rebranded LMArena — the crowdsourced, vote-on-two-anonymous-responses leaderboard that's become the closest thing the field has to a single scoreboard for who's actually winning in AI.
[O] And joining us to walk through it is Aurelio, a researcher who's studied this paper closely. Welcome, Aurelio.
[G] Glad to be here. This is one of the more consequential audits published on evaluation infrastructure in the last couple of years, precisely because so much rides on Arena's numbers being trustworthy.
[S] Let's set the stage. Why does Chatbot Arena matter enough that thirty-some pages auditing its plumbing is worth writing?
[G] Because it solved a real problem. Static multiple-choice benchmarks age badly — models get tuned to them, and they don't capture open-ended, real-world use. Arena lets anyone submit a prompt, watch two anonymous responses, and vote for the better one — a test set that renews daily, much harder to game than a fixed benchmark.
[O] That's exactly why it earned outsized influence — model releases get timed around it, journalists cite Arena rank as if it were settled science, even academic papers use Arena standing as a credibility signal.
[S] So the paper isn't asking whether Arena's questions are contaminated. It's asking something the field had mostly taken on faith.
[G] Right — is the machinery that turns millions of anonymous votes into one ranked list actually fair? A few of the authors had a personal reason to ask: they'd submitted Cohere's Command and Aya models to Arena and noticed their own open-weight models seemed to get shown to users far less often than proprietary competitors at a similar rank.
[S] Which we should flag up front — Cohere is a commercial competitor to OpenAI, Google, and Meta, all of whom come out looking bad in this paper. Worth keeping in mind as we go.
[G] It is, and the authors say so themselves — they disclose their own submission history directly in the paper. Disclosing a conflict doesn't resolve it, it just means you can weigh the evidence knowing where it came from.
[O] So how did they actually build the case? This isn't a survey of vibes, they assembled real data.
[G] Five overlapping datasets, spanning January twenty twenty-four through April twenty twenty-five — about two million battles across two hundred and forty-three models and forty-two providers. They combined Arena's public releases, a proprietary battle feed Arena shares with select providers like Cohere, a purpose-built crawl to catch behavior Arena doesn't publish, prompts from Cohere's own API traffic, and leaderboard snapshots reconstructed from Arena's Hugging Face commit history.
[S] That crawl matters a lot. Arena strips private battles out of everything else it releases, so without a dedicated scrape, you'd never see the private testing at all.
[G] Exactly the point. The mechanism underneath all this is the Bradley-Terry model — the same paired-comparison framework used to rate chess players. Each model gets a latent strength score, and the probability model i beats model j is a logistic function of the score gap. It's principled, but only holds under three assumptions: unbiased sampling — battles a model plays are representative, not cherry-picked after the fact; transitivity — rankings compose correctly across models that never played each other; and a fully connected comparison graph — every model links, directly or through a chain, to every other model.
[O] And the paper's whole structure is basically: pick one of those three assumptions, then show exactly how a real Arena policy breaks it.
[G] That's a fair read. The first is private testing. If a provider tests N variants privately and publishes only the best one, the number that reaches the public leaderboard isn't a single unbiased draw from that provider's true skill, it's the maximum of N draws — and the expected value of a maximum is provably larger than any single draw, whenever there's more than one variant and any variance in the estimates. They prove it with order statistics in an appendix, and simulate it: starting from a true score of twelve hundred, the expected best-of-N score climbs well above twelve hundred as you test more variants.
[S] That's a clean theoretical argument, but simulations can show anything you want them to show. Did they test it for real?
[G] They did, and this is the part I'd flag as unusually rigorous for a benchmark critique. Cohere's own authors submitted two byte-for-byte identical checkpoints of Aya-Vision eight-B to the live Arena, without disclosing the duplication. Same weights, same everything. Separately, they also submitted two genuinely different Aya-Vision thirty-two-B checkpoints, each of which had looked like the strongest final candidate on different internal evaluations.
[O] Turning an abstract worry about selection bias into a live field experiment against the actual production ranking system. Most people writing benchmark critiques don't have the standing, or the nerve, to run that experiment on the real thing.
[S] Let's get to the actual numbers, because that's where this stops being theoretical.
[G] On private testing: auditing that dedicated crawl from January through March twenty twenty-five, they found twenty-seven private Meta variants tested ahead of the Llama four release, in a single month. Ten for Google, seven for Amazon, single digits for OpenAI, Nvidia, Alibaba, Reka, StepFun, and X-A-I. Counting Meta's parallel testing on Arena's vision leaderboard brings its total to forty-three. No academic lab shows up in that data at all.
[O] Which the authors read as evidence that only a subset of providers even knew private testing was an option in the first place, not that everyone had equal access and only some used it.
[S] And the identical-checkpoint result we opened with — ten sixty-nine versus ten fifty-two for the exact same Aya-Vision eight-B weights, four models sitting in that gap. The genuinely different thirty-two-B checkpoints spread even wider, ten ninety-seven versus ten fifty-nine, nine models in between.
[G] There's a second effect worth naming too — it's not only a score-selection trick, it's a data-acquisition tactic. When Cohere went from testing one variant to three, the share of Arena's daily prompt volume their models captured rose from five point nine percent to nineteen point four percent. More variants means more exposure, independent of any score benefit.
[O] Now the data-access numbers, because these are the ones that surprised me most. Google and OpenAI are each estimated to have received roughly a fifth of all prompts and battle outcomes ever recorded on the Arena, nineteen point two percent and twenty point four percent respectively.
[G] Zoom out to the top four: OpenAI, Google, Meta, and Anthropic together account for sixty-two point eight percent of all Arena data. That's sixty-eight times the combined share received by Allen AI, Stanford, Princeton, and Berkeley put together.
[S] Sixty-eight times. That's not rounding noise in a busy system, that's a structurally different regime for academic labs versus the big four.
[G] By license class it's just as stark. Eighty-three open-weight models combined got an estimated twenty-nine point seven percent of all Arena data. Forty-one fully open-source models got eight point nine percent.
[O] And they didn't just measure the access gap, they measured what it buys. They fine-tuned a seven-B Cohere base model three ways — zero, thirty, and seventy percent Arena data in the training mix — and measured win rate against Llama three point one eight-B-Instruct on ArenaHard, a five-hundred-prompt benchmark ninety-eight point six percent correlated with live Arena.
[G] Win rate went twenty-three point five, to forty-two point seven, to forty-nine point nine percent — relative gains of eighty-one point seven and over a hundred twelve percent over baseline. And this was deliberately conservative — a modest, publicly derivable slice of Arena data, nowhere close to what a provider with full API access could assemble.
[S] But here's the tell — did that same lift show up on a benchmark that has nothing to do with Arena?
[G] It didn't. On MMLU, out of distribution, performance slipped from sixty-six point five percent to sixty-four point four percent at the thirty-percent mix, before partially recovering to sixty-five point nine at seventy percent. That divergence — up on Arena's own proxy, flat or down everywhere else — is the paper's strongest evidence that what's being bought is fit to Arena's distribution, not general capability.
[O] And then there's deprecation, the most quietly damning number in the whole paper. Arena's own backend lists forty-seven models as officially deprecated. The authors' audit finds two hundred and five of the two hundred and forty-three tracked models have been silently deprecated, sampling reduced to near zero, no public notice.
[G] Sixty-four percent of that silently-dropped group is open-weight or fully open-source. By license class, eighty-seven point eight percent of open-weight models and eighty-nine percent of open-source models eventually get deprecated or go inactive, versus eighty percent of proprietary models.
[S] And why does that matter for the ranking itself, not just fairness optics?
[G] Because Bradley-Terry rankings assume the comparison network stays connected, and that the task distribution stays comparable across models. In their own four-model toy simulation, deprecating just one model partway through a shift in what people are asking is enough to flip the relative rank order of the models left standing. Silent deprecation isn't just an accounting gap, it's a mechanism that can distort who appears to be winning.
[O] Okay, let's argue about what this actually means. My steelman: this is self-correcting science working as intended. A community leaderboard had a real trust problem, and someone with the access and the technical chops to prove it ran the experiments on the live system, not just in theory, and produced five concrete, implementable reforms.
[S] My deflationary case: read the language. The abstract and conclusion use words like "willful engagement" and call this "a new low for the AI research field" — strong language about intent. What's actually demonstrated is opportunity plus a suspicious pattern: Meta tested twenty-seven variants, and providers have topped the leaderboard within days of each other, multiple times. That's consistent with gaming. It's also consistent with a large lab just testing a lot of models before shipping.
[G] That's a real tension, and to their credit the authors flag it themselves — their own recommendations admit "there are valid reasons to offer private testing," like internal regression checks and staged rollouts. That sits awkwardly next to "new low for the field" in the conclusion. What's rock solid is the mechanism: best-of-N selection provably violates Bradley-Terry's unbiased-sampling assumption, shown both in theory and on the live leaderboard. Whether specific labs did it to deceive, or just because that's how model development works, is a harder claim, argued more by pattern than direct evidence of intent.
[O] Fair. But Aurelio, LMArena actually responded to this publicly, right? Walk us through their side.
[G] They did, and it's worth taking seriously rather than treating this paper as the final word. LMArena's response disputes several numbers. On open-model representation, they put combined open models at forty point nine percent of the leaderboard, arguing the paper's tally undercounts open-weight models like Llama and Gemma. On the score-boost claim, they say the roughly hundred-point figure comes from a simulation with an arbitrarily chosen variance, not real Arena data — their own measured effect is closer to eleven Elo points after fifty tests and three thousand votes, real, but far smaller than the headline suggests. And on the identical checkpoints, they argue the scores fall within overlapping confidence intervals — ordinary statistical noise, not proof of exploitable bias.
[S] That's a different story than "systematic gaming" — and it isn't only LMArena saying this is overstated. The paper's own summary box claims testing ten variants yields about a hundred points, while the body text describing that same simulation says testing twenty variants yields about fifty — and the chart matches the body text, not the summary box. So the "hundred points" figure people are most likely to repeat is the paper's own inflated shorthand for its own finding.
[G] The paper also doesn't demonstrate a counterfactual re-ranking of today's top models under its reforms — a real gap. Every distortion is quantified via simulation or a narrow controlled experiment, never as "here's the leaderboard once you fix all five things." And the concrete field experiments, the identical checkpoints and the thirty-two-B variants, both landed in the ten-fifty-to-eleven-hundred range, nowhere near the fourteen-hundred-plus scores at the actual top.
[O] Which doesn't mean it's not happening at the top too, just that this paper's own live experiments don't reach that far up the leaderboard to show it directly.
[S] There's also an interested-party wrinkle worth being honest about. LMArena notes their private-testing policy has been public since March of twenty twenty-four, and that they'd already helped Cohere evaluate nine pre-release models, more than most competitors got. That doesn't disprove the paper's broader findings, but it complicates the "undisclosed policy that only benefits our rivals" framing.
[G] It does. My honest read, a bit beyond the paper itself: the core statistical argument — that best-of-N selection breaks an explicit assumption of Arena's ranking model — survives this rebuttal intact, because it's a property of statistics, not of Arena's specific numbers. What's genuinely contested is magnitude and intent: how many points this is worth, and whether it reflects coordinated gaming or uneven awareness of a permitted practice.
[O] Zoom out, why should someone who doesn't build chatbots care about any of this?
[G] Because the failure mode generalizes past Arena. Any evaluation that lets a participant choose which of several private attempts to report is running a best-of-N selection process, whether that's a public leaderboard, an internal eval suite, or a paper's own ablation table. The math doesn't care what domain you're in.
[S] There's a contamination analogue worth naming, for anyone who thinks Arena is immune because it's dynamic. Seven point three percent of prompts from one month reappear verbatim the next, rising to nine percent under a relaxed similarity threshold, after de-duplication already removes roughly a fifth of monthly prompts as near-duplicates. A provider with continuous, high-volume API access has effectively already previewed next month's test distribution.
[O] Which is a good reminder that dynamic and leak-proof aren't the same property, even when a benchmark's whole pitch is that it renews itself.
[G] The deeper issue is what happens when a field concentrates trust in one human-preference leaderboard as the tiebreaker for who's winning. Any single instrument, however well-designed, becomes a target the moment enough rides on it — that's Goodhart's Law, and the paper opens with exactly that quote. The fix isn't necessarily trust Arena less, it's don't make any one leaderboard load-bearing enough that gaming it is worth the effort.
[G] If there's one sentence to take from this paper, it's that Chatbot Arena's rankings currently measure some mix of model capability and a provider's access to and engagement with the Arena mechanism itself, and untangling those two is exactly what the paper's five reforms are trying to do.
[O] My takeaway is that this is what mature evaluation science looks like, someone ran the actual experiment on the actual system instead of just complaining about it, and produced fixes an organizer could ship next quarter.
[S] Mine is: read the rebuttal before you repeat the headline numbers. Sixty-eight times more data and a new low for the field are very different claims, and only one of them survived LMArena's response intact.
