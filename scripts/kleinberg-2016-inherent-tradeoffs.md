It's one of three independent groups that landed on close variants of the same result within about six weeks of each other in twenty sixteen.
[S] Let's set the scene, because the motivating dispute is genuinely striking. May twenty sixteen, ProPublica publishes an investigation into COMPAS, a risk tool used in bail, sentencing, and parole decisions.
[G] Julia Angwin and colleagues found that among defendants who did not go on to reoffend, Black defendants were far more likely than white defendants to have been flagged high risk. Among defendants who did reoffend, white defendants were far more likely to have been flagged low risk. That's a claim about unequal error rates.
[O] And the tool's defenders came back with a completely different statistical claim.
[G] Right, Dieterich, Mendoza, and Brennan at Northpointe argued the tool is well calibrated. A given score means roughly the same thing regardless of race, among people scored, say, seven out of ten, about the same fraction actually go on to reoffend whether they're Black or white. That's a real, independently defensible property.
[S] So you've got "the tool over-flags Black defendants" and "the tool's scores mean the same thing across race," both defensible, apparently contradicting each other, and both drawn from the same Broward County data.
[G] And before this paper, nobody had a general account of whether those two things could coexist by design, or whether this one tool just happened to land in some unlucky spot.
[O] That's the gap this paper closes.
[G] The authors strip away everything specific to criminal justice, no COMPAS, no Broward County, and ask a pure question: for any procedure assigning risk scores across two groups, can you simultaneously have well-calibrated scores and equal average scores for people who share the same true outcome? Answer that abstractly, and you get an abstract, permanent answer that applies to lending, medical testing, ad delivery, anything with two groups and a probability estimate.
[S] So walk us through the machinery, Rosalind. What actually is a risk assignment in this framework?
[G] Start simple. Every person has a feature vector, everything a decision-maker can observe about them, and a hidden true label, positive or negative. For a given feature vector there's a true probability that a person with it is positive, and the model assumes the procedure knows that probability exactly. People also belong to one of two groups, but critically, once you know someone's feature vector, knowing their group tells you nothing extra about their true label. A risk assignment sorts people into bins, each bin carrying a score meant as the probability that anyone in it is positive, and it's allowed to split people with identical features across bins at random, so the framework covers both hard yes-no classifiers and continuous, randomized scoring rules.
[O] That's a very general setup.
[G] Deliberately so, because generality is what makes the negative result strong later. Three fairness conditions sit on top of it. Calibration within groups says: for every score level, in every group, the actual fraction of people at that score who are truly positive matches the score. Balance for the negative class says people who are truly negative get the same average score whether they're in group one or group two. Balance for the positive class says the same thing for people who are truly positive.
[S] And those balance conditions are not what people usually call statistical parity.
[G] Correctly flagged, and the paper is careful about this. Statistical parity asks for equal average scores across the whole group, positive and negative pooled together, a much blunter requirement that's often neither feasible nor desirable. Balance for the positive and negative classes only asks that, once you condition on someone's actual outcome, their score doesn't depend on their group. It's a narrower, more targeted notion, and it's the one closest to "equal error rates."
[O] So the whole trilemma is calibration plus both of those balance conditions, all three at once.
[G] Exactly, and the proof that you generally can't have all three is remarkably short. Fix a group. Calibration forces the total score handed out to that group to equal the number of people in that group who are actually positive. Now let the average score given to negative people be one unknown, and the average score given to positive people be a second unknown, the same two unknowns for both groups, because balance says those averages don't vary by group. That turns each group's condition into one linear equation in those two unknowns.
[S] Two groups, two lines.
[G] Two lines in the same two unknowns. If both groups have the same base rate, meaning the same overall fraction of truly positive people, the two equations describe the exact same line, so any point on it works, and lots of solutions exist. If the base rates differ, the two lines are distinct, and two distinct lines in a plane intersect in exactly one point. The authors work that point out directly: an average score of zero for the negative class and one for the positive class. Which is just another way of saying perfect prediction, no residual uncertainty at all.
[O] So the only ways out are coincidence, equal base rates by luck, or omniscience.
[S] Say the theorem itself, precisely.
[G] If a risk assignment satisfies calibration and both balance conditions, then either the two groups' base rates are equal, or the predictor achieves perfect prediction, meaning every feature vector maps to a probability of exactly zero or one. Those are the only two escapes, and the proof holds no matter how the risk assignment is computed, an algorithm, a human judge, a committee, doesn't matter.
[O] That's a clean binary statement, satisfied or not. Real risk scores are never exactly anything, though. What happens if you only need approximate calibration and approximate balance?
[G] That's the paper's second theorem, and it's arguably the more practically important one. For any small tolerance, if a risk assignment satisfies approximate versions of all three conditions, the underlying instance has to be within some function of that tolerance of one of the two special cases, approximately equal base rates, or approximately perfect prediction. Critically, that function doesn't collapse the trilemma. It scales like the square root of your tolerance, not the tolerance itself, which is a noticeably looser bound. A small violation across all three conditions only guarantees you're somewhere in a fairly wide neighborhood of one of the two escapes, not pinned tightly to it.
[S] So even the sloppy, real-world version of this doesn't quietly reopen the door.
[G] It doesn't, and that's the point of including it. It preempts the objection that this whole thing is a knife-edge mathematical curiosity that evaporates the moment you add measurement noise.
[O] There's a second piece of this paper I want on the table, because it's less quoted but just as sharp. What happens in the lucky case, when base rates are actually equal?
[G] Section four takes that up. When base rates match, fair assignments do exist, the trivial one that gives everyone the same score is fair by construction. So the authors ask whether there's a fair assignment that's also informative, that beats just handing everyone the population average. They show the most accurate possible assignment, the one that scores each feature vector at its own true probability with no aggregation at all, is well calibrated but generally violates both balance conditions. Any non-trivial assignment that is fair costs you accuracy relative to that ideal. Fairness and maximal informativeness trade off against each other even in the friendly case.
[S] Is there at least always a good compromise available?
[G] They prove the set of achievable fairness gaps across calibrated assignments forms a continuous interval, so a fair, non-trivial assignment exists exactly when some calibrated assignment favors group one and another favors group two, because you can always interpolate between them to land at zero gap. But there's a sting in the tail. Once you require that everyone sharing the same features must land in the same bin, no randomized splitting allowed, deciding whether a non-trivial fair assignment even exists becomes NP-complete. They prove it with a reduction from Subset Sum.
[O] So the statistical impossibility has a computational cousin.
[G] Right, and it's a separate obstacle. Even in the one regime where fairness is mathematically achievable, actually finding it, in the realistic case where you can't randomly split otherwise-identical people, is as hard as a canonical NP-complete problem.
[O] Okay, let me make the strongest optimist case on the table before anyone takes it apart. Before this paper, the COMPAS fight looked like someone was lying, or incompetent, or cherry-picking. This paper says: no, calibration and equal error rates are two different, independently reasonable properties, and whenever base rates differ, you mathematically cannot have both. That reframes the whole debate from a scandal into an honest trade-off you have to make a values judgment about. That's real clarity, and it generalizes past COMPAS to any domain with unequal base rates across groups.

[O] So what does someone doing evaluation work more broadly take from this on a Monday morning, Rosalind?
[G] That any single fairness or performance metric is implicitly trading off against some other metric you didn't name, and this paper hands you the actual algebra for one of the sharpest examples. It's exactly the kind of result that should make an evaluator suspicious whenever a system reports one clean number as proof of fairness or quality.
[S] It also predicted the field's next decade fairly well. Nobody found a hole in the theorem, the math holds. Instead, people routed around the premise. Dwork and colleagues' earlier individual-fairness proposal sidesteps the whole group-level trilemma by comparing similar individuals directly, at the cost of needing someone to define similarity in the first place. And roughly a decade later, multicalibration reframes which groups the calibration condition even applies to, many overlapping, computationally identifiable subgroups instead of two coarse buckets, which redistributes where the trade-off bites without repealing it.
