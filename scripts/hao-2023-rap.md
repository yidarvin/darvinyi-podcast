[O] A thirty-three billion parameter open model just beat G-P-T-four at stacking blocks, using nothing but search, by thirty-three percent relative.
[S] Sure, but strip away the headline and what you're really looking at is one small model doing dozens of extra forward passes for every single one G-P-T-four does.
[O] Call it what you want. It's still the same underlying language model, taught to imagine ahead instead of guessing once and hoping.
[S] Imagining ahead is a nice story. What I want to know is whether that imagination is ever actually checked against reality.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar dig into one paper from litsearch dot darvinyi dot com. Today's paper is "Reasoning with Language Model is Planning with World Model," by Shibo Hao and colleagues at U C San Diego and the University of Florida, published at E-M-N-L-P in twenty twenty-three. It's better known by its acronym, R-A-P, for Reasoning via Planning.
[S] And it's on the docket because it's one of the cleanest demonstrations we've seen that treating reasoning as planning, with an explicit world model and search, actually changes what a language model can solve, not just how it explains itself.
[O] Joining us to walk through it is Priya, who's spent a lot of time with this paper. Priya, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is one of my favorite papers from that period. It's got a genuinely different idea buried inside a very approachable set of experiments.
[S] Priya, set the scene. Why did the authors think chain of thought needed fixing at all? It had already become the default way people got LLMs to reason.
[G] It works remarkably well for a lot of tasks, but the authors point to one specific failure. On a block-stacking planning benchmark from Valmeekam and colleagues, G-P-T-three managed a success rate of only one percent, against seventy-eight percent for humans on the same problems. That's not a small gap, that's a task where the model is basically failing outright.
[O] Which is striking, because these are the same kinds of models that ace grade-school math questions with a few chain of thought examples.
[G] Exactly, and that contrast is the paper's whole diagnosis. Chain of thought generates one linear trace, forward, autoregressively, with no way to look ahead or reconsider a step once it's written. The authors argue that's fine for shallow problems, but real planning requires something chain of thought structurally can't do: simulate what a future state will look like, compare alternatives, and back out of a path that's going nowhere.
[S] So the claim is that LLMs are missing an internal world model, the thing that lets you imagine "if I do this, then that happens" before you commit to it.
[G] That's their framing exactly. They trace it back to the cognitive science literature on human mental models, the idea that people planning a course of action explore alternatives, roll forward the likely outcomes, and revise earlier steps. A single forward pass of text generation has none of that machinery.
[O] Were other people trying to fix this at the same time?
[G] Yes, and the paper is careful about it. Tree of Thoughts, from Yao and colleagues, came out concurrently and also builds a tree of reasoning paths using heuristic search like depth or breadth first search. The authors' distinction is that none of these methods, Tree of Thoughts included, formally instantiate a world model, an explicit state, and a reward signal into one unified framework. R-A-P's claim to novelty is putting all three of those pieces together in a principled way, borrowed from planning and reinforcement learning.
[O] Okay, walk us through the actual mechanism. How does one frozen language model become two different things?
[G] It's a clever reuse of the same weights with two different prompts. First, the LLM plays the reasoning agent: given the current state, it samples an action from a prompt describing what's allowed. Then, with a completely different prompt, the same LLM plays the world model: given that state and the action just taken, it predicts what the next state looks like. Chain those together and you get an explicit trace of alternating states and actions, not just a growing block of text.
[S] What does "state" even mean here? That feels like it could get slippery outside a toy domain.
[G] It's genuinely task-specific, and that's worth being honest about. In Blocksworld, a state is just the current arrangement of blocks described in plain language, and an action is one of four verbs — pick up, put down, stack, unstack — applied to a block. In GSM8K math problems, a state is the set of intermediate variable values solved so far, and an action is a sub-question that derives the next one. In PrOntoQA, the logical reasoning benchmark, a state is the fact currently under consideration, and an action is choosing which logical rule to apply next. Same scaffold, three very different fillings.
[O] And the search comes in on top of that.
[G] Right. Once you have states, actions, and a way to score them, a reward, you can use Monte Carlo tree search to explore the space instead of committing to one path. Each node in the tree is a state, each edge an action, and the algorithm tracks a running value estimate for every state-action pair, called Q, that estimates expected future reward. One iteration has four phases: selection, where you walk down the existing tree favoring a mix of high-value and under-explored branches, using an upper confidence bound rule; expansion, where you sample several candidate actions from the leaf and predict their resulting states; simulation, where you roll a lightweight policy forward to a terminal state to estimate the branch's value; and back-propagation, where the terminal reward gets pushed back up to update every value estimate on the path.
[S] That's a lot of extra LLM calls for one reasoning step. What's actually feeding the reward function? How does it know a step is good?
[G] Up to four signals, and this is where it gets a bit ad hoc, if I'm honest. There's the log probability of the action itself, basically the model's raw instinct. There's confidence in the predicted state, measured by resampling the world model's answer several times and taking how often the majority answer shows up, used heavily in GSM8K. There's self-evaluation, where you literally ask the model "is this reasoning step correct?" and use the probability it assigns to the word "yes" as the reward. And there are task-specific heuristics, like comparing the current block arrangement against the goal in Blocksworld.
[O] "A bit ad hoc." Say more.
[G] The paper is upfront that which combination of those four signals to use, per task, was chosen, in their words, "based on heuristics and our exploratory experiments." It's not one fixed recipe that transfers automatically. Someone has to go figure out what reward mix works for a new domain.
[S] That's worth flagging early, because "general framework" is doing some work in the abstract that the reward design doesn't quite back up.
[G] Fair, and we'll come back to that. One more piece worth naming: after the search budget runs out, ten or twenty iterations in the main experiments, R-A-P doesn't just take the highest-scoring path. It reads off the trace whose leaf node was visited most often, which the authors found worked better in practice than picking by raw score. And for tasks where you only need a final answer rather than a full trace, math, logic, there's R-A-P Aggregation: run several searches, collect the different answers, and take the plurality vote. It's basically self-consistency, but over search trajectories instead of independent samples.
[O] Let's get to the headline number, because it's a good one.
[G] On Blocksworld, split into two, four, and six-step problems, plain chain of thought with the base model, LLaMA thirty-three B, basically collapses on harder cases: seventeen percent success on two-step, two percent on four-step, zero on six-step. R-A-P with twenty search iterations gets to one hundred percent on two-step, eighty-eight percent on four-step, and forty-two percent on six-step. Averaged across all two hundred one test cases, weighted by group size, that's about sixty-four percent success for R-A-P against a base model that "completely fails," in the authors' words, on the harder problems.
[S] And the G-P-T-four comparison?
[G] That's the thirty-three percent relative figure from the cold open. G-P-T-four with plain chain of thought reaches a weighted average of forty-eight percent on that same Blocksworld set. R-A-P with LLaMA thirty-three B and twenty iterations reaches sixty-three point seven percent. So a much smaller open model, given search, beats a much larger closed model given one shot, by about a third relative.
[O] What does the actual mechanism of the win look like? Not just the number, the why.
[G] There's a nice qualitative example in the paper. Both chain of thought and R-A-P make the identical wrong first move. They both start stacking toward the second goal condition first, which blocks the first goal from ever being satisfiable. Chain of thought just commits to that path and fails. R-A-P makes the same mistake in some of its search iterations, but because it's exploring a tree rather than one line, it also tries the alternative branch, finds the one that satisfies both goals, and that's the trace that gets returned.
[S] Does the pattern hold outside Blocksworld, or is that the one domain built for exactly this kind of search?
[G] It holds, with smaller margins. On GSM8K, R-A-P without aggregation gets forty-eight point six percent, and with aggregation, fifty-one point six percent. Chain of thought alone is twenty-nine point four percent, and even boosted with self-consistency across ten samples, it only reaches forty-six point eight percent. Least-to-most prompting, the closest prior method conceptually, tops out at forty-two point five percent with self-consistency. On PrOntoQA, R-A-P gets ninety-four point two percent answer accuracy and seventy-eight point eight percent full-proof accuracy, against chain of thought's eighty-seven point eight and sixty-four point eight. So the edge over the best chain of thought variant is smaller here than on Blocksworld, but it's consistent across three structurally different tasks.
[O] And they didn't just stop at the smaller model. There's a generalization check too.
[G] Right, there's a follow-up experiment that swaps in a bigger model, Llama two, seventy billion parameters, and runs the full six hundred two case Blocksworld set, split into an "easy" condition where demonstrations are matched to the test case's difficulty, and a "hard" condition where they aren't. R-A-P with ten iterations gets sixty-five percent on easy and fifty-one percent on hard. Chain of thought gets eight percent and five percent. The gap holds up on a bigger, harder benchmark and a different model family.
[S] What about the reward ablations, because you flagged the reward design as hand-tuned, and I want to know how much that hand-tuning is actually buying.
[G] A lot, on Blocksworld specifically. Drop all three reward signals and success collapses from ninety-one percent, with all three combined, down to two percent. Using just the action-likelihood signal alone gets you to forty-six percent. So the combination genuinely matters, it's not riding on one dominant signal, but it does mean each task's best mix was found empirically, not derived from first principles.
[O] Okay, my strongest case: this is the first time I've seen "reasoning as planning" actually cash out as a repeatable performance gain, not just a nice metaphor. A smaller model outperforms a bigger one on a genuinely hard structured task, the win replicates across three different domains, and it survives a change of base model. If search over a self-simulated world model is this cheap to bolt onto an existing LLM, that's a real lever, not a paper trick.
[S] My strongest case against: none of this tells you what it costs. Every R-A-P iteration samples several candidate actions, predicts several resulting states, and rolls out a simulation to a terminal state. That's many LLM calls per single reasoning step chain of thought does in one shot. The paper reports its hardware, four A-5000 GPUs, but never reports the actual multiplier: how many forward passes, how many tokens, what the wall-clock or dollar cost is relative to chain of thought. Tree of Thoughts, the paper's own cited concurrent work, does report that overhead. R-A-P doesn't. "R-A-P beats G-P-T-four" reads to me like trading compute for accuracy without ever telling you the exchange rate.
[G] Priya here — you're both right, and the paper genuinely doesn't resolve it. The within-model comparisons are clean: same LLaMA thirty-three B backbone, same four-shot prompt budget, for chain of thought, least-to-most, and R-A-P. That part of the optimist case holds up completely. But the skeptic's point about the G-P-T-four comparison stands unchallenged in the text itself. It's arithmetically correct, but it mixes model scale with inference-time search budget, and there's no accounting of what that budget costs in absolute terms.
[O] Fine, point taken on the accounting gap. But even granting that, the mechanism story is real, right? It's not just "more compute wins." The case study literally shows R-A-P making the same mistake as chain of thought and then correcting it through backtracking. That's the world-model idea doing actual work, not just brute-force sampling.
[S] I'll grant that's a genuinely different mechanism than plain self-consistency, which just resamples independently and votes. Backtracking through a tree with a value estimate is structurally different. What I won't grant yet is that it generalizes past clean, symbolic, deterministic domains. Blocksworld, GSM8K, and PrOntoQA all have checkable, rule-based transitions. Nobody's tested this on anything messier.
[G] That's a fair boundary, and it's one the paper doesn't push past either. It's explicit in its own limitations section that it only uses frozen LLMs and doesn't touch fine-tuning or external tools. I'd add one more gap neither of you has raised: the world model's own accuracy is never measured directly. Every claim about the world model being reliable is inferred backward from whether the final task succeeded, not from checking whether predicted states actually matched what should happen. We know the reward signal correlates with success. We don't actually know how often the simulated states themselves are correct.
[O] That's a good one. So even the "world model" framing is a little bit of an act of faith.
[G] My honest read, going a step beyond what's in the paper: it's plausible the model gets a lot of state predictions wrong, and R-A-P still wins because the reward and the search structure are forgiving of individual bad predictions, not because the world model is faithful. The paper's design doesn't let you tell the difference.
[O] So if this holds up at scale, what changes?
[G] The most direct implication is for anyone building agentic systems or evaluation harnesses that lean on chain of thought as the reasoning primitive. R-A-P is a concrete argument that search over an explicit state representation, even a crude self-simulated one, buys real accuracy on tasks with genuine planning structure, not just on shallow arithmetic.
[S] From an evals standpoint, I'd flag the flip side: this is also a reminder that "beats G-P-T-four" headlines need an inference-budget asterisk attached. If a benchmark leaderboard doesn't report compute-matched comparisons, a method like this can look like a bigger win than it is once you account for cost.
[G] And that maps onto a broader thread in the reasoning literature since this paper. A lot of later work on test-time search and process reward models inherited exactly this tension between accuracy gains and unreported inference cost.
[O] My takeaway: this is the paper that made "reasoning is planning" feel literal rather than poetic, and I think a lot of what came after in test-time search owes it a debt.
[G] If there's one sentence to take from this paper, it's that giving a language model an explicit state to reason over, and letting it search rather than commit, turns a shockingly weak planner into a genuinely competent one on tasks with real structure.
[S] Mine: real gains, real mechanism, but an unreported compute bill and a hand-tuned reward function mean "general framework" is aspirational until someone reports the cost and tests it somewhere messier than Blocksworld.
