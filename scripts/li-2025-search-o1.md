[O] Here's the moment that got me: a reasoning model is halfway through a chemistry problem, it doesn't know the structure of a compound, and instead of admitting that, it just guesses — and the guess cascades into a wrong final answer.
[S] And here's the fix that has me squinting: give that same model a button it can press to fire a live web search mid-thought, any time it feels uncertain. That's a lot of trust to hand a chain of thought that's already prone to confabulating.
[O] Except the paper shows the model naming its own uncertainty out loud — "perhaps," "wait," "alternatively" — dozens of times per answer. It already knows when it's guessing. Search-o-one just gives it somewhere to go instead of guessing.
[S] Knowing you're guessing and knowing what to search for aren't the same skill. Let's see if the model's actually good at the second one, or if it just gets lucky against a live search index.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the litsearch dot darvinyi dot com map. Today it's Search-o-one: Agentic Search-Enhanced Large Reasoning Models, by Xiaoxi Li and colleagues at Renmin University of China and Tsinghua University, published at EMNLP twenty twenty-five. Joining us is Marcus, who's spent real time inside this one.
[G] Happy to be here. Quick note going in: I'm not one of the authors, so when I say "they" did something, that's the paper's team, not me.
[S] Marcus, one sentence before we dig in — what is Search-o-one actually adding on top of a model like QwQ or o1?
[G] It gives the reasoning model two new abilities layered on top, with no retraining: the freedom to trigger a web search whenever it hits a knowledge gap mid-chain, and a second pass that condenses whatever comes back into one clean fact before it re-enters the reasoning.
[O] Let's start with why that's needed. What's actually going wrong with these models today?
[G] Large reasoning models like OpenAI's o1, Qwen's QwQ, and DeepSeek-R1 solve hard problems by generating a long internal chain of thought before answering. That's genuinely powerful for logic, but the chain only ever draws on what got memorized during pretraining. The authors ran a simple diagnostic to show how often that runs out: they sampled QwQ-32B-Preview on the G-P-Q-A diamond set, one hundred ninety-eight PhD-level science questions, and counted hedge words like "perhaps," "alternatively," "wait," and "likely" inside the reasoning. "Perhaps" alone shows up an average of thirty point four times per output under direct reasoning, with no retrieval at all.
[O] So the model is basically telling on itself, over and over, in the middle of solving the problem.
[S] Sure, but standard retrieval-augmented generation already exists to fix knowledge gaps. Why isn't bolting that on enough?
[G] Because it barely helps, and the paper's own numbers show it. Standard R-A-G retrieves a fixed batch of documents once, up front, for the whole question, then hands them to the model alongside the prompt. On that same uncertainty count, Standard RAG only brings "perhaps" down to twenty-six point four occurrences — barely below direct reasoning's thirty point four, and worse than what the agentic version the paper builds achieves. The structural problem is that a single upfront retrieval can't anticipate the different knowledge needs of each step in a long chain, and dumping long, redundant documents straight into the reasoning stream disrupts its coherence.
[S] So what's actually new here, mechanically? Walk me through what happens when the model decides it needs to look something up.
[G] While it's generating the reasoning chain, the model can emit a search query wrapped in a special pair of tokens marking the start and end of a search. The instant that closing tag appears, generation pauses, the query gets pulled out and sent to the live Bing Web Search A-P-I — top ten results, U-S English region — and the actual page text is fetched with a tool called Jina Reader. This can fire more than once inside a single reasoning session, so different steps of a long chain can each trigger their own separate lookup exactly when they need it, which a single upfront retrieval structurally can't do.
[O] And that on-demand timing is the whole unlock — the retrieval finally matches the shape of the reasoning instead of guessing what the model will need before it's even started thinking.
[S] Fine, but raw web pages are noisy. Doesn't dumping a full page back into the chain of thought make coherence worse, not better?
[G] That's exactly the failure the second piece is built to solve. The authors found LRMs specifically struggle to fold long, retrieved pages into an ongoing chain without breaking it. So instead of splicing the raw document in, a separate generation pass — same backbone model, different prompt — reads the search query, the reasoning so far, and the retrieved pages, and produces one short, distilled statement of just the fact that's needed. Only that gets spliced back into the chain, and reasoning resumes from there. The paper's own worked example is a model trying to figure out the structure of a compound called trans-Cinnamaldehyde: instead of a full retrieved webpage, it gets handed back one clean sentence stating the molecule's actual structure, and that's the version of the chain that lands the correct final answer.
[O] Does any of this require retraining the base model?
[G] No training at all. It's purely an inference-time framework, wrapped around the same QwQ-32B-Preview backbone in every experiment in the paper. There's also a batch-inference mechanism underneath it: many questions run in parallel, and whenever any sequence in the batch needs a search, the queries across the whole batch get extracted and refined together before generation resumes. That's what makes running this across thousands of evaluation questions computationally tractable.
[S] And if a chain gets stuck searching and never lands on an answer — what happens then?
[G] There's a back-off strategy, applied to every retrieval-based method in the paper, not just Search-o-one: if a final answer never gets produced, the system just falls back to whatever direct reasoning without retrieval would have said. It's a sensible safety valve, but I'll flag now, since we'll come back to it — the paper never reports how often that back-off actually fires.
[O] Let's get to the numbers. I want the headline first.
[G] On five reasoning benchmarks — G-P-Q-A across physics, chemistry, and biology, three math sets, and a coding benchmark — Search-o-one beats every baseline built on the same QwQ-32B backbone. G-P-Q-A overall pass at one goes from fifty-eight point one under direct reasoning with no retrieval, up to sixty-three point six with Search-o-one. Averaged across all five reasoning datasets, the paper reports Search-o-one beating the naive agentic-R-A-G baseline by four point seven percent and beating plain direct reasoning by three point one percent.
[S] The naive agentic baseline already gets you most of the way there, though — sixty-one point six on G-P-Q-A versus Search-o-one's sixty-three point six. How much of that win is genuinely the Reason-in-Documents piece, versus just noise?
[G] Fair pressure. Two points on G-P-Q-A alone might sound modest, but it holds up across the math and code sets too, and there's a cleaner piece of evidence in a scaling experiment: even retrieving a single document, Search-o-one already beats both direct reasoning and standard R-A-G retrieving ten documents. That says the refinement mechanism, not just more retrieved text, is doing real work — otherwise more raw documents would just keep winning.
[O] And the human-expert comparison is the one everyone's going to quote.
[G] On the extended G-P-Q-A set — five hundred forty-six questions — Search-o-one's overall score of fifty-seven point nine beats the overall accuracy of every human-expert group tested: physicists at thirty-nine point nine, chemists at forty-eight point nine, biologists at thirty-seven point two. It even beats physicists and biologists inside their own specialty: sixty-eight point seven versus physicists' own fifty-seven point nine in physics, sixty-nine point five versus biologists' own sixty-eight point nine in biology.
[S] Every group? I thought there was a number that goes the other way.
[G] There is, and it's the one worth sitting with. Chemistry is the domain where Search-o-one loses outright — forty point seven against chemists' own seventy-two point six. That's a big gap, in the one PhD-level domain where the model's edge basically disappears.
[O] What about the open-domain question answering side — that's where a lot of agentic-search papers actually fall apart.
[G] It sharpens the story rather than breaking it. On single-hop questions, using sets like Natural Questions and TriviaQA, agentic search barely moves the needle over standard R-A-G — forty-seven point eight versus forty-seven point six average exact match — because a one-hop question only ever needs one lookup. On multi-hop sets — HotpotQA, two-W-I-K-I, MuSiQue, and Bamboogle — Search-o-one's average exact match beats standard R-A-G by twenty-nine point six percent and beats naive agentic R-A-G by five point three percent. The gains concentrate exactly where the mechanism should help: repeated, step-specific lookups, not retrieval in general.
[S] One more number I want on the record: standard R-A-G actually beats Search-o-one on TriviaQA specifically, doesn't it?
[G] It does — sixty-five point six versus Search-o-one's sixty-three point four exact match. It's a good reminder that the multi-hop story doesn't generalize to every single-hop case, even though the paper's framing leans hard on the wins.
[O] Okay, let's argue this out. My case for the defense: this is a training-free method bolted onto an off-the-shelf model, and it doesn't just nudge scores — it beats domain-expert humans overall, and inside two of three specialties on their own turf. That's what letting a model decide, on its own, when it doesn't know something is supposed to buy you, with a receipt attached.
[S] I'll grant the core ablation chain is clean — direct reasoning, standard R-A-G, naive agentic R-A-G, and Search-o-one all run on the identical QwQ-32B-Preview backbone, so comparing those four is fair. But two things bother me. First, this system hits live Bing search at evaluation time, not a frozen corpus, against benchmarks like Natural Questions and HotpotQA that are built from Wikipedia and have been public online for years. Nothing in the method filters out pages that discuss the benchmark itself.
[G] That's a real gap, and it's worth noting the authors treat it asymmetrically. LiveCodeBench gets an explicit freshness cutoff — problems from August through November twenty twenty-four, chosen specifically to avoid contamination. The open-domain Q-A sets get no equivalent guard. The paper doesn't measure or discuss this directly, so I genuinely can't tell you how much of the multi-hop edge is search-time leakage versus better step-by-step reasoning. That's an open question the paper leaves on the table.
[O] What about the closed-source rows in the results tables — GPT-4o, o1-preview, DeepSeek-R1-Lite? Search-o-one beats several of those too.
[S] Also worth flagging clearly: those numbers aren't reproduced under the paper's own harness. The paper states outright that closed-source model results are, quote, sourced from their official releases. So any framing that says Search-o-one beats o1-preview is not a controlled comparison — it's a QwQ-based system's numbers next to borrowed numbers from an entirely different evaluation setup.
[G] Agreed, and that's an important asterisk on the headline. The comparison that actually isolates this paper's contribution is the same-backbone chain: direct reasoning, standard R-A-G, naive agentic R-A-G, and Search-o-one, all on QwQ-32B-Preview, all run by the same authors under the same harness. That one I'd score clearly in Search-o-one's favor.
[S] Last flag, and it's the one that bugs me most: cost and latency. Every search call adds a Bing round trip, a page fetch, and a second full generation pass for the refinement step. None of that latency or A-P-I cost is quantified anywhere in the paper, and neither is how often that back-off strategy we mentioned earlier — falling back to direct reasoning when no answer comes out — actually triggers. If it's firing a lot, it could be quietly masking failures in the search loop itself.
[G] Both true, and both absent from the paper. I'd add one more: Reason-in-Documents is effectively a summarization step sitting inside the reasoning chain. An error introduced there wouldn't show up as a reasoning failure or a retrieval failure — it would look like a distillation failure, and the paper doesn't separate that failure mode out from the other two anywhere in its analysis.
[O] So where does that leave the headline claim — beating human experts on G-P-Q-A overall?
[G] Standing, but narrower than the framing suggests. It's a real result on a real benchmark, produced by a training-free mechanism doing exactly what it was designed to do. It's just not evidence that this system reasons better than a chemist about chemistry, and it doesn't settle how much of its open-domain edge is reasoning versus a live search index doing quiet work in the background.
[O] If this holds up under more scrutiny, the practical story is simple: agentic search becomes cheap, training-free scaffolding you wrap around any reasoning model you already have, not a separate system you have to build and train from scratch.
[S] The evaluation story is the one I keep coming back to, though. As agentic live search becomes standard practice, any benchmark drawn from public, previously indexed web content stops being a clean measurement of reasoning and starts partly measuring whether the search engine can just find the answer. Benchmarks are going to need the same kind of freshness guard LiveCodeBench already has, or this exact contamination question is going to keep recurring in every paper that does something similar.
[G] I'd agree with that, and add that it cuts both ways for evaluation design generally — once a system can call out to live external tools mid-reasoning, the boundary between "the model reasoned well" and "the model's tools did the work" gets genuinely harder to draw, and this paper is an early, useful example of exactly that blur.
[G] If I had to leave you with one line, it's this: the paper's real contribution isn't "let models search" — it's controlling when they search, and giving them a clean channel for the answer to re-enter the reasoning chain without breaking it.
[O] My takeaway: this is what agentic behavior earning its name looks like — a model that notices its own uncertainty and does something useful about it, training-free, and the G-P-Q-A number is hard to argue with.
[S] Mine's more of a caveat: believe the same-backbone ablation, be careful with the human-expert headline, and keep an eye out for whoever reruns this exact framework against a frozen retrieval index instead of live search — that number will tell us how much of this is reasoning and how much is the web doing the work.
