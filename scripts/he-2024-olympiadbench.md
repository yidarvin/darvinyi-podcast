[G] Saturation, stated plainly. By early twenty twenty-four, GPT-4 with the right prompting technique was hitting ninety-seven percent on GSM8K, the grade-school arithmetic set, and eighty-four point three percent on MATH, which is competition-level but still leans on a fairly well-worn problem structure. [O] So math had a ceiling problem. What about physics?
[G] Physics had the opposite problem — it never had a good benchmark to begin with. SciQ, ScienceQA, MMLU-STEM, and the Chinese equivalent C-Eval-STEM all pose physics as elementary-to-high-school multiple choice. A model can pattern-match its way through those without doing any real physical reasoning.
[S] And on the multimodal side, where diagrams actually matter?
[G] MathVista and MMMU exist, and they're genuinely broad, multi-discipline suites with real images. But the authors' complaint, which I think is fair, is that neither one concentrates on the complexity of the mathematics itself. They're wide, not deep.
[O] So the gap is a specific intersection nobody had filled: genuinely olympiad-hard, free-response instead of multiple choice, multimodal because real geometry and physics need diagrams, bilingual, and fully worked with expert solutions.
[G] Exactly, and the authors are explicit that they wanted, quote, the pinnacle of human intellectual achievement as the yardstick — international and national math and physics olympiads, plus the hardest tier of the Chinese College Entrance Exam, the Gaokao. They also cite AlphaGeometry from DeepMind as part of the motivation, a system that had just come close to matching International Math Olympiad gold medalists on geometry proofs specifically. If a narrow system can get that close on one subfield, a general benchmark needs enough headroom to actually track the race as it happens.
[S] A high bar to set. Walk us through how they actually built it.
[G] The final dataset is eight thousand four hundred seventy-six problems, split six thousand one hundred forty-two math to two thousand three hundred thirty-four physics, and two thousand one hundred twenty-five English to six thousand three hundred fifty-one Chinese. Fifty-seven percent, just under four thousand nine hundred problems, include at least one image that's load-bearing for the solution, not decorative. And every single problem, one hundred percent of them, ships with an expert step-by-step solution.
[O] That full-coverage solution set stands out. Most benchmarks give you a final answer and stop there.
[G] It does double duty. It made annotation and grading easier, since annotators could check outputs against a reference path, and it makes the dataset useful on its own for studying how a model reasons, not just whether it lands on the right number. Average question length is two hundred fifty-three tokens, average solution length three hundred forty-seven, with a real long tail — some questions run past three thousand seven hundred tokens.
[S] Where does the raw material come from? "Olympiad problems" isn't a self-verifying claim.
[G] Three sources: global math and physics olympiad problems, regional and national Chinese competitions, and Gaokao mock questions for math and physics. They pull PDFs from official competition websites, run them through Mathpix for OCR into markdown, and then manually verify every single conversion against the original PDF, because OCR on dense mathematical notation is never clean on the first pass.
[O] And after conversion?
[G] Deduplication using a small language model trained on mathematical-symbol corpora, comparing items by cosine similarity, followed by manual classification — every problem gets a subfield label, a question-type label, and for open-ended items, an answer-type label. Five answer types total: numeric, expression, equation, interval, and tuple.
[S] Why build that much taxonomy around answer types? That's an unusual amount of structure for a benchmark.
[G] Because it's the trick that makes automatic grading possible at this difficulty level at all. Free-response competition math doesn't reduce to string matching. So they built what amounts to a small computer-algebra grading pipeline. Numeric answers are checked against a tolerance, one times ten to the negative eighth by default, relaxed per problem for physics, where measured constants carry their own precision. Equations get normalized to one side and divided to test mathematical equivalence. Intervals and tuples are compared element by element. And free-form expressions go through SymPy, checking whether the symbolic difference between the model's answer and the gold answer simplifies to exactly zero.
[O] So it's not "does the string match," it's "is this actually the same mathematical object."
[G] Right, and that's a meaningfully higher bar than most auto-graded math benchmarks clear. One more structural detail worth knowing: physics olympiads often use what are called progressive problems, a shared setup with a chain of sub-questions where a later answer depends on an earlier one. The dataset preserves that structure in a dedicated context field, rather than flattening every sub-question into an independent item.
[S] And the twenty-one percent of the set that's theorem proving — how does grading work there?
[G] It doesn't, automatically. Six thousand seven hundred twenty-eight problems, seventy-nine percent, are open-ended with a definitive answer this pipeline can check. The remaining one thousand seven hundred forty-eight, twenty-one percent, are proofs, and there's no reliable automatic grader for a natural-language proof. The authors fall back to manual sampling there, which I'll come back to.
[O] And the actual prompting protocol — how were the models asked these questions?
[G] Deliberately minimal. One fixed zero-shot prompt template, in English or Chinese to match the problem's own language, used identically across every model, with no per-model prompt engineering at all. The template states the required answer type explicitly and instructs the model to end with a boxed final answer, so the automatic parser has something reliable to extract. And crucially, the dataset's own subfield and knowledge-point labels are withheld from the prompt — the goal is testing native ability, not primed retrieval.
