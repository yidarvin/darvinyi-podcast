[G] Glad to be here. This is one of those benchmarks that's less a leaderboard and more a mirror — it tells you exactly where today's agents are strong, and exactly where they quietly fall apart.
[S] Delyth, start with the gap. Why did OpenAI feel the need to build yet another benchmark in a year already crowded with them?
[G] Because the existing ones measured a narrow slice of coding. HumanEval, MBPP, APPS — write a function from a docstring. Even SWE-bench, a real step forward, is about patching a bug in an existing repository. None of that touches what a machine learning engineer does day to day: read an open-ended problem, wrangle messy real data, choose and train a model, debug it when it underperforms, manage compute and runtime, and iterate toward a competitive result, autonomously, over many hours.
[O] So it's not "can the model write code," it's "can the model run the whole R and D loop without a human in it."
[G] Exactly, and the authors build around two gaps prior benchmarks left unsolved. First, representativeness — tasks that look like real contemporary ML engineering, not toy problems with clean APIs. Second, a human anchor: not "the agent scored forty percent," but "it performed like a competent human practitioner, or didn't."
[S] And Kaggle solves both of those at once, because it already comes with real messy data and a leaderboard full of real humans.
[G] Right. Every Kaggle competition ships with a leaderboard of thousands of human attempts and a medal system already calibrating what "good" means. That's the anchor you can't easily build from scratch.
[O] There's a sharper reason to build this now: an agent that can do ML engineering end to end is, by definition, an agent that can improve ML systems, including the ones training it.
[S] Which is why MLE-bench gets name-checked as a candidate metric in OpenAI's Preparedness Framework, Anthropic's Responsible Scaling Policy, and Google DeepMind's Frontier Safety Framework.
[G] Right, and the authors are explicit: the point is to watch this capability climb before it becomes safety-relevant, not after.
[O] Okay, let's get into how it's actually built. Delyth, walk us through the curation.
[G] It starts from five thousand, six hundred seventy-three completed competitions in the Meta Kaggle dataset. They drop the loosely-vetted Community competitions, leaving five hundred eighty-six, and every one gets manually screened by at least two ML engineers at leading AI companies for relevance and whether the grading and a sensible train-test split can be reconstructed. What survives is seventy-five competitions, plus a separate seven-competition development split so agent-builders have something to tune on without touching the test set.
[S] They're graded by difficulty too, not just dumped in a pile.
[G] Right, each one is annotated by complexity from the point of view of an ML engineer today. Low, under two hours excluding training time — twenty-two competitions, thirty percent. Medium, two to ten hours, thirty-eight competitions, half the set. High, more than ten hours, fifteen competitions, twenty percent. They span natural language processing, computer vision, and signal processing, and the original prize pools total just under two million dollars, about twenty-six thousand dollars per competition on average.
[O] What does one "sample" actually hand the agent?
[G] Four things. A description scraped from the competition's Overview and Data tabs. The dataset — usually re-split, since Kaggle rarely releases the original test set, so the team carves out a fresh ten percent holdout and checks it scores similarly to the original. Grading code, re-implemented locally to match the competition's own metric, whether that's AUROC or something domain-specific. And a snapshot of the Private leaderboard, taken between May and August twenty twenty-four. The agent reads the description, works autonomously, and writes one file, a submission dot csv, graded locally and ranked against the real humans.
[S] Talk about the medal system — the headline number lives or dies on how that's defined.
[G] Kaggle awards bronze, silver, and gold based on leaderboard rank, and the thresholds scale with how many teams entered — for a competition with a thousand or more teams, bronze is the top ten percent, gold roughly the top zero point two percent. MLE-bench applies those same thresholds to agent submissions, as if the agent had competed at the time. The headline metric is simply the percentage of attempts earning any medal, bronze or better. It's deliberately brutal — only nine humans in all of Kaggle's history have earned medals across seventy-five different competitions.
[O] Which tells you the ceiling here isn't "get a passing grade," it's "compete with the best humans alive at this."
[S] Runs are stochastic — I assume they're not reporting just one number per model.
[G] Right, they also use the pass-at-k estimator from the Codex paper: the probability at least one of k sampled attempts earns a medal, an unbiased estimate from however many seeds they ran.
[O] What does the agent actually run inside?
[G] An Ubuntu Docker container with thirty-six virtual CPUs, four hundred forty gigabytes of RAM, and a single twenty-four gigabyte A10 GPU, up to twenty-four hours per competition, repeated across three seeds. There's a local validation server that checks whether a submission is well-formed, mirroring Kaggle's daily submission checks, but it never reveals a score — no peeking before the clock runs out.
[S] And they test more than one way to wire an agent into it.
[G] Three open-source scaffolds. AIDE, from Weco AI, purpose-built to tree-search candidate Kaggle solutions. MLAB, the ResearchAgent from MLAgentBench. And OpenHands' CodeActAgent. Two rules keep it honest: the submission has to be produced by a model, not hand-written from the agent's own memorized world knowledge, and the agent can't look up solutions online.
[O] How do they enforce "no cheating" on an agent running a full day unsupervised?
[G] Two tools. A rule-breaking detector that has GPT-4o read the agent's logs and flag manually writing the submission or calling an outside model. And a plagiarism detector, Dolos, comparing submitted code against the top fifty public notebooks and disqualifying anything more than sixty percent similar.
[S] Let's get to results, because I want the real numbers, not the marketing framing.
[G] The best configuration overall is o1-preview with AIDE: sixteen point nine percent any-medal rate, almost double the next-best model. For context, o1-preview averages seven gold medals across the seventy-five competitions, and Kaggle's own Grandmaster title only requires five golds. The authors flag their own caveats — not every competition grants medals, the datasets and grading are slightly modified, and today's agents have more recent technology than many original competitors did.
[O] Break down the scaffold comparison, because this is one of the more surprising findings.
[G] Hold the model fixed at GPT-4o and just swap the scaffold: AIDE gets eight point seven percent, OpenHands four point four percent, MLAB point eight percent. That's more than a ten-times swing from the same underlying model, purely from how the harness is built. AIDE wins because it keeps relentlessly prompting for improvements until the twenty-four-hour clock or its five-hundred-node search budget runs out. MLAB and OpenHands often just quit within minutes, despite being told to optimize for the full day.
[S] So half the leaderboard is actually a leaderboard of scaffolds, not models.
[G] Fair read of that slice. Holding AIDE fixed: o1-preview sixteen point nine percent, GPT-4o eight point seven percent, Claude three point five Sonnet seven point six percent, Llama three point one four hundred five billion three percent. Valid-submission rates track it — o1-preview eighty-two point eight percent, GPT-4o about fifty-five percent, Llama around twenty-seven percent.
[O] Which tells me a lot of the gap between models isn't reasoning quality, it's just failing to finish the job.
[G] The paper says exactly that — agents often fail to produce a valid submission at all, and transcripts show they frequently don't even use the validation server sitting right there. They also routinely mismanage compute, overloading disk or RAM until their own process gets killed, and rarely budget for how long their code will run.
[S] What happens with more than one attempt?
[G] Substantial gains. o1-preview goes from sixteen point nine percent at pass at one to thirty-four point one percent at pass at eight — roughly doubling by six attempts. Here's the striking bit: GPT-4o with six attempts, seventeen percent, actually matches o1-preview's single-shot score. A cheaper model rolling the dice more times can buy what a pricier model gets in one shot.
[O] That's a genuinely optimistic result — throughput and iteration substitute for raw capability.
[S] Sure, but that only works if you're willing to pay for eight full attempts. What about just throwing more hardware at it?
[G] Almost the opposite finding. CPU-only, the standard single A10, and two A10s land within a point of each other — nine point one, eight point seven, and ten point two percent — statistically indistinguishable. They checked the "extra GPU" logs by hand and found GPT-4o with AIDE never even tried to use the second card.
[O] So the bottleneck genuinely isn't compute, it's the agent's own strategy and follow-through.
[G] That's the paper's read, yes. Time is a bit more useful but with steep diminishing returns — stretching the limit from twenty-four hours to one hundred hours lifts GPT-4o from eight point seven percent to eleven point eight percent, and most of those medals are won in just the first couple of hours.
[S] Now the part I want to spend time on: contamination. This is built entirely from public Kaggle data.
[G] And the authors are upfront that's the central worry. First check, familiarity versus performance — they measure GPT-4o's mean token probability, essentially how "familiar" the base model seems with a competition's page and top discussion posts, and correlate that with score. Result: a Pearson correlation of negative zero point two four, p-value zero point zero four. Being more familiar with a competition does not predict doing better on it.
[S] And the obfuscation test?
[G] They manually rewrote all seventy-five descriptions to strip out any mention of Kaggle or the competition's name, then reran GPT-4o with AIDE. Original scored eight point five percent, obfuscated eight point four percent — essentially no difference. Combined with the plagiarism detector and log analyzer finding zero medal-winning cheats, their conclusion is no systematic inflation from memorization.

Neither can catch the subtler failure the authors themselves flag: a model absorbing a winning solution's high-level strategy from a discussion post without copying a single line of code.
[O] They do note GPT-4's base model could reproduce actual rows from the Titanic dataset when prompted, so they're not pretending contamination is impossible.

[G] There's a figure plotting medal rate against each competition's original end date. Agents win medals steadily through roughly twenty twenty-two, then the rate collapses toward zero on more recent ones. The paper reads that as newer competitions being harder. Plausible, but it's also exactly the pattern you'd expect if older, more-discussed competitions had simply leaked further into pretraining data. [S] One more thing — cost. A full run of the main experiment is eighteen hundred GPU-hours, twenty-four hours times seventy-five competitions, and o1-preview with AIDE used a hundred twenty-seven point five million input tokens and fifteen million output tokens for one seed across all seventy-five. Not a benchmark most labs can casually rerun.
[G] Accurate, and the same trade-off SWE-bench and similar agentic benchmarks made — realism and scale cost you reproducibility.
[O] My strongest point before we wrap: trajectory matters more than the snapshot. Multiple attempts nearly double the score, and the paper notes AIDE's own creators claimed it beats more than half of human Kaggle competitors, while state-of-the-art models here only clear the median about ten percent of the time on this harder set. That gap between the marketing claim and measured reality is exactly why this benchmark needs to exist.
[S] My landing point: read "sixteen point nine percent" as the single best twenty twenty-four model, in a scaffold hand-tuned for this exact task shape, on a dataset almost certainly in its training mixture. Real number, narrower claim than "agents can do machine learning engineering."
[O] Where does this connect back to evaluation practice?
[G] A few transferable lessons. Report the scaffold alongside the model, since we just watched it swing a score by more than ten times. Run more than one contamination probe and be honest about which failure modes they can't catch — this paper does that well. And when a benchmark's difficulty tracks time suspiciously, flag the confound rather than picking the flattering explanation.
[S] I'd add: a single thresholded pass-fail metric hides enormous variance. The per-category breakdown swings from around sixty percent on image regression down to zero on image segmentation. One headline number is convenient, but it throws away real signal.
