[S] It's on the docket because that forty-seven percent figure is still the most cited number in automation-exposure research more than a decade later, and a good chunk of the field's later work exists specifically to argue with it.
[O] Joining us to walk through it is Rebecca, a researcher who has studied this paper closely. Rebecca, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. This one's worth revisiting because almost nobody who cites the forty-seven percent actually knows how it was produced, and the how matters enormously here.
[S] So set the stage. What was the state of the art before these two took a swing at it?
[G] The dominant frame was Autor, Levy, and Murnane's task model from two thousand three: computers substitute for routine, rule-based work and complement non-routine cognitive work. Autor and Dorn used that lens in twenty thirteen to explain why middle-income jobs had been hollowing out for three decades. Frey and Osborne's complaint is that the frame was calibrated to the computer revolution of the nineteen-eighties through the two-thousands, and machine learning and mobile robotics were starting to touch tasks that used to count as non-routine, driving, sorting, even legal and clerical judgment.
[O] So the categories themselves were going stale, not just the underlying data.
[G] And there wasn't even a good quantitative precedent to update, because the closest existing exercise wasn't about automation, it was about offshoring. Blinder in two thousand nine hand-eyeballed O-Net job descriptions and produced a subjective offshorability index, concluding roughly twenty-two to twenty-nine percent of U.S. jobs were offshorable. Jensen and Kletzer took the opposite approach in two thousand five, building a purely objective ranking from standardized O-Net variables.
[S] Objective sounds like the better approach on its face.
[G] Except Blinder himself showed it produced nonsense results: that objective method ranked lawyers and judges as highly tradable, while calling data-entry keyers and telephone operators nearly impossible to move offshore. So going into this paper, the field had two flawed methods built for a different question, offshorability, and nothing built to measure automatability directly, occupation by occupation, against a specific list of engineering bottlenecks.
[O] Let's get into how they actually built the thing, Rebecca.
[G] They start qualitatively. Frey and Osborne ran a workshop with machine learning researchers at Oxford's Engineering Science Department and came out with three categories of engineering bottleneck, things the machine learning and robotics of twenty thirteen still couldn't do. Perception and manipulation: handling irregular objects in unstructured physical environments. Creative intelligence: originality that can't be specified as a clean objective function. And social intelligence: negotiation, persuasion, care work.
[S] Those are qualitative categories. How do you turn creative intelligence into a number you can regress on?
[G] They map each bottleneck onto measurable O-Net variables, nine in total. Perception and manipulation gets Finger Dexterity, Manual Dexterity, and Cramped Work Space. Creative intelligence gets Originality and Fine Arts. Social intelligence gets Social Perceptiveness, Negotiation, Persuasion, and Assisting or Caring for Others. Every detailed occupation in their dataset becomes a nine-dimensional feature vector built from those nine scores.
[O] And the dataset itself, how many occupations are we talking about?
[G] Seven hundred two detailed occupations, aggregated from nine hundred three raw O-Net titles up to the government's six-digit occupation classification. They drop roughly four point six three million jobs sitting in all-other catch-all titles with no O-Net record, about three percent of total employment, so not a big loss.
[S] And none of those seven hundred two have a ground-truth label yet.
[G] Correct, that's the crucial step. Frey and Osborne, with a group of machine learning researchers, hand-labelled seventy of the seven hundred two occupations, ten percent, with a binary tag: one if the job's tasks could be sufficiently specified, given big data, to be performed by state-of-the-art computer-controlled equipment, zero otherwise. They were selective about it, too, only labelling occupations they personally felt confident about.
[O] Which is already worth flagging as a modeling choice, before we even get to the statistics.
[S] Agreed, hold that thought, we'll come back to it.
[G] Those seventy labels train a Gaussian process classifier, a non-parametric model that places a probability distribution over functions mapping the nine O-Net variables to a continuous automatability score, then squashes that score through a logistic link to get a class probability, one over one plus e to the minus the latent score. They tested three covariance kernels, an exponentiated quadratic, a rational quadratic, and a plain linear kernel, which is mathematically just logistic regression, validating each with a hundred random fifty-fifty splits of the seventy labels.
[S] And the fancy kernel wins, presumably, or they wouldn't have used it.
[G] It does, but narrowly. Exponentiated quadratic scores an AUC of point eight nine four. Rational quadratic, point eight nine three, basically tied. Plain logistic regression, point eight two seven. So the Gaussian process buys a real but modest improvement over an unglamorous linear baseline.
[O] What does the model actually do with the other six hundred thirty-two occupations, the ones nobody labelled?
[G] Retrains on all seventy labels with the winning kernel, then computes the posterior mean probability for each unlabelled occupation using expectation propagation. That's the inference trick you need because the logistic likelihood isn't conjugate to a Gaussian prior, so there's no closed-form posterior. The output is a continuous probability of computerisation for every one of the seven hundred two occupations.
[O] Let's get to the number everyone quotes.
[G] They weight all seven hundred two probabilities by two thousand ten Bureau of Labor Statistics employment and threshold at point three and point seven. Forty-seven percent of total U.S. employment lands in the high-risk band, above point seven. Nineteen percent is medium risk, between point three and point seven. Thirty-three percent is low risk, below point three.
[S] Give me the extremes.
[G] Recreational Therapists come out least computerisable in the entire ranking, at point zero zero two eight. Telemarketers come out most computerisable, at point nine nine. And critically, neither occupation was in the seventy hand-labelled training examples, they're genuine held-out predictions, which is real evidence the model generalizes at least at the tails.
[O] And the shape of the high-risk group makes sense on its face, transportation, logistics, office and admin support, most of production. That reads like the obvious first wave.
[G] That's how the paper frames it, tying it to things happening in twenty thirteen, Google's driverless car program, warehouse robotics, big-data document processing. But the more interesting finding is that huge parts of services, sales, and construction land in high-risk too, cashiers, counter and rental clerks, telemarketers among them. Those jobs involve customer interaction, which you'd think would protect them, but the paper's argument is that kind of interaction doesn't actually demand much social intelligence by their definition.
[S] What's happening in the middle band, the nineteen percent?
[G] The paper calls it a technological plateau. Manual dexterity, finger dexterity, and cramped-work-space scores are all elevated there relative to the low-risk band, installation, maintenance, and repair work especially. Those jobs are gated on the perception-and-manipulation bottleneck, which the authors expect to erode only gradually, so they sit in limbo rather than either extreme.
[O] And then the wage and education check, that's the part I find most persuasive.
[G] Both average median wage and the share of workers with a bachelor's degree fall sharply and close to monotonically as computerisation probability rises. High-probability occupations cluster around thirty thousand dollars in median wage and under twenty percent bachelor's attainment. Low-probability occupations sit near sixty to sixty-five thousand dollars and over fifty-five percent. The authors read that as a truncation of labor-market polarization: instead of hollowing out the middle, the pattern of the prior three decades, they predict computerisation mainly eats low-skill, low-wage work going forward, while high-skill, high-wage jobs stay comparatively protected.
[S] There's a table too, breaking that down by the underlying O-Net variables directly.
[G] Comparing low-risk and high-risk occupations on the raw bottleneck scores: persuasion averages forty-eight in low-risk jobs versus thirty-two in high-risk. Social perceptiveness, fifty-one versus thirty-seven. Originality, fifty-one versus thirty-two. Fine arts, twelve versus about one. Those all move the direction you'd expect. Manual dexterity is the exception, it's actually higher in the high-risk group, twenty-two versus thirty-six, telling you those jobs demand some physical dexterity, just not enough on the dimensions that matter more.
[O] I want to make the strongest case for this paper, because it's stronger than its critics usually admit. This is twenty thirteen, and nobody had systematically operationalized what a computer still can't do into a testable, occupation-by-occupation model before. They picked three specific bottlenecks, tied them to measurable government data covering every job in the economy, validated the classifier honestly with a hundred held-out splits, then checked their predictions against two variables the model never saw during training, wage and education. Both landed where theory predicted. That's a real, falsifiable structure, not vibes.
[S] I'll grant the falsifiable structure. My problem is what it's built on. The entire model rests on seventy hand-picked labels from a workshop of machine learning researchers guessing about jobs most of them don't do. And these weren't random guesses across the distribution, the authors say they only labelled occupations they felt most confident about, which means the hardest, most ambiguous cases, exactly where a model most needs signal, are the ones missing from training.
[G] That selection detail is real, and it's in the paper. It doesn't invalidate the classifier's internal validation, an AUC of point eight nine four is a legitimate result on the labels they have, but it does mean seventy confident labels and seventy representative labels are different claims, and only the first one is true here.
[S] Here's my bigger objection, the one the field actually organized around. This is an occupation-level model, it scores a whole job as one automatable-or-not object. Three years later the OECD redid this with Arntz, Gregory, and Zierahn, using a large skills survey that captures the actual tasks workers do inside each job, rather than treating the job as one bundle. Their number was about nine percent of jobs at high risk across OECD countries. Not forty-seven. Nine, roughly a five-fold reduction from running essentially the same exercise at task granularity instead of occupation granularity.
[O] That's a big gap to explain away.
[G] The paper anticipates the shape of that critique, if not its size. Their own limitations section says they don't capture within-occupation variation from computerizing tasks that just free up time for humans to do other tasks in the same job. That's exactly the OECD's point. But Frey and Osborne never quantify how much of the forty-seven percent that caveat eats into. They flag the hole and don't measure it.
[S] Which is the difference between an honest limitation and a load-bearing one.
[O] Fair, though the OECD's nine percent has its own critics too, task-level surveys have their own measurement problems. Neither number is the truth so much as two granularities producing two different pictures.
[G] That's a reasonable read. Neither paper settles it definitively.

[S] One more structural gap. The paper spends real space discussing both prior offshoring methods, Blinder's index and the Jensen and Kletzer ranking, and says both have obvious drawbacks. It never actually runs its own ranking against either one on a shared set of occupations. We're told the new method is better. We're not shown it.
[G] Also correct, and also just not in the paper.
[O] Fine, so what would actually move the needle for you, Rebecca, on trusting a number like this more?
[G] Three things, going slightly beyond the paper. An external audit of the seventy training labels with multiple independent raters and a disclosed agreement rate, so we'd know how contested those calls really were. Published per-occupation credible intervals, since the Gaussian process already computes that variance and the paper never reports it. And a genuine retrospective: check how employment in the high, medium, and low risk categories actually moved over the following decade. The limitations section gestures at exactly that check. Nobody has run it at matching granularity.
[S] I'll take all three. My last flag on the numbers themselves: they never publish per-occupation uncertainty even though it's sitting right there in the model. A confidently estimated point eight three and a coin-flip point eight three look identical in every table and figure they show.
[G] Also accurate.
[O] Okay, where does that leave the headline number?
[G] My read, going a little past the paper itself: forty-seven percent is a real, internally consistent estimate of occupation-level automatability under a twenty-thirteen reading of machine learning and robotics, with genuine weaknesses in the training-label selection and the illustrative presentation. It's not a jobs-lost forecast, and the paper never claims it is. Its lasting importance is as the reference point everyone else pushes off of: the OECD cut it to nine percent by changing the unit of analysis, and a decade later Eloundou and colleagues found the wage and education gradient completely inverted for language-model exposure, rising with wages and education instead of falling, because the automating technology shifted from robotics to language itself.
[S] That inversion is the detail I keep coming back to. It means which jobs are at risk was never a fixed fact about the jobs. It's a fact about which technology you assume is doing the automating.
[O] Which is a genuinely useful, humbling result for anyone building an exposure or risk metric today. The metric is only as stable as your assumption about what the automating system can and can't do, and that assumption ages fast.
[G] That's the through-line to today's evaluation debates too. This paper's core failure mode, validating a scoring method against illustrative examples that were quietly part of the training set, is close to the same failure mode a contamination check is designed to catch in a modern language-model benchmark. The tools changed. The discipline required didn't.
[G] If you take one thing from the paper itself, it's this: forty-seven percent was a careful answer to a narrower question than most people who cite it realize.
[O] My one thing, even a flawed exposure model, checked honestly against wages and education, taught the field more than a decade of job-loss headlines did on its own.
[S] Mine, always ask what granularity a percentage was computed at before you repeat it. Occupation and task are not the same unit, and this paper is the clearest case study you'll find of that gap.
