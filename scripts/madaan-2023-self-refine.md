[G] The starting observation is mundane. People rarely produce their best draft on the first try, and there's no principled reason to expect a language model to be different. But every existing fix for that baked in extra machinery. Learned refiners like PEER or Self-Correct train a second model on input, output, and feedback examples specific to one domain. RLHF-style approaches, the kind behind InstructGPT, fold a reward signal into the weights through reinforcement learning, which means collecting a large human preference dataset and running an expensive training loop.
[O] So both existing routes cost you either a bespoke dataset or a full training run, and both are tied to one task.
[G] Right. And the question this paper asks is whether any of that machinery is actually necessary, or whether a single off-the-shelf model, prompted three different ways with nothing but a handful of examples, can generate something, critique it, and rewrite it, using only what it already knows. No new training data, no separate critic network, no gradient step, anywhere.
[S] That's a big claim to rest on prompting alone. What's actually different here from just telling a model to try again?
[G] That's the right question, and it's the paper's central bet: that the content of the self-critique is what does the work, not simply getting a second attempt. There's a direct experiment testing exactly that, and we'll get to it.
[O] Walk us through the mechanics before we get to results.
[G] There's one frozen model, and it plays three roles through three different prompts. First it generates an initial output from the input, using a generation prompt. Then it plays critic: same model, different prompt, looking at its own output and producing feedback. Then it plays editor: it takes the input, the output, and the feedback together, and produces a revised output.
[S] Same weights the entire way through. No second model anywhere in the loop.
[G] No second model anywhere. The loop then repeats, feedback then refine then feedback again, for up to four iterations. And critically, every refinement step conditions on the entire history of drafts and feedback so far, not just the most recent pair. The model effectively sees its own trajectory of attempts before deciding what to write next.
[O] What makes the feedback actually useful instead of just noise?
[G] The authors are specific about this. The feedback prompts are built to produce output that is actionable, meaning it names a concrete fix, and specific, meaning it points at a concrete phrase or line to change. Their own example is code that sums the first N integers with a for loop. Generic feedback would say improve the efficiency of the code. The feedback they actually elicit says this code is slow because it uses a for loop, which is brute force, a better approach is to use the closed form, N times N plus one, divided by two. That second kind of feedback is the entire point of the prompt design.
[S] And when does the loop decide to stop?
[G] Either a fixed iteration cap, or the model's own feedback signals that it's satisfied, which is worth sitting with for a second, because that stopping signal is also generated by the same prompted model. There's no external judge deciding when refinement is done.
