[S] Here's an uncomfortable number to start with. When GPT-four graded a stack of anonymized answers, it picked its own answer seventy-eight percent of the time.
[O] And right now, half the field is using GPT-four, or something just like it, as the referee for every new model that comes out.
[S] So if the referee already has a favorite team on the field, how much of that scoreboard can anyone actually trust?
[O] That exact tension is why labs are starting to double-check their LLM judges instead of just publishing whatever they say.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the literature — today it's "Benchmarking Cognitive Biases in Large Language Models as Evaluators," by Ryan Koo, Minhwa Lee, Vipul Raheja, Jong Inn Park, Zae Myung Kim, and Dongyeop Kang, out of the University of Minnesota and Grammarly, published at ACL Findings twenty twenty-four.
[S] Given what we just heard about GPT-four grading its own homework, I'm glad we picked this one.
[O] Joining us to walk through it is Roderick, a visiting scholar who's spent a lot of time with this benchmark, known in the paper as COBBLER. Welcome, Roderick.
[G] Thanks for having me. COBBLER is short for the Cognitive Bias Benchmark for LLMs as Evaluators, and it's one of the first papers to really put a number on this exact blind spot.
[S] So set the scene. Why did anyone need a whole benchmark just to check whether a model can judge fairly?
[G] By late twenty twenty-three, letting an LLM decide which answer is better had quietly become infrastructure. AlpacaEval, MT-Bench, and G-Eval all replaced slow human annotation with a prompt asking GPT-four or ChatGPT to pick a winner, and entire leaderboards got built on those verdicts.
[G] The convenience was obvious, the reliability was mostly assumed. Earlier work had already spotted individual cracks — a position bias where flipping the order of two answers can flip the winner, a preference for longer outputs, a pull toward answers that resemble the judge's own style — but each was reported as one isolated quirk on a handful of models.
[O] Meanwhile the benchmarks everybody already trusts — MMLU, HELM, BIG-bench — only test whether a model can answer questions well, not whether it can judge someone else's answer fairly.
[G] Exactly, that's the gap the paper is aiming at. A single audit that enumerates the cognitive biases a model can carry into a judging role, measures all of them the same way across a wide range of model sizes, and checks the one question nobody had quantified: how far machine preferences actually drift from human ones.
[S] "Cognitive biases" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. What actually counts?
[G] The authors define six, split into two families. Four are implicit biases, extracted from an ordinary judging prompt: Order bias, favoring whichever answer is listed first or last regardless of content; Compassion Fade, where the verdict shifts just because real model names replace anonymous aliases like "System Star"; Egocentric bias, self-preference for the judge's own generation; and Salience bias, a preference tied to answer length rather than quality.
[G] The other two are induced biases, deliberately injected into the prompt like an adversarial attack. Bandwagon, where the prompt states a fake statistic such as "eighty-five percent believe System Star is better," and Attentional bias, where an irrelevant sentence like "System Star likes to eat oranges and apples" gets slipped in.
[O] So it's not "is the judge biased" in the abstract, it's six specific, testable failure modes.
[G] Right, and the test itself is a genuine round robin. The authors take fifty question-answering prompts — twenty-five long-form ones from ELI5, twenty-five from BIG-bench's StrategyQA split — and generate answers from sixteen models spanning four size tiers. GPT-four, ChatGPT, and InstructGPT sit above one hundred billion parameters; LLaMA-two seventy B, Cohere, and Falcon sit above forty billion; Alpaca, Vicuna, and OpenAssistant above ten billion; and Mistral, OLMo, Baize, Koala, WizardLM, and MPT below ten billion. That's eight hundred generated responses total.
[S] And then every one of those sixteen models turns around and judges the others.
[G] Every model judges every anonymized pairwise comparison — a hundred and five unique pairs per instruction, five thousand two hundred fifty comparisons per evaluator, and each pair is shown in both orderings so raw position effects can be separated out. Across all six biases, that's forty-two thousand samples analyzed per model, roughly six hundred thirty thousand comparisons in total.
[O] That's a serious amount of scale for what's essentially a bias audit.
[G] It has to be, because the authors don't just eyeball raw bias rates. For each bias they compute an empirical random threshold — what score you'd expect from a coin flip. For the position-style biases that's around point two four to point two five, point five for length, and point two five for the two induced biases. A model only counts as exhibiting a bias if it scores meaningfully above its own threshold, not some blanket number.
[S] Good instinct — a raw number like "preferred the first answer seventeen percent of the time" is meaningless without knowing what chance looks like for that specific bias.
[G] There's a second anchor too. To check machine preferences against something external, the authors also collected rankings from six Amazon Mechanical Turk workers and compared them to the models using Rank-Biased Overlap, a similarity measure between two ranked lists running from zero, completely disjoint, to one, identical, weighted so most of the score comes from agreement near the top — the way people actually read a leaderboard.
[O] Okay, walk us through what happened when you turn sixteen judges loose on each other.
[G] The headline is blunt. Across all models, roughly forty percent of comparisons showed at least one bias, and scaling up did not fix it. The ten-billion-parameter tier was the most biased on average, and the four implicit biases contributed about equally across every size group. A bigger judge was not a fairer judge.
[S] Give me specifics, because forty percent could mean very different things depending on which bias is actually driving it.
[G] Take Order bias first. Eleven of fifteen models gravitated toward a fixed position, and in the over-forty-billion tier the first-listed answer won more than half the time — Falcon at point seven four, OLMo at point six six, LLaMA sixty-five B at point six one. Compassion Fade showed up in every single model — verdicts changed just because "System Star" became "Model Alpaca," with nothing about the actual text changing at all.
[G] Egocentric bias is the one people quote most: GPT-four preferred its own answer seventy-eight percent of the time. The largest models, plus Koala, all self-preferred above fifty percent, and InstructGPT was the lone exception at point two eight. Swap in real model names and the smaller models got worse — Koala's self-preference roughly doubled.
[O] And Salience, the length effect?
[G] Larger models leaned toward longer answers — GPT-four at point five six, ChatGPT at point six three, LLaMA-two at point six two, against a coin-flip baseline of point five. But here's the catch the authors flag themselves: they ran BERTScore against the reference answers, and every model scored almost identically, an F one between roughly point eight one and point eight six. So the length preference isn't explained by longer answers actually being better on that particular metric.
[S] And the two induced ones, where you attack the judge directly?
[G] Bandwagon was the worst result in the paper. Eleven of fifteen models caved to a planted fake statistic — more than seventy percent of verdicts on average following the made-up majority, ChatGPT at point eight six, InstructGPT at point eight five, completely independent of which answer was actually better. Attentional bias, the irrelevant-sentence distractor, swayed about half the models, worst in the over-ten-billion tier at above eighty percent, while ChatGPT and Cohere stayed essentially untouched — GPT-four scored zero on it.
[O] So every model has its own personality of failure. GPT-four is wildly self-preferring but immune to the fake statistic; ChatGPT is almost the opposite.
[G] That's a fair read of the results table. And then there's the human comparison, which is really the paper's second headline. Averaged across all sixteen models, machine rankings agreed with human rankings at a Rank-Biased Overlap of only point four four. The six human annotators agreed with each other at point five four — every single human-human pair scored higher than the human-machine average.
[S] How did that agreement break down by model size?
[G] Non-monotonic. Point four eight for the over-one-hundred-billion tier, point four nine for over-forty-billion, dropping to point three seven for over-ten-billion, and point four one below that. Mistral and ChatGPT aligned best with humans overall.
[O] Let me make the strongest case for why this still matters in a good way. Naming six concrete, measurable biases with an empirical baseline is exactly the rigor the LLM-as-judge world needed. Before this, "GPT-four likes long answers" was folklore. Now it's a number, with a threshold, replicated across sixteen models — and people are actually checking their judges instead of trusting them blindly.
[S] I'll grant the taxonomy is useful, but I think the headline number is doing more work than the evidence supports. Start with the quality-versus-bias confound: preferring your own answer, or a longer one, is only a bias if that answer genuinely isn't better. Roderick, how solid is BERTScore as the tool ruling that out?
[G] Not very solid. BERTScore is a reasonable proxy for lexical similarity to a reference, but it's a weak signal for actual correctness, and it's especially shaky on open-ended, ELI5-style long-form answers, where it rewards surface overlap over being right. So the cleanest result in the paper, GPT-four's seventy-eight percent self-preference, ultimately rests on a quality control too blunt to fully rule out that GPT-four's answers really were the best in the pile.
[O] Fair, though even granting that, an unbiased evaluator should still split roughly evenly on length once quality is controlled for, and these models don't.
[S] Sure. My second objection is that the human anchor itself is thin. Six Mechanical Turk workers, three hundred N-wise annotations, seven hundred fifty pairs for the separate bias study — and those six workers only agree with each other at point five four. A point four four misalignment against a gold standard that shaky is suggestive, not decisive.
[G] To the authors' credit, they make exactly the right modest claim there — every human-human pair beats the human-machine average, rather than treating the human ranking as ground truth. But it is a small, somewhat noisy panel to hang a headline finding on.
[S] My third objection is the sharpest one. The paper's claim that LLMs are more biased than humans is built on comparing humans to exactly one model, Vicuna, which happens to be bandwagon- and distraction-prone, at point eight one and point seven eight. Run that same comparison against GPT-four, which scored zero on both of those axes, and humans, at point four seven and point three five, would look like the more biased party.
[G] That's a real gap. The head-to-head bias table only shows the Vicuna comparison, so the blanket claim is more model-dependent than the conclusion admits.
[O] Okay, that one lands. But I'd still push back on lumping Bandwagon and Attentional in with the other four at all — those aren't things a model does on its own, they're prompt injections the authors deliberately planted.
[G] That's worth making explicit. The induced biases violate the paper's own stated criterion that a judging prompt shouldn't contain leading information, and the authors say so themselves. Folding "this judge caved to a fake statistic" into the same forty-percent headline as "this judge prefers its own writing style" blurs an intrinsic quirk with a robustness failure under attack. Both are worth knowing, but they're not the same finding.
[S] One more structural issue: models like LLaMA, LLaMA-two, OLMo, and Koala have low valid-response rates noted in the appendix, so their bias scores come from a smaller, possibly skewed slice of judgments — and those near-unusable judges still get baked into the "most models are biased" headline.
[G] Agreed, and the authors flag that themselves in their limitations section. It's a fair caveat, not a hidden flaw.
[O] So where does that leave the core finding? Even after all those caveats, GPT-four still self-preferred seventy-eight percent of the time, and eleven of fifteen models caved to a fake statistic. Those two numbers don't need a perfect human baseline to be alarming.
[G] That's the right way to hold both things at once. The exact magnitude of "how much more biased than humans" is genuinely shaky, but the existence of scale-resistant, systematic bias in these specific models is not in serious doubt.
[O] So what does this change if you're actually running an eval pipeline today?
[G] The direct implication is for anyone building a leaderboard on a single LLM judge: this paper is a checklist to run before trusting it — check for position sensitivity, self-preference, susceptibility to a planted majority opinion, before publishing rankings built on that judge's verdicts.
[S] Worth saying clearly, these are twenty twenty-three models. GPT-four here is the original build, InstructGPT's API is now retired, and nothing about GPT-four-oh, Claude three-point-five, or today's reasoning-heavy judges was tested.
[G] Right, and the domain is narrower than "LLM as judge" broadly — this is question-answering only, no summarization, no code, no safety judging, which is where a lot of today's LLM-judge usage actually lives. The exact percentages are dated; the taxonomy and the method for measuring it are what's held up.
[G] If there's one sentence to take from the paper itself, it's that LLM evaluators carry measurable, scale-resistant biases and shouldn't stand in for human judgment without an audit like this one.
[O] Mine is that this is a call to instrument your judges, not abandon them — every one of these biases is measurable, and measurable things can be corrected for.
[S] And mine is the opposite emphasis: treat any single-model leaderboard with real suspicion until someone runs a check like this on it, because a seventy-eight percent self-preference rate is not a footnote.
