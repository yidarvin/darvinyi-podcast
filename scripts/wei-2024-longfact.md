[S] And I'm your skeptic. Today's paper is "Long-form factuality in large language models," by Jerry Wei, Chengrun Yang, Xinying Song, Yifeng Lu, and colleagues at Google DeepMind, posted in March of twenty twenty-four and later published at NeurIPS.
[O] It's on the docket because it solves two problems at once: how do you even ask a model a question that forces a long, detailed answer, and once you have that answer, how do you grade it fact by fact without paying a human four dollars a pop. Joining us to dig into it is Cordelia, a researcher who's spent real time with this paper. Welcome, Cordelia.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is a fun one, because it's really three separate contributions bolted together, and each one is doing genuine work.
[S] Let's set up the gap first. Before this paper, what did factuality benchmarking actually look like?
[G] Mostly short-answer. TruthfulQA, HaluEval, FreshQA, HalluQA — you ask a model something with one definite answer, like how old the world's oldest verified living person is, and you grade a single sentence. FELM mixes short and long responses. The one real exception is FActScore, from Min and colleagues, which does grade long-form output, but only in one domain: Wikipedia-style biographies.
[O] So nobody had a benchmark that was both long-form and broad in topic.
[G] Right, and that gap matters because a typical open-ended question you'd actually ask a model — explain a scientific concept, describe a historical event — produces a paragraph with a dozen or more separate claims tangled together. None of the existing short-answer sets test that shape of response at all.
[S] And grading is the harder half of that gap, isn't it? A short answer has one correct target you can check against.
[G] Exactly, a long-form response doesn't. The paper's own phrasing is that it's difficult to compile predetermined answers that comprehensively and non-redundantly cover all the facts a response might contain. Standard automated metrics like BLEURT and ROUGE need a fixed reference to compare against, so they don't transfer to open-ended text. That leaves human annotation as the fallback.
[O] Which is slow and expensive.
[G] And, as this paper sets out to show, not even particularly accurate. That's the real twist — it isn't just proposing a cheaper substitute for humans, it's arguing the substitute is actually better.
[S] Okay, walk us through what they actually built. Start with the prompts.
[G] That's LongFact. They used GPT-4 to generate fact-seeking questions about a specific concept or object within each of thirty-eight manually chosen topics, split across four supercategories — twelve in STEM, eleven in humanities, eleven in social sciences, and four more in an "other" bucket. For each topic they generated thirty unique prompts, across two separate tasks called LongFact-Concepts and LongFact-Objects, giving eleven hundred forty prompts per task, twenty-two hundred eighty prompts total. Each one is written to pull out a multi-paragraph answer, not a single sentence.
[O] So step one solves the "how do you even ask the question" problem.
[G] Right. Step two is the harder one — SAFE, the Search-Augmented Factuality Evaluator. Given a long response, a language model first splits every sentence into individual, self-contained facts. Self-contained means it resolves pronouns and vague references — "it opened in the twentieth century" becomes "The Eiffel Tower opened in the twentieth century."
[S] Then what, it just Googles each one?
[G] Essentially, yes, but with reasoning in the loop. For every fact that passes a relevance check, the model acts as a search agent — it issues a Google Search query through the Serper API, reads back three results, and either issues another query or reasons its way to a verdict: supported, or not supported. It's allowed up to five search steps per fact before it has to commit.
[O] That's the part I find genuinely clever — using the model as an agent that can iterate, rather than a one-shot classifier.
[G] It matters in practice. There's an example in the paper involving the actress Elsa Pataky — the fact under test is "her contributions have been significant." A human annotator, working only from her Wikipedia page, marked it not-supported. SAFE ran three separate searches, on her film career, her industry impact, and her brand partnerships, and reasoned its way to supported. That's a claim that genuinely needs multi-step evidence gathering, not a single lookup.
[S] And the third piece is the scoring metric.
[G] Right, F1 at K. Prior work like FActScore only measured precision, the fraction of stated facts that check out. But precision alone rewards saying almost nothing — a one-sentence, fully-supported answer scores perfectly. So the authors add a recall term. You pick a target number of supported facts, K, standing in for how much detail you actually want. Recall climbs with the number of supported facts up to K, then flattens, so piling on more true statements past that point doesn't help the score any further. Precision and recall then combine the normal way, as a harmonic mean.
[O] What K did they actually use?
[G] Two values in the main benchmark: sixty-four, the median number of relevant facts across all the responses they collected, and one hundred seventy-eight, the maximum. So one score rewards a typically detailed response, the other rewards being maximally thorough.
[S] Worth flagging now, before we get to results — that fact-splitting step is itself a judgment call by a language model, not a mechanical process.
[G] Right, and the authors say so directly in their limitations section. A weaker model doing that decomposition might lump two separate claims into one fact, or split one claim into two, which skews the raw counts before any search even happens. It's a real source of noise sitting underneath every number we're about to discuss.
[S] Let's get to the headline claim, then — SAFE beating humans. What's the actual setup?
[G] They took a validation set from Min and colleagues — four hundred ninety-six prompt-response pairs, manually split into sixteen thousand eleven individual facts, each hand-labeled supported, irrelevant, or not-supported. They ran SAFE over the same facts and compared verdicts directly.
[O] And?
[G] Seventy-two percent straight agreement between SAFE and the human labels. Then, for the twenty-eight percent where they disagreed, the authors pulled a random sample of one hundred of those facts and re-adjudicated them by hand, this time with full internet access rather than just Wikipedia, and used that as ground truth.
[S] So a third, independent-ish pass.
[G] Against that pass, SAFE was correct seventy-six percent of the time, the original human annotation only nineteen percent, with the remaining five percent judged as neither. Roughly a four-to-one win for the automated grader on the cases where they actually disagreed.
[O] And the cost angle is almost as important as the accuracy angle.
[G] It is. Grading all four hundred ninety-six responses with SAFE cost about ninety-six dollars total in language-model and search calls, or nineteen cents per response. Min and colleagues reported four dollars per response for the crowdsourced annotation. So SAFE isn't just more accurate on this test, it's more than twenty times cheaper.
[S] Okay, that's the validation study. What about the actual benchmark — which models come out more factual?
[G] With SAFE validated, they ran it on thirteen models across four families — Gemini, GPT, Claude, and PaLM-2 — on the same two hundred fifty random prompts from LongFact-Objects, decoding up to one thousand twenty-four tokens at a temperature of zero. The trend is monotonic with capability. GPT-4 Turbo tops both K settings, at ninety-five percent F1 at sixty-four and sixty-six point four percent F1 at one hundred seventy-eight. Gemini Ultra is close behind. The un-tuned PaLM 2 trails badly, at thirty-one point one and thirteen point two percent.
[O] Bigger really does mean more factual, at least within a family.
[G] Broadly, yes. GPT-4 Turbo beats GPT-4 beats GPT-3.5 Turbo, Gemini Ultra beats Gemini Pro, and the RLHF-tuned PaLM 2 beats the un-tuned version. One wrinkle worth flagging: Claude 3 Sonnet, the smaller model, scores almost identically to Claude 3 Opus, the bigger one, and the authors don't have an explanation, since they don't have access to the models' internals. And Claude 3 Haiku actually scores lower than the much older Claude Instant, which the authors attribute to Haiku favoring precision over recall — it says less, but what it says checks out more often.
[O] Okay, time for the actual argument. My case first: this is a genuinely well-earned result. Most LLM-as-judge papers publish an agreement percentage and call it a day. This one goes a step further, disagrees with humans, and then actually re-adjudicates who was right, with a real ground-truth pass. That instinct is rare, and it's the correct one.
[S] I'll grant the instinct is good. My problem is who's doing the checking at every single stage. GPT-4 writes the LongFact prompts. GPT-3.5 Turbo runs SAFE and grades every response, including responses from GPT-4 and GPT-3.5 themselves. And when SAFE and the crowdworkers disagreed, it's the paper's own researchers who re-adjudicated those cases and declared SAFE the winner. There's no independent third party anywhere in that chain.
[G] That's a fair read, and it's worth being precise about what is and isn't tested. The seventy-two, seventy-six, and twenty-times numbers are all validated on that borrowed four-hundred-ninety-six-pair set. The actual headline result, the thirteen-model, twenty-two-hundred-eighty-prompt leaderboard, has no parallel human-graded run on those same two hundred fifty prompts to cross-check against. Trust in that leaderboard is inherited from the validation study, not independently confirmed on it.
[O] But that's true of essentially every automated benchmark that's ever shipped — you validate a method once, then you trust it going forward. Re-running human annotation on every subsequent evaluation would defeat the entire point of building the cheaper method.
[S] Sure, but then the size of the validation gap matters more, not less. And there's a real gap the paper reports on itself. [S] There's a second thin spot in the same neighborhood: they swap in an open-source model, Mixtral eight-by-seven-B, in place of GPT-3.5 Turbo inside SAFE, on just twenty questions from the FActScore biography set.
[G] Right, six hundred fifty-eight atomic facts total, one hundred ninety-six of them rated differently by the two settings. [O] To be fair to the authors, a lot of this is self-flagged. Their limitations section is unusually candid — they name Google Search as ground truth as an inherent weakness, they explicitly say they're labeling facts as supported by search results rather than claiming global truth, and they call out F1-at-K's gameability by fact repetition as an open problem, with a de-duplication fix they didn't implement.
[S] I'll give them credit for the candor. But naming a limitation in a paragraph and it actually being tested are different things. Nobody checked whether GPT-family outputs get an easier ride from a GPT-family grader.
[G] Correct, that specific test doesn't appear anywhere in the paper. Nothing in the results forces you to conclude it's happening — Gemini Ultra's precision is close enough to GPT-4 Turbo's that a heavy thumb on the scale seems unlikely — but you're right that it's an absence, not a clean bill of health.
[S] There's a related, more mundane version of that worry too: contamination. LongFact's prompts were released publicly right alongside the paper. Any model trained afterward could plausibly have seen the exact prompt text during training, which would inflate its factuality score for reasons that have nothing to do with being more truthful.
[G] Also untested. It's a completely reasonable thing to worry about for any of the post-twenty-twenty-four models that get run against LongFact going forward, and the paper doesn't have anything to say about it, because at the time it was written, none of the benchmarked models could have trained on it yet.
[O] Where I still land is that "not perfectly proven" isn't the same as "not trustworthy." This is still the most rigorously self-checked LLM judge I've seen published, even with every gap Cordelia and I are flagging.
[S] Agreed, it clears the bar for the field. I just don't think the field's current bar is high enough for clearing it to mean "safe to build on without a second look."
[O] Let's zoom out. If this holds up, what changes?
[G] The template probably matters more than this specific paper. Decompose a response into atomic units, ground each one against an external, dynamic source instead of a frozen reference, and validate the resulting grader against a disagreement-adjudication study instead of a bare agreement number. That recipe is already showing up in other long-form evaluation work, not just factuality specifically.
[S] Which is exactly why the unresolved parts matter beyond this one paper. If everyone copies this recipe, the same blind spots — no independent adjudicator, no tested self-preference, thin professional-domain coverage — propagate into every benchmark built on top of it.
[O] Although that cuts the other way too — better to surface these failure modes now, on one well-documented method, than discover them years from now, buried inside ten different derivative benchmarks nobody's re-checking.
[G] The paper is explicit that it's measuring factuality against what Google Search returns, not some platonic notion of truth. That's a narrower and more honest claim than a lot of factuality work makes, and it's worth holding onto that distinction anytime SAFE-style grading shows up somewhere else.
[G] If there's one line to take from the paper itself, it's that a search-grounded language-model agent, checked carefully against a real disagreement study, graded long-form factuality more accurately and far more cheaply than a crowdsourced human pipeline, on the exact slice of data the authors tested it on.
