[S] Here's a number that should make every eval person raise an eyebrow: a fourteen-billion-parameter model just posted sixty-nine point three on HealthBench, ahead of GPT-5's sixty-seven point two.
[O] And not the polished Instruct release either, the raw base model, before any of this training, only scored twenty-two point eight. That's a forty-six and a half point jump from one recipe.
[S] Built entirely on rubrics the model learned to satisfy, graded by another model, on a benchmark that is itself graded by a model. I want to know whose ruler we're actually reading before I call that a win over GPT-5.
[O] Fair question. But before the suspicion, look at what unlocked the jump: a fully automated way to write rubrics good enough to train on. That's worth understanding first.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an AI optimist, an AI skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the map at litsearch dot darvinyi dot com.
[S] Today's paper is RubricHub: A Comprehensive and Highly Discriminative Rubric Dataset via Automated Coarse-to-Fine Generation, from Sunzhu Li, Jiale Zhao, and colleagues at Li Auto, yes, the Chinese electric-vehicle company, together with Zhejiang University, the Chinese University of Hong Kong Shenzhen, and Nanyang Technological University. Posted on arXiv in January of twenty twenty-six.
[O] It's on the docket because it sits right next to two papers we've already covered, OpenRubrics and Reinforcement Learning with Rubric Anchors. This one is chasing the same bottleneck at bigger scale: rubrics good enough, and plentiful enough, to actually train a general-purpose model.
[S] Joining us is Rowan, who's spent real time with this pipeline. Rowan, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. This paper takes the rubric idea and asks a genuinely engineering question: can you manufacture rubrics automatically, at the quality a physician or a professor would write by hand?
[S] Start with why rubrics exist at all. Why not just keep doing reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards, the recipe behind DeepSeek R1 and the o1-style reasoning models?
[G] Because that recipe needs a ground-truth checker. Math and code hand you one for free, the answer is right or it isn't. Most real queries don't work that way. Write a poem, explain a diagnosis, draft a legal brief, there's no answer key, so verifiable-reward R-L has nothing to grade against.
[O] And the rubric is the workaround, decompose "is this good" into a checklist of gradeable criteria, and let an L-L-M judge each one.
[G] Right, that turns a fuzzy preference into something that looks verifiable. But the paper argues the existing rubric supply has three failure modes. The good rubrics, HealthBench's physician-written criteria, PaperBench's expert checklists, cost real human labor and don't scale. The automatically generated ones that exist, like Rubrics as Rewards, stay confined to a couple of specialized domains. And the criteria that do get generated automatically tend to be coarse, "is it on topic, is it coherent", so a mediocre answer and a genuinely excellent one both score near the top.
[S] Which kills the training signal. If every rollout already passes the rubric, there's no gradient to climb.
[G] Exactly, and the paper has a clean illustration of that. Ask for a short poem about autumn, get back a competent answer. A coarse rubric, related to autumn, coherent structure, scores it a perfect one point oh. The model can't learn anything from a perfect score. Add one more discriminating criterion, something like "conveys emotion through imagery instead of just stating the feeling directly," and the same poem drops to point six. Same answer, but now there's something specific to optimize against.
[O] That's the whole thesis in one example. The bottleneck isn't having a rubric, it's having a rubric with teeth.
[S] So how do you manufacture rubrics with teeth, automatically, across every domain?
[G] A three-stage pipeline they call Coarse-to-Fine Rubric Generation, run over roughly a hundred and ten thousand open-ended queries pulled from existing datasets. Stage one is response-grounded and principle-guided generation. Generating a rubric purely from the question invites what they call rubric drift, criteria that are generic or just hallucinated. So the generator is conditioned on an actual reference response, to anchor the criteria in something concrete, and it's constrained by four fixed meta-principles: consistency and alignment, structure and scope, clarity and quality, reasoning and evaluability.
[O] So it's not brainstorming criteria in a vacuum, it has to justify each one against a real answer.
[G] Right. Stage two is multi-model aggregation. Any single model bakes in its own blind spots, so they generate parallel candidate rubrics from heterogeneous frontier models, GPT-5.1 and Gemini 3 Pro Preview, pool everything, and distill it down into a compact, deduplicated base rubric. That removes single-source bias and widens coverage.
[S] And stage three, difficulty evolution, sounds like the actual novelty here.
[G] It is, and I'd call it the load-bearing idea in the whole paper. A base rubric captures correctness, but top models max it out fast and lose their gradient. So they take a pair of consensus-high reference responses, answers multiple models agreed were strong, and mine the specific nuance that separates excellent from exceptional. That's where a generic check like "is the code correct" hardens into something like "does the code handle the edge case in linear time." Those additive criteria merge with the base rubric into the final rubric.
[O] What does that buy you in practice?
[G] Even a top-tier model, Qwen3 two-thirty-five B, averages only around point six on these evolved rubrics. That's the headroom the paper wants, a rubric that stays hard even for a strong model, instead of saturating.
[S] What's actually in the finished dataset?
[G] They call it RubricHub, about a hundred and ten thousand query-rubric pairs across five domains. Medical and Science each make up twenty-seven point one percent, Instruction Following twenty point nine percent, Writing fifteen point nine percent, Chat nine percent. Density varies a lot by domain, Writing rubrics average thirty-two criteria per query, Medical thirty-one, Science twenty-eight, Chat twenty-five, and Instruction Following just five, because those tend to be crisp, single constraints rather than sprawling qualitative checklists.
[O] And then the training recipe built on top of it.
[G] Two stages. RuFT, rubric-based rejection-sampling fine-tuning: sample several candidate answers per query, score each by the fraction of weighted criteria it satisfies, throw out anything below a threshold, and keep only the single best response as an S-F-T target. That gives a curated thirty-thousand-example cold start. Then RuRL, rubric-based R-L: a unified grader assigns each criterion a binary pass or fail, an L-L-M judge for the qualitative criteria, a rule-based check for the objective ones, and the reward is the weight-normalized sum of those pass-fail scores, optimized with D-A-P-O inside the verl framework, trained separately per domain.
[S] Let's get to the number that opened the show. Walk me through it precisely.
[G] They post-train Qwen3-14B starting from the base checkpoint, not the released Instruct model. Base alone scores twenty-two point eight on HealthBench. Add RuFT and it jumps to forty-four point four. Add RuRL on top and it's sixty-six point two. The full RuFT-then-RuRL pipeline reaches sixty-nine point three, past GPT-5's reported sixty-seven point two, and twenty-two point six points above Qwen3-14B's own official Non-thinking Instruct release, which scores forty-six point seven. That ordering, base, then RuFT, then RuRL, then RuFT into RuRL, holds strictly across every benchmark they test, at both four billion and fourteen billion parameters.
[O] What's the single most dramatic number in the whole results section?
[G] General chat, on Arena-Hard V-2. Base Qwen3-14B scores five point two. After the full pipeline, seventy-four point four. That beats GPT-4.1's forty-nine point one and DeepSeek V3.1's sixty-two point four. Across the rest of the nine-benchmark sweep, the trained fourteen-B model lands at ninety-two point six on IFEval, fifty-one point four on IFBench, seventy-nine point four on WritingBench, seventy on CreateWriting-V3, eighty-three point two on the medical benchmark LLMEval-Med, and eighty-six point two on ResearchQA.
[S] Before I get impressed, is any of that just "more R-L," rather than specifically better rubrics?
[G] That's the control they run, and it's the cleanest evidence in the paper. They take the public Rubrics-as-Rewards medical questions, keep everything else fixed, same questions, same training setup, and just regenerate the rubrics with their own pipeline. HealthBench goes from forty-seven point seven up to sixty-two point one, with no extra data and no extra R-L. Add the full RubricHub dataset and it's sixty-six point two. Run the complete pipeline and you get the sixty-nine point three. Same ladder on ResearchQA, seventy-six point seven up to eighty-six point two, and more modestly on the verifiable science benchmark, GPQA-Diamond, fifty-two point one up to fifty-eight point five.
[O] So rubric quality itself is doing real, isolable work, not just riding along with more compute.
[G] Fair read of that specific experiment. The ablations back it up. Starting from naive single-model rubric generation, HealthBench sits at sixty point nine. Adding the principle-guided, response-grounded constraint moves it to sixty-three point eight. Adding multi-model aggregation, sixty-five. Adding difficulty evolution, the full pipeline, sixty-six point two, and on LLMEval-Med specifically, difficulty evolution is the biggest single jump of the four, taking it from seventy-five point six to seventy-nine point five.
[S] One more knob before we argue: does more rejection sampling actually help?
[G] It does. Raising the number of candidate answers per query from one to twelve lifts the selected training-set rubric score from around sixty-three to about eighty, and HealthBench climbs from forty-three point six to forty-eight point eight. More candidates, filtered by the rubric, means a better cold start.
[S] And what happens if the rubric is allowed to penalize bad answers, not just reward good ones?
[G] It gets worse, which is genuinely interesting. Positive-only criteria beat positive-plus-penalty criteria, sixty-six point two versus sixty-three point two on HealthBench. The authors attribute that to the grader being unreliable specifically on negative criteria, worse at correctly flagging what's missing or wrong than at confirming what's present.
[O] Okay. Let me make the strongest optimist case, because I think it's genuinely strong. This paper takes a real, named bottleneck, good rubrics don't scale because they need expert humans, and replaces it with a fully automated pipeline that produces measurably better rubrics than the best public alternative, isolated cleanly in a controlled swap. Then it takes a base model with essentially no alignment and, using a recipe you could describe in two sentences, nearly triples its HealthBench score and takes Arena-Hard V-2 from five to seventy-four. That's real capability sitting latent in a base model, unlocked by a better reward signal, and I think it generalizes well beyond medicine.
[S] I'll take the Arena-Hard jump at face value, that's a big, clean number. But look at what's holding up the headline claim, "beats GPT-5." The rubrics are written by GPT-5.1 and Gemini 3 Pro. The reward during training is assigned by a judge model called G-P-T-oss. And HealthBench, the benchmark carrying the whole story, is itself scored by an L-L-M reading a rubric. Every step of that chain is a model judging a model. Rowan, does the paper give us any reason to believe sixty-nine point three means better medical answers, and not just "better at satisfying this particular grader's rubric"?
[G] Fair pressure point, and the paper's own evidence only partially answers it. What they do validate carefully is grader reliability, comparing L-L-M judges against nine hundred forty human-labeled criteria. Cohen's kappa climbs as high as point eight oh and F-one as high as point nine one, with reliability saturating around thirty billion parameters. That tells you the grader agrees with humans about whether a single criterion is met.
[S] Which isn't the same claim as "the trained model's final answers are better," by human judgment, than GPT-5's.
[G] Correct, that's the gap. There's no blinded human head-to-head between the trained fourteen-B model and GPT-5 anywhere in the paper. And it's worth saying plainly, the follow-up paper that cites this one, on reward hacking in rubric-based R-L, specifically shows that this style of training buys completeness while quietly shedding conciseness, relevance, and factual correctness, because rubric weight skews so heavily toward whether something is present rather than whether it's right.
[O] That's a real concession, but I'd push back on treating it as fatal. The positive-only finding actually cuts a little in the paper's favor, they diagnosed that the grader is unreliable on penalties and designed around it, instead of pretending penalties worked.
[S] Or, less charitably, they tuned the reward toward exactly the axis the grader can reliably reward, presence and completeness, which is the same axis that inflates a rubric score without necessarily inflating quality. Rowan, how apples-to-apples is the GPT-5 comparison itself?
[G] Not fully. Several of the proprietary numbers in the comparison table, including GPT-5's sixty-seven point two on HealthBench, are marked with a dagger, meaning they're pulled from official blog posts or leaderboards rather than re-run under the paper's own harness. And the win is domain-shaped. On HealthBench, Arena-Hard V-2, WritingBench, all open-ended, all L-L-M-graded, the gains are enormous. On the one genuinely verifiable benchmark in the suite, GPQA-Diamond, the trained model reaches fifty-eight point five, a real gain over the twenty-two point eight base, but far below Gemini 3 Pro's ninety point eight and GPT-5's eighty-five point seven.
[S] It doesn't even sweep the rubric-trained competition. Against Baichuan-M2, a same-generation model that's more than twice the size at thirty-two billion parameters, it wins four of five domains but loses on Writing.
[G] That's right, it edges out on WritingBench itself but falls behind on CreateWriting-V3. So the honest headline is narrower than "beats GPT-5."
[O] So what would you call it instead?
[G] It beats GPT-5's reported score on one specific, model-graded, open-ended benchmark. On the domain where you can check the answer without asking another model, the gap to the frontier is still wide. The paper's own limitations section says as much, it's explicit that RubricHub mostly covers non-verifiable domains and doesn't touch competitive math, coding, or long-horizon agentic tasks.
[S] I'll score that one to me, then, the strong claim doesn't fully survive contact with the paper's own caveats.
[O] I'll take a partial concession. But the underlying recipe, manufacture better rubrics, cold-start with rejection sampling, reinforce with the rubric as reward, is real and reusable, even if the "beats GPT-5" framing needs an asterisk.
[S] Where does this actually matter for how people build evals, beyond this one paper?
[G] Two places. First, it's a fairly direct argument that judge reliability and rubric design are the real bottleneck in open-ended R-L, more than model scale, since their own ablation shows most of the grader-agreement gains arrive by thirty billion parameters, with almost nothing left going to two-hundred-thirty-five billion. Second, it's a caution for anyone building a benchmark graded by the same family of model being optimized against it. The paper doesn't run a contamination check between its training query pools, WildChat, LMSYS, the medical and research sets, and its own eval benchmarks, and given how self-referential this loop already is, that's exactly the check I'd want before trusting the headline number long-term.
[O] Which is a fixable gap, not a fatal one, seeds, confidence intervals, a leakage check, and a real human comparison would go a long way.
[G] If you want the paper's own claim in one line: fine-grained, automatically evolved rubrics measurably beat coarse or hand-me-down ones, and that difference alone, no extra data, no extra R-L, moves HealthBench meaningfully on its own.
[O] My one line: this is the first rubric-generation paper we've covered that isolates rubric quality from everything else and shows it matters on its own terms, and that's a genuinely useful methodological contribution.
