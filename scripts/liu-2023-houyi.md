[S] Today's paper is "Prompt Injection Attack against L-L-M-integrated Applications," by Yi Liu and colleagues, a twelve-author team spanning universities in Australia, Singapore, China, and the U.S., posted to arXiv in twenty twenty-three.
[O] It's usually just called by its project name, HouYi, after a figure from Chinese mythology, an archer famous for hitting targets nobody else could reach. Joining us to walk through it is Naomi, who's studied this work closely. Welcome, Naomi.
[G] Thanks for having me. One thing upfront, since it shapes how we should talk about this all episode: this is a defensive security paper. Every vulnerability found here was privately disclosed to the vendor before any of it became public, and the team went out of their way to avoid real-world damage while testing.
[O] Good place to start, actually. Naomi, what problem were the authors solving? Prompt injection wasn't a new idea in twenty twenty-three.
[G] It wasn't new as a concept. People already had a small grab bag of tricks: appending a command to your input, throwing in escape characters to try to confuse the model about instruction versus data, or typing some version of "ignore the previous instructions" and hoping. All three had mostly been tried against toy demos or bare API calls to GPT-3.
[S] So what was actually missing?
[G] Evidence that any of it worked against the products people were shipping. Chatbots, writing assistants, code assistants, business-analysis tools, the wave of apps that appeared in the months after ChatGPT launched. The authors ran a pilot study first, specifically to test that gap.
[O] And the pilot study is where the paper gets interesting fast, because the existing playbook mostly failed.
[G] It did. Ten real commercial applications, two from each of five categories: chatbot, writing assistant, code assistant, business analysis, creative generation. They hit all ten with the existing attack repertoire, five attempts each. Result: two out of ten showed any success, and even one of those two was shaky, a code-generation tool happened to output an HTML snippet containing the words "hello world" as ordinary code, not an actual hijack.
[S] So eight out of ten resisted every trick already in the literature. That's a real finding on its own, it says the field's mental model of prompt injection was built on toy examples, not deployed systems.
[G] That's exactly the authors' point, and they dig into why. Three reasons, and they matter because they set up the rest of the paper. First, a lot of applications don't treat what you type as an instruction to obey, they treat it as data to analyze. Tell a decision-support tool to ignore its previous instructions, and it just analyzes that sentence as more input to reason about, the same way it would analyze anything else you typed. Second, plenty of apps impose format constraints on input and output that act as accidental sanitization, structure an attacker has to fight that a bare model call doesn't have. Third, multi-step pipelines with response-time limits mean even a successfully hijacked generation can time out before a user ever sees it.
[O] So the target moved. It's not "can you trick a raw language model," it's "can you trick a language model wrapped in a product, with formatting rules, timeouts, and a role it's been told to play."
[G] That's the reframe the whole paper is built on.
[S] So walk us through the fix. What did they actually build?
[G] The design insight is borrowed almost one-to-one from classical web security. Think about how S-Q-L injection works: a crafted string doesn't just append a malicious command, it first closes off the query it's being inserted into, so the database treats what follows as a fresh, standalone statement. HouYi applies that same logic to prompts.
[O] So instead of just tacking a command onto the end and hoping the model notices, you have to actually convince it the previous context is finished.
[G] Right. Every HouYi attempt is built from three components, and I'll describe these at the structural level rather than give you anything closer to a working example. The first is a framing component, text that mimics an on-topic, legitimate request, so the injected material doesn't look anomalous. The second is a separator component, whose whole job is forcing a context break, convincing the model that whatever came before is finished and a new instruction is starting. The paper tests three separator strategies: one based on syntax and formatting cues, one that exploits the natural break between languages, and one that's purely semantic, a sentence that logically closes off the preceding task. The third component is the actual malicious ask, what the paper calls the disruptor, shaped to match the application's expected output format and kept short, since longer generations are more likely to trip a timeout.
[S] That third piece is doing the actual harm. The other two are just packaging.
[G] That's a fair way to put it, and it maps directly onto why the pilot study's naive attempts failed. They mostly skipped straight to the disruptor with no framing and no real separator, and got treated as data, not instruction.
[O] And this isn't hand-crafted per application, right? There's automation underneath it.
[G] There's a three-phase loop. First, context inference: an auxiliary model studies the target application's documentation and sample interactions to infer what it's for and what format it expects. Second, generation: that inferred context drives the framing, separator, and disruptor pieces. Third, feedback: the assembled attempt is sent to the real application, and a separate model judges whether the response indicates success. If not, the framing and separator get regenerated with a different strategy and it tries again. The whole system runs to about two thousand, one hundred and fifty lines of Python.
[S] So it's not one exploit, it's an exploit-generating loop that adapts per target.
[G] Which is really the contribution the authors are proudest of, not any single trick, but a systematic, adaptive methodology, in the same spirit as an automated web-application security scanner.
[O] Let's get to the headline number. Thirty-six applications, eighty-six point one percent.
[G] The evaluation expanded past the original ten to thirty-six real commercial applications, drawn from the same public directory of trending L-L-M tools. Five exploit categories were tried against each one: prompt leaking, code generation, content manipulation, spam generation, and information gathering, five attempts per category. HouYi successfully compromised thirty-one of the thirty-six, at least once, across all those attempts.
[S] "At least once" is doing some work in that sentence. Hold that thought, I want to come back to it.
[G] Fair, we will. The five that resisted are informative on their own. Some ran narrow, domain-restricted models unsuited to general instructions. Some chained enough internal parsing and formatting steps that a text-based attempt got mangled along the way. One combined multiple modalities in a way that blunted a purely text-based approach.
[O] What about the separator strategies, which one actually did the work?
[G] There's an ablation isolating exactly that. Syntax-only, escape-style tricks alone, was the weakest, several applications already strip stray characters like that. Semantic-only, logically closing sentences, was the strongest single strategy. Language-switching wasn't the top performer, but it uniquely cracked an application none of the others could touch. Running all three together, what the full system does, beat every single-strategy version, evidence the strategies are complementary, not redundant.
[S] That's a legitimate ablation. I'll grant that up front.
[O] Now tell us about the vendors, because this is where it stops being abstract.
[G] Ten vendors confirmed the vulnerabilities the team found. By explicit permission, three are named in the paper: Notion, reported at over twenty million users; Writesonic, over two hundred thousand active users; and a smaller tool called Parea. The rest are anonymized in the paper's tables, because their vendors hadn't cleared public disclosure at the time of writing.
[O] Walk us through what actually happened with those three, at the level you're comfortable describing.
[G] Against Writesonic, a language-switching approach got the application to leak its own internal system prompt verbatim, the exact instructions the developers had written to shape the product's behavior. The authors used that leaked text to build a functioning clone and confirmed it behaved almost identically to the real thing. Writesonic's developers confirmed the leak. Against Parea, a semantic separator let an attacker get the application to execute arbitrary requests, unrelated code generation, at the developer's expense, since Parea is a free product that pays for the underlying model calls itself. The authors estimated that abuse pattern, if sustained, could cost Parea's developers roughly two hundred fifty-nine dollars a day, extrapolated from a measured token-processing rate at standard GPT-3.5 pricing.
[S] Two case studies, both real, both vendor-acknowledged. I want to be clear I'm not disputing those happened.
[G] They're the strongest evidence in the paper, and I'd stand behind them as genuine findings.
[O] One more number before we get to the debate, the defenses.
[G] The authors also tested six previously published countermeasures: instruction-based warnings, restructuring where user input sits relative to the instructions, wrapping input in random delimiters, sandwiching it between two copies of the instructions, tagging it with structured markup, and using a second model purely to screen for adversarial input. The claim is that HouYi got past all six, through manual inspection.
[S] "Through manual inspection" is exactly the phrase that's going to come up again in a minute.
[O] Okay, let's argue this properly. My case: this is one of the first papers to take prompt injection out of the demo and prove it at scale against products people actually use, with a systematic methodology instead of ad hoc guessing, and with real vendors, including one used by tens of millions of people, confirming the holes. The responsible-disclosure process here is a model for how this kind of research should be done.

My strongest honest claim: this is a real, vendor-confirmed demonstration that a systematic attack methodology works broadly against twenty twenty-three-era commercial L-L-M apps, but it's a snapshot, not a reproducible benchmark, and the eighty-six percent figure shouldn't be quoted as a stable industry base rate.
[O] And mine: the methodology and the named case studies are exactly the kind of concrete, disclosed evidence this field needed at the time, even if the topline percentage is softer than the abstract makes it sound.

[O] Let's zoom out. Why does a twenty twenty-three paper about breaking chatbots still matter for anyone building with L-L-Ms today?
[G] Because the root cause hasn't gone anywhere. The moment an application merges a user's text with its own instructions into one shared channel, and hands that combined text to a model that can't reliably tell which parts are commands and which parts are just words, you have this exposure. That's true whether the untrusted text comes from a chat box, a fetched web page, or an uploaded document. This paper happens to test the chat-box case specifically, but the underlying mechanism generalizes to anything an application feeds a model without a clean boundary.
[S] And that's exactly why the discussion section matters as much as the exploit numbers. What do the authors actually say helps?
[G] They walk through several published mitigation patterns: warning the model to be wary of embedded commands, restructuring where user input sits in the prompt, wrapping it in delimiters, sandwiching it, tagging it as data with structured markup, or using a second model purely to screen for adversarial content. Their finding, thinly demonstrated as it is, is that none of those fully closed the gap on its own. The deeper lesson isn't any one of those tricks, it's the principle underneath all of them: content the application didn't generate and can't vouch for has to be treated as data, never promoted to the same trust level as an instruction, no matter how it's phrased or what language it arrives in.
[O] Separation of data and instructions, basically the same lesson as classical injection defense, just ported over.
[G] Exactly, and provenance matters too, knowing where a piece of text came from, and treating anything from an untrusted source as suspect by default, rather than trying to pattern-match specific bad phrases after the fact.
[S] Which is also why the responsible-disclosure framing here matters beyond ethics. Ten vendors got a heads-up before any of this was public, which is the same posture you'd want for any live vulnerability in deployed software.
[G] That's the right way to read the paper's actual contribution, not "here's how to attack a chatbot," but "here's evidence this class of bug is common, systematic, and worth vendors treating with the same seriousness as any other injection vulnerability."
[O] My takeaway is that this is exactly the kind of disclosed, vendor-confirmed security research a maturing L-L-M industry needed, even with the softer edges around the headline number.
