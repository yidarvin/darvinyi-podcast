[O] So which is it? Can GPT-four do legal reasoning or can't it?
[S] Wrong question. This paper's whole argument is that "legal reasoning" was never one skill.
[O] Fair — but notice who's making that argument. Not just computer scientists. Forty co-authors from law and computer science wrote the test together.
[S] Which is either the most credible thing about this benchmark, or a very elaborate way of grading your own homework. We'll get into which.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take one paper apart. Today's paper is LegalBench: A Collaboratively Built Benchmark for Measuring Legal Reasoning in Large Language Models, from Neel Guha, Julian Nyarko, Daniel Ho, Christopher Ré, and colleagues — forty co-authors across twenty institutions, published at NeurIPS's Datasets and Benchmarks track in twenty twenty-three.
[S] It's on the docket because it's a benchmark paper where the benchmark itself is almost secondary — the real claim is about who gets to build one.
[O] And to keep us honest on the legal side, we've brought in Marguerite, who's studied this work closely.
[G] Glad to be here. Short version: forty co-authors, spanning law schools, legal practice, and computational-legal research, built this eval together, task by task, instead of a small AI team guessing at what "legal reasoning" means.
[S] Set the stage — why did law need its own benchmark?
[G] Two problems, both named directly in the paper. First, the legal-NLP benchmarks that existed were built for a different era of models — BERT-style systems trained by fine-tuning on large annotated corpora. Those measured whether you could train a specialized model on law, not whether a general model could reason about law from a handful of examples, which is exactly the ability generating the excitement. And because legal annotation is expensive, the field had ended up with only a handful of datasets — one-off megaprojects like CUAD, or whatever "natural" labels happened to already exist.
[O] And the second problem?
[G] A vocabulary gap. Tell a lawyer "GPT-four scores seventy-eight percent on legal tasks" and that's meaningless — seventy-eight percent of what? Spotting the issue? Recalling the rule? Applying it? Without a typology grounded in how lawyers actually describe their own work, a score can't translate into anything a practitioner can act on.
[S] And there's a stated stake behind this — it's not academic curiosity.
[G] Right. The paper opens by noting lawyers and administrators were already debating whether LLMs could draft briefs, review contracts, help close the U.S. access-to-justice gap. But the risk is asymmetric: a hallucinated rule or a botched clause does real harm, often to people least able to absorb it. Guessing wasn't an option.
[O] Which is why the bet here is unusual for a benchmark paper — don't scrape one together, convene one.
[G] Exactly. Put legal professionals in the lead, have them hand-craft tasks they find practically useful, and organize everything around a framework lawyers already use, so the score means the same thing to a machine-learning researcher and a litigator.
[S] How does a benchmark actually get built by committee? That sounds like scope creep waiting to happen.
[G] It's more disciplined than that. In August of twenty twenty-two, the team published an open call for tasks, circulated through mailing lists and legal computational conferences. Submissions were vetted for legal correctness before inclusion, and every contributor whose task made it in became a co-author on the paper.
[O] Where do the tasks themselves come from?
[G] Three sources. Some are existing legal datasets reformatted for few-shot prompting — the clearest example is CUAD, a contract-clause dataset originally built for span-extraction. LegalBench restructures it into dozens of yes-or-no clause-classification tasks instead. Second, datasets legal scholars had hand-coded for prior research but never released. Third, entirely new tasks built specifically for this benchmark by the lawyers on the author list. In total, a hundred sixty-two tasks from thirty-six distinct corpora.
[S] Why organize around few-shot at all, instead of keeping the original fine-tuning formats?
[G] Because it changes the economics. Fine-tuning needs a large labeled training set per task, and legal annotation is expensive, so historically you got very few tasks. Few-shot only needs a handful of demonstrations, which frees the effort that would've gone into training sets and lets you spend it on breadth instead.
[O] Now the organizing idea — IRAC. Most listeners will half-remember this from a law-school anecdote.
[G] Issue, rule, application, conclusion — what every first-year law student gets drilled on. Spot the issue, recall the governing rule, apply it to the facts, reach a conclusion. LegalBench turns each step into its own task category, then adds two more skills that don't fit neatly inside IRAC: interpreting legal text, and understanding legal argument — what role a sentence plays inside a judicial opinion.
[S] Six categories total, then. Does any single task test the whole chain, issue through conclusion?
[G] No — and that's a deliberate choice, not an oversight. Every task evaluates exactly one reasoning type in isolation. The authors are upfront that a real legal matter chains all four IRAC steps together, each building on the last, and this benchmark doesn't yet test that chain end to end.
[O] Give me the subtle one — rule-application versus rule-conclusion. Those sound nearly identical.
[G] They run on the same fact patterns, which is what makes it interesting. Conclusion only checks whether the predicted outcome is right. Application asks the model to explain how the rule maps onto the facts, graded on two axes: is it correct, and does it contain analysis. An explanation that restates the rule and the facts and lands on the right answer, but never articulates the connecting inference, counts as correct but contains no analysis — exactly the conclusory writing that fails a law-school exam.
[S] Who grades "contains analysis"? That's not something you regex your way to.
[G] It can't be automated, so a law-trained human graded it, against a released answer guide documenting the required inferences and common errors. Because that's expensive, they only ran it for the three commercial models. Everything else is scored automatically — exact match, F1 for multi-label tasks, and for most of the benchmark, balanced-accuracy.
[O] Why balanced-accuracy instead of plain accuracy?
[G] Because many of these classification tasks are lopsided — far more "no" answers than "yes." Plain accuracy lets a model win by always guessing the majority class. Balanced-accuracy averages recall across classes instead, so on a binary task, random guessing nets you around fifty percent no matter how skewed the underlying data is.
[S] And the models under test?
[G] Twenty language models across eleven families, at four size points. Three commercial: GPT-4, GPT-3.5 — the text-davinci-003 variant — and Claude-1.3, all queried between May and August of twenty twenty-three. The other seventeen are open-source, capped at thirteen billion parameters — Falcon, MPT, Vicuna, Flan-T5, Llama-2, WizardLM, and BLOOM among the families. Every task got a hand-written instruction and zero to eight in-context demonstrations, and the authors say plainly they didn't heavily optimize prompts — they wanted a deliberate lower bound, not the best score achievable with clever engineering.
[S] Numbers, then. Headline first.
[G] The headline is really the spread, not one number. GPT-4 across the five automatically graded categories: issue-spotting eighty-two point nine, rule-recall fifty-nine point two, rule-conclusion eighty-nine point nine, interpretation seventy-five point two, rhetorical-understanding seventy-nine point four. That's a thirty-point range on a single model.
[O] Best model, full stop, though?
[G] Best almost everywhere, but the size of its lead moves around a lot. Its biggest edge over the other commercial models is issue-spotting — more than twenty points ahead, while GPT-3.5 and Claude-1.3 sit within about two points of each other there.
[S] Where does the lead disappear?
[G] Rule-recall. Claude-1.3 is essentially tied with GPT-4 — fifty-seven point seven versus fifty-nine point two — and it beats GPT-4 outright on three individual tasks, including a rule question-answering task and one on New York judicial ethics. Rule-recall is the closest thing here to a hallucination probe, and it's notable that's exactly where the frontier models converge.
[O] What about the open models?
[G] Not entirely off the map. Flan-T5-XXL, an eleven-billion-parameter open model, beats both GPT-3.5 and Claude-1.3 on issue-spotting and rhetorical-understanding. And on one rhetorical task specifically, an open model called Incite-Base essentially ties GPT-4 — eighty point six versus eighty-one point eight.
[S] And the floor?
[G] Brutal. The worst model tested, a seven-billion-parameter Vicuna variant, scores three point nine on issue-spotting — worse than random guessing, mostly because it produces malformed output the automatic grader can't parse at all.
[O] Now the result I'd want a listener to actually remember.
[G] The manually graded rule-application numbers. GPT-4 scores eighty-two point two on correctness, seventy-nine point seven on analysis — a small gap. GPT-3.5 drops from fifty-eight point five correctness all the way to forty-four point two on analysis. Its explanations are far more often right-but-conclusory than actually wrong — it reaches the answer and doesn't show the work.
[S] Any read on what's actually driving GPT-4's edge, concretely?
[G] There's a worked example on a trademark-distinctiveness task called Abercrombie. GPT-4's gain over GPT-3.5 is concentrated in the ambiguous middle categories — "suggestive" marks jump from thirty-eight to seventy percent, "arbitrary" marks from forty-one to eighty-two — while both models already sit around seventy-two percent on the easier "descriptive" category and both ace the trivial ends. The gap is really about handling ambiguity, not a uniform lift.
[O] And the prompt-engineering findings have practical value beyond this one paper.
[G] Two small studies. Spelling out the legal rule inside the prompt helps on some tasks and does nothing on others, which suggests it depends on how often that rule shows up in pretraining text. And switching from legalese to plain English improved GPT-3.5 on four of five tested tasks, by as much as twenty-one points — but on one contract task, the technical phrasing won instead.
[O] Let me make the strongest case for what this proves. This is a serious, broad snapshot — twenty models, eleven families, six genuinely distinct reasoning types, graded with a metric that resists the majority-class trick — and, this is the part I keep coming back to, designed by the people who actually do this work for a living, not researchers guessing at what matters. That's a template, and I think it's the paper's real contribution.
[S] I don't dispute the template. I dispute reading any of these numbers as "can do law." Start with the metric. Balanced-accuracy on mostly binary tasks means chance is around fifty percent — so a lot of the mid-fifties scores in this paper are barely a coin flip dressed up as a result.
[G] That's fair, and it compounds with something the authors chose deliberately: LegalBench only includes tasks with objectively correct answers. The reasoning lawyers are actually paid for — the contested cases where reasonable minds differ — is explicitly out of scope. High scores here are necessary, but nowhere close to sufficient, for "this model can practice law."
[O] Okay, but that's an honest scoping choice, clearly flagged in the paper. What's the part that isn't flagged quite as clearly?
[S] Contamination. Marguerite, how direct are the authors about that, really?
[G] Quite direct, actually, more than most papers manage. They say plainly, quote, it is thus possible that some LegalBench tasks leaked into pretraining data. Many tasks are reformatted from public corpora — CUAD, terms-of-service datasets — that almost certainly sit inside web-scale pretraining. There's an appendix documenting each task's prior online availability.
[S] Documentation isn't decontamination, though. And the awkward part is that the strongest scores — commercial models, on interpretation and rhetorical tasks built from public legal text — are exactly where leakage would inflate results the most.
[O] Point taken. What about task balance? I noticed the six categories aren't evenly sized at all.
[G] Far from it. Of the hundred sixty-two tasks, a hundred nineteen are interpretation tasks — parsing and classifying legal text. Rule-recall, arguably the most diagnostic category for hallucination, has only five tasks total. And by legal domain, it's dominated by contracts and corporate law, which the authors attribute to those documents being commercially available and immediately useful, not to those being the most important legal skills to measure.
[S] So a model's overall vibe on this benchmark is disproportionately a model's skill at reading contracts, not a balanced picture of legal reasoning generally.
[G] That's a fair read of the composition, yes.
[O] Last thing before we move on — the few-shot sensitivity result. That one actually surprised me.
[G] It should. There's a figure comparing GPT-3.5 against an open model across five different randomly chosen sets of in-context demonstrations, same task, same model each time. On one task, called "overruling," the best prompt for the open model beats its own worst prompt by more than twenty points. That's noise from which examples happened to land in the prompt, larger than many of the gaps between models the paper reports elsewhere.
[S] Which should make anyone quoting a single LegalBench number nervous about how many decimal places of confidence they're implying.
[O] And also — U.S. law, English only. Though to be fair, the authors say this themselves, we're not the ones discovering it.
[G] They flag it plainly in their own limitations section — skewed toward American law, English-only, no multilingual or international tasks — and say they hope future contributions broaden it.
[O] Stepping back — what actually changes if you take all this seriously?
[G] Practically, the paper's own capability numbers are already dated — GPT-4, GPT-3.5, and Claude-1.3 have all been superseded since. What doesn't age as fast is the method: experts as co-authors, not annotators, organized around a typology the target profession actually recognizes. That's the piece later benchmarks borrowed — expert-authored, rubric-graded benchmarks in law and other high-stakes domains, like PRBench's legal split, HealthBench, and ResearchRubrics, all used a similar playbook afterward.
[S] I'll grant that much. If you're building an eval for any domain where "correct" requires professional judgment — medicine, accounting, engineering — hire the professionals to help write the test, don't reverse-engineer it from scraped data. That part traveled well.
[O] So the disagreement isn't really about the template. It's about whether anyone should still be citing the twenty twenty-three numbers as current.
[S] Right. Cite the method. Don't cite the scoreboard.
[G] If there's one line from the paper worth keeping, it's that legal competence isn't one number — it has to be assessed reasoning type by reasoning type, and this typology is the first shared vocabulary for doing that.
