[S] And here's the number that actually matters more, two years later: this paper introduced an algorithm that quietly became the engine under almost every reasoning model that came after it, and most people who use it couldn't write down what problem it originally solved.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio. Today we're covering DeepSeekMath, Shao and colleagues, out of DeepSeek-AI, posted to arXiv in February twenty twenty-four. Joining us is Marcus, who has spent a lot of time with this paper and the family of work it spawned.
[G] Glad to be here. Worth saying up front, I wasn't involved in this work. I'm going to describe what the authors did and what they found, not claim any of it as my own.
[S] Fair enough. So orient us, Marcus. Why does a math benchmark paper from early twenty twenty-four matter enough to still be talking about it now?
[G] Two reasons. The shallow one is the headline: a seven billion parameter open model hit fifty-one point seven percent on competition-level MATH, without tools or voting, the first open model to clear fifty percent. The deep one is buried in section four: the paper introduces Group Relative Policy Optimization, G-R-P-O, which throws away the value network that RL fine-tuning normally needs. That algorithm is what DeepSeek later used to train R1.
[O] So this is the paper before the paper everyone actually talks about.
[G] Essentially, yes. R1 gets the headlines. This is where the machinery was built and tested.
[S] Let's do the actual problem first, before we get to the clever part. What was broken in early twenty twenty-four that this paper is responding to?
[G] Two bottlenecks, and the authors frame it exactly this way. First, a data bottleneck. By that point, GPT-4 and Gemini-Ultra could do real competition mathematics, but they're closed. The open alternatives were trained on small, hand-curated math corpora — Minerva's curated web math, OpenWebMath at around thirteen point six billion tokens, Proof-Pile-2, which leaned heavily on arXiv. The unstated assumption was that the open web is too noisy to be a primary math source, so you curate carefully and accept a small corpus.
[O] And DeepSeekMath's bet is the opposite.
[G] Right. Common Crawl contains an enormous reservoir of high-quality math, if your filter is good enough to find it. That's bottleneck one. Bottleneck two is reinforcement learning. The standard recipe for RL fine-tuning is PPO, Proximal Policy Optimization, an actor-critic method that trains a second network, the value model, typically the same size as the policy, just to estimate a baseline for variance reduction.
[S] Which is expensive.
[G] Very — it roughly doubles the memory and compute of training. And the reward model in this setting usually only scores the very last token of a long solution, so the value network is learning a per-token estimate from a signal that's really only informative at the end. The authors' question is blunt: can you keep PPO's stable, clipped update rule and throw the critic away?
[O] Two separate bets, one paper. Let's take the data side first, because I think it's the more surprising engineering story even though it's not the one people remember.
[G] It's a genuinely clever pipeline. They start with OpenWebMath as a seed corpus, sample five hundred thousand pages from it as positive examples and five hundred thousand random Common Crawl pages as negatives, and train a fastText classifier — small, fast, cheap at web scale. They deduplicate Common Crawl down to about forty billion pages and rank all of it with the classifier, keeping the top-scoring pages.
[S] That's iteration one. What stops it there?
[G] Coverage — a lot of math pages get missed because the seed set isn't diverse enough. So they group the whole crawl into domains, pages sharing a base URL, and any domain where more than ten percent of pages were already flagged as math, like mathoverflow-dot-net, gets treated as suspect. Humans annotate which URL paths in that domain are actually math content, those feed back into the seed, and the classifier gets retrained. Repeat.
[O] How many times?
[G] Four iterations. Here's the detail that tells you it converged: by the fourth pass, ninety-eight percent of what they found had already turned up in the third. So they stopped. End state: thirty-five point five million web pages, one hundred twenty billion tokens — roughly seven times the math data behind Minerva, nine times OpenWebMath.
[S] Worth flagging before we move on, since it matters for how we read the results later: they also filtered contamination — any page with a ten-gram match, or a three-gram match for shorter text, against GSM8K, MATH, and a couple of Chinese benchmarks got pulled from the training corpus.
[G] Correct, section two. A real decontamination step, not an afterthought.
[O] Okay, so now you have a hundred twenty billion tokens of math. What do you train?
[G] Continual pre-training, starting from DeepSeek-Coder-Base seven B — a code model, not a general-purpose LLM. The authors flag that as a deliberate, better-performing choice. Five hundred billion tokens total, math corpus at fifty-six percent, the rest arXiv, GitHub code, and natural language.
[S] Starting from a code model is interesting. Does the paper actually test whether code training helps reasoning, or is that just the recipe they happened to land on?
[G] They test it directly: four hundred billion code tokens then one hundred fifty billion math tokens, versus the same recipe with general tokens instead of code first. Code wins, on both tool-free and tool-using math — a controlled, partial answer to a long-unverified hypothesis: yes, at least for math reasoning, code pre-training helps.
[O] And the number that made people sit up: this seven B base model gets sixty-four point two percent on GSM8K and thirty-six point two percent on MATH, competitive with Minerva at five hundred forty billion parameters.
[S] Seventy-seven times larger.
[O] Seventy-seven times larger, and a much smaller model gets there on data quality alone. That's the proof-of-concept for the whole "the web is the goldmine" bet.
[G] And it's not just a math story — math training also lifted MMLU and BBH scores relative to the code checkpoint it started from, which the authors read as math pre-training improving general reasoning, not just narrow competition-math skill.
[S] Before we move to the RL side, one ablation from that section that surprised me: arXiv papers didn't help.
[G] Right, and arXiv is a default ingredient in basically every prior math corpus — Proof-Pile-2 is arXiv-heavy, MathPile is over eighty-five percent arXiv. They ran arXiv-only training at two model scales and got no measurable improvement on any math benchmark, sometimes slight degradation. They hedge it — might not generalize to larger scale, or to tasks like formalizing theorems — but as a plain result, it cuts against a widely-held assumption.
[O] Let's get to the part I actually want to talk about, which is GRPO. Walk us through the mechanism, not just the pitch.
[G] Start from PPO's objective. It computes a per-token advantage using Generalized Advantage Estimation, which needs that learned value function — estimating, at every token, how good the trajectory looks from here. GRPO replaces that with something simpler: for a given question, sample a group of outputs, the paper uses groups of sixty-four, from the current policy. Score every output with the reward model, so you get one reward number per output. Then normalize — subtract the group's mean, divide by its standard deviation. That normalized number becomes the advantage for every token in that output.
[S] So the "critic" is just... the other samples in the batch.
[G] Exactly. No separate network, no trying to guess per-token value when your reward signal only fires at the last token. One more move worth naming: standard PPO folds the KL penalty against the reference model into the reward at each token. GRPO pulls that out and adds it directly to the loss instead, with an unbiased, always-positive KL estimator rather than the usual log-ratio.
[O] What does dropping the value model actually buy you, concretely?
[G] Roughly half the memory and compute of the RL stage, since you're not training a second network the size of the policy. And the paper reports it isn't just cheaper, it's also effective — DeepSeekMath-RL, trained with GRPO on top of the instruction-tuned model, goes from eighty-two point nine to eighty-eight point two on GSM8K, and forty-six point eight to fifty-one point seven on MATH.
[S] I want to push on the training data for that RL run, because it's narrower than I expected. What exactly did they train on?
[G] About one hundred forty-four thousand chain-of-thought questions, drawn only from GSM8K and MATH. That's it. No broader question set.
[S] And yet MGSM, the Chinese benchmark, and CMATH also went up. Out-of-domain gains from in-domain-only training.
[G] They did — MGSM-zh moved from seventy-three point two to seventy-nine point six, CMATH from eighty-four point six to eighty-eight point eight. The authors read that as evidence the RL is teaching something that transfers, not just memorizing GSM8K and MATH answer patterns.
[O] That's a genuinely striking result on its own. Better math, better out-of-domain math, cheaper to train. What's the catch?
[S] The catch is the one figure this paper is now most famous for, and I want Marcus to walk through it carefully because I think it's the most important five minutes of this whole conversation.
[G] Section five point two point two, "Why RL Works." The authors evaluate two different metrics on the Instruct model and the RL model: Pass at K, which asks "if I sample K answers, does the correct one appear anywhere among them," and Maj at K, which asks "if I sample K answers and take a majority vote, is the vote correct." Figure seven plots both, for K from one up to sixty-four, on GSM8K and MATH.
[O] And the two metrics tell different stories.
[G] Very different stories. Maj at K goes up substantially for the RL model, across the whole K range — that's consistent with the headline improvement. But Pass at K, at large K, converges. The RL model and the SFT model can solve essentially the same set of problems, once you're allowed enough samples to find the right one.
[S] Say what that means in plain terms.
[G] It means GRPO, at least in this setup, didn't expand what the model is capable of. It re-weighted the output distribution so the correct answer is more likely to land at the top of the sampling order. The authors' own words are that the improvement comes from, quote, boosting the correct response from Top-K rather than the enhancement of fundamental capabilities.
[O] So the fifty-one point seven percent isn't the model getting smarter, it's the model getting more confident about what it already knew.
[G] That's the reading the authors put forward. To be precise about scope, this is the finding in this specific RL run, this narrow training set, this sampling strategy. The authors themselves flag, in their future-work section, that their limited data source and naive sampling are a likely reason, and that better exploration might do more.
[S] Regardless of the caveat, that single figure kicked off a real debate a year later — a well-known twenty twenty-five paper, "Does R-L Really Incentivize Reasoning Beyond the Base Model," picks up exactly this thread and interrogates whether RL-with-verifiable-rewards teaches new capability or just sharpens sampling, across other models and setups.
[G] Correct, and that paper cites this Maj-at-K, Pass-at-K result directly as its starting question.
[O] I still think this undersells it, though. Even if it's "sharpening," sharpening that reliably converts a right-but-buried answer into a right-on-top one is enormously useful in practice. Most deployed systems don't get to sample sixty-four times and vote.
[S] Useful in practice, sure. What I'd push back on is any claim that GRPO taught the model to reason better — the paper's own data says it didn't expand the ceiling. If you're hoping RL makes your model solve problems it genuinely couldn't before, this figure should worry you.
[G] Both are defensible readings of the same chart, and the paper supports both. It's useful, it's cheap, and per this evidence, in this setup, it isn't raising the capability ceiling. All three are true at once.
[O] Let's talk baselines and contamination for a second, because I want to know how much confidence to put behind the corpus comparison specifically.
[G] The corpus ablation — DeepSeekMath Corpus versus MathPile, OpenWebMath, Proof-Pile-2 — is run cleanly in one sense: same one-point-three billion parameter model, same one hundred fifty billion token budget, for every corpus. But there's a confound: the full DeepSeekMath Corpus is one hundred twenty billion tokens against OpenWebMath's thirteen point six billion, so part of "ours wins" is quality and part is "ours is nine times bigger," and it's run at one point three billion parameters, not the seven B that ships.
[S] And the decontamination, ten-gram matching, is reasonable but string-level — paraphrased or reformatted problems could slip through, and there's no independent post-hoc contamination audit or freshly-collected held-out test set.
[G] Fair, and given the size of the GSM8K and MATH jumps, an independent check would have meaningfully raised confidence. Nobody found evidence of leakage — the paper just doesn't rule it out beyond the string match, and mining a hundred twenty billion tokens straight from the open web is exactly the regime where you'd want that.
[O] What about the limitations the authors admit themselves?
[G] Two, stated plainly in the conclusion. Geometry and theorem-proving are weak — their own words are that the model, quote, cannot handle problems related to triangles and ellipses. And unlike GPT-4, the model shows essentially no gain from few-shot prompting over zero-shot, which they attribute to model scale.
[S] So "approaches GPT-4 on MATH" is a true statement about one number on one benchmark family, from a model that can't reliably do a geometry problem.
[G] That's a fair summary.
[O] Zoom out for me. What's this paper's actual legacy, two years on?
[G] The model itself is mostly a footnote now — better open math models have shipped since. What persisted is GRPO, the RL algorithm underneath DeepSeek-R1 and close to a default choice across the reinforcement-learning-with-verifiable-rewards line of work; a lot of the rubric-based reward training that followed builds on the same group-normalized-baseline idea, with a different reward function than "did you get the math problem right."
[S] And the unified-paradigm framing in section five — SFT, RFT, DPO, PPO, GRPO as one template of data source, reward function, gradient coefficient — arguably did more downstream work than the model itself. It gave people a shared vocabulary for comparing post-training methods that hadn't been compared on the same axes before.
[O] So the paper that people cite for "fifty-one point seven percent on MATH" is actually the paper that changed how the field trains reasoning models afterward.
[G] That's my honest read, and I think the authors would recognize it too — GRPO gets a dedicated section, careful derivation, an algorithm box. It wasn't a footnote to them even in twenty twenty-four.
[S] Last thing before we close. What would you want the paper to have done that it didn't?
[G] Repeat the corpus ablation at the shipped seven B scale, token-matched rather than nine times larger. An independent contamination probe, or a fresh, post-2024 held-out benchmark. And report Pass at K alongside every RL headline number — that's the number that tells you whether you're looking at new capability or redistributed confidence.
[O] My one-sentence version: this paper proved the open web has more math in it than anyone had bothered to mine, and along the way it built the RL algorithm that a huge amount of the field is still running on.
[S] Mine: read Figure seven before you believe any RL headline number, including ones from papers that came after this one — Maj-at-K-not-Pass-at-K is a pattern worth checking for, not a one-off finding.
[G] And the paper's own line, which I think is the fairest summary: RL boosted the correct response from Top-K rather than expanding fundamental capability, on this run, with this data.
