[O] Here's a sentence that should worry anyone who grades models on the final answer alone: a language model can get a math problem completely right while reasoning about it completely wrong, and a metric that only checks the number will never see it happen.
[S] And here's the sentence that should make you less worried. DeepMind trained a reward model that only ever saw whether the final answer was correct, and it ended up agreeing with human judges about which individual reasoning steps were correct eighty-five percent of the time. More than it agreed with its own training labels.
[O] Which is either a happy accident or a reason to never trust outcome-only supervision again, and I genuinely don't think you'll know which by the end of this episode.
[S] I don't either, and that's the paper.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a guest scholar take apart one paper from the litsearch atlas. Today's paper is "Solving Math Word Problems with Process- and Outcome-based Feedback," from Jonathan Uesato, Nate Kushman, Ramana Kumar, and colleagues at DeepMind, posted to arXiv in November twenty twenty-two.
[S] It's a quiet paper by today's standards, but it's sitting at over eight hundred citations, and it's essentially the direct ancestor of the process reward model work that shows up in every serious reasoning model since.
[O] To help us get into it, we're joined by Rachel, who's studied this paper closely. Rachel, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. That framing is right. This is one of those papers that's more cited for the question it sharpens than for the specific numbers, though the numbers hold up too.
[O] Set up the gap for us. Why did anyone need to run this comparison?
[G] By late twenty twenty-two, chain-of-thought prompting had already shown that asking a model to write out its reasoning improves accuracy on multi-step problems. But prompting isn't training. Once you fine-tune or reinforcement-learn a model to reason, you have to pick what you're grading it on, and the two obvious choices are the final answer or every step along the way.
[S] Outcome-based versus process-based supervision.
[G] Right, and the worry is they're not interchangeable. A model chasing final-answer reward can find a shortcut that lands on the right number for the wrong reasons, and an outcome-only metric is structurally blind to that, by construction. The paper points at education specifically, where an answer without a comprehensible explanation may confuse more than it explains.
[O] Hadn't people shown this trade-off before, though?
[G] Only on algorithmic tasks, sorting lists, executing little programs, where a step's correctness can be checked automatically against a ground-truth trace. Nobody had run a controlled comparison on natural language, where a human has to judge correctness. That's why they land on G-S-M-eight-K, the grade-school math word problem dataset: integer answers you can check by exact match, human-written traces already in the data, and a scope small enough to afford hiring annotators to grade individual steps by hand.
[S] Walk me through how they actually built the comparison.
[G] Everything sits on one shared backbone, a seventy-billion-parameter, Chinchilla-optimal pretrained model from the Hoffmann and colleagues work. That matters, because it means the comparison is about supervision method, not model size. From that backbone there are three ways to get a policy: five-shot prompting with no fine-tuning, supervised fine-tuning on the dataset's own human traces, which is their process-based baseline, or reinforcement learning through expert iteration, alternately generating candidates, filtering them, and distilling the filtered set back into the policy.
[G] The filtering step is where process versus outcome actually gets varied, in three flavors. Final-Answer R-L, the Star recipe, samples ninety-six full solutions per problem and keeps only the ones whose final answer matches, using zero information about the steps. O-R-M-R-L trains an outcome-supervised reward model, a classifier predicting whether a full solution reaches the right answer, and keeps the single highest-scoring one. P-R-M-R-L scores every individual step with a reward model trained on human step-correctness labels, and greedily keeps the best next step, up to fifteen steps deep.
[S] And that step-level human data is the one genuinely new collection effort in the paper.
[G] It is. Annotators see a model-generated solution and mark the first step with what the instructions call a major mistake: incorrect information, or a step you couldn't undo and still reach the right answer. After filtering low-agreement raters and ambiguous problems, about twenty percent of the raw data, they end up with nine thousand, eight hundred fifty-six step-level labels across one thousand, five hundred sixty solutions.
[O] That's a real annotation effort.
[G] Expensive enough that they only ran it on one dataset, and say so directly: recruiting annotators with the domain expertise to judge reasoning traces accurately is a large up-front cost.
[S] And at test time?
[G] Two decoding strategies. With no reward model, majority voting, essentially self-consistency across the ninety-six samples. With a reward model, R-M-weighted decoding, or verifier voting: weight each candidate by the reward model's probability, aggregate by final answer, and return the best sample behind the winning answer. And throughout, two separate scorecards, never folded into one number. Final-answer error, exact-match on the integer. Trace error, the fraction of final-answer-correct solutions that still contain a wrong reasoning step, per human raters. Splitting those apart is the paper's central move, designed specifically to expose the gap between looks right and reasoned correctly.
[O] What about latency? Ninety-six samples plus a reward model sounds expensive to run.
[G] It is, and they actually report the greedy, single-sample case too, which is the cheap, fast setting. There, reinforcement learning against a reward model still helps a lot: the supervised fine-tuned model alone gets forty-one point one percent final-answer error greedy, and adding O-R-M reinforcement learning brings that down to thirty-one point two percent, with no extra sampling cost at inference. So the reward model's value isn't purely about reranking a big batch, it also improves the policy you'd actually deploy under a latency budget.
[S] Let's get to the numbers.
[G] Headline first: their best recipe, supervised fine-tuning plus O-R-M reinforcement learning, reranked with the O-R-M, improves final-answer error from sixteen point eight percent to twelve point seven percent, and trace error from fourteen percent to three point four percent. Let it abstain on the hardest thirty percent of questions and final-answer error drops to two point seven percent.
[O] Which of those numbers is doing the interesting work?
[G] Trace error, and here's why. Finding one: outcome and process supervision reach nearly the same final-answer error on their own. Supervised fine-tuning gets twenty-two point three percent; Final-Answer R-L, using maybe one to four tokens of label per question instead of a full trace, gets twenty-three point five percent. With reward-model reranking, fourteen point eight versus sixteen point six percent.
[S] So for final-answer accuracy alone, outcome supervision is nearly free.
[G] That's the paper's framing too. But finding three breaks that story: low trace error isn't a free byproduct. Despite similar final-answer accuracy, the outcome-based model's trace error is nineteen point eight percent against eleven point four percent for the process-based one, with no reward model involved. Getting the right number for the wrong reasons at almost double the rate. And that gap survives reranking: twelve point four percent versus four point four or three point five percent.
[O] Now the one everyone remembers this paper for.
[G] Finding two. The O-R-M is trained on nothing but final-answer correctness. But check its per-step predictions against the human process labels, and it agrees with them eighty-five percent of the time, versus seventy-seven percent agreement with its own outcome training labels. The authors' read is that it's simply easier for the model to learn "is this step locally sound" than to internally verify a multi-step arithmetic answer, so an outcome-only signal ends up teaching something closer to step-level judgment as a side effect.
[S] Which explains findings one and three both. If reward models secretly learn process, training against one should behave more like process supervision than raw outcome optimization.
[G] Exactly the chain the paper draws. Training against the O-R-M instead of raw final-answer correctness cuts trace error from twelve point four percent to five point five percent.
[O] The abstaining result felt like the most practically useful number here.
[G] It's striking. The fine-tuned model with P-R-M reranking sits at fourteen point one percent final-answer error. Abstain on the thirty percent hardest questions and error on the rest drops to two point seven percent; at fifty percent abstention, one point five percent. And it works better for models with lower baseline trace error, because the reward model's confidence is more meaningful when the steps behind it are more often correct.
[S] Any generalization check?
[G] A modest one, zero-shot on the pre-algebra split of the MATH dataset: their best model gets sixty-four point six percent final-answer error, worse than Minerva's twenty-nine percent, which is a much larger math-specialized model, but well below GPT-3's ninety-two point three percent. No supervision type shows a consistent advantage out of distribution.
[O] Let me stake out the optimist case. This paper turns process-versus-outcome supervision from a philosophical debate into a measured empirical question on a real natural-language task, not a toy sorting problem. And the core surprise, an outcome-only reward model quietly behaving like a process judge, is the empirical seed for the process reward model work that scaled up six months later and became the default verifier architecture for reasoning models.
[S] I'll grant the discovery is real. My problem is the "beats state of the art" framing. The sixteen point eight to twelve point seven and fourteen to three point four percent headline deltas are measured against Codex at one hundred seventy-five billion parameters and PaLM at five hundred forty billion. This paper's models all run on one shared seventy-billion-parameter backbone.
[G] Fair catch, and to their credit the authors flag it once: their Star reproduction improves from the original paper's reported eighty-nine percent error to twenty-three point five percent, and they say explicitly part of that comes from a better base model. But the caveat doesn't travel into the abstract's headline numbers, so a skimming reader could credit the whole gain to the supervision recipe.
[O] It doesn't touch the internal comparison, though, which is the part I actually care about. Every variant shares the same backbone.
[S] Agreed on that. My second concern is the annotation budget behind the trace-error numbers: nine thousand, eight hundred fifty-six step labels, and evaluation on only two hundred solutions per model across ten models, double-rated. They also tried a cheaper alternative, a heuristic step-level verifier from the Li and colleagues paper that checks intermediate calculations by string matching instead of paying for human labels, and it came in slightly worse, fifteen point nine percent final-answer error instead of fourteen point one. So the human annotation is buying something, but it's a small, expensive something.
[G] The paper is honest about the resulting uncertainty. The headline three point four percent trace error carries a reported range of zero to six point eight percent, depending on whether both raters or just one have to agree on an error, and there's no formal inter-rater reliability statistic beyond noting low-agreement annotators were filtered out. That's more false precision than the data really supports, even though nothing here is hidden.
[S] My last one is the authors' own concern, not mine. Section four point two says outright they expect process and outcome supervision to align more closely for math than other domains, because wrong arithmetic reasoning rarely lands on the correct number by accident. In domains with more spurious correctness, tool use, persuasion, long summaries, the central finding might just not hold, and they don't test anywhere it would get stress-tested.
[G] That's the honest limitation, in their own words, and I'd weight it more than the abstract's confident tone invites you to. One more the paper doesn't touch at all: contamination. G-S-M-eight-K predates this model's pretraining by about a year, and it's never discussed.
[O] Where does that leave us?
[G] Point to the optimist on the core discovery surviving scrutiny, it replicates and it's the direct ancestor of what came next. Point to the skeptic on the baseline framing and the generalization limits being real and under-emphasized in the abstract.
[O] Six months later, "Let's Verify Step by Step" scaled this exact idea up by two orders of magnitude, roughly eight hundred thousand step labels instead of under ten thousand, on harder problems, and process reward models became close to the default verifier behind reasoning models.
[G] That lineage is real. This is the smaller, earlier, more careful sibling of that later result, and most of its caveats, the small annotation budget, the narrow domain, are exactly what the follow-up work set out to fix.
[S] Which is the right way to read a precursor paper. It doesn't need to be the final word, it needs to ask the right question precisely enough that the next paper knows what to scale.
[O] The broader lesson generalizes past math too. Final-answer accuracy is a leaky proxy for correct reasoning anywhere a model can get lucky, agentic tool use, multi-hop retrieval, any place a broken intermediate step can still stumble into a plausible output.
[G] The paper makes that point directly: outcome-based supervision is appropriate when you have a reliable, complete way to evaluate the final result, and process-based supervision matters most everywhere else, especially where wrong process can hide behind a right-looking answer.
[S] There's a safety angle in there too, about process feedback avoiding what they call tampering incentives, where an outcome-optimizing agent might find it easier to influence the metric than the underlying task.
[G] Right, and it connects to some of the same authors' earlier work on reward tampering. It's more speculative than the empirical results, and the paper is careful to flag it as forward-looking rather than something G-S-M-eight-K itself demonstrates.

[G] A reward model trained to grade only the final answer can end up, without anyone asking it to, grading the reasoning along the way, and that's both the paper's most reassuring finding and the reason you shouldn't trust outcome-only supervision outside of arithmetic yet.
