[G] The setup is a small arms race that had started to look won. A wave of LLM defenses, representation rerouting, latent adversarial training, decoupled refusal training, had pushed automated jailbreak success down to single digits, sometimes close to zero, against a whole battery of standard attacks. Names like GCG, AutoDAN, PAIR, GPTFuzzer, AutoPrompt. On the standard robustness benchmarks, the problem looked nearly solved.
[O] Which is the kind of result that gets cited as "safety mostly handled, ship it."
[G] Exactly the authors' worry. And their argument is that those benchmarks are all testing the wrong threat model. Almost every one of those automated attacks works in a single turn, one crafted prompt, one response. But that's not how anyone actually uses a chat assistant, and it's not how a motivated bad actor would behave either. Real use is a conversation. Someone probes, gets a refusal, reframes, tries again. The claim here is that certifying a defense against single-turn automated attacks tells you almost nothing about whether it survives an actual conversation.
[S] Why hadn't anyone tested that properly before?
[G] Cost, mostly. Running a rigorous human red-teaming study is orders of magnitude more expensive than running an automated attack script overnight. Scale AI happens to run commercial red-teaming engagements at real volume, so they were in an unusual position to pay for this and to publish the tactics playbook their own red teamers had built up over what they describe as dozens of prior commercial projects. That's part of why this paper exists at all and not, say, an academic lab with a smaller budget.
[O] So walk us through what they actually built. Marcus, what's the machine here?
[G] Four defenses, all attack-agnostic, meaning none of them were specifically trained against one particular attack, which keeps the comparison from being rigged. There's RR, representation rerouting, the circuit-breakers approach. LAT, targeted latent adversarial training. DERTA, decoupled refusal training, which tries to push refusal behavior deeper into a response instead of just the first few tokens. And CYGNET, Gray Swan AI's closed-source circuit-breaker product, which they attacked through its public API rather than open weights. Three of the four sit on the same eight-billion-parameter Llama 3 base model, under one shared, generic system prompt: "You are a helpful AI Assistant."
[S] And the threat model itself, what can a red teamer actually do?
[G] Deliberately close to a real ChatGPT-style interface. Black box only, no access to internals or output probabilities. No prefilling the assistant's response. No access to the private assistant token that marks where the model's turn begins. What they are allowed is multiple turns of conversation, and the ability to toggle sampling temperature across zero, point five, and one. That's actually more restrictive than a lot of automated attacks, which include two white-box methods, AutoPrompt and GCG, that assume weight and gradient access no human red teamer in this study ever had.
[O] So the white-box attacks get a cheat code the humans don't.
[G] They do, and the authors keep them in anyway, deliberately, as a stress test that's stronger than the deployment threat model, on the logic that if you're going to trust a defense in production, you want it to survive even attacks it technically shouldn't have to face. There are six automated attacks total, four black-box, AutoDAN, GPTFuzzer, PAIR, and a zero-shot LLM-as-attacker method, plus those two white-box ones. And they also compute an ensemble score across all six, counting a behavior as broken if any single attack cracks it. That ensemble is the real upper bound automated methods get compared against.
[S] Now the human side. How do you turn "a person tried to jailbreak a model for half an hour" into a number you can trust?
[G] Through a fairly elaborate pipeline built specifically to suppress false positives. Up to two independent red teamers each get thirty minutes, working from scratch, no knowledge of what the other tried, and self-rate the attempt as failed, partially successful, or fully successful. A "fully successful" claim then goes to a human reviewer, checked against the same rubric an automated harm classifier would use. If that reviewer rejects it, a third red teamer attempts the same behavior again from zero, followed by a second reviewer. Only after clearing all of that does a GPT-4o classifier, running the standard HarmBench classifier prompt, get the final say. And that same classifier is used to score the automated attacks too, so the success bar is identical across attack types.
[O] That's a lot of friction just to call something a jailbreak.
[G] It's deliberately conservative. And it matters for how you read the top-line numbers later, because this pipeline is built to undercount, not overcount, human successes.
[S] What about the tactics themselves? I don't want us describing anything operational here, but conceptually, what's the shape of what worked?
[G] Right, and I'll stay at the category level, which is exactly how the paper itself organizes it. They label every successful jailbreak with one dominant tactic from a seven-category taxonomy the red teamers had developed organically across those commercial engagements. Direct Request, just asking outright. Echoing, getting the model to expand on something harmful already present in the prompt. Injection. Output Format tricks. Request Framing, wrapping the ask in a fictional, urgent, or emotional pretext. Obfuscation, burying the real request inside symbols or misleading text. And Hidden Intention Streamline, a sequence of individually innocuous turns that only add up to something harmful across the whole conversation. That last one is the tell. It literally cannot exist as a single-turn attack. It's structurally a multi-turn phenomenon.
[O] Which already hints at where this is going.
[G] It does. And there's a second, separate arm of the study worth flagging before results: unlearning. Forty-three multiple-choice WMDP-Bio questions, converted to free response. [S] One more thing I'd flag on rigor. The success criterion itself leans on a GPT-4o classifier, and the authors report it as a genuinely mixed instrument. On the human side it runs the opposite direction, used as a final, conservative filter. Across the four defenses, it only accepted eighty-eight point four to ninety-four point three percent of jailbreaks the human reviewers had already approved. [O] Does any of this connect to broader evaluation practice, or is it a fairly self-contained result?
[G] There's a real connective thread. The authors point out that refusal training in most of these defenses happens on short, single-turn conversations, which other work has already characterized as safety that's only a few tokens deep. That's the same shallow-signal problem evaluation researchers run into elsewhere, a benchmark score that looks solid because the benchmark itself doesn't probe the failure mode that actually matters in deployment. The unlearning result generalizes that point past jailbreaking specifically. If an automated probe says knowledge is gone, that's evidence about the probe's reach, not necessarily about whether the knowledge is gone. And there's a quieter methodological note worth naming, the authors disclosed their findings to the defense authors before publication and had legal review strip sensitive and export-controlled material out of the released dataset. That's a reasonable template for how this kind of red-teaming research should be published going forward.
[S] It also argues, implicitly, for defense-in-depth rather than a single robustness number. None of these four defenses were tested here with rate limits, guard models, or perplexity filters, all deliberately excluded to isolate the core defense. Production systems typically layer several of those together, so this paper is a lower bound on what a real deployed system would face, not necessarily on how it would fare.
