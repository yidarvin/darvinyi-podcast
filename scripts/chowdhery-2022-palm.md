[O] Picture this: Google trains a five hundred forty billion parameter model across two entire data center buildings, exchanging gradients over the network every single step, staying bitwise identical the whole way through.
[S] And the very same month that paper comes out, a completely different paper proves the model burned a huge amount of compute it didn't need to burn.
[O] Sure, but you're burying the lede. This is also the model that crossed a line nobody expected this early — on a benchmark built specifically to be brutal, it beat the average human score.
[S] "Average" human, on a curated subset, measured by ten crowdworkers a task. Let's not let one adjective quietly carry that whole claim.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an AI optimist, an AI skeptic, and a visiting scholar pull apart one paper from the front lines of machine learning research.
[S] Today's paper is "PaLM: Scaling Language Modeling with Pathways," led by Aakanksha Chowdhery and colleagues at Google Research, posted in April twenty twenty two — the same month Chinchilla landed, which becomes the whole plot of this episode.
[G] Thanks for having me. PaLM is fun to revisit, because it's simultaneously a genuine engineering triumph and a cautionary tale, and the paper itself doesn't quite know which one it is yet.
[S] Julian, set the scene. Why did this paper need to exist at that particular moment?
[G] Because the field was mid-pivot. Through twenty nineteen, the dominant recipe was models like BERT and T5: pretrain with a masked or span-corruption objective, then finetune on labeled data for every single downstream task, which means new weights and new data collection each time. Then GPT-3 showed a decoder-only model could be steered few-shot, from just a task description and a handful of examples in the prompt, with no gradient updates at all.
[O] And once that door opened, everyone sprinted through it.
[G] Exactly. GLaM, Gopher, Chinchilla, Megatron-Turing NLG, LaMDA — all chasing few-shot state of the art through some mix of more parameters, more training tokens, cleaner data, or sparsity. The authors framed PaLM as answering two open questions at once. The science question: does dense scaling keep paying off, or has it plateaued? And the systems question: nobody had trained a dense model anywhere near five hundred forty billion parameters efficiently — the prior ceiling was around four thousand ninety six TPU v three chips confined to one system.
[S] So it's a bet on two things simultaneously — that scale still works, and that you can actually build the plumbing to test that at this size.
[G] That's the paper in one sentence, yes.
[O] Walk us through what PaLM actually is, architecturally.
[G] It's a standard decoder-only Transformer trained left to right on next-token prediction — nothing exotic about the objective. The interesting choices are all in the details that let a five hundred forty billion parameter dense model train efficiently and decode cheaply. It uses SwiGLU activations in the feedforward blocks instead of ReLU or GELU, which costs an extra matrix multiplication but buys quality per FLOP. It also uses a "parallel" layer formulation, where attention and the feedforward block both read from the same normalized input and get summed, instead of feeding one into the other in sequence.
[S] And that buys you what, exactly?
[G] Roughly fifteen percent faster training at scale, because the two blocks' input projections can be fused into one matmul. The authors saw a small quality hit at the smallest model size but none at sixty two billion, so they extrapolated it would be quality-neutral at five hundred forty billion too — which is itself a bet, not a measurement.
[O] What else is in the recipe?
[G] Multi-query attention, where key and value projections are shared across heads — it barely changes quality but makes autoregressive decoding far cheaper on accelerators. Rotary position embeddings instead of absolute ones. No biases anywhere in the dense layers, purely for training stability at this scale. And a two hundred fifty six thousand token SentencePiece vocabulary that splits numbers into individual digits and falls back to raw bytes for anything unrecognized, built for a corpus that's roughly half social media conversations, over a quarter filtered web pages, and the rest books, code, Wikipedia, and news.
[S] So the model itself is almost boring by design.
[G] Right, and that's deliberate. The real story is Pathways, the system that gets it to actually run. PaLM five hundred forty billion trained on six thousand one hundred forty four TPU v four chips, split across two separate pods of three thousand seventy two chips each, with zero pipeline parallelism. Each pod holds a full copy of the model, computes gradients on its half of the batch, and then the two pods exchange gradients over the datacenter network and apply identical updates — bitwise identical, every step.
[O] Bitwise identical across two buildings sounds fragile. What actually breaks?
[G] Loss spikes — about twenty of them over the course of training, at irregular and sometimes very late points. The fix was unglamorous: roll back to a checkpoint roughly a hundred steps before the spike, skip the two hundred to five hundred batches around it, and resume. They tested whether it was "bad data" by retraining on those exact batches from an earlier checkpoint, and saw no spike. So it's the combination of specific data with a specific parameter state — not a clean, generalizable failure mode they could patch.
[S] And the payoff for all that plumbing?
[G] Forty six point two percent model FLOPs utilization — a metric the authors introduce specifically because they think the "hardware FLOPs" numbers other labs reported were being gamed by counting recomputation. For comparison: GPT-3, on Nvidia V one hundred GPUs, was reported around twenty one percent; Gopher around thirty two and a half; Megatron-Turing NLG around thirty. Forty six percent at this parameter count, without pipelining, was a genuinely new ceiling.
[S] Let's get to results. What's the headline number?
[G] Few-shot PaLM five hundred forty billion sets a new state of the art on twenty eight of the twenty nine most widely used English benchmarks, measured against the best per-task score pulled from GLaM, GPT-3, Megatron-Turing NLG, Gopher, Chinchilla, and LaMDA. On MMLU, five-shot, it averages sixty nine point three, edging past Chinchilla's seventy billion parameter model at sixty seven point five.
[S] "Best per-task score pulled from six different models" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. That's not the same as beating any single rival across the board.
[G] Correct, and the paper doesn't hide the framing — it's explicit that this is a curated basket, not a head-to-head sweep against one competitor. It's a real result, but "state of the art on twenty eight of twenty nine" is a stronger-sounding claim than "beat one fixed rival everywhere."
[O] What about BIG-bench? That's the one I find remarkable.
[G] BIG-bench was a brand-new suite at the time, over a hundred fifty deliberately hard tasks. PaLM five hundred forty billion's five-shot aggregate score exceeded the average score of the human crowdworkers who attempted the same tasks, and it beat the prior state of the art on forty four of fifty eight tasks evaluated across all three model sizes. Roughly twenty five percent of tasks showed what the authors call "discontinuous" improvement — a sharp jump from sixty two billion to five hundred forty billion, instead of the smooth, log-linear gain the smaller models would predict.
[O] That's the "emergence" result everyone talks about.
[G] It is. Take one example the paper walks through, a task called logical sequence: normalized accuracy goes thirteen percent, twenty five percent, eighty seven percent across the three sizes. A straight-line projection from the first two points predicts about thirty seven percent at five hundred forty billion. The actual number is eighty seven — fifty points above what the trend line said to expect.
[S] And "beats average human" — average of how many people, on what?
[G] Around ten crowdworkers per task, explicitly the average, not the best. The paper is careful to say PaLM stays well below best-human performance on the same suite, and on thirty five percent of individual tasks, average humans still beat it outright.
[O] Now the result I think is the biggest deal: reasoning.
[G] Combine scale with chain-of-thought prompting — writing out intermediate reasoning steps before the final answer — and PaLM five hundred forty billion hits fifty eight percent on GSM8K, grade-school math word problems, using eight-shot prompting plus a simple external calculator. Prior state of the art was fifty five percent, from Cobbe and colleagues, and that came from finetuning a separate model plus training a dedicated verifier. The calculator alone was never worth more than five points; the lift comes from chain-of-thought plus scale. Across seven reasoning benchmarks, that combination matched or beat finetuned state of the art on four, and came close on the rest.
[S] Any trade-off? What did that cost in code or multilingual performance?
[G] Almost nothing, which is the surprising part. Only about twenty two percent of the training corpus was non-English, and PaLM still pushed few-shot translation past prior state of the art on several language pairs. On code, it saw just thirty nine billion code tokens in training, a small slice of that in Python, and still hit twenty six point two pass-at-one on HumanEval — competitive with Codex twelve billion's twenty eight point eight, on roughly fifty times fewer Python tokens.
[O] Did they look at anything beyond raw capability?
[G] Yes, memorization and bias both get full sections. On memorization, the paper is unusually candid: the five hundred forty billion model exactly reproduced fifty-token training continuations two point four percent of the time, versus one point six percent for the eight billion model. Bigger models memorize more, and it climbs steeply with how often a string was duplicated in training.
[S] And bias?
[G] Winogender coreference accuracy, which tracks gender bias in occupation pronouns, improves with scale, and PaLM sets a new state of the art there. But a co-occurrence study on race and religion prompts found the model will falsely reinforce stereotypes — Islam paired with words like terrorist and extremist, for instance — and that pattern held consistently across all three model sizes. Scale didn't fix it. The sixty two billion and five hundred forty billion models even had nearly identical toxicity profiles, which suggests the training data is driving that behavior more than parameter count is.
[O] Okay, let's argue this properly. My case: this is the paper that proved the scaling hypothesis wasn't running out of road. Twenty eight of twenty nine on English benchmarks, a five-shot model beating average humans on a benchmark built to be adversarial, and reasoning that rivals purpose-built finetuned pipelines, using nothing but a bigger model and a better prompt. That's a step change in what one frozen model can do out of the box.
[S] Here's my deflationary case, and it isn't subtle: the same month this paper landed, Chinchilla landed too, and Chinchilla is the referee here. PaLM trains five hundred forty billion parameters on seven hundred eighty billion tokens — about one point four tokens per parameter. Chinchilla's own analysis puts the compute-optimal ratio closer to twenty tokens per parameter. And the paper's own table proves the point: a seventy billion parameter Chinchilla lands within two points of PaLM five hundred forty billion on MMLU average. Eight times fewer parameters, almost the same score.
[G] You're both right, and the paper half-admits it in its own open-questions section — it explicitly flags that it can't rule out data quality as the real driver, since the sixty two billion model beats GPT-3 despite far lower training compute. On the capability point: those claims hold up. Twenty eight of twenty nine, the reasoning results — a smaller, better-trained model doesn't erase what PaLM demonstrated was possible. But on efficiency, the skeptic is just reading the paper's own numbers correctly.
[S] What about the "emergent" framing specifically? That's the part I trust least.
[G] Fair, and it's aged the worst of anything in here. The discontinuous-improvement framing got picked up as evidence for genuine emergent capabilities switching on at scale. Later work, Schaeffer and colleagues, argued a lot of that discontinuity is a metric artifact — swap in a smoother, continuous scoring metric instead of an all-or-nothing one, and some of those cliffs flatten into curves. That paper cites this one directly. The phenomenon in the data is real; the interpretation of it as new capabilities switching on is contested.
[O] I'll take that point. But the contamination work still deserves credit — that's not nothing for twenty twenty two.
[G] Agreed, genuinely a strength. They ran an explicit n-gram overlap analysis between training and eval data, split ten of the twenty nine benchmarks into contaminated and clean subsets, and found roughly as many positive as negative deltas on the clean subsets — evidence against contamination meaningfully inflating the headline numbers. That's more rigorous than most contemporaries. The catch is it's entirely self-reported: the dataset and the weights were never released, so nobody outside Google could rerun the check.
[O] So what actually changed because of this paper?
[G] Two things, in different directions. On capability, PaLM is a big reason chain-of-thought plus scale became the default recipe for reasoning tasks going forward. On methodology, the field's compute budgets pivoted hard toward Chinchilla's ratio instead — models like Llama, which cite PaLM directly, trained token-rich rather than parameter-rich from that point on. And there's a smaller, sharper lesson buried in here for anyone doing evaluation work: reporting "state of the art on N of M benchmarks" against the best score from a basket of other models is a weaker claim than beating one fixed baseline everywhere, and it's worth reading that fine print every time you see the headline number.

[G] If I had to leave you with one line: PaLM proved dense scaling hadn't plateaued and that chain-of-thought plus scale could rival finetuned reasoning — and it proved both in the same month it became the lesson in how not to spend your compute.
[O] My one line: this is the paper where "just add parameters" peaked, and it peaked spectacularly.
