[O] But on Remedies, the easiest category, one of those same models hits one hundred percent — perfect. That spread tells you exactly where legal language actually gets hard.
[S] Or it tells you the easy questions were never worth testing in the first place, and the paper spent most of its budget proving what any lawyer already knew.
[O] Except nobody had built the dataset to check that assumption at scale for merger agreements, until now.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio. I'm your optimist host, and across the table, as always, is our skeptic.
[S] Today we're covering MAUD: An Expert-Annotated Legal NLP Dataset for Merger Agreement Understanding, by Steven Wang and colleagues, out of ETH Zurich, Harvard, and The Atticus Project — an EMNLP twenty twenty three paper.
[O] And joining us is Genevra, a visiting scholar who's spent real time with this dataset and what it tests. Genevra, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. MAUD is a quietly important paper — the reading-comprehension half of a project that started with contract extraction, built specifically around the highest-stakes contract type there is: the agreement that makes or breaks a merger.
[S] Set up the actual problem, Genevra. Why did merger agreements need their own benchmark?
[G] By twenty twenty one, transformers had already matched or beaten humans on general reading comprehension — SQuAD two point oh, SuperGLUE, both effectively solved. But that's short, everyday text. Whether a model can read genuinely specialized legal prose was untested, and legal text is exactly where length and ambiguity bite hardest.
[O] And merger agreements are about as high-stakes as contract review gets.
[G] Right. Public company acquisitions run into the hundreds of billions of dollars a year, and their pivotal clauses are called deal points — things like the definition of a Material Adverse Effect, or whether a deal's consideration is a fixed ratio or a fixed value. Reading those clauses correctly is precisely the judgment M and A attorneys are trained and paid to make.
[S] The same lab had already done half of this problem, hadn't they, with CUAD?
[G] Exactly the framing the authors use. Contract review is two steps: extract the relevant clause, a span-extraction task, then interpret what it means, a reading-comprehension task. The earlier benchmark from the same group, CUAD, covered extraction across general commercial contracts. For the interpretation half, the authors say there was no large-scale, expert-annotated reading-comprehension dataset for contract review, or for any English-language legal task at all.
[O] So MAUD fills that second half for merger agreements.
[G] And it doesn't invent its own taxonomy of what counts as a deal point. It's built directly on the American Bar Association's twenty twenty one Public Target Deal Points Study, an existing professional standard M and A lawyers already use.
[S] Walk us through what MAUD actually is, mechanically.
[G] For that ABA study, the association appointed an M and A attorney to design one hundred and thirty standardized deal-point questions. Ninety two made it into MAUD. The task: given a deal-point question and the extracted clause text, a model picks the correct answer, or answers, from a predefined, ABA-standardized list of choices.
[O] Multiple choice, not free text.
[G] Multiple choice, which matters for how results should be read. Several of the ninety two questions are multi-label, more than one answer can be true at once, so the authors recast each as a set of binary questions — taking the effective count from ninety two to one hundred and forty four.
[S] And where does the actual contract text come from?
[G] One hundred and fifty two English-language public merger agreements, pulled from the SEC's EDGAR filing system. Annotators extracted eight thousand two hundred and twenty six unique deal-point texts, then answered questions about them to produce thirty nine thousand two hundred and thirty one question-answer examples. Add those together and you get the paper's headline number: forty seven thousand four hundred and fifty seven total annotations.
[O] And that's presumably grouped somehow — nobody wants forty seven thousand ungrouped labels.
[G] Twenty two deal-point types, the ordinary-course covenant, the Material Adverse Effect definition, and so on, rolling up into seven scoring categories: General Information, Conditions to Closing, Material Adverse Effect, Knowledge, Deal Protection and Related Provisions, Operating and Efforts Covenants, and Remedies.
[S] Now the part I actually want to interrogate — the annotation process, because that's where the five-million-dollar figure comes from.
[G] Over ten thousand hours, thirty six annotators, all law students and experienced lawyers, all unpaid volunteers from a non-profit legal organization. Before labeling, every law student went through seventy to one hundred hours of training: lectures and workshops from practicing M and A lawyers, plus quizzes.
[O] That's most of a part-time semester of training before a single label gets written.
[G] It is. Each annotation was produced by a team of three students working to consensus, then reviewed by an experienced M and A lawyer. Disagreements escalated to a panel of three to five expert lawyers. At a prevailing M and A billing rate of five hundred dollars an hour, the authors estimate the dataset's pecuniary value at over five million dollars.
[S] Genevra, is that a marketing number or a real one?
[G] It's a real, if approximate, accounting of professional time — documented in enough detail that the order of magnitude is trustworthy, even if the exact figure is a back-of-envelope multiplication.
[O] What about the actual data splits — how do you evaluate something like this fairly?
[G] MAUD ships as three sub-datasets. Main, twenty thousand six hundred and twenty three examples, original deal-point text extracted verbatim. Abridged, fourteen thousand nine hundred and twenty eight examples, joined substrings of the main texts with an "omitted" marker, meant to teach a model to focus on the pertinent text. And Rare Answers, three thousand six hundred and eighty examples, where legal experts minimally edited main-dataset text to manufacture answer classes too rare to otherwise support a train, dev, test split.
[S] That third one is a flag on its own — synthetic data patching a class-imbalance problem.
[G] It is, and it's handled carefully. The test split reserves twenty percent of the combined main and abridged data, and the rare-answers examples never enter it — they only ever appear in train and dev. So rare-answer performance is never actually measured on held-out data.
[O] And the metric they used to score all this?
[G] Because most questions have heavily imbalanced answer distributions, the headline metric is minority-class AUPR, area under the precision-recall curve, computed per answer, averaged into a per-question score, then averaged across all one hundred and forty four questions, over three runs.
[S] What models did they actually test?
[G] All fine-tuned encoders, nothing generative. In the single-task setting, one model per question: BERT, RoBERTa, LegalBERT, DeBERTa, and BigBird, ranging from about one hundred and ten to one hundred and eighty four million parameters. In the multi-task setting, one shared encoder with a hundred and forty four classification heads, tested on RoBERTa, LegalBERT, and DeBERTa — BERT dropped as dominated by RoBERTa and LegalBERT, BigBird dropped for compute.
[O] Why BigBird specifically, out of that whole list?
[G] Because twenty seven point six percent of the unique deal-point texts, and half of all examples, run longer than RoBERTa's five hundred and twelve token limit. Every model except BigBird truncates there. BigBird, initialized from RoBERTa, uses sparse attention to handle sequences up to four thousand and ninety six tokens, long enough to cover every text without truncation.
[O] So, the actual numbers. How did the models do?
[G] Comfortably above random, well short of solved. Random guessing scores sixteen point eight percent overall AUPR. Single-task BERT gets fifty two point six percent, RoBERTa fifty five point five, LegalBERT fifty five point nine, DeBERTa fifty seven point one, and BigBird the best at fifty seven point eight. Multi-task is generally a bit lower: RoBERTa fifty one point four, LegalBERT fifty five point eight, DeBERTa fifty three percent.
[S] So single-task consistently beats multi-task.
[G] For RoBERTa and DeBERTa, by around four percentage points. LegalBERT is close to a tie between the two settings, which the paper doesn't fully explain.
[O] What's the real story inside those averages — the category breakdown?
[G] That's where it gets interesting. Remedies, General Information, and Operating and Efforts Covenants are essentially solved — Remedies hits one hundred percent AUPR for single-task LegalBERT, General Information reaches eighty seven point two percent for DeBERTa, Operating and Efforts hits eighty nine percent for LegalBERT. But three categories sit near a coin-flip-adjusted floor: Deal Protections tops out at fifty eight point six percent, Material Adverse Effect at fifty point nine, and Conditions to Closing at just forty eight point two.
[S] Is there a pattern behind those three hard categories, or is it just noise?
[G] There's a clean pattern, and it's the paper's central finding: those are exactly the categories with the longest texts. Material Adverse Effect has ninety nine percent of its texts over the five hundred and twelve token limit, Conditions to Closing forty three point nine percent, Deal Protections twenty one point seven percent — also, by the paper's own account, the categories attorneys find hardest to review.
[O] Did just having more data actually help here?
[G] Measurably. Training RoBERTa on random subsets, the full training set beats a fifty percent subset by seven point three percentage points, and a five percent subset by twenty three point seven points — the paper's argument for why a dataset this large was worth building.
[S] And legal-specific pretraining — does LegalBERT actually earn its name?
[G] Yes, modestly but consistently. It beats plain BERT and edges out RoBERTa in the single-task setting, beats DeBERTa in multi-task, and posts the top micro F one score in every category except Knowledge. Real, if not huge.
[O] You mentioned a companion extraction task too.
[G] Released in the appendix — contract spans, so someone could also test "find the clause," not just "interpret it." A RoBERTa baseline there scores only nineteen point seven percent AUPR, far below the forty two point six percent RoBERTa managed on CUAD's own extraction task. Finding the clause is apparently harder than interpreting it.
[O] Let's argue this properly. Optimist case: this is the first time anyone built a large, expert-verified reading-comprehension benchmark for the single highest-stakes contract type in corporate law, with a documented ten-thousand-hour annotation pipeline behind it, and the results already show two real, useful signals — legal pretraining helps, and scale helps a lot. That's exactly the foundation a field needs before it can measure real progress.
[S] Deflationary case: every model here is a fine-tuned encoder between one hundred and ten and one hundred and eighty four million parameters. There's no zero-shot or few-shot large language model baseline anywhere, no GPT three, no GPT three point five, even though the paper cites GPT three and flags "prompt-friendly" question descriptions as future work. By EMNLP twenty twenty three, in-context learning was the obvious comparison, and it's simply absent.
[G] Both points are grounded in the paper. That gap is real — "substantial room for improvement" is measured against small fine-tuned encoders, not the regime that was already standard by publication. The paper simply doesn't say whether a prompted frontier model would crush these categories or trip over the same long-text problem.
[O] Even so, the annotation quality is the real asset, and that holds up regardless of which model reads it.
[S] Does it, though? The authors admit they didn't retain the records needed for inter-annotator agreement. The only signal on label quality is that reviewing lawyers agreed with student annotations roughly eighty percent of the time, plus one small post-hoc check.
[G] Worth being precise about that check. On Material Adverse Effect, one of the hardest categories, an independent attorney not involved in the original process answered the questions for ten contracts and disagreed with the gold label three times out of four hundred and forty. After conferring with the annotation team, all three were resolved in favor of the original gold label.
[O] Three out of four hundred and forty is reassuring.
[S] It's reassuring and small — ten contracts, one category, one outside reviewer. It doesn't give you an inter-annotator agreement number for the ninety two questions as a whole, and it can't, because the underlying records weren't kept.
[O] What about the abridged-versus-main split — does that inflate anything?
[G] A little, and the authors report it plainly. Abridged subscores run higher than main subscores across the board — multi-task LegalBERT, for instance, scores seventy three point three percent on abridged text versus seventy percent on main text. Abridged texts are joined substrings with the irrelevant parts already stripped out, so the dataset has effectively pre-done some of the "find the relevant passage" work, which reads a bit optimistic against raw, un-segmented contract text.
[S] There's run-to-run noise too. The paper's own appendix says overall AUPR varies by one to two percent across runs — single-task BigBird's fifty seven point eight percent, DeBERTa's fifty seven point one, RoBERTa's fifty five point five — a chunk of that ranking sits inside the noise band.
[O] So "bigger and newer models do better" is real, but the exact ordering between DeBERTa and BigBird shouldn't be read too literally.
[G] That's the right level of confidence. The trend, legal pretraining helps, scale helps, is real and reproducible. The precise leaderboard ranking among similarly-sized models is thinner than a table makes it look.
[S] One more, and it's the sharpest: contamination. These are public EDGAR filings, and MAUD is released under a permissive Creative Commons license on GitHub. Any future large language model trained on a broad web or legal crawl could easily have seen this exact text, and possibly the labels, before anyone evaluates it.
[G] A legitimate forward-looking risk the paper couldn't have addressed, since it predates the large-scale LLM-evaluation era it's now being pulled into. The leakage the authors do handle is narrower: keeping main and abridged examples from the same contract in the same split, so training and test never share a near-duplicate.
[O] So internal leakage is handled, external contamination against future models isn't, and couldn't have been anticipated in twenty twenty three.
[G] Correct on both counts.
[S] Does any of this connect to evaluation practice outside legal NLP?
[G] The clearest lesson is construct validity in multiple-choice framing. MAUD tests whether a model can pick the ABA's pre-written answer from a fixed list, given text a human already extracted — a real skill, but a step removed from what a lawyer actually does, which is draft and negotiate language, not select among options someone else already wrote.
[O] It's the same gap a lot of specialized benchmarks have — testing recognition instead of production.
[G] Exactly, and the authors are candid about that boundary. They explicitly say the deal points here don't represent every deal point that matters in a real merger agreement, and that MAUD should not be used as the sole source for building an AI tool for merger review or drafting.
[S] There's also a coverage limitation worth naming: these are U S public-company deals over two hundred million dollars that closed in twenty twenty one. Private deals, smaller deals, other years, other jurisdictions — none of that is represented, and the authors say so themselves.
[G] My one-sentence takeaway is that MAUD's lasting contribution is the annotation, not the twenty twenty three model scores. Five million dollars of verified legal judgment now exists as a target for models that didn't exist when the paper was written, and it already lives on as a task suite inside LegalBench.
[O] Mine is that going from a coin-flip floor to consistently above fifty percent on the three hardest categories, with real gains from legal pretraining and scale, is exactly the kind of early signal that tells a field where to invest next.
[S] And mine is that until someone runs an actual frontier language model against these one hundred and forty four questions, with contamination checked and a real inter-annotator agreement number in hand, "substantial room for improvement" is a claim about small encoders, not about what's actually achievable here.
