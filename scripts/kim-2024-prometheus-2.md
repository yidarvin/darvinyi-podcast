[S] Here's the uncomfortable part of using a language model as a judge: the judge you trust most is a black box you don't own, and every open alternative built before this paper does only half the job, either scoring one response on a rubric, or picking the better of two, never both.
[O] And yet by mid twenty twenty four there was an open model claiming to do both, landing within striking distance of GPT-4 and human raters, and runnable on your own hardware.
[S] "Striking distance" is doing a lot of work in that sentence, especially once you learn the model was trained on feedback that GPT-4 itself wrote.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a guest scholar take apart one paper from the frontier. Today's paper is "Prometheus 2: An Open Source Language Model Specialized in Evaluating Other Language Models," by Seungone Kim and colleagues out of KAIST AI and LG AI Research, presented at EMNLP twenty twenty four.
[S] It's on the docket because it's the paper that made "run your own judge" a credible engineering choice instead of a compromise, and joining us to get into the internals is Torin, a researcher who's studied this work closely. Welcome, Torin.
[G] Thanks for having me. The short version of why this paper matters is that it's the first serious attempt to make one open model genuinely good at both major ways people ask a language model to grade another language model's output.
[O] So set the scene for us. Why did we need this at all?
[G] Using a strong proprietary model as a judge is now the default way to score open-ended generations at scale, but the paper is direct about why that's uncomfortable: you can't inspect the judge, you can't pin its version, and you can't guarantee it'll even be available tomorrow. That pushes people toward evaluator models they can run themselves.
[S] But open evaluators up to that point weren't good enough to actually trust, right?
[G] Right, and the paper is specific about how they failed. First, their scores diverged sharply from humans. The strongest prior open evaluator, the original Prometheus at thirteen billion parameters, correlated only point four four nine with human ratings on a benchmark called FLASK, while GPT-4 reached point six seven nine on that same comparison.
[O] That's a big gap.
[G] It is. Second, every open evaluator did exactly one job. One trained for direct assessment, scoring a single response on a one-to-five scale, literally could not do pairwise ranking, picking the better of two responses, and vice versa. Both formats show up constantly in real evaluation pipelines, so you needed two separate models.
[S] And the third failure?
[G] Fixed, generic criteria. Existing open judges were built around attributes like helpfulness and harmlessness, with no way to hand them a custom, instance-specific rubric, the kind of criterion a real evaluation usually needs.
[O] So the target is basically: pull an open model out of what the paper calls the weak evaluator group, models that don't even correlate well with each other, into the strong group, where humans, GPT-4, and Claude-3-Opus all agree with one another, and do it on both formats, with arbitrary criteria.
[G] Exactly the framing in their opening figure. That's the gap Prometheus 2 is built to close.
[S] Walk us through how they actually did it.
[G] The core idea is to treat direct assessment and pairwise ranking as two related but distinct skills, train a specialist for each, and then merge the two specialists into one model, rather than training one model on both formats at once.
[O] Why not just train on both together? That seems like the obvious move.
[G] They tried that too, as a baseline called joint training, and I'll get to why it underperforms. But first, the data. Everything starts from an existing resource, the Feedback Collection from the original Prometheus paper: one thousand evaluation criteria, twenty thousand instructions, twenty thousand reference answers, and one hundred thousand individual judgments, each one a response paired with a one-to-five score and GPT-4-written verbal feedback.
[S] That's the direct-assessment side. Where does the pairwise data come from?
[G] That's the paper's new contribution, the Preference Collection, the first pairwise dataset with over a thousand instance-wise criteria. The construction is clever. Since the Feedback Collection already has five scored responses per instruction, they pair them up, which gives ten combinations per instruction, roughly two hundred thousand pairs. The existing one-to-five scores tell you which response in each pair should win, so no new labeling is needed there. Then they prompt GPT-4-1106 to write fresh comparative feedback that contrasts the two responses directly, rather than reusing the old single-response write-ups.
[O] Did they check that the new feedback was actually good?
[G] They ran a human verification study, five annotators, two hundred sampled instances, three checks. Coherence between the feedback and the scoring decision passed at ninety-nine point five percent. Suitability against the stated criterion passed at ninety-eight point five percent. And on criticality, whether the new comparative feedback actually uses information from both responses rather than just stapling together two independent write-ups, annotators preferred it eighty-eight percent of the time.
[S] Okay, the data's solid on its own terms. Now the actual merging trick.
[G] They train two separate models on the same base checkpoint, one purely on the Feedback Collection for direct assessment, the other purely on the Preference Collection for pairwise ranking. Then they merge the weights into a single final model. The simplest recipe is linear: the merged model is alpha times the direct-assessment specialist's weights, plus one minus alpha times the pairwise specialist's weights.
[O] And alpha is just a mixing knob.
[G] Right, and linear isn't the only way to merge. They test six recipes total, plain linear, Task Arithmetic, TIES, two DARE variants, and SLERP, which is a spherical interpolation. DARE-Linear came out on top, so that's what ships in the final models.
[S] What's DARE actually doing, briefly?
[G] It's a sparsification step before merging, it randomly drops and rescales a fraction of each model's weight changes before combining them, which reduces interference between the two specialists' updates. The paper treats it as an empirically chosen ingredient from prior work, not something they derive from first principles here.
[O] And the base models?
[G] Two sizes. Mistral seven B Instruct becomes Prometheus-2 seven B, and Mixtral eight-by-seven B Instruct becomes Prometheus-2 eight-by-seven B, the larger one trained with LoRA. One more detail worth keeping: at inference, direct assessment is run reference-based, you give the model a reference answer alongside the response being scored, while pairwise ranking is run reference-free.
[O] Let's get to numbers. How did it actually do?
[G] Eight benchmarks total, split evenly. Four for direct assessment, scored by Pearson correlation against a reference judge, Vicuna Bench, MT-Bench, FLASK, and Feedback Bench. Four for pairwise ranking, scored by accuracy against human labels, HHH Alignment, MT-Bench Human Judgment, Auto-J Eval, and Preference Bench, which is Prometheus 2's own in-domain test set.
[S] Start with the headline.
[G] The headline is the FLASK-versus-humans number, because that's the cleanest human-agreement figure in the paper. Humans and GPT-4 correlate at point six seven nine there. The best prior open model, Prometheus thirteen B, only reached point four four nine with humans. Prometheus-2 eight-by-seven B reaches point five five five, which the authors describe, accurately, as halving the gap.
[O] And beyond that one number?
[G] Across the direct-assessment benchmarks, both Prometheus 2 sizes beat the prior open evaluators, the original Prometheus and a model called Auto-J, by at least point two Pearson, consistently. And the scoring decisions of Prometheus 2, GPT-4-1106, Claude-3-Opus, and humans all correlate above point five with each other on every benchmark, that's literally the strong evaluator group from the opening figure, and Prometheus 2 is now sitting inside it, while base models and single-format baselines mostly stay below point five.
[S] What about pairwise?
[G] On all four pairwise benchmarks, the Prometheus 2 models score highest among open evaluators. The detail I'd flag: HHH Alignment is the in-domain benchmark for a specialist model called PairRM, and Auto-J Eval is the in-domain benchmark for Auto-J, and Prometheus 2 eight-by-seven B beats both specialists on their own home turf. On MT-Bench Human Judgment it reaches seventy-one point nine six percent agreement with humans, against seventy-nine point nine for GPT-4-1106 and seventy-seven point six five for Claude-3-Opus, again roughly halving the accuracy gap on the out-of-domain sets.
[O] Beating a specialist on its own benchmark is a real result, that's not a cherry-picked comparison, that's the opponent's home field.
[S] It's suggestive. I'd want to know more about what beating means there before I call it decisive, we'll get to that.
[G] There's also an analysis section I'd put right alongside the results, because it explains why merging works at all. Joint training, one model trained on both datasets together, actually underperforms training on a single format alone, that's negative transfer. Weight merging, by contrast, beats both the jointly trained model and either single-format specialist, that's positive transfer. And they ruled out plain ensembling as the explanation: merging two direct-assessment models together, or two pairwise models together, from different random seeds, doesn't help and sometimes hurts. Only merging across formats helps.
[O] So the gain is specifically from combining the two skills, not just averaging out noise.
[G] That's their claim, and it comes with a clean control experiment for it. One more wrinkle: the benefit is asymmetric. Sweeping alpha, direct-assessment performance peaks around alpha equals point five, but pairwise performance peaks at alpha equals point three, meaning the pairwise-trained weights help direct assessment more than the direct-assessment weights help pairwise.
[O] Okay, I want to make the strongest case for this paper, because I think it's easy to undersell. This is a genuinely useful, cheap, reproducible recipe: two specialists, six merge recipes tried, one clear winner, and the resulting model beats every open alternative on every one of eight benchmarks. Beating PairRM on PairRM's own benchmark and Auto-J on Auto-J's own benchmark is a specific, falsifiable claim, and it holds. Plus the Preference Collection itself, a pairwise dataset with over a thousand custom criteria, is a reusable artifact independent of the model. That's real infrastructure for the field.
[S] I'll grant the engineering result, weight merging beating joint training, and the same-format ablation ruling out plain ensembling, that's a clean, credible finding on its own terms. What I don't buy is the framing that this model closely mirrors human and GPT-4 judgment. Every number in this paper is a correlation or an agreement rate with GPT-4-1106, Claude-3-Opus, or humans, it's evaluating an evaluator entirely by proxy, and the paper says as much in its own limitations section.
[O] Proxy metrics are still evidence, though.
[S] Sure, but look at where the proxy signal actually comes from. The verbal feedback in both training sets, the Feedback Collection and the new Preference Collection, was written by GPT-4. So when Prometheus 2's scores correlate well with GPT-4's scores, some real chunk of that is the model successfully imitating GPT-4's scoring distribution, not independent judgment converging on the same answer by a different route.
[G] That's a fair read of what the paper itself flags as a limitation, it explicitly calls its own approach an indirect method for exactly this reason. It's worth separating the two proxy targets, because they tell different stories. Against GPT-4 and Claude, Prometheus 2's correlations are close to the proprietary models' correlations with each other. Against humans, the metric that isn't contaminated by shared training provenance, it's clearly weaker: point five five five versus point six seven nine on FLASK, seventy-one point nine six versus seventy-nine point nine on MT-Bench Human Judgment. Halving the gap is the paper's own honest phrase, and it's accurate. Closely mirrors oversells it a little.
[O] So the human numbers are the ones to trust, and by that standard it's a real but partial win.
[S] There's a second issue I want on the table. Two of the eight benchmarks, Feedback Bench and Preference Bench, are in-domain test splits of the very synthetic collections used for training. That's where you see the point eight eight to point nine oh scores. The paper labels this honestly, but it's easy to skim past and read those numbers as generalization when they're closer to a held-out split of the training distribution.
[G] Right, and I'd add one more. The merge method and the alpha value were both selected by sweeping performance across the same eight evaluation benchmarks used to report the final numbers. That's not contamination in the traditional sense, but the merge hyperparameters are lightly fit to the test suite they're then evaluated on.
[O] Okay, that one actually moves me a bit, a held-out set for picking alpha would have made the headline numbers more convincing.
[S] There's a subtler gap too. Correlation on the final scalar score says nothing about whether the verbal feedback itself is correct or useful. A judge can land the right one-to-five number for the wrong reason, and the paper only checked feedback coherence at dataset-build time, never whether Prometheus 2's own generated feedback is trustworthy at inference.
[G] That's accurate, and it connects to something the authors list explicitly in their limitations. The model is locked to a one-to-five Likert scale or an A-versus-B pairwise call. No one-to-ten scale, no ranking five responses at once, no checklist evaluation, formats a proprietary model can just be prompted into, but an open model apparently can't without retraining.
[O] Zoom out for a second, if this recipe holds up, what changes?
[G] Practically, it gives evaluation pipelines a judge you can inspect, pin a version of, and run on your own hardware, with a custom criterion written into the prompt rather than a fixed helpfulness rubric. For a research group building a domain-specific benchmark, that flexibility is the actual selling point, more than the raw correlation numbers.
[S] Though I'd flag the circularity for anyone building an evaluation pipeline on top of this: you'd be using a language model, trained on another language model's judgments, to judge a third language model's outputs. At some point that chain needs an anchor outside itself, human labels, and this paper is honest that human agreement is exactly where the residual gap is largest.
[O] Which is also the evaluation-practice lesson, contamination and judge reliability aren't side issues, they're the main event once you're using any language model as a judge, open or closed.
[G] The authors say it plainly in their conclusion, this should be used alongside human evaluation, not instead of it. That's not a hedge, it's the actual scope of the claim.
[G] If I had to leave listeners with one sentence: merging a direct-assessment specialist and a pairwise-ranking specialist beats training either alone or training both jointly, and that's a real, reusable finding, but the headline of matching GPT-4 and humans is carried more by the GPT-4 side than the human side.
[O] My takeaway is that this is the paper that made running your own judge a credible engineering choice instead of a compromise, even if it's not yet a replacement for careful human evaluation.
