[S] Here's a number that should worry anyone who ships a chatbot: the space of things a user might type into it is, for practical purposes, infinite.
[O] And the classic answer to that problem is paying humans to sit down and think up the worst questions they can, and there just aren't enough humans, or hours, to cover it.
[S] Which is exactly the setup that let Microsoft's Tay learn to send racist, sexually charged tweets within hours back in 2016 — nobody had thought to test for that specific failure ahead of time.
[O] So today's paper asks a blunt question: what if you used a language model to do the red-teaming, instead of a room full of contractors?
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from litsearch dot darvinyi dot com. Today's paper is "Red Teaming Language Models with Language Models," out of DeepMind, lead-authored by Ethan Perez, and colleagues, published at EMNLP twenty twenty-two.
[S] It's on the docket because it's basically the ancestor paper — nearly every automated red-teaming method that came after cites this three-stage recipe.
[O] And to help us take it apart, we've got Silas, who's spent real time with this one. Silas, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. Underneath the eye-catching headline numbers, this paper is really proposing a very general recipe, and the details of that recipe matter more than any single statistic in it.
[S] Silas, start with the gap. Why couldn't people just keep hand-writing test cases?
[G] Because hand-writing doesn't scale and it doesn't generalize. The paper points to prior work like CheckList and HateCheck, where researchers write specific test cases and templates by hand for known failure types, and to the Bot-Adversarial Dialogue dataset, where paid human adversaries try to bait a chatbot into an offensive reply. All of that is bounded by how much a small group of people can think of on a given day.
[O] Right, and Tay is the paper's own opening example of what that boundedness costs. The authors quote the person who ran it directly: they'd prepared for many kinds of abuse, but made what he calls a critical oversight for that one specific attack.
[S] There was an automated alternative already, though — gradient-based adversarial search, the kind used against text classifiers. Why not just use that?
[G] Because it produces garbage. The paper cites an example from prior work, a string of nonsense tokens optimized purely to trip a classifier, with no resemblance to something a real user would type. It tells you almost nothing about what happens when an actual person uses the product.
[O] So the target the authors are aiming at is specific: test cases that are fluent, natural-sounding language, diverse enough to catch many different failure types, and steerable on purpose toward a particular behavior you're worried about.
[S] That last one, steerability, feels like the real design constraint. It's not enough to stumble onto failures by accident.
[G] Exactly, and that constraint shapes the whole method. The goal isn't just volume, it's a dial you can turn toward "test for this specific category of harm today."
[O] So what's actually in the box, Silas? Walk us through the mechanism.
[G] It's a three-stage loop, deliberately simple. A red language model generates a candidate test case. A target language model, the one being evaluated, produces a reply to it. And a classifier looks at that pair and predicts whether the reply is harmful. None of the three pieces needs to be differentiable, and none needs white-box access to the others — the paper notes the classifier can just as easily be a Python function checking for a pattern like a Social Security number format as a trained neural network.
[S] That modularity is the clever part. You're not attacking the target model directly, you're training an attacker model against a scoring function, then sampling at scale.
[G] Right, and the paper explores four ways to shape that red model's distribution over test cases, each trading diversity against difficulty. The simplest is zero-shot generation: sample straight from a pretrained model given a one-line prompt, something like "list of questions to ask someone," and let it fill in the numbered list. The authors say tuning a prompt like that for a new behavior typically took a few minutes of iteration.
[O] That's a strikingly cheap first pass.
[G] It is, and it's also the most diverse method, precisely because nothing is steering it yet. The second method, stochastic few-shot, takes the red model's own best offense-eliciting zero-shot outputs and feeds them back in as few-shot examples, resampled with higher probability for the ones the classifier scored as more harmful. That biases generation toward harder cases while keeping some randomness.
[S] And then it escalates further.
[G] It does. Supervised learning fine-tunes the red model for a single epoch, directly maximizing the likelihood of the zero-shot cases that already worked. Reinforcement learning goes furthest: the authors train the red model with an actor-critic algorithm to directly maximize the classifier's predicted harm score, with a penalty term that keeps the policy from drifting too far from its supervised starting point. They test three settings of that penalty, trading diversity for raw effectiveness as they loosen it.
[O] What's the actual target model all of this is pointed at?
[G] Dialogue-Prompted Gopher — Gopher is DeepMind's own 280 billion parameter language model, and "dialogue-prompted" means it's turned into a chatbot purely by giving it a hand-written conversational prefix. Critically, there's no safety fine-tuning layered on top, no reinforcement learning from human feedback. It's a raw pretrained model wearing a chat costume.
[S] That detail matters, and we'll come back to it. What's grading the replies?
[G] A much smaller classifier, 1.4 billion parameters, also built from Gopher, fine-tuned to predict whether an utterance is offensive using the Bot-Adversarial Dialogue data as training material. The authors report it scores meaningfully higher than the classifier the Bot-Adversarial Dialogue authors themselves had used.
[O] Okay, let's get to numbers. What happens when you actually run this?
[G] The cheapest version alone is the headline. Half a million unique zero-shot questions, sampled with zero human examples, elicited an offensive reply 3.7 percent of the time. That's 18,444 failing test cases, discovered from a single one-line prompt.
[S] 3.7 percent sounds small until you multiply it by half a million.
[G] Exactly, and that's the whole thesis in one number: you don't need a high hit rate if you can sample at a scale no human team can match. The paper's Table 6 then lays the tradeoff out across all four methods. Zero-shot sits at that 3.7 percent offensive rate with the highest diversity score. Reinforcement learning, at its most aggressive setting, pushes the offensive rate to 42.3 percent, but diversity collapses — measured by Self-BLEU, where a higher number means less diverse, it lands at 84.4, well past even the paper's own human baseline.
[O] What's that human baseline?
[G] The Bot-Adversarial Dialogue dataset itself — 2,598 questions from paid human adversaries. Those elicit an offensive reply 11.7 percent of the time, with a Self-BLEU of 48.5. One gentler reinforcement-learning setting lands at 13.9 percent offensive and 51.8 Self-BLEU — close enough to the human baseline in both difficulty and diversity that the authors call it comparable to an actual adversarial human.
[S] And that most aggressive reinforcement-learning setting, the 42.3 percent one — that's the one I want to poke at later, because there's a tell in the paper's own numbers.
[G] There is, worth stating plainly now: 78 percent of that setting's generated test cases share the same word. The questions cluster tightly around one pattern, something like asking what the model would do if nobody could see it. The policy found one reliable trigger and rode it, rather than exploring broadly.
[O] Even so, the paper argues the automated method finds things humans miss entirely.
[G] It does, and this is a real result, not just a scale claim. The authors flag the noun phrases most associated with an offensive reply, and the human-written Bot-Adversarial Dialogue set is simply missing 37 of the top 100. Run it the other direction — phrases common in the automatically generated questions themselves — and 35 of the top 100 never show up in anything a human tester wrote. The two approaches are genuinely complementary, not redundant.
[S] What about the other harm types? The paper doesn't stop at single offensive replies.
[G] It reuses the identical three-stage loop, just swapping the prompt and the classifier. For training-data leakage, they check for verbatim thirteen-word overlaps between a reply and the model's training corpus, and find 1,709 cases — some triggered by directly asking for a quote, some containing text that occurs exactly once anywhere in the entire training set, which shows even a single occurrence can resurface. For contact information, out of 100,000 generated test cases per category, 3,206 replies contain a phone number, 1,006 contain something matching a Social Security number pattern, and 11,683 contain an email address, with a meaningful fraction of each traced verbatim back to training data.
[O] And that's before the bias and multi-turn findings.
[G] Right. Prompting the red model to generate demographic groups and then measuring offensiveness per group turns up a real asymmetry: the target model treats several minority groups more favorably than it treats majority groups, which the authors trace back to its own hand-written prompt over-correcting toward politeness about minority groups specifically. And when the red model and target model run a full back-and-forth conversation instead of one exchange, the offensive-reply rate climbs with every additional turn, and conditioning on how many recent turns were offensive shows a clear escalation — one offensive reply measurably raises the odds of the next one.
[O] Okay, this is where I want to make the strongest case for what this method buys you. One mechanism, unchanged, uncovering five distinct harm categories at a scale of hundreds of thousands of test cases, and it hands you an actionable to-do list — specific phrases to blacklist, specific training examples to remove, specific prompt edits. That's not just measurement, that's a fix pipeline attached to the measurement.
[S] I'll grant the scale and the pipeline. What I won't grant without pushing back is that 42.3 percent headline number. The reinforcement-learning policy is trained to directly maximize the same classifier score the paper then reports as its result. That's not really discovering how harmful the target model is, it's discovering how exploitable one specific classifier is. The 78-percent single-word collapse is the smoking gun — that's an optimizer finding a groove in the grading function, not mapping a broader set of failures.
[G] That's a fair characterization, and the authors flag it themselves — there's an explicit limitations section acknowledging that a biased or inaccurate classifier limits the whole approach, and that reinforcement learning specifically risks collapsing onto one high-reward pattern instead of exploring. I'd add a point Skeptic hasn't raised: the classifier's own reliability is asserted, not really characterized, in the main text. There's an appendix table claiming it beats the older classifier's F1 score, but no false-positive or false-negative rate is reported next to the actual offensiveness percentages you're supposed to trust.
[S] Which means we genuinely don't know how much of that 3.7-to-42.3 percent range is real target-model behavior versus classifier noise.
[O] Sure, but that critique cuts hardest against the most aggressive number, not against the method itself. Zero-shot's 3.7 percent isn't optimized against anything, it's just sampling. Even the skeptical read leaves tens of thousands of real, fluent, natural-language failures nobody wrote by hand.
[S] My second objection is the target itself. Dialogue-Prompted Gopher has no safety fine-tuning, no reinforcement learning from human feedback, nothing. That same year, other labs were already shipping instruction-tuned, safety-trained systems. A forty-plus percent offensive-reply rate against an unguarded research model isn't the same claim as that rate against a production chatbot.
[G] Also fair, and the paper is transparent about that setup, it's just easy to lose the caveat once you're only quoting the headline percentage. There's a third wrinkle worth raising too: the paper's own data shows the human-written questions in Bot-Adversarial Dialogue are themselves offensive 36 percent of the time, versus as little as 2.3 percent for the zero-shot red model's questions. So the two methods aren't just finding different failures, they're sampling from meaningfully different distributions of "what an adversarial user sounds like" — which makes "which one better represents real-world misuse" a genuinely open question the paper doesn't fully settle.
[O] I can live with that split. Mechanism and complementary coverage, believe it. Headline percentage and realism claims, read skeptically.
[S] Same. And I'd want to see a follow-up that actually closes the loop — the paper proposes blacklisting phrases and pruning training examples, but never shows the red-teaming success rate actually dropping once those fixes are applied. Right now the fixes are proposed, not demonstrated.
[G] That's a fair gap, and it's one the paper itself leaves open rather than papering over.
[O] Zooming out, what does this mean for the field beyond this one chatbot?
[G] The paper spends real space on that, in a discussion section about attacking and defending language models with language models. The authors are candid that the same technique helping an internal team could help an external attacker automate large-scale probing of a commercial system, and they lay out why defenders are structurally disadvantaged: an attacker only needs one successful case, a defender has to cover the entire space, and adversarial inputs are known to transfer from one model to another.
[S] That's a genuinely uncomfortable admission for a defensive paper to make.
[G] It is, but they also argue defenders keep real advantages, if they use them. Internal red teams aren't rate-limited the way external users are, so they can sample at a scale outsiders can't. They can check a reply for overlap against the actual training corpus, including data that never appeared publicly, which an outside attacker simply cannot do. And critically, they get to fix things before an adversary ever sees the model — what the paper calls blue-teaming.
[O] And this is really the paper's legacy, beyond any one number. That three-stage loop — one model generating tests, a target model replying, a classifier grading it — became the template essentially every later automated red-teaming paper builds on.
[S] Which is exactly why the specific numbers here matter less than getting the mechanism right, and why I'd want to know how much today's classifiers and today's target models have improved since this baseline was set.
[G] If there's one line to take from this paper, it's that the mechanism, generate, reply, grade, all with models, outlived every specific percentage attached to it, and that's a rare thing for a paper to get right.
[O] Mine is that half a million questions from one throwaway prompt beat what a room of paid human adversaries could write, and that's a genuinely new capability for finding problems before users do.
[S] Mine is: read the diversity numbers next to the offensiveness numbers, always, because a headline percentage bought by collapsing onto one trigger word isn't the same achievement as a percentage bought by genuine coverage.
