[O] In May of twenty nineteen, a team out of N Y U, Facebook AI Research, DeepMind, and the University of Washington built a benchmark specifically engineered to be hard to beat. They called it SuperGLUE, sticky by design.
[S] And it got un-stuck in about a year and a half. That's the whole story right there: the benchmark meant to hold the line got closed to within a point of human performance before most of the field had even finished migrating off the one it replaced.
[O] Or, read it the other way: a benchmark that gets solved by genuinely better science isn't a failure, that's the system working exactly as intended.
[S] We'll see if that survives contact with the numbers.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio. Today we're digging into SuperGLUE, A Stickier Benchmark for General-Purpose Language Understanding Systems, by Alex Wang and colleagues, out of N Y U, Facebook AI Research, DeepMind, and the University of Washington, presented at NeurIPS in 2019.
[S] Joining us is Eleanor, who's spent a lot of time with this paper and the whole GLUE-to-SuperGLUE lineage. Eleanor, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is one of those papers that's more interesting for what happened to it after publication than for what it says on the page, so I'm glad we're doing it.
[O] That's exactly why it's on the docket. It reads as a benchmark paper, but the real story underneath is a stress test of what benchmarks even are.
[S] So set the stage. What was GLUE, and why did it need replacing after, what, about a year?
[G] GLUE launched in twenty eighteen: nine English language-understanding tasks, all built on existing public datasets, folded into one macro-averaged leaderboard number, with the test labels held privately by the organizers. The idea was that a model sharing knowledge across all nine tasks should beat one that just overfits each task separately. For about a year, that's exactly what happened, and GLUE became the de facto scoreboard for the whole pretraining wave.
[O] And that wave moved fast. The strongest baseline with no pretraining scored sixty-three point seven. An ELMo-based model reached sixty-six point five. OpenAI's GPT hit seventy-two point eight. BERT jumped to eighty point two.
[G] And by early July twenty nineteen, an XLNet ensemble reached eighty-eight point four, which beat the non-expert human estimate of eighty-seven point one by one point three points, and actually exceeded the human number on four of the nine individual tasks.
[S] So the thermometer hit the top of the glass.
[G] That's their framing too. Once you're above the human line, the remaining gap is too small to distinguish models, even though the paper is careful to say the high-level goal, general-purpose language understanding, plainly hadn't been achieved.
[O] And they had proof of that sitting right inside GLUE itself, didn't they? The diagnostic set.
[G] Yes. GLUE shipped a hand-built diagnostic testing specific linguistic phenomena, entailment scored with a metric called R three. On that diagnostic, the best models scored point four two, against an average human score of point eight oh, near or below chance on things like disjunction and downward monotonicity, phenomena that need real logical structure rather than surface pattern matching.
[S] So models were acing the main leaderboard while flunking a targeted probe of whether they actually understood language.
[G] That's close to the authors' own conclusion. GLUE was no longer a suitable metric for quantifying progress. So they built a harder one.
[O] Walk us through what actually changed. Eleanor, take us into the design.
[G] They kept GLUE's basic shape: public leaderboard, fixed set of tasks, single macro-average, held-out test labels, an evaluation server. But they push on five axes. Harder tasks. More diverse formats, adding question answering and coreference resolution beyond GLUE's sentence and sentence-pair classification. Comprehensive human baselines on every task, not just the diagnostic. An upgraded toolkit called jiant. And tighter usage rules to slow overfitting to the private test set.
[S] How do you pick eight tasks in a principled way instead of just grabbing whatever looks hard?
[G] They ran an open call for proposals to the NLP community and got about thirty submissions. Then they filtered against explicit criteria: a task has to be solvable by a college-educated English speaker with no domain expertise, it needs an automatic metric that actually tracks human judgment, which rules out most generation tasks where BLEU and ROUGE correlate poorly with quality, it needs public training data, a simple input-output format, and a permissive license.
[O] And then the real filter.
[G] Right, this is the clever part. For every surviving candidate, they ran both a BERT baseline and a human baseline, and kept only the tasks that were hard for BERT but comfortable for humans. Headroom became the literal inclusion criterion, not a guess.
[S] Eleanor, isn't that a bit circular, though? You're defining difficulty as whatever beats today's BERT, not difficulty in any absolute sense.
[G] It's a fair pushback, and the paper doesn't claim otherwise. It's explicitly calibrated to a 2019 baseline. That's the price of an empirical filter instead of an intuitive one.
[O] So what survived?
[G] Eight tasks. BoolQ, yes-no questions over a Wikipedia passage, drawn from real anonymized Google search queries. CB, the CommitmentBank, three-way entailment on how committed a writer is to an embedded clause, with only around two hundred fifty training examples. COPA, causal reasoning, pick the more plausible cause or effect of a premise. MultiRC, multi-sentence reading comprehension where a question can have several correct answers, each scored independently. ReCoRD, cloze-style commonsense question answering over CNN and Daily Mail news. RTE, textual entailment carried over from GLUE. WiC, word sense disambiguation cast as binary classification. And WSC, the Winograd Schema Challenge, also recast as binary classification.
[O] That's a genuinely different shape than GLUE. Real question answering, real coreference.
[G] And there's a nice fix buried in WSC. GLUE's version, WNLI, had an adversarial train-dev split, where a premise sentence could reappear in the dev set with a different hypothesis and a flipped label. A model that memorized training data could score below chance. The SuperGLUE authors just removed that trap, no shared sentences across splits.
[S] How small are these datasets, really? You said CB has two hundred fifty training examples.
[G] Nearly half the eight tasks have fewer than a thousand training examples, and all but one have fewer than ten thousand. ReCoRD is the outlier with over a hundred thousand. That's a deliberate low-data design, meant to reward sample-efficient transfer rather than brute-force fitting.
[O] And scoring is still one number?
[G] One number, plus two diagnostics riding alongside that don't count toward it. Each task's metrics get averaged into a task score first, accuracy and F1 for CB, F1 over all answer options and exact match for MultiRC, then the eight task scores are averaged equally, regardless of how many test examples each task actually has. The two side diagnostics are A X b, the broad-coverage linguistic diagnostic carried over from GLUE, scored with Matthews correlation, and A X g, a Winogender bias diagnostic pairing accuracy with a gender parity score.
[S] And the human numbers, how were those actually collected? That ends up mattering a lot later.
[G] Mechanical Turk crowdworkers, paid twenty-three dollars and seventy-five cents an hour. Two steps: a training phase where workers annotate up to thirty examples and check themselves against ground truth, then an annotation phase where five workers each label a sample of one hundred test examples, majority vote decides the label. WSC was small enough that they annotated the full test set instead of a sample.
[O] So it's a real methodology, not a back-of-envelope guess.
[G] It is. And the paper is unusually transparent about what didn't make the cut. They tested candidates like Quora Insincere Questions, PAWS, and a gendered pronoun coreference task called GAP, and dropped several of them specifically because BERT already matched or beat the human baseline. On PAWS-Wiki, for instance, BERT hit ninety-one point nine percent accuracy against an eighty-four percent human baseline, so out it went.
[S] So the benchmark isn't just difficulty-screened, it's survivor-biased toward whatever still beat BERT in spring twenty nineteen specifically.
[G] That's a fair read, and it's exactly the tension we should get into.
[O] Let's get to numbers first. What did the baselines actually score?
[G] Most-frequent-class baseline scores forty-seven point one. A bag-of-words GloVe average, forty-four point three, both near chance on several tasks, which confirms the tasks aren't gameable by priors alone. Fine-tuning BERT-large separately per task lifts the average by about twenty-five points to sixty-nine. Then BERT-plus-plus, which adds STILTs-style intermediate fine-tuning, MultiNLI before BoolQ, CB, and RTE, SWAG before COPA, pushes it to seventy-one point five.
[S] And human performance?
[G] Eighty-nine point eight. BERT-plus-plus against that estimate leaves a gap of eighteen point three points, which the paper itself rounds to nearly twenty.
[O] That's a real gap. That's not benchmark theater.
[G] It's real, but wildly uneven across tasks. The worst is WSC, a thirty-five point seven point chasm, sixty-four point three against a perfect one hundred. BERT actually scores worse there than the trivial most-frequent baseline, which sits at sixty-five point one. The authors attribute that to the dataset's small size and no data augmentation. COPA is next worst, a twenty-six point two point gap against another perfect human score. The smallest gaps, around ten points each, land on BoolQ, CB, RTE, and WiC.
[S] What about the diagnostics, Eleanor, the parts that actually probe understanding rather than task performance?
[G] That's where it gets uncomfortable for the headline number. On the broad-coverage linguistic diagnostic, BERT-plus-plus scores thirty-eight Matthews correlation against a human estimate of seventy-seven. And on the Winogender bias diagnostic, the baselines post near-perfect gender parity scores, but that's an artifact, not a strength, because they're achieving parity by predicting near chance across the board. The paper itself says a high parity score is meaningless without accuracy behind it.
[O] So the diagnostics are basically screaming that nothing about core competence has actually been demonstrated, even while the main leaderboard shows twenty points of visible headroom.
[G] That's the honest read of their own numbers. And the authors expected that eighteen-point gap to be durable. Human performance is perfect on WSC and COPA, and in the mid-to-high nineties on three more tasks. Their own words were that closing it should, quote, require substantive innovations in sample-efficient, transfer, multi-task, and self-supervised learning.
[O] Okay, this is the part I want to defend. This was not a lazy benchmark. They ran real human baselines on every task, they screened for headroom empirically instead of by intuition, and when the gap did close, it closed because of exactly the kind of substantive innovation they predicted would be needed: scale, self-supervised pretraining, and transfer learning all maturing together. That's the benchmark working. It measured something real, and the field cleared the bar the hard way.
[S] I don't buy the clean version of that story. SuperGLUE launched with BERT-plus-plus at seventy-one point five. Within about a year and a half, T5 at eleven billion parameters reached eighty-eight point nine, inside about a point of the human estimate, and models passed human performance outright soon after. A benchmark explicitly named "stickier" came unstuck about as fast as the one it replaced. That's not a one-off, that's the same structural failure twice in a row: a fixed task suite with a fixed human ceiling measures this year's models, not the task's actual difficulty.
[O] But the headroom was verified empirically, against real humans doing the task. That's not nothing.
[S] Which humans, though? Non-expert Mechanical Turk workers, a hundred sampled per test set, majority vote of five. That's a reasonable crowd estimate, but it's a floor, not an expert ceiling. When later models "surpassed humans," they surpassed that specific floor. Bowman's own group made roughly this point afterward, that the superhuman framing partly reflects a weak reference, not necessarily a solved task.
[G] That's fair, and it's grounded in what actually happened afterward, though I'd flag it goes slightly beyond what this specific paper claims. What the paper itself says is narrower: substantive innovation would be needed to close the gap. And technically, that held. T5 is exactly a self-supervised, transfer-learning advance, the category they named. They just badly underestimated how fast scale would deliver it.
[O] Eleanor, hold on, isn't that a little generous, though? The paper specifically expected the WSC and COPA gaps to be relatively difficult to close, because human performance there is perfect. If that specific prediction failed within about a year, doesn't that undercut the headroom argument, not just the timeline?
[G] That's a sharper version of the same point, and it's fair. The overall claim, that substantive innovation would be needed, held up. But the specific confidence about which gaps would be hardest, WSC and COPA, that part didn't survive contact with scale.
[S] There's a second problem the paper doesn't address at all: contamination. Every task's text comes from sources heavily represented on the open web, Wikipedia for BoolQ, CNN and Daily Mail for ReCoRD, PASCAL RTE text for RTE. As pretraining corpora grew to web scale, that source text plausibly leaked into pretraining with zero auditing. SuperGLUE predates contamination auditing as a discipline, so every post-2019 leaderboard gain is confounded with an unmeasured amount of leakage.
[O] That's a real gap, I'll grant it, there's genuinely no contamination analysis anywhere in this paper.
[G] Also true, and worth stating precisely: it's not that the paper got this wrong, it's that the field hadn't developed the tooling yet. That's a fair critique of the era more than a flaw unique to this paper.
[S] One more, and this is a pure statistics complaint. The headline is one equal-weighted average across tasks ranging from CB's two hundred fifty test examples and WSC's one hundred forty-six, up to ReCoRD's ten thousand, with no confidence intervals anywhere. A one or two point move on that average could just be noise on the small tasks.
[G] That one's hard to argue against, because the paper simply doesn't report variance anywhere. It's a real methodological gap, not a matter of interpretation.
[O] So where does that leave the scorecard? I still think this counts as a genuine, well-designed instrument that correctly diagnosed a real problem, ran the right experiment to fix it, and then measured real progress, even if that progress arrived faster than expected.
[S] And I still think "faster than expected" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. If your sticky benchmark comes unstuck in about eighteen months, twice in a row, with the same design flaw both times, that's not bad luck. That's the design.
[G] Split the difference honestly. The task-selection methodology was genuinely rigorous, more rigorous than most benchmarks of its era, and it did surface a real, verified gap. But the paper inherited GLUE's one structural weakness, a fixed suite plus a fixed non-expert human ceiling, and that weakness is exactly what a decade of scaling was always going to exploit. Both things are true at once.
[O] So what's the actual lasting contribution, if the leaderboard itself didn't last?
[G] I'd call it negative knowledge. SuperGLUE is the experiment that convinced a lot of the field that static, single-number NLU leaderboards can't keep pace with scaling. You can trace the aftermath directly: Dynabench tried living, adversarially-updated test sets. MMLU, BIG-bench, and HELM pushed toward broader, harder-to-saturate frontier evaluation. And a lot of current evaluation has shifted toward LLM-judge and arena-style comparison specifically because fixed reference answers keep getting outrun.
[S] For anyone building an eval today, the practical lesson is concrete: don't treat a fixed human ceiling as a stopping point, budget for contamination audits from day one instead of retrofitting them years later, and report per-task variance so a few points of leaderboard movement isn't mistaken for a real capability jump.
[O] And don't let the headline number bury the diagnostic. A model can look nearly done on the average while still failing the part that was actually measuring understanding. The gap between A X b at thirty-eight and human at seventy-seven never closed, even after the main leaderboard did.
[G] If there's one line to take from the paper itself, it's this: SuperGLUE correctly diagnosed that GLUE was dead and built a genuinely sharper instrument, but it inherited GLUE's fatal feature, and paid for it on almost the same timeline.
[O] My takeaway is that the difficulty-screening methodology here was real research craft, and the fact that it got beaten fast is a story about the pace of the field, not a failure of the benchmark's design process.
[S] Mine is blunter: if your benchmark's core claim rests on a human ceiling that turns out to be a floor, on a static test set with no contamination controls, expect it to get solved faster than you think, no matter how carefully you picked the tasks.
