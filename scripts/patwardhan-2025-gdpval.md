[G] Glad to be here. GDPval is OpenAI's attempt to answer a very specific question, not "can a model reason," but "can a model produce the actual deliverable a professional gets paid to produce."
[S] Percival, start with the gap. Why did they think we needed yet another benchmark?
[G] The argument is about timing. The usual way people measure AI's economic impact is lagging, adoption rates, usage logs, GDP growth attributed to AI. The authors point to history, electricity, the airplane, the computer's productivity paradox, and note the gap between an invention existing and an economy absorbing it can run years or decades.
[O] So by the time the macro numbers move, the capability shift that caused them already happened a long time ago.
[G] That's the claim, yes. Their fix is to skip the lagging indicators and measure capability directly, right now, how good is a model, today, at the work people are actually paid to do.
[S] Which sounds reasonable until you ask what "the work people are paid to do" even means as a benchmark. What existed before this?
[G] Mostly two families. Academic reasoning tests, Humanity's Last Exam, MMLU, GPQA, built for difficulty with a single checkable answer. And narrow, realistic ones, like the SWE-bench line and SWE-Lancer, which live entirely inside software engineering.
[O] Neither one looks anything like a financial analyst building a model in Excel, or a nurse writing a care plan, or a mechanical engineer marking up a CAD file.
[G] Right, and that's the specific hole GDPval is built to fill, multi-file, multi-modal deliverables where structure, formatting, and relevance matter as much as being technically correct, and where there's no single answer key at all.
[S] Walk me through how they actually picked what counts as "the economy." That selection process is doing a lot of work.
[G] Two steps. First, the nine sectors that each contribute over five percent of U.S. GDP, finance and insurance, health care, manufacturing, professional and technical services, real estate, government, among others. Second, within each sector, the five occupations earning the most total wages that are, in their term, predominantly digital.
[O] How do you even decide if an occupation is "digital"?
[G] They pulled every task an occupation performs from O*NET, the Department of Labor's occupational database, and had GPT-4o classify each one as digital or not, weighted by O*NET's own relevance and frequency scores. An occupation qualified if at least sixty percent of its task content came back digital, and that measure was cross-checked against an established economics framework, the Acemoglu-Autor task-content model, for validity.
[S] So a language model did the initial occupational classification for a benchmark that then evaluates language models.
[G] Fair to flag. The forty-four occupations that came out of this collectively earn about three trillion dollars a year in wages.
[O] And the tasks themselves, who's actually writing these?
[G] Recruited industry professionals, minimum four years of experience, a demonstrated history of promotion or management, plus a video interview, a background check, training, and a quiz. The average expert had fourteen years of experience, with prior employers including Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, Boeing, and Massachusetts General Hospital.
[S] That's a serious bar for "here's a task."
[G] It has to be, each task is a real pair, a request, often with reference files, and a deliverable, an actual work product, averaging seven hours of expert effort, some running to multiple weeks, and priced by completion time times the occupation's median wage.
[O] What kind of deliverables are we talking about?
[G] Genuinely broad. A manufacturing engineer's CAD assembly, a financial analyst's report, a nurse's care documentation, a film editor's cut, a customer service reply. Up to seventeen reference files per task in the open subset, thirty-eight in the full set.
[S] And quality control on that? Anyone can call their spreadsheet representative.
[G] Every task in the full set went through an iterative pipeline, automated model screening plus human expert review, an average of five reviews per task, minimum three, with tasks revised between rounds. Two cuts ship, the held-out full set, and an open-sourced gold subset of two hundred twenty tasks, five per occupation, scrubbed of anything that could identify the expert who wrote it.
[O] Now the part I find genuinely clever, how do you even grade something like a CAD file or a slide deck against a rubric?
[G] You don't use a rubric at all, that's the design choice. There's no answer key. The primary metric is a blinded human pairwise comparison, an occupational expert sees the request and reference files, then ranks unlabeled deliverables, one from the model, one the human's own gold-standard version, judging both correctness and subjective quality, structure, formatting, aesthetics.
[S] So the metric is just, how often does the model's answer get picked over the human's.
[G] Exactly, that's the win rate, the fraction of comparisons where the model deliverable is preferred. Each model was sampled three times per prompt and each sample graded by three different experts, nine comparisons per prompt per model across all two hundred twenty gold tasks. Grading a single comparison took, on average, over an hour.
[O] That's expensive.
[G] Very, which is why they also trained an automated grader, offered publicly through OpenAI's evals service. It reaches sixty-five point seven percent agreement with human graders, about five points below the seventy point eight percent humans reach with each other. The authors call it a proxy, not a substitute.
[O] Let's get to the scoreboard, because it's a good one for the optimist case. Who won?
[G] On the gold subset, Claude Opus four point one was the strongest model, forty-seven point six percent of its deliverables graded as a win or a tie against the human's own work, just short of the fifty percent parity line. GPT-5 high followed at thirty-eight point eight percent, then o3 high, o4-mini high, Gemini 2.5 Pro, and Grok 4, with GPT-4o trailing at twelve point four.
[S] Nearly a four-times spread from bottom to top. That's a real capability gradient, whatever else is true about the measurement.
[G] It is, and the two leaders split cleanly. Claude was stronger on aesthetics, document formatting, slide layout, output files like PDF and PowerPoint. GPT-5 was stronger on accuracy, following instructions precisely, getting calculations right.
[O] And OpenAI's own model lineage over time, that's the trend line I opened with.
[G] Plotting their models by release date, GPT-4o to o3 to GPT-5, performance climbs roughly linearly toward the parity line. The authors don't over-extrapolate that slope, but it's clearly the paper's quietly load-bearing claim.
[S] Why do models lose the ones they lose? That tells you more than the headline number.
[G] They built a clustering pipeline over the experts' written justifications. Grok 4 and Gemini 2.5 Pro lost overwhelmingly to instruction-following failures, promising a deliverable and not providing it, ignoring reference data, wrong file format. GPT-5's profile is different, its biggest issue is formatting, with the fewest instruction-following failures of any model. Accuracy errors, hallucinated data, wrong calculations, were the smallest bucket across the board.
[O] Which is the good news buried in the bad news, most of these losses are mundane, not deep reasoning failures.
[S] And mundane failures should be cheap to fix. Did they test that?
[G] They did. Pushing GPT-5's reasoning effort from low to medium to high moved the win-plus-tie rate from thirty-two point seven up to thirty-eight point eight percent, and o3 tracked the same shape. A formatting-and-thoroughness prompt, render files as images and inspect them, use LibreOffice for PDFs, plus best-of-four sampling with a GPT-5 judge, lifted GPT-5 another five points to forty-three point one percent. It eliminated black-square artifacts that had hit over half of generated PDFs, and cut egregious PowerPoint errors from eighty-six percent to sixty-four.
[O] That's a five-point jump from prompting alone, no retraining. That's the kind of headroom that gets closed fast.
[S] Or it's evidence the baseline numbers were being dragged down by fixable sloppiness rather than a real capability ceiling, which cuts both ways when you're trying to read a single headline percentage.
[O] Now the economics, because faster and cheaper is the number everyone will actually repeat.
[G] There's a naive version of that number and a real one. Naively, a model produces a deliverable something like ninety times faster than the seven-hour human baseline, and over five thousand times cheaper, since API costs are so low relative to a salaried hour. But that ignores quality entirely.
[S] Which is the number I'd actually trust, what happens once a human has to check the work.
[G] Under a realistic try-the-model-then-fix-it-yourself workflow, the human baseline averages four hundred four minutes and three hundred sixty-one dollars per task, and reviewing a model's output adds another hundred nine minutes and eighty-six dollars. GPT-4o comes in at zero point four six, worse than not using a model at all, because its quality is low enough that you mostly redo the work.

[G] Yes, an under-contextualized version with prompts cut to about forty-two percent of their original length. GPT-5's performance dropped visibly. So the headline numbers are best read as an upper bound that assumes the ambiguous part of the job is already solved for the model.

A deliverable that matches a human's on a clean, bounded task carries no accountability, no client relationship, no stakes if it's wrong. Percival, what does the severity breakdown show?
