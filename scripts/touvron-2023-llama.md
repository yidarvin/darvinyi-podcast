[O] And walking us through it is Freya, a researcher who's spent real time with this paper. Freya, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. It's a fun one to revisit, because so much of what's now the default open-model recipe — the architecture, the instinct to train small models much longer — traces straight back to this single paper.
[S] Let's set the stage first. What was actually broken that this paper was responding to?
[G] By early twenty twenty-three, the recipe for a frontier model was a closed one. GPT-3, Gopher, Chinchilla, PaLM — all trained on data that was proprietary, undocumented, or both. Some of the source papers describe their mixes as things like books, two terabytes, or social media conversations, with no way for an outside researcher to reproduce or even inspect what actually went in.
[O] So you could read about these models but never really study them.
[G] Right, and that's how the authors frame it — academic study of large models had become spectating. You could read the paper, but you couldn't run, probe, or fine-tune the actual artifact.
[S] There's also a scaling-laws wrinkle underneath this, isn't there? It's not purely a data-licensing complaint.
[G] It's the sharper idea in the paper, yes. The dominant scaling result at the time was Chinchilla, from Hoffmann and colleagues in twenty twenty-two. Chinchilla asks: for a fixed training compute budget, what's the best split between model size and token count? Its answer was that most existing frontier models were oversized and undertrained.
[O] Which sounds like good news on its face — train smaller, train smarter.
[G] It is good news, but the authors point out that objective quietly ignores something that matters enormously in production: inference cost. Once a model is deployed, you pay for its parameter count on every single token of every request, forever. Training is a one-time bill. Serving is the bill that never stops.
[S] So LLaMA reframes the actual target.
[G] That's the whole thesis in one line. Not the model that's cheapest to train to a given quality, but the one that's cheapest to serve at that quality. And that points the opposite direction from Chinchilla — toward smaller models trained on far more tokens than a compute-optimal recipe would ever recommend.
[O] Let's get concrete, then. What did they actually build?
[G] Four dense decoder-only transformers — seven billion, thirteen billion, thirty-three billion, and sixty-five billion parameters. The two smaller ones each see one trillion tokens; the two larger ones see one point four trillion. And that token count really is the point of the whole paper — a Chinchilla-style recipe would have stopped the seven billion model much earlier, but the training loss curves hadn't flattened even at a trillion tokens, so they just kept going.
[S] Walk me through the actual inference-cost argument. Why does pushing tokens up win?
[G] It's simple once you see it. Training compute scales with both model size and token count, roughly six times the parameter count times the token count. But inference cost per token scales with model size alone. So if you fix a quality target, you want to push tokens up and parameters down, even if that means a bigger training bill, because you're trading a one-time cost for a recurring one.
[O] And the training mix — since public-data-only is the other half of the pitch?
[G] Entirely reproducible from open sources. CommonCrawl is sixty-seven percent of it, C4 another fifteen, GitHub four and a half percent — Apache, BSD, and MIT licensed code only — Wikipedia four and a half percent across twenty languages, books from Project Gutenberg and the Books3 slice of the Pile at four and a half percent, arXiv two and a half percent, and Stack Exchange two percent. Tokenized with byte-pair encoding, falling back to raw bytes for anything unrecognized.
[S] That arXiv-plus-books slice sounds thin for a model that's supposed to carry a lot of knowledge.
[G] It is thin, and that's exactly what comes back to bite them later in the results. ArXiv, Gutenberg, and Books3 together sum to only one hundred seventy-seven gigabytes, against models like Chinchilla and PaLM trained on up to two terabytes of books.
[O] Is the architecture itself the recipe everyone still copies today?
[G] Pretty much. Three changes from the original transformer, all borrowed from work published after GPT-3. Pre-normalization with RMSNorm, so each sub-layer normalizes its input rather than its output, for training stability. SwiGLU in place of ReLU, borrowed from PaLM. And rotary positional embeddings, RoPE, replacing absolute position embeddings at every layer. None of these three ideas originate in this paper — assembling all three into one open, documented recipe is what made it the template everyone copied afterward.
[S] Anything unusual in the actual training mechanics, or is that mostly standard?
[G] The optimization itself is standard AdamW, a cosine schedule decaying to ten percent of peak learning rate, weight decay of point one, gradient clipping at one, four-million-token batches. What's genuinely engineered is the efficiency layer underneath it — a memory-efficient causal attention implementation from the xformers library that never materializes the full attention matrix, manually written backward passes for activation checkpointing instead of relying on autograd, and model-and-sequence parallelism to overlap compute with GPU communication.
[O] Does that efficiency work show up as an actual number, or is it just described?
[G] It shows up concretely. Training the sixty-five billion model, their code processes about three hundred eighty tokens per second per GPU on two thousand forty-eight A100 eighty-gigabyte GPUs, so the full one point four trillion token run finishes in roughly twenty-one days.
[S] That's an unusually reproducible-sounding number for a frontier lab paper.
[G] It is, and they extend the same transparency to carbon cost. They estimate about two thousand six hundred thirty-eight megawatt-hours to develop the full model family across roughly five months on those same GPUs, working out to around one thousand fifteen tons of CO2 equivalent under a US-average grid assumption — and they argue explicitly that releasing the weights means the rest of the community never has to pay that training cost again.
[S] Let's get to the numbers, then. What's the actual headline?
[G] Two headlines, really. LLaMA thirteen billion outperforms GPT-3, at one hundred seventy-five billion parameters, on most benchmarks, while running on a single GPU. And LLaMA sixty-five billion is competitive with Chinchilla seventy billion and PaLM five hundred forty billion across a twenty-benchmark suite of zero- and few-shot tasks.
[O] Give me specifics. Where does it actually win outright?
[G] On eight common-sense reasoning benchmarks, zero-shot, the sixty-five billion model beats Chinchilla on every single one except BoolQ, and beats PaLM five-forty on every one except BoolQ and WinoGrande. On closed-book NaturalQuestions, exact match, it scores twenty-three point eight zero-shot — ahead of GPT-3 at fourteen point six, Chinchilla at sixteen point six, and PaLM five-forty at twenty-one point two — climbing to thirty-nine point nine at sixty-four-shot.
[S] What about the harder stuff — math and code?
[G] On GSM8k, the sixty-five billion model reaches fifty point nine, or sixty-nine point seven with majority voting over a hundred samples, and it edges out Minerva sixty-two billion despite having zero fine-tuning on math data — Minerva, by contrast, was specifically fine-tuned on math text. On code, HumanEval and MBPP pass-at-one, the thirteen billion model already beats LaMDA at one hundred thirty-seven billion parameters, and the sixty-five billion model clears PaLM sixty-two billion.
[O] So basically a clean sweep across the board.
[S] Not quite. There's one place it loses, and it's not a small one.
[G] MMLU. The sixty-five billion model averages sixty-three point four, five-shot, trailing Chinchilla at sixty-seven point five and PaLM five-forty at sixty-nine point three by a few points, across most domains. And the authors' own diagnosis is the data mix we just talked about — that thin one hundred seventy-seven gigabytes of books and academic text.
[S] And they more or less admit the base model isn't finished, don't they?
[G] They do, implicitly. They ran one instruction-tuning experiment, following the same protocol as Chung and colleagues' earlier instruction-tuning work, calling the result LLaMA-I, and MMLU jumps from sixty-three point four to sixty-eight point nine with just that light touch. That's a strong signal the released base weights are a starting point, not the finished product.
[O] And on the safety side — bias, toxicity, truthfulness?
[G] Mixed, and reported honestly. On TruthfulQA, the sixty-five billion model scores point five seven truthful and point five three truthful-and-informative — well above GPT-3's point two eight and point two five — but the rate is still low enough that the authors flag their own model as likely to hallucinate incorrect answers. Toxicity on RealToxicityPrompts actually rises with model size, and CrowS-Pairs bias comes in at sixty-six point six, marginally below GPT-3's sixty-seven point two and OPT's sixty-nine point five, but with a notably elevated score specifically in the religion category.
