Joining us is Freya, who has spent real time with this paper.
[G] Glad to be here. Quick housekeeping note: I wasn't involved in this work, so when I say "the authors" or "they show," that's Shao and the Stanford team, never me.
[S] Freya, one-sentence version. What is STORM, actually?
[G] STORM stands for Synthesis of Topic Outlines through Retrieval and Multi-perspective question asking. In one line, it's a system that researches a topic before writing about it, simulating interviews between several question-asking personas and a search-grounded expert, then turning what it learns into an outline and a full article with citations.
[O] Start with the gap. Why isn't this just, ask an L-L-M to write a Wikipedia article and be done with it?
[G] Because writing theorists call what happens before you draft a sentence the pre-writing stage, and prior Wikipedia-generation work quietly skips it. WikiSum treats the task as summarizing documents already handed to it. A separate line of work by Fan and Gardent assumes the outline already exists and just expands each section. Neither assumption holds in the real setting, where finding trustworthy sources and building an outline is exactly the expensive, expert-level work.
[S] So every existing benchmark quietly assumed away the hardest part.
[G] That's the authors' own framing, yes. Their comparison table shows STORM's setup is the only one that's simultaneously open-domain, full-article length, and given neither an outline nor references up front.
[O] And the two obvious shortcuts, direct generation and plain retrieval-augmented generation, both fail for a related reason?
[G] Direct generation from the model's own parametric knowledge hallucinates, especially on long-tail topics it hasn't seen much of. Plain retrieval isn't much better, because it searches on the bare topic string, and most of what a good article needs can't be surfaced that way, it takes follow-up questions a layperson wouldn't think to ask. And if you just prompt a model to "ask thirty questions about this topic," it produces shallow what, when, and where questions that skim the surface.
[S] So what's STORM's actual bet?
[G] Two hypotheses. First, diverse perspectives produce diverse questions. The paper's own example: an event planner researching the twenty twenty-two Winter Olympics opening ceremony asks about transportation and budget, where a layperson just asks who's attending. Second, in-depth questions require iterative research, meaning a good follow-up question only occurs to you once you've actually read the answer to the previous one.
[O] Walk us through the actual pipeline, Freya.
[G] Four moves. First, discover perspectives: STORM prompts the model to name related Wikipedia pages, pulls their tables of contents through the Wikipedia API, and uses that scaffold to identify several distinct editorial perspectives that could collectively cover the topic. A fixed "basic fact writer" perspective is always added too, so the plain facts never get lost among the specialists.
[S] And each perspective becomes its own little agent?
[G] Right. Each becomes a Wikipedia-writer persona interviewing a topic expert. The writer asks one question per turn, conditioned on the topic, its perspective, and everything said so far, so it can ask genuine follow-ups. The expert doesn't just hallucinate an answer, it breaks the question into search queries, filters results with a rule-based check built on Wikipedia's reliable-sources guidelines, and only then synthesizes a grounded answer. Every source it touches gets added to a shared reference set, across up to five rounds per perspective, in parallel.
[O] So the follow-up questions really do come from having just read the previous answer.
[G] Exactly, that's the second hypothesis playing out directly in the transcripts the paper shows.
[S] What happens after all those interviews, straight to writing?
[G] Not quite. Third, curate the outline: the model first drafts an outline from its own parametric knowledge alone, which the authors find already gives good high-level structure, then it refines that draft using everything gathered across the conversations. Fourth, write with citations: for each heading, relevant documents are pulled from the reference set by Sentence-BERT similarity, the section is drafted with citations, a pass deletes information repeated across sections, and a final lead paragraph is synthesized in Wikipedia's own house style.
[O] What's actually running underneath all this, which models?
[G] It's all zero-shot prompting in the DSPy framework, no per-domain prompt engineering. Both the number of perspectives and conversation rounds are set to five. Question-asking uses GPT-3.5-turbo, while outline refinement and the final article use GPT-4, because the authors found GPT-3.5 wasn't faithful to its sources when generating cited text. The expert is grounded through a commercial search engine, You dot com, with the ground-truth Wikipedia page excluded from every search result.
[S] That's a lot of machinery, times six perspectives, times up to five rounds each. Not free.
[G] Fair, and the paper genuinely doesn't report cost or latency anywhere, that's a real gap. What it does report is exactly what all that research buys, and that's worth getting into properly.
[O] Before the results, how did they even grade something like this?
[G] They built a new dataset called FreshWiki: the top one hundred most-edited Wikipedia pages per month, from February twenty twenty-two through September twenty twenty-three, filtered to B-class quality or above, specifically to dodge pre-training leakage. List articles and pages with no subsections are excluded. The average article runs eight point four sections, fifteen point eight headings, roughly two thousand one hundred fifty-nine words, and ninety point one references.
[S] Ninety references for a single article, that's the actual sourcing bar they're chasing.
[G] It is. And to grade an outline against that without demanding exact heading matches, they define heading soft recall, matching headings by Sentence-BERT embedding similarity so "Aftermath" and "Consequences" count as a near-hit, plus heading entity recall, the share of named entities in the human headings that the generated outline covers.
[O] Give us the headline outline numbers.
[G] With GPT-4 as the outline model, STORM reaches ninety-two point seven three heading soft recall and forty-five point nine one entity recall. The best baseline, an extended retrieval approach called RAG-expand, reaches ninety-one point three six and forty-three point five three. Plain direct generation sits at eighty-seven point six six and thirty-four point seven eight.
[S] The soft-recall margin over RAG-expand is thin, about a point and a half. The entity-recall gap is more real.
[G] That's a fair read of that one row. But switch to the weaker GPT-3.5 as the outline model and the gap widens sharply: STORM reaches eighty-six point two six against seventy-three point five nine for plain retrieval-augmented generation. Plain retrieval actually hurts the weaker model relative to direct generation, because dumping unorganized retrieved text into its context window makes outlining harder, not easier.
[O] So the interviewing process matters most exactly when the underlying model is weaker at synthesizing on its own.
[G] That's the authors' own reading too, and the ablations back it. Removing the conversation simulation drops GPT-3.5 soft recall from eighty-six point two six down to seventy-seven point nine seven. Removing perspectives entirely drops it to eighty-four point four nine. And the clearest signal is in sourcing itself: the full pipeline gathers ninety-nine point eight three unique references on average, against thirty-nine point five six without the conversation step and fifty-four point three six without perspectives.
[S] So perspective-guided interviewing is roughly doubling to tripling how many sources actually get surfaced.
[G] Right, and that number is the mechanism the rest of the paper's article-level gains trace back to.
[O] What about the finished articles, not just outlines?
[G] STORM beats the strongest baseline, an outline-driven retrieval system the paper calls oRAG, on ROUGE-1, forty-five point eight two against forty-four point two six, on entity recall, fourteen point one oh against twelve point five seven, and on rubric scores for Coverage at four point eight eight, Relevance at four point four five, and Interest at three point nine nine, all statistically significant. Take away the outline stage entirely and ROUGE-1 collapses from forty-five point eight two down to twenty-six point seven seven, even with all the same gathered conversations still available. Outline-then-write is doing real structural work, not decoration.
[S] And citations, is any of it actually attributed correctly?
[G] A separate check, using a Mistral seven-billion-parameter model to judge whether a cited passage actually entails the sentence it's attached to, finds eighty-four point eight three percent citation recall and eighty-five point one eight percent precision.
[O] That leaves about fifteen percent of sentences unsupported. What's actually going wrong there?
[G] We'll get into exactly why in a minute, it's one of the more interesting findings in the whole paper. But first, what did actual Wikipedia editors say, not just automatic metrics?
[S] Right, that's the part I trust more anyway.
[G] Ten experienced editors, each with at least five hundred edits and a year of experience, rated twenty article pairs, STORM against oRAG, on a one-to-seven scale. STORM wins decisively on Organization, four point zero zero against three point two five, with a p-value of point oh oh five, meaning twenty-five percent more of STORM's articles were judged well-organized. Preferred overall, twenty-six votes to fourteen.
[O] That's the single strongest number in the whole paper for me.
[S] What about interest and coverage?
[G] STORM leads on both, Interest at four point zero three against three point six three, Coverage at four point zero zero against three point five eight, ten percent more articles judged good coverage, but both fall just short of the conventional significance cutoff, at p-values of point oh seven seven and point oh eight four.
[O] And the closing survey?
[G] Every single editor agreed STORM would help their own pre-writing stage. Eighty percent said it would help them edit an article on an unfamiliar topic. Seventy percent thought it could be useful to the Wikipedia community at large, with only ten percent disagreeing.

Perspective-guided, grounded interviewing beats bolting retrieval onto generation, roughly triples the sources gathered, and it isn't just an automatic-metric artifact, real Wikipedia editors backed the organization win with a p-value under one percent. That's a genuinely different, and cheap, way to buy better grounded writing.

[G] One more flag: the evaluation scaffolding itself has soft spots. Prometheus, the thirteen-billion-parameter rubric grader, runs without the reference answer it was designed to use, because it didn't fit the context window, and articles are trimmed to two thousand words before grading. The citation judge is that same seven-billion-parameter model with the fifteen percent false-negative rate we just discussed. The outline metric reports recall only, against a single human article as ground truth, so a longer, more sprawling outline can inflate the score. And the human study is thin, twenty pairs, two raters each, with Krippendorff's alpha between point two two and point three nine across every dimension, so even the organization win rests on a fairly noisy signal.
[O] That's a lot of caveats stacked underneath one headline win.

[O] Does any of this connect to how the field grounds and grades L-L-M generation more broadly?
[G] FreshWiki itself is a genuinely thoughtful defense, recent, heavily-edited pages chosen specifically to dodge pre-training leakage. But it's partial: a live search can still surface Wikipedia mirrors or derivatives even with the target page excluded, and the underlying real-world events may already sit somewhere in pre-training. And the paper's own ethics section names the fix it doesn't build yet: there's no content-sifting or bias-correction module, so promotional tone from the open internet flows straight into the article. Seven of ten editors in this study called STORM's output "emotional" or "unneutral."
