[S] Picture an assistant graded almost entirely by another copy of itself, with no human ever writing down a single example of "this answer was harmful."
[O] That's basically what this paper claims to pull off. A harmless assistant, trained on zero human labels identifying harm.
[S] Zero is doing a lot of work in that sentence. There's still a human-written rulebook sitting underneath the whole thing.
[O] Sure, but the rulebook is about sixteen short principles. Tens of thousands of labels compressed into something you could read over coffee.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a guest scholar take one paper apart. Today it's "Constitutional AI: Harmlessness from AI Feedback," from Yuntao Bai and colleagues at Anthropic, posted to arXiv in December of twenty twenty-two.
[S] It's on the docket because it coined R-L-A-I-F, reinforcement learning from AI feedback, and basically every "train a judge model to grade your model" technique since traces back to this one.
[O] Joining us to walk through it is Elena, a researcher who's spent real time with this paper. Elena, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. It's a genuinely clever piece of engineering, and unusually candid about where its own evidence is thin. We should get into both.
[S] Start with the problem. Why wasn't the team's own prior recipe good enough?
[G] Their earlier paper, usually just called the H-H RLHF paper, trained a helpful and harmless assistant on human comparison labels, tens of thousands of them. Two things bothered them about it. First, the labels are opaque. Nobody can read a hundred thousand pairwise judgments and summarize what behavior they actually steer toward. Second, and more visibly, the harmlessness training made the model evasive. Crowdworkers had rewarded "I can't answer that," so once the model hit a sensitive topic it would get stuck stonewalling for the rest of the conversation.
[O] Which is a real failure mode. A model that refuses everything is harmless, but it's also useless.
[G] That's the authors' own framing, almost word for word. Push helpfulness up and the model gets more willing to comply with bad requests. Push harmlessness up and it dodges everything, including questions it should just answer.
[S] So what's the actual fix here, mechanically, not just "use AI instead of humans"?
[G] Two moves. First, replace the mountain of human harm labels with a short written constitution, about sixteen principles, and have the model critique and revise its own answers against it. Second, once that's done, replace the human harmlessness labeler in the reinforcement learning loop with an AI grader comparing responses against that same constitution. That second piece is R-L-A-I-F.
[O] And the motivation isn't just cost-cutting.
[G] No, the paper frames it as scalable oversight. As models get more capable, you can't have a human read every output forever. This is a deliberately extreme test of how far you can push supervision down to a handful of written principles and let the model do the rest.
[O] Walk us through stage one.
[G] They start from a helpful-only RLHF model, one trained to comply, full stop, with no harm training at all. They feed it a prompt designed to elicit something bad, let it answer, then append a critique request tied to one of the sixteen principles, drawn at random, asking it to identify what's wrong with its own answer. Then they append a revision request, and the model rewrites its own answer in light of the critique.
[S] So it's arguing with itself.
[G] Right, and you can chain it. Draw a fresh principle, critique and revise again, up to four times per prompt. Then they finetune a fresh pretrained model on the final revisions, mixed with ordinary helpful responses so it doesn't forget how to be useful. That model is called SL-CAI, for supervised constitutional AI.
[S] Does the critique step actually matter, or could you just ask for a revision directly?
[G] They tested that. Skipping the critique and revising directly is slightly worse for small models, and about the same for large ones. They kept the critique step anyway, mainly for transparency, since it leaves a legible trace of why the answer changed.
[O] What's the scale here, how many prompts?
[G] For the supervised stage, about a hundred eighty-three thousand red-teaming prompts, a mix of human-written and model-generated, each getting up to four critique-revision passes. Plus around a hundred thirty-five thousand ordinary helpfulness prompts sampled straight from the helpful model, no revision needed.
[S] And stage one is meant to be the whole story?
[G] No, and this is important. The paper is explicit that stage one is mostly scaffolding. It gets the model on-distribution, so the reinforcement learning stage that follows doesn't have to explore as far. The real gains come from stage two.
[O] Which is the R-L-A-I-F part.
[G] Right. Take the supervised model, generate two candidate responses to a red-team prompt, and instead of asking a human crowdworker which one is less harmful, ask another model, the feedback model. Format it as multiple choice: here's the conversation, here's a principle, which response would a wise, ethical, polite person choose, option A or option B. Take the model's log-probabilities on A and B, normalize them, and that becomes a soft preference label.
[S] Soft, meaning not just zero or one.
[G] Exactly, and that turns out to matter. Soft labels, the actual normalized probabilities, train a noticeably better preference model than hard zero-one labels, because those probabilities are reasonably well calibrated. Those AI-generated harmlessness labels get mixed with the existing human helpfulness labels into one combined preference model, and the supervised model is then reinforcement-learned against it, exactly like standard RLHF, except the harm grader is now the model itself.
[O] You mentioned chain-of-thought comes in somewhere too.
[G] As an optional add-on. Instead of outputting log-probabilities cold, you prompt the feedback model to reason step by step before answering, which makes the label more transparent and, on their held-out evaluation, more accurate. But there's a side effect. Once the model commits to a chain of reasoning, it becomes almost certain in its answer, probabilities near zero or one, and that overconfidence teaches the policy to produce extreme, over-committed responses.
[S] How do they fix that?
[G] A blunt fix. They clamp the chain-of-thought probabilities to sit between forty and sixty percent, no matter how confident the reasoning sounded. They'd tried a wider clamp, twenty to eighty percent, first, and forty to sixty worked better. It's an engineering patch, not a principled one, and they say so plainly.
[S] Let's get to the actual numbers. Does this beat plain human-feedback RLHF?
[G] The headline plot is harmlessness Elo versus helpfulness Elo for a fifty-two billion parameter model, from crowdworker comparisons, about ten thousand two hundred helpfulness comparisons and eight thousand one hundred harmlessness comparisons across twenty-four model snapshots. The RL-CAI models, especially the chain-of-thought version, sit above and to the right of the standard RLHF frontier, more harmless at any given level of helpfulness. The paper calls that a Pareto improvement.
[O] Give me actual scale, not just "moved up and to the right."
[G] Roughly, in Elo terms: the pretrained base model sits around minus ninety-five on harmlessness. Helpful-only RLHF is around minus forty-five. Their earlier helpful-and-harmless RLHF model lands around plus seventy-five. The supervised-only model, SL-CAI, is close to zero, better than helpful-only, worse than the earlier harmless model. RL-CAI reaches roughly plus one hundred twenty, and with chain-of-thought, roughly plus one hundred ninety.
[S] So the reinforcement learning stage is doing almost all the work. SL-CAI alone barely beats the old baseline.
[G] That's exactly the paper's own reading. They cross-check with a separate metric too: an absolute harmfulness score, zero to four, from a model trained to predict how successfully a crowdworker baited harmful content out of it, measured over sixty-four held-out red-team prompts and two hundred fifty-six responses each. Helpful-only RLHF climbs to about three and a half over training, getting more willing to help with dangerous requests. RL-CAI and RL-CAI with chain-of-thought fall to about point seven, matching the earlier harmless model almost exactly.
[O] And the evasiveness problem, the whole reason they built this, does it actually go away?
[G] Yes, and it's probably the most concrete win in the paper. RL-CAI is, in their words, virtually never evasive. It engages with sensitive prompts and states its objection instead of stonewalling.
[S] There's a catch mentioned in the paper itself though, some kind of over-training failure.
[G] Right, and they call it out honestly. Push RL-CAI training too far and it Goodharts, the model gets overly harsh, or starts appending stock comfort language to nearly every red-team reply, phrases like "you are valid, valued, and cared for," regardless of whether it fits the conversation. That's reward over-optimization, the failure mode Gao and colleagues formalized separately, showing through here. It's part of why the sixteen-principle ensembling and the confidence clamp exist, to slow that drift down.
[O] Alright, let's argue about what this actually proves. Optimist case first: this is the paper that showed you can replace an enormous, opaque pile of human harm labels with something you can read in five minutes, and get a model that's simultaneously more harmless and not evasive. That's not a marginal tweak, that's a different way of supervising a model altogether, and it's not just theory. RL-CAI beats the earlier human-feedback harmless model on both of the paper's own metrics.
[S] I'll take the other side. Look at how that Elo comparison was actually run. The paper admits, in its own text, that the crowdworkers grading these comparisons were given a new instruction this time, to prefer the non-evasive response whenever two answers were equally harmless. That instruction didn't exist in the prior human-feedback paper, and the authors say directly that the change compresses the old harmless model's score and inflates the helpful-only model's score.
[O] So some of that "improvement" might just be the ruler changing, not the model.
[S] That's my read. You optimize RL-CAI specifically to be non-evasive, then change the grading criteria to reward exactly that trait, and declare a win on the new scale.
[G] You're both grounded in what's on the page. The instruction change is real, the authors state it themselves, and they even note the crowdworker vendor changed between the two papers, from Upwork and MTurk to a company called Surge AI. Part of the gap genuinely is a different measuring instrument. But it doesn't erase the result. RL-CAI still wins under the new instructions, and the evasiveness reduction is separately confirmed by the absolute harm score, which uses a different rubric entirely and never got that instruction change.
[S] Here's a second concern, and it's not about the ruler, it's about who's holding it. The feedback model grading harmlessness comes from the same base model family as the policy being trained. That's the model grading itself.
[O] Is that actually different from RLHF grading itself with a human-trained preference model?
[S] It's the same structural worry, but at least a human wrote the original labels. Here the whole harm signal, top to bottom, comes from one lineage of models agreeing with itself.
[G] And the paper doesn't dodge that. There's also a smaller admission worth flagging: a footnote says the constitution's sixteen principles were, quote, chosen in a fairly ad hoc and iterative way for research purposes. Increasing the number of principles doesn't even move the preference-model score in their own ablation, though it does seem to make the revisions more diverse. What the paper doesn't do at all is test the thing you'd most want tested from something calling itself harmless: robustness to adversarial attacks, jailbreaks, novel red-teaming. They name that explicitly as future work. It's not measured anywhere in this paper.
[O] That feels like the real gap to me, more than the labeling instruction. You can build the most elegant self-supervision loop in the world, but if nobody's tried to break it—
[S] —you don't know how harmless it is under pressure. You know how it looks against the prompts it was built to handle.
[G] Which is a fair place to land. The mechanism, self-critique plus an AI grader replacing thousands of human labels, is genuinely demonstrated. The size of the effect, and especially its robustness, is much less pinned down than the headline chart suggests. It's also worth saying the "zero human labels" framing is narrower than it sounds. Helpfulness labels are still human, the constitution is human-written, the few-shot examples that keep the model from getting confused about critique versus revision are hand-written, and the red-team prompts were originally human-seeded. This reduces and reshapes human oversight. It doesn't remove it.
[S] There's one more admission worth putting on the table, from the paper's own broader impacts section. The authors write plainly that lowering the barrier to steering a model's behavior cuts both ways. It also lowers the barrier to training a model to behave badly on purpose, and removing human review makes it easier to deploy systems nobody has actually watched closely.
[G] That's right there in the discussion, not buried. Their counterargument is that this also means you no longer need a standing army of human red-teamers doing the rather grim work of trying to provoke a model into saying something awful. Whether that trade nets out well probably depends on who's writing the constitution and what actually ends up in it.
[O] Zoom out for a second. Why does this paper still get cited so heavily, years later? Something like three thousand three hundred citations at this point.
[G] Because R-L-A-I-F became a template, not just a one-off result. Once you show a written rubric and an AI grader can replace tens of thousands of human comparison labels, that idea gets reused everywhere, rule-based reward papers, checklist graders, rubric-anchored judges. Anthropic's own later published constitution for its models descends directly from this line of work.
[S] It matters just as much for anyone building an LLM-as-judge evaluation pipeline, which by now is most of the field. The self-grading worry we raised, the feedback model and the policy sharing a family, is exactly the failure mode later self-preference research goes on to study. Panickssery and colleagues, among others, later found models rating outputs from their own family more favorably in comparable setups.
[G] Right, and that's the throughline. If your evaluation signal is itself a model, you've inherited this paper's central tension: is the grader independent enough to trust, or is it just agreeing with itself in a more sophisticated way?
[G] If there's one sentence: a short, human-written constitution can replace tens of thousands of harm labels and produce a harmless assistant that actually explains itself instead of stonewalling. The mechanism holds, even where the measured size of the effect is softer than the headline chart suggests.
[O] My takeaway: this is the paper where AI-graded training stopped being a thought experiment and became something that visibly worked, on a real fifty-two billion parameter model, not a toy example.
[S] Mine: read the Pareto plot as "the technique works," not as a precise, apples-to-apples measurement of the gap, and remember nobody had tested this thing against a genuinely adversarial attacker yet. Go read the full writeup on the litsearch site for the figures.
