[O] The reigning world computer chess champion just lost a hundred-game match without winning a single one, and the program that beat it had been studying chess for about nine hours.
[S] Nine hours, starting from nothing but the rules of the game — no opening book, no endgame tables, no human games to learn from. I want to know exactly what "beat" means here before I get excited, but yes, that is a strange number.
[O] It gets stranger. The same setup crossed superhuman in shogi in under two hours, and in Go in eight — one algorithm, barely tuned per game, three different board games.
[S] One algorithm run three separate times from a blank slate is a very different claim than "one algorithm learned three games," and that distinction is exactly what I want to spend this episode on.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper at a time.
[S] Today it's "Mastering Chess and Shogi by Self-Play with a General Reinforcement Learning Algorithm" — David Silver, Thomas Hubert, Julian Schrittwieser and colleagues at DeepMind, posted in December of 2017 and later published in Science under the name AlphaZero.
[O] Joining us is Callum, who has spent a lot of time with this paper and its aftermath. Callum, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. This is the paper that turned "self-play reinforcement learning works in Go" into "self-play reinforcement learning works, full stop" — and it did that by taking away almost everything that made Go convenient in the first place.
[S] Let's set the stage. Before this paper, world-class computer chess meant Stockfish: a linear evaluation function built from handcrafted features — piece-square tables, king safety, pawn structure, mobility — refined by human grandmasters over years, wrapped in an alpha-beta search sped up by quiescence search, transposition tables, and endgame tablebases covering positions with six or seven pieces.
[G] That lineage runs all the way back to Claude Shannon's nineteen fifty paper on programming a computer to play chess, and Deep Blue beating Kasparov in nineteen ninety-seven was the landmark middle chapter. By 2017, Stockfish was the reigning Top Chess Engine Championship winner, still built on that same alpha-beta tradition, just refined further.
[O] And then earlier that same year, AlphaGo Zero showed something completely different was possible in Go — a network trained purely by self-play, zero human games, no handcrafted evaluation at all, reaching superhuman strength from scratch.
[S] But Go is a suspiciously convenient test case for a neural network. It's translationally invariant, which matches a convolutional network's weight sharing. It's symmetric under rotation and reflection, so you get eight-fold data augmentation for free. One action type — place a stone. Binary outcome — win or lose, no draws.
[G] Right, and chess and shogi share almost none of that structure. Pawns move differently depending on which rank they're on, castling differs kingside versus queenside, a queen can influence the whole board from one square. Both games can end in a draw — chess is widely believed to be a theoretical draw under perfect play. And shogi adds something neither Go nor chess has: a captured piece switches sides and can be dropped back onto the board anywhere by its new owner, which inflates the action space considerably.
[O] So the real question this paper is asking: was AlphaGo Zero's self-play result a genuine reinforcement learning breakthrough, or just an artifact of Go happening to fit a convolutional network perfectly?
[S] And can one algorithm, given nothing but each game's rules, actually out-play the accumulated hand-engineering of Stockfish and Elmo — the reigning world computer shogi champion — starting from random play?
[O] Callum, walk us through the actual machine. What is AlphaZero, mechanically?
[G] At its core there's a single deep neural network, written f-theta of s, that takes a board position and outputs two things: a policy p, a probability over every legal move, and a value v, a single number estimating who's winning. Monte Carlo tree search — MCTS — uses that network to run simulated games forward from the current position, favoring moves the policy likes, moves the value estimates as strong, and moves that haven't been visited much yet. After enough simulations, the visit counts at the root give you a search policy, call it pi, that's usually much stronger than the raw network's policy p was on its own. That's the non-obvious part: the tree search is a policy-improvement operator. You train the raw network to imitate its own tree-search-improved self, over and over, and the whole system climbs.
[S] So self-play here isn't just "the network plays itself" — it's the network chasing a smarter, search-amplified version of itself.
[G] Exactly that. Play a full game using pi at every move, record the outcome z — plus one for a win, zero for a draw, minus one for a loss — and update theta by gradient descent on one loss function: the squared error between the value prediction and the actual outcome, plus the cross-entropy between the policy and the search probabilities pi, plus a weight-regularization term. That's the identical three-term loss AlphaGo Zero used.
[O] So what's actually different from AlphaGo Zero, if the network and the loss are the same?
[G] Four things, and every one of them is Go-specific plumbing torn out. First: AlphaGo Zero assumed a binary win-or-loss outcome. AlphaZero optimizes the full expected outcome including draws, since chess and shogi can end drawn and Go can't. Second: no symmetry exploitation. AlphaGo Zero got eight-fold data augmentation for free from Go's rotational and reflectional symmetry — chess and shogi rules are asymmetric, so AlphaZero trains and evaluates on the raw board only. Third, the big procedural change: AlphaGo Zero periodically froze a "best player," measured a freshly trained candidate against it, and only started generating new self-play games from the candidate once it won by a fifty-five percent margin. AlphaZero drops that entirely — one network, continuously updated, always generating its own next batch of self-play games. Fourth: AlphaGo Zero tuned its search hyperparameters specifically for Go using Bayesian optimization. AlphaZero reuses one fixed hyperparameter set across all three games — the sole exception is the exploration noise added at the root, scaled to each game's typical branching factor: point three for chess, point one five for shogi, point zero three for Go.
[S] That last detail is really the whole ballgame for the "general" claim — one configuration, minus a single noise parameter, works across three structurally different games.
[G] That is the headline claim, yes. Everything else the network receives is strictly rules-encoding, never evaluation help: input planes shaped to each board's grid — seventeen planes for Go, a hundred nineteen for chess, three hundred sixty-two for shogi, each carrying an eight-step move history — and an output policy shaped to each game's legal moves, four thousand six hundred seventy-two possible moves for chess including underpromotions, eleven thousand two hundred fifty-nine for shogi once you include the piece-drop moves. Training ran seven hundred thousand steps, mini-batches of four thousand ninety-six positions, from randomly initialized weights, with each search using eight hundred simulations, using up to five thousand first-generation TPUs to generate self-play games and sixty-four second-generation TPUs to actually train the network.
[O] And that compute budget wasn't even uniform across the three games.
[G] No — nine hours and forty-four million self-play games for chess, twelve hours and twenty-four million games for shogi, thirty-four hours and twenty-one million games for Go. Different board sizes and typical game lengths, so wall-clock time and game count vary a lot even though the step count, seven hundred thousand, was fixed.
[S] Let's get to the numbers. How fast did this actually work?
[G] Startlingly fast. In chess, AlphaZero passed Stockfish's strength after just four hours of training, three hundred thousand steps. In shogi, it passed Elmo in under two hours, a hundred ten thousand steps. In Go, it passed AlphaGo Lee — the version that beat Lee Sedol — after eight hours, a hundred sixty-five thousand steps. All three start from literally random play.
[O] Four hours to superhuman chess is the number that stops people mid-sentence.
[S] Training-curve crossings are one thing. What happened in an actual match?
[G] They ran dedicated hundred-game tournaments at one minute per move, once each network finished its full seven-hundred-thousand-step run. AlphaZero and a previously published three-day AlphaGo Zero each ran on a single machine with four TPUs. Stockfish and Elmo ran on sixty-four CPU threads with a one-gigabyte hash table, their strongest standard tournament configuration. Final score: twenty-eight wins, seventy-two draws, zero losses against Stockfish. Ninety wins, two draws, eight losses against Elmo. Sixty wins, zero draws, forty losses against the three-day AlphaGo Zero.
[O] Zero losses to the reigning world computer chess champion. That's not a close result.
[S] It's the Elmo score that jumps out more to me — ninety wins out of a hundred. But I want the mechanism underneath it: how many positions is each side actually looking at?
[G] That's the number the paper leans on hardest. AlphaZero evaluates about eighty thousand positions per second in chess, forty thousand in shogi. Stockfish evaluates seventy million a second. Elmo, thirty-five million. AlphaZero is winning while searching roughly a thousand times fewer positions — it uses the network to focus almost all its search on a handful of promising lines, which the authors point out is closer to what Shannon originally proposed for chess in nineteen fifty than to brute-force alpha-beta.
[O] So it isn't out-calculating Stockfish, it's out-judging it.
[G] That's the honest way to put it. Figure two backs it up further — as you give both sides more thinking time per move, AlphaZero's Monte Carlo search gains strength faster than either alpha-beta engine's, closing the gap and then pulling ahead. That's real evidence against the old assumption that alpha-beta is inherently the superior search for these games.
[S] One more result worth stating plainly: the opening analysis.
[G] Right — they checked the twelve most popular human chess openings, each played more than a hundred thousand times in an online database, and found AlphaZero independently rediscovered and played every single one of them during self-play, with no human data involved. Starting from each of those openings and playing full hundred-game matches, it still beat Stockfish overall — two hundred forty-two wins to five losses as White, forty-eight wins to nineteen losses as Black, with draws filling the rest. So this isn't a system that only wins from positions it invented for itself.
[O] Okay, let me make the big case. This is proof that one recipe — self-play plus tree search, almost no per-game tuning — generalizes across totally different game structures: symmetric and asymmetric, binary and draw-prone, a simple action space and a drop-based one. That's not a Go trick, that's reinforcement learning finally doing what people hoped it would since Arthur Samuel's checkers program.
[S] Let me push back on "general," because I think the word is doing more work than the method earns. AlphaZero trains three completely separate networks — one from-scratch run per game. Nothing learned in chess transfers to shogi. That's a general procedure applied three times, not a general model. "A single AlphaZero algorithm," the abstract says, and that phrasing invites you to picture one thing that plays all three games. It doesn't.
[G] That's a fair distinction, and worth being precise about — it's the paper's own actual claim, even though the framing in the title and abstract reads as more unified than the training procedure delivers. Point to the skeptic.
[O] Fine, but the harder thing to wave away is the head-to-head result itself. Zero losses to Stockfish is zero losses.
[S] Is it, though? Look at the hardware. AlphaZero and AlphaGo Zero ran on a single machine with four TPUs. Stockfish and Elmo were capped at sixty-four CPU threads and a one-gigabyte hash table — a configuration DeepMind chose, not an independently audited neutral tournament setting. TPUs and CPU threads aren't the same unit of compute, so "who had the bigger machine" isn't even a clean question. And this drew real contemporaneous pushback — Stockfish's own developers noted the version and settings used weren't necessarily its strongest, and that larger hash tables were cheap and standard by 2017.
[G] That criticism landed at the time and it's legitimate. To the paper's credit, it does report the evaluation-speed numbers transparently, eighty thousand versus seventy million positions a second, which is the honest way to let a reader judge the trade-off. But the bare win-draw-loss table, without that context sitting right next to it, invites reading the match as more hardware-neutral than it actually was. Point to the skeptic again.
[O] I'll take a point back, though: the Elmo result. Ninety wins out of a hundred, plus the opening-rediscovery result — independently finding all twelve major human openings from zero human data. You can't wave that away as a rigged benchmark. That's breadth of understanding.
[G] Agreed, and I'll add something neither of you has raised yet: there's no ablation in this paper isolating which of the four changes from AlphaGo Zero actually mattered. Was it dropping the best-player checkpoint? The symmetry removal? We don't know which piece was load-bearing for generalizing beyond Go, because all four were changed at once.
[S] And the compute. Five thousand TPUs for self-play generation, per game, is not a number most labs, let alone individual researchers, could reproduce in 2018. No reference implementation was released either.
[G] Also true, and it mattered in practice — the open-source Leela Chess Zero project took many months of volunteer-donated compute just to approach comparable strength, precisely because nobody outside DeepMind could simply run this. So here's the adjudication: the central empirical result, the same lightly-tuned recipe reaching superhuman play in two structurally different games, holds up and is genuinely important. But "general" describes a procedure, not a shared model, the hardware comparison was contested rather than neutral, and the compute footprint made this nearly unreproducible for close to two years.
[O] Where does the lineage go from here?
[G] Two years later, DeepMind published MuZero, which pushed exactly one step further — it drops even the rules of the game, learning its own internal model of how the environment behaves instead of being handed it. AlphaZero's contribution was proving the self-play-plus-search recipe wasn't a Go-specific fluke. MuZero's was proving you didn't even need to hand the algorithm the rules.
[S] There's an evaluation lesson here too, beyond the chess trivia. Any time a lab reports a head-to-head win against a baseline it also configured — the opponent's hardware, its settings, its time control — that comparison deserves exactly the scrutiny we just gave it. Report the compute budget next to the scoreboard, the way this paper did with its positions-per-second numbers, or the win-loss table alone oversells the result.
[O] That instinct, publish the resource asymmetry rather than just the scoreboard, is one every benchmark paper since should have absorbed.
[G] If there's one sentence to take from this: tabula rasa self-play reinforcement learning, stripped of every Go-specific convenience, still reaches superhuman play in two structurally very different games from nothing but the rules — that's a genuine, load-bearing result, not a lucky repeat.
[O] My takeaway is that this is the paper where self-play reinforcement learning stopped being a Go party trick and became a method serious people had to reckon with as a general recipe.
[S] Mine is to read the word "general" with an asterisk — one procedure, three separate networks, a contested hardware comparison — and the result is still remarkable, but check the fine print before you repeat the headline.
