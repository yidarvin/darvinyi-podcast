[G] Three things, and the paper lays them out explicitly. Financial NLP had small pretrained models, things like finBERT and FinBERT, all under a billion parameters, none of them instruction-following. [O] So you get scale, but nothing you can build on.
[G] Exactly. Nobody outside Bloomberg can fine-tune it, inspect it, or even reproduce its numbers, because its test sets were never published either. Second gap, no open instruction-tuning data for finance. Everyone doing general-domain instruction tuning had things like Alpaca or Vicuna to build on. Finance had nothing comparable. Third gap, evaluation. There was an existing benchmark called FLUE, from a paper called FLANG, that covered sentiment, headline classification, named entity recognition, and question answering. Solid tasks. But FLUE never asked whether a model could do anything with real market outcomes, no stock prediction, nothing that maps a language task onto a trading decision.
[S] Which matters, because financial NLP existing in a vacuum from financial prediction is exactly the kind of gap a benchmark can paper over.
[G] That's precisely the authors' argument for adding it. They write that financial prediction tasks are, in their words, more aligned with real-world scenarios and more challenging than the pure language tasks. So PIXIU is designed from the start to close all three gaps at once, an open model, open instruction data, and a benchmark that pairs language understanding with an actual forecasting task.
[O] Three gaps, three deliverables. That's a clean scope for a first paper in a new subfield.
[S] Walk us through what's actually in the box. Start with the dataset.
[G] That's FIT, the Financial Instruction Tuning dataset. It's built from five task families, financial sentiment analysis, news headline classification, named entity recognition, question answering, and stock movement prediction. For each task, the authors took existing, expert-annotated datasets rather than generating instructions synthetically with something like self-instruct, which is how Alpaca was built.
[O] Why does that choice matter?
[G] A few reasons the paper gives directly. Expert-annotated data is higher quality, it carries no commercial-use restriction the way data distilled from ChatGPT does, and critically, it already spans multiple formats, plain news text, tables pulled from earnings reports, and historical stock price time series. So FIT ends up multi-modal by construction, not as an afterthought.
[S] How big is it, and where does the data actually come from?
[G] One hundred thirty-six thousand, six hundred and nine instruction samples in total, built from nine underlying datasets. Sentiment comes from the Financial Phrase Bank and FiQA-SA. Headline classification comes from a gold-commodity news dataset with nine yes-or-no tags per headline. Named entity recognition comes from real financial agreements filed with the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission. Question answering comes from FinQA and ConvFinQA, both built over corporate earnings reports, some of it multi-turn. Stock movement prediction comes from three datasets, BigData22, ACL18, and CIKM18, each pairing a stock's tweets with its historical prices, labeled Rise or Fall based on a threshold on the next closing price move.
[O] And domain experts wrote the actual instruction prompts.
[G] Ten different phrasings per dataset, for most of them, so the model sees the same underlying task asked in different words. That's meant to make the model robust to how a prompt is worded, not just to which dataset it came from.
[S] Then FinMA is what you get by fine-tuning on all of that.
[G] Right. FinMA is LLaMA fine-tuned on FIT, and there are three variants. FinMA-7B and FinMA-30B are trained only on the four NLP tasks, sentiment, headline, NER, question answering. FinMA-7B-full adds the fifth task, stock prediction, into the fine-tuning mix. Training itself is fairly standard, the AdamW optimizer, a small learning rate, fifteen epochs for the seven-billion-parameter model and twenty for the thirty-billion-parameter one, run on stacks of A100 GPUs, up to a hundred twenty-eight of them for the thirty-billion version.
[O] And the third piece is the benchmark itself.
[G] FLARE, the Financial Language Understanding And Prediction Evaluation benchmark. Four financial NLP tasks over six datasets, plus the one stock movement prediction task over three datasets, so five tasks and nine datasets in total for this version. And there's a design decision worth flagging here. Because BloombergGPT never released its test data, the authors built FLARE's test sets to match BloombergGPT's reported test set sizes and distributions, so its published numbers could still be compared against, even without access to its actual test items.
[S] That's a workaround, not a real apples-to-apples comparison.
[G] It's a reasonable workaround given the constraint, and the authors are explicit about doing it that way. But you're right that it's distribution-matched, not identical-item, and that distinction matters later.
