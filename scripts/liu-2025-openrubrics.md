[S] Picture a reward model reading two answers to the same prompt: "describe a vivid character, in fewer than two paragraphs." One answer obeys the limit. The other blows past it and drifts into an unrelated multiple-choice question, and it gets picked as the winner anyway.
[O] Because it's longer and more image-rich, and that's exactly the kind of thing reward models have always had a soft spot for.
[S] Right, and this isn't hypothetical. It's a real failure, from two current, size-matched reward models, sitting in this week's paper.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an AI optimist, an AI skeptic, and a visiting scholar work through one paper from the map at litsearch dot darvinyi dot com.
[S] Today it's OpenRubrics: Towards Scalable Synthetic Rubric Generation for Reward Modeling and L-L-M Alignment, from Tianci Liu, Ran Xu, and colleagues at Purdue, Emory, Georgia Tech, and U Albany, posted in the fall of twenty twenty-five.
[O] And joining us to walk through it is Naomi, who's read this one closely. Naomi, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is one of those papers where the core trick is almost embarrassingly simple to describe, and that's exactly what makes the results worth taking seriously.
[S] Let's start with the gap. Why do we need rubrics as rewards at all? Why isn't a scalar preference score enough?
[G] Because a single number can't tell you why one answer beat another. Standard reward models score under a ranking loss, or reason step by step to a verdict, but either way you get an opaque judgment. For subjective tasks, long-form question answering, general helpfulness, medical advice, that one number flattens a lot of things you'd actually want to check separately: is it factual, is it on topic, does it honor the explicit constraints in the prompt.
[O] So rubrics decompose that into a checklist, criteria you can actually inspect instead of trusting a black box.
[G] Exactly, and that idea isn't new to this paper, it traces back to prior work on rubrics as rewards and to benchmarks like HealthBench, which pays clinicians to hand-write medical rubrics. The problem is that hand-written rubrics don't scale. The obvious workaround, prompting a frontier model to invent a rubric on the fly, has two failure modes the authors target directly: weak quality control, since nothing checks whether the generated rubric is any good, and runaway cost if every judgment calls a commercial A-P-I.
[S] So the pitch is: stop hand-writing rubrics, stop calling an expensive A-P-I at inference time, build one dataset of prompt-rubric pairs once, and distill it into a small model that writes rubrics for free after that.
[G] That's the bet. And critically, they don't just prompt for a rubric in a vacuum, which is the part worth dwelling on.
[O] So how do you actually get an open model to write a good rubric instead of a generic one?
[G] The authors split rubrics into two kinds. Hard rules are explicit, checkable constraints straight from the prompt, things like "rewrite this in the negative form" or "keep it under two paragraphs." Principles are the implicit qualities a strong answer has that nobody wrote down: stay on topic, preserve the original meaning, use vivid imagery. Hard rules are easy to extract. Principles are the hard part, because you're inferring what "good" looks like without anyone stating it.
[S] And that's where the actual technique comes in, the thing they call C-R-G.
[G] Contrastive Rubric Generation. Instead of asking a model "what makes a good answer to this prompt" in isolation, they condition the generator on the prompt plus a ranked list of responses, at minimum a chosen and a rejected answer from an existing preference pair. The generator has to explain why the winner beats the loser. That contrast is what pulls out discriminative, ranking-aware criteria, instead of generic platitudes like "be helpful and accurate."
[O] That's a nice move, you're not asking the model to imagine quality from nothing, you're asking it to explain a judgment it already has evidence for.
[G] Right, and it's cheap, because chosen-rejected pairs already exist across six public datasets they draw from: UltraFeedback, Magpie, Skywork-Preference, Synthetic-I-F, MegaScience, and Medical-o1. Together those give them thirty-five thousand seven hundred prompts and about eighty-five thousand six hundred pairwise judging instances after processing.
[S] But a generated rubric is still just an L-L-M's opinion of what matters. How do they know a given rubric is actually reliable, and not just plausible-sounding nonsense?
[G] That's the second piece, preference-label consistency filtering. They take the generated rubric, feed it back to the same instruction-tuned model, and ask it to re-judge the original pair using only that rubric as the scoring guide. If the rubric, applied as written, reproduces the known human label at least half the time across the induced comparisons, it's kept. If not, it's discarded.
[O] So the filter is asking, "if I hand this rubric to a judge, does the judge land back on the answer we already know is right?"
[G] Exactly. Only rubrics that pass, and only the specific pairs they get correct, make it into the final training set. From that filtered data they train two separate models, both starting from Qwen three, an eight-billion-parameter backbone, with a four-B version reported too. A rubric generator learns to write the rubric from just the prompt, plain supervised fine-tuning. A separate judge learns to read the prompt, the two candidate responses, and the generated rubric, and output a preference. At inference you run the generator, then hand its rubric to the judge, and that two-stage pipeline is what they call Rubric-R-M. There's also a voting-of-five variant that samples five judge trajectories and takes a majority vote.
[S] Okay, let's get to the actual numbers, because "beats baselines" needs a number attached to it.
[G] The headline is an average across eight reward-modeling benchmarks, RewardBench, R-M-Bench, RewardBench two, a cluster of instruction-following evals, and HelpSteer three. Rubric-R-M at eight-B scores seventy point one on that average. The best size-matched, seven-B-scale white-box baseline they test, R-M-R-one, tops out at sixty-one point seven. That's the plus eight point four point margin the abstract leads with. The four-B variant still reaches sixty-eight point four, ahead of every seven-B baseline in the comparison.
[O] And the voting-of-five ensemble pushes it further still.
[G] It does, seventy-three point zero for eight-B with voting, which edges past R-M-R-one at fourteen-B, at seventy-one point seven, and past an A-P-I pipeline using G-P-T four point one Mini to write rubrics and Gemini two point five Flash-Lite to judge them, at seventy-one point three. A distilled eight-B open model, with voting, beats a model nearly twice its size and a commercial A-P-I stack.
[S] The number I actually want is the one that isolates the rubric contribution, not just "our model beats other models."
[G] That's the naive-versus-tuned comparison, and it's the cleanest number in the paper. Take that same Qwen three eight-B backbone, untuned, and just prompt it directly to write a rubric and then judge with it. It scores fifty-seven point seven. Fine-tune that same backbone on the OpenRubrics data, same benchmarks, and it jumps to seventy point one. That's a twelve point four point gain from training on this dataset, and it's the number the authors lean on hardest to argue the rubrics themselves are doing real work.
[O] Where do those gains concentrate?
[G] Instruction-following benchmarks that reward fine-grained constraint checking, FollowBench and InfoBench, where the ensembled model hits eighty-one point five and eighty-three point eight, well ahead of reasoning-based baselines like JudgeLRM and R-R-M.
[S] What happens when Rubric-R-M isn't just judging, it's actually steering a policy through training?
[G] That's where it gets more interesting, because now you're asking whether the reward signal survives contact with real optimization. On IFBench, a hard instruction-following benchmark, a policy trained with Rubric-R-M as the D-P-O reward hits thirty point three. A strong recent baseline called RLCF gets twenty-eight point two. RLMT variants land around twenty-two point four to twenty-two point eight. Here's the detail worth flagging: several of the training signals in that comparison, plain D-P-O, S-F-T, P-P-O, actually leave the policy below its own twenty-eight point two starting point. Training against the wrong reward can make a model worse at following instructions, not better.
[O] That's a genuinely useful finding on its own, independent of whose method wins.
[G] Agreed. On IFEval and InfoBench, Rubric-R-M gets the policy to seventy-nine point five and eighty-three point zero, ahead of D-P-O with Skywork or ArmoRM rewards, which land around seventy-six. And on general alignment, Arena-Hard, AlpacaEval, and WildBench, it's again the best among the open-source reward signals tested: fifty-three point one on style-controlled Arena-Hard, forty-seven on AlpacaEval, fifty-three point six macro on WildBench, a plus four point nine gain over the untrained base policy.
[S] You mentioned a medical domain study before we started rolling. That's HealthBench?
[G] Right, and it's arguably the most dramatic result in the paper. A version of Rubric-R-M fine-tuned specifically on the science slice of OpenRubrics scores sixty-eight point three as a judge on HealthBench, beating R-R-M-seven-B's sixty-three point three, and closing in on R-M-R-one at fourteen-B, which gets sixty-nine point nine. Voting-of-five pushes that to seventy-two point nine. The naive, untuned Qwen three eight-B pipeline on the same task manages only fifty-one point eight, so the rubric-tuning gap here is plus sixteen point five, even larger than the general-domain gap. Used to actually train a policy with D-P-O, it lifts HealthBench from a base of twenty-one point six up to twenty-three point eight, ahead of ArmoRM's twenty-two point five and R-M-R-one-seven-B's twenty-two point seven.
[O] And presumably this doesn't cost you in speed, since the whole pitch is that it's cheaper than a frontier A-P-I or a long chain of thought.
[G] It holds up there too. Scoring a hundred samples takes about a hundred thirty-one seconds with Rubric-R-M, even running two eight-B models back to back, generator then judge. R-R-M-seven-B takes around two hundred three seconds, and the R-M-R-one family takes anywhere from roughly a hundred seventy to three hundred eighty seconds. Rubrics are also cacheable, so at scale you're not regenerating them for every comparison.
[O] Okay, let me make the strongest case for this, because I think it's genuinely strong. This is a cheap, scalable recipe: turn preference pairs already lying around into rubrics for free, using contrast instead of guesswork, self-filter the noisy ones, distill into a small model that runs in under two hundred seconds per hundred judgments. It beats every same-size baseline, it beats a fourteen-B model with voting, it transfers into actually training policies, and its biggest win lands exactly where you'd most want an interpretable, checkable reward: medicine. That's not incremental.
[S] I'll grant the engineering is real and the sweep is broad, current baselines, several benchmark families, both judging and policy training. But I want to push on what that plus twelve point four actually proves. The only control is the untuned base model. There's no "fine-tune this same Qwen three eight-B on these exact same pairs, but without any rubrics at all" ablation anywhere in the main results. So that gain bundles at least three things together: in-domain fine-tuning, the contrastive generation technique specifically, and the consistency filter. We can't see how much of it is "any fine-tuning on this data helps" versus "rubrics, specifically, are the active ingredient."
[G] That's a fair read, and it's the sharpest gap in the paper. There's no C-R-G-versus-filter ablation either, no run that keeps the filter but swaps in plain, non-contrastive rubric generation. So the paper demonstrates "Rubric-R-M beats baselines" more rigorously than it demonstrates "rubrics, and specifically contrastive rubrics, are why."
[S] There's something that worries me more, actually: circularity. The training pairs for MegaScience and Medical-o1, the exact domains behind the biggest HealthBench win, aren't human-labeled. They're built by an ensemble of open-source reward models, Athene-R-M and Skywork-Reward-V2, ranking sampled responses. So you've got model-generated labels, filtered by a consistency check that uses the same generator model as both writer and judge, at a threshold of point five, which is barely better than a coin flip.
[O] Okay, that one gives me pause. If the labels a rubric gets checked against are themselves model opinions, the filter can only catch disagreement between models, not a blind spot they all share.
[G] Exactly right, a consistency check can't rescue you from an error every model in the loop makes the same way. There's a related distribution point too: FollowBench and InfoBench, the two benchmarks where Rubric-R-M shows its biggest instruction-following gains, are built by sampling responses from the same Qwen three eight-B and fourteen-B family used to construct the training preferences in the first place. That's not contamination in the strict sense, the eval prompts aren't training prompts, but it's favorable overlap the paper doesn't quantify or control for.
[S] So my scorecard: the headline number is real, and the baselines are legitimately strong and current, JudgeLRM, R-R-M, and R-M-R-one are all from twenty twenty-five. But "rubrics are the mechanism" is asserted more than it's isolated, and the standout result, plus sixteen point five on HealthBench, is exactly the result most exposed to that same-family circularity.
[O] Fair, point to you on attribution. But I'd still credit the case study they do offer as real, if qualitative, evidence: on a RewardBench example, two current baseline reward models both pick the response that literally violates the prompt's own two-paragraph limit, because it's longer and more image-rich. Rubric-R-M's rubric lists the paragraph count as a hard rule, checks it first, and gets the right answer.
[G] That example is worth taking seriously precisely because it's not a benchmark average, it's a mechanism you can read line by line. The rubric names three hard rules and five principles, checks the hard rule first, and the reasoning is inspectable in a way a scalar reward, or an opaque chain of thought, is not. Whatever you think of the ablation gap, that inspectability is a real property of this design, and scalar reward models don't have it.
[S] Widening the lens for a second, this connects to something we come back to constantly on this show: judge reliability.
[G] It does. If your evaluation pipeline, whether it's a benchmark or a reward model, is ultimately another L-L-M's judgment, then what that judge is actually checking, and whether you can inspect it, matters as much as its raw accuracy. A rubric that names a specific hard rule and gets it wrong is a debuggable failure. A wrong scalar reward is just a wrong number with no way in.
[O] And that's relevant beyond alignment. For anyone building agentic evals or auto-graders, a contrastively-derived rubric might be a cheaper path to a usable checklist than paying a domain expert, as long as you know where its blind spots are likely to sit.
[G] The authors are upfront that this is bounded by what they actually tested, though. Their own limitations section flags three things: rubrics inherit whatever biases live in the underlying models and datasets, especially for subjective or culturally specific criteria; the framework is pairwise only, no absolute scoring, no ranking beyond two responses at once; and everything here is offline D-P-O, not online reinforcement learning with real exploration dynamics. Those are the paper's own hedges, not mine.
[G] If there's one sentence to leave with, it's that contrasting a good answer against a bad one is a cheap, effective way to make an L-L-M explain what actually makes a response better, and that explanation turns out to be worth training on.
[O] My takeaway is more practical: an eight-B open model, with a two-stage rubric-then-judge design, is now beating fourteen-B reasoning judges and commercial A-P-I pipelines on cost, and on several benchmarks, on accuracy too.
