[O] Here's the headline the authors themselves put in their abstract: a fifteen-billion-parameter open model that matches or beats Code Llama at thirty-four billion parameters. Twice the size, beaten by the smaller, fully open model.
[S] Here's the sentence three pages later that headline conveniently steps around: on the benchmark most developers actually use to judge a code model, plain HumanEval completion, DeepSeek-Coder at thirty-three billion parameters still wins outright. Both of those are true at once.
[O] Both true, and that tension is basically the whole episode.
[S] Along with a decontamination pipeline that, by the authors' own admission, only checks five of the fifteen-plus benchmarks they report numbers on.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a guest scholar sit down with one paper from the litsearch dot darvinyi dot com atlas and figure out what actually holds up.
[S] Today's paper is StarCoder two and The Stack v-two, the next generation, from the BigCode community, sixty-four authors led by Anton Lozhkov, Raymond Li, and Loubna Ben Allal, posted to arXiv in February twenty twenty-four.
[O] Joining us to walk through it is Ingrid, who has spent a lot of time in this paper's tables. Ingrid, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. This is a paper I keep coming back to, because it tries to do two hard things at once, be maximally transparent about its training data and still be competitive on the leaderboard, and it mostly succeeds at both, with some real scars to show for it.
[S] Set the stage for us. Why did BigCode need a second attempt at this?
[G] The first StarCoder, in twenty twenty-three, already proved an open-data model could be competitive. The problem by early twenty twenty-four was scale. Code Llama and DeepSeek-Coder were releasing weights but not training data, so nobody outside those labs could check contamination or attribution, and the BigCode community's own answer, the first Stack, was six and a half terabytes across three hundred eighty-four languages, harvested by file extension. That's small next to the three-to-four-trillion-token runs the closed labs were shipping.
[O] So it's not "can open data work," it's "can open data scale without the transparency becoming a tax on performance."
[G] Exactly the framing the paper uses. And their answer is to stop crawling GitHub fresh and instead build on top of the Software Heritage archive, a nonprofit library that already maintains a deduplicated snapshot of essentially all public source code.
[S] Why does that partnership matter mechanically, not just as a nice-sounding collaboration?
[G] Because Software Heritage had already done the expensive part. Their archive is structured as a fully deduplicated Merkle graph of a September twenty twenty-three snapshot, so the team extracts the latest revision of each repository's main branch and gets content-hash deduplication for free, instead of re-solving it themselves. They also stopped guessing languages from file extensions and ran a proper classifier, go-enry, which surfaced six hundred fifty-eight distinct languages before filtering down to a final six hundred nineteen. The raw pull comes out to sixty-seven and a half terabytes, roughly ten times the first Stack.
[O] Ten times bigger and somehow more careful about licensing, not less.
[G] That's the part I'd actually call the paper's most distinctive engineering contribution, a license-assignment cascade. First they check GitHub's own repo-level license metadata. That's missing for the vast majority of repositories, about ninety-seven percent, so for those they fall back to file-level detection with a tool called ScanCode, and then propagate a detected license to other files sitting in the same directory. Only files that resolve to permissive or unlicensed survive. Copyleft and commercial code get dropped outright, which is a stricter policy than "take everything and let downstream users sort out the licensing."
[S] And source code is just one ingredient.
[G] Source is one of eight sources, deliberately. GitHub issues and pull requests, roughly three hundred million PRs backed by fifteen terabytes of underlying code, with usernames pseudonymized in the conversation threads. Jupyter notebooks, an initial pool of eleven million, filtered and near-deduplicated down to around four million converted scripts. A separate Kaggle notebook pipeline. Scraped documentation. And math and reasoning data, things like the GSM8K training split, Deepmind Mathematics, arXiv LaTeX sources, Wikipedia, and OpenWebMath.
[O] That's a lot of surface area for something to leak through.
[G] Which is why every one of those sources with real reuse risk goes through five separate filters, near-deduplication using MinHash and locality-sensitive hashing on the SantaCoder recipe, PII redaction with a model called StarPII that strips names, emails, keys, passwords, and IP addresses, benchmark decontamination, malware scanning with ClamAV plus community signatures, and opt-out removal driven by BigCode's "Am I in The Stack" tool. After all of that, filtering, deduplicating, subsampling, the actual training set lands at about three terabytes, over nine hundred billion unique tokens. Four times the first StarCoder's training set, and still a small fraction of that sixty-seven-and-a-half-terabyte raw archive.
[S] Walk us through what they actually trained on top of that corpus.
[G] Three dense transformer models, three billion, seven billion, and fifteen-billion parameters. Two architecture changes from the first StarCoder matter. They swap learned positional embeddings for rotary position embeddings, and they replace multi-query attention with grouped-query attention, keeping the key-value heads low, two for the small model, four for the other two, so inference doesn't slow down. Base training runs at a four-thousand-token context for three-point-one to four-point-one trillion tokens, that's four and a half to five and a third epochs over the unique-token pool, which is well past what compute-optimal scaling laws would recommend if you were only optimizing training compute. Then there's a long-context stage that extends every model out to sixteen thousand tokens using a sliding window and FlashAttention-two.
[O] Deliberately over-training past compute-optimal, because inference cost matters more than training cost once you're shipping a model people actually run.
[G] Right, that's the standard justification, and it's the same one Llama two and other contemporaries used. One more training detail worth knowing before we get to results: the fifteen-billion model was supposed to use the same rotary encoding setting as the smaller two, but a bug in parsing the training configuration means it trained with a different value instead. The authors disclose this themselves. It's a genuine within-family confound, and we'll come back to it.
[S] Let's get to the numbers, because that's where the abstract's confidence gets tested.
[G] On HumanEval-plus and MBPP-plus, the stress-tested completion benchmarks, StarCoder two at three billion parameters is the strongest small model on every column tested, and it even edges out the previous generation's fifteen-billion base model. That's roughly a sixty percent relative gain over its own three-billion predecessor on HumanEval-plus. The fifteen-billion model clears Code Llama's thirteen-billion version by a wide margin, forty-six point three versus thirty-seven point eight on HumanEval, and gets close to, sometimes past, Code Llama at thirty-four billion and DeepSeek-Coder at thirty-three billion, both more than double its size.
[O] That's the headline, and it's real.
[G] It's real, with one asterisk right in the same table. DeepSeek-Coder at thirty-three billion still leads outright on raw high-resource completion, fifty-four point three versus StarCoder two's forty-six point three on HumanEval. Where the fifteen-billion StarCoder two actually pulls ahead of both larger models is math and reasoning. Eight-shot GSM8K, using program-synthesis prompting, StarCoder two-fifteen-B scores sixty-five point one percent, ahead of Code Llama's fifty-four point two and DeepSeek-Coder's fifty-eight point seven, despite being under half their size. Same pattern on CRUXEval, a benchmark that has the model simulate what a Python function actually does when it runs.
[S] So "matches or beats models twice its size" is true on reasoning and false on the benchmark most people check first.
[G] That's the precise reading, yes. And there's a result the paper doesn't fully explain. The seven-billion model is the one outlier in the whole family. It beats Code Llama at seven billion and beats its own predecessor, but trails DeepSeek-Coder's six-point-seven-billion model by a wide margin, and the authors write, directly, that it isn't clear to them why the seven-billion model underperforms relative to its three-billion and fifteen-billion siblings.
[O] I want to sit in the optimist case for a second, because I think it's stronger than "a plausible headline." A three-billion-parameter model beating a fifteen-billion-parameter model from the same lineage, on the same architecture family, one generation later, that's a real efficiency story, not just a compute-scaling story. And the governance side isn't decoration either. Every training file traceable to a Software Heritage persistent identifier, an opt-out tool that actually removed repositories, over fifteen hundred of them, after a real cutoff date. That's auditability nobody else in this weight class ships.
[S] I'll grant all of that, genuinely. My problem isn't the governance, it's what the governance doesn't cover. Section three-point-three of the paper is explicit, decontamination removes files containing docstrings or solutions from exactly five benchmarks, HumanEval, MBPP, APPS, GSM8K, and DS-1000. But this model gets evaluated on well over a dozen benchmarks, MultiPL-E, CRUXEval, RepoBench, HumanEvalFix, a security suite, several bias suites. The paper never says whether any of those were swept for leakage.
[G] That's accurate, and it's worse than it first sounds, because the decontamination step explicitly excludes documentation, arXiv, and Wikipedia from the sweep. Those are exactly the natural-language sources where a benchmark problem is most likely to appear paraphrased in prose, not as a literal string match, which is all their filter looks for.
[S] There's a second thing that bothered me more the longer I sat with it. Almost none of the headline pass-at-one numbers carry a variance estimate. HumanEval, MBPP, GSM8K, single greedy-decoding runs, no seed range. The one partial exception is CRUXEval, where they borrow someone else's noise estimate from the original benchmark paper, about zero-point-two percent sampling noise, to caveat a couple of close calls. That's fine for the big gaps, nobody doubts a fifteen-point lead is real. It's not fine for the seven-billion-versus-Code-Llama-seven-billion comparison, thirty-five point four against thirty-three point five on a hundred sixty-four-problem benchmark. That gap is well within range of moving with a different sampling seed, and it's sitting right next to an anomaly the authors admit they can't explain.
[O] Okay, that one lands. If you can't explain why your middle model underperforms and you also can't rule out that the comparison itself is noise, that's a real gap, not a nitpick.
[G] To the paper's credit, it discloses a second confound alongside the rotary-encoding bug I mentioned earlier. Repository-level fill-in-the-middle, learning to complete code with both a prefix and a suffix, ran at a lower rate than intended for most of training, and the authors say that explains the fifteen-billion model's comparatively weak fill-in-the-middle score. Two disclosed implementation bugs, both affecting the flagship model specifically, both complicating any clean comparison within the family.
[O] I do want credit given where it's due, though. Most papers don't disclose their own training bugs at all.
[S] Agreed, and I'll go further, that's a point in favor of the whole open-science approach, the fact that we're even having this conversation with the receipts in hand is downstream of them publishing everything. But there's one more finding I think undersells itself in the paper, buried in the security section. On the "Asleep at the Keyboard" benchmark, which checks whether generated code contains the vulnerability a scenario is testing for, StarCoder two at seven and fifteen billion parameters has a higher insecure-code rate than every comparably sized model on the table, including their own predecessor.
[G] That's right, and the authors' own explanation is plausible, not damage control, more functionally correct code plausibly means more code that actually reaches the vulnerable code path, whereas a model that generates broken code half the time never gets far enough to be insecure. But plausible isn't verified, and it's the kind of finding a security-focused ablation could actually test rather than gesture at.
[O] So where does that leave the headline claim, on balance?
[G] Split, deliberately. The training-data transparency is a genuine structural advance, the SWHID traceability, the license cascade, the opt-out tooling that actually removed repositories on a real deadline, that's harder to fake and more valuable long-term than any single benchmark number. And the size-class claims for the three-billion and fifteen-billion models are real, verifiable state-of-the-art results within their weight classes, on the benchmarks the paper actually controls for contamination. But "punches above its weight class" needs to be read benchmark by benchmark, not as a blanket property of the family, because the paper's own contamination sweep doesn't reach most of what it reports, and nearly every number is a single run with no confidence interval attached.
[S] If I'm reviewing this for a lab deciding whether to build on it, that's not a rejection, it's a to-do list. Multi-seed evaluation on the close calls, a contamination audit that actually covers the docs and arXiv slices, and a real investigation into why the middle-sized model is the family's weak link.
[O] And this connects to something bigger than one code model. Every trend line in frontier labs points toward training data nobody outside the lab can inspect. This paper is a working demonstration that you can publish the provenance of every training token and still land near the top of your size class. That's the template worth other groups copying, regardless of what happens to these specific benchmark numbers in six months.
[G] I'd add one implication for how any model gets evaluated, not just this one. The gap between claiming decontamination and actually decontaminating every benchmark you report numbers on is exactly the kind of thing an eval-methodology reviewer should check as a standard item, the way you'd check for a held-out test set. This paper happens to make that gap unusually easy to spot because it tells you precisely which five benchmarks the sweep covers. Most papers don't even give you that much to audit.
[S] My one-line takeaway, the fifteen-billion model's transparency is real and rare, but treat every "matches models twice its size" claim as benchmark-specific until someone runs the multi-seed, full-coverage version of this evaluation.
[O] Mine is the flip side, a three-billion-parameter model beating its own fifteen-billion predecessor, on fully open, fully audited data, is the actual proof of concept here, and it's a stronger result than the size-class comparisons that get all the attention.
[G] And the paper's own takeaway, in their words, is that full transparency about training data can coexist with frontier-adjacent performance. The tables show that's true in places and overstated in others. For the figures, the full benchmark tables, and the license-cascade diagram, the writeup is on the litsearch site.
