[O] Here's a claim that should stop you mid-scroll: a new Scale AI paper argues that most of what looks like reasoning gains from reinforcement learning is not new capability at all, it's the model getting better at doing what it could already do.
[S] And here's the flip side of that same paper: they also built a method that produces genuine gains on the hardest problems, by handing the model a hint exactly when it is completely stuck.
[O] So which is it, is reinforcement learning teaching new tricks, or just polishing old ones?
[S] The paper's honest answer is mostly the second, with a clever patch for the first. That tension is the whole episode.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a guest scholar take apart one paper from the litsearch dot darvinyi dot com index. Today's paper is "Adaptive Guidance Accelerates Reinforcement Learning of Reasoning Models," from Vaskar Nath, Elaine Lau, Anisha Gunjal, Manasi Sharma, Nikhil Baharte, and Sean Hendryx at Scale AI, posted in twenty twenty-five.
[S] Joining us is Maya, who has studied this paper closely. Maya, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is a paper I keep coming back to, because it sits right at the center of a live argument about what reinforcement learning is actually doing to these reasoning models.
[O] Which is exactly why it's on the docket. Everyone training reasoning models right now needs an answer to that question.
[S] Maya, let's set the stage. What's the debate this paper is stepping into?
[G] Reasoning models like DeepSeek-R1 and OpenAI's o1 are trained with reinforcement learning from verifiable rewards, RLVR for short. You give the model a math or code problem with a checkable answer, sample a response, and reward it if the answer matches. That recipe clearly boosts benchmark scores. What was contested is why. A concurrent paper the authors cite, from Yue and colleagues, argued that RLVR mostly just biases the model's output distribution toward answers it could already reach, and that at very high sampling budgets, k equals two hundred fifty-six, the model's actual reasoning boundary can even shrink.
[O] So the worry is that RL isn't expanding what the model can do, it's just making it more consistent at what it already could do sometimes.
[G] Right. And this paper adds a second, more mechanical problem on top of that. The standard training algorithm, GRPO, group relative policy optimization, computes its learning signal by normalizing rewards within a group of rollouts for the same prompt. If every single rollout for a hard prompt is wrong, all the rewards are identical, the normalized advantage is exactly zero, and that prompt contributes no gradient whatsoever.
[S] Which means the hardest problems, the ones you'd most want the model to learn from, just go silent.
[G] Exactly, and the field's response so far has mostly been to give up on them. DAPO, one of the strongest recent recipes, explicitly filters out and discards prompts where every rollout is correct or every rollout is wrong, specifically to stabilize training. That fixes the noise problem, but it also means the hardest, most informative prompts get thrown away right when the model most needs them.
[O] So the question this paper asks is, instead of discarding those dead prompts, can you resurrect them?
[G] So the paper does two things. First, it builds a precise way to measure the capability question rather than just argue about it. They define every gain from RL as the sum of two disjoint terms, relative to the frozen starting policy. Self-distillation is a problem the base model could already solve with multiple attempts, pass at sixteen, that RL converts into a reliable one-shot answer, pass at one, at temperature zero. Capability gain is a problem the base model could not solve even at pass at sixteen, that the RL-trained model then solves at pass at one. That's genuinely new problem-solving ability, not just sharpening.
[O] And they don't just eyeball this on one model, they run it at real scale.
[G] Five scales of Qwen two point five, from half a billion parameters up to seventy-two billion, over five hundred thousand problems spanning math, science, and code. Ten independent trials with different random seeds, so the counts have real error bars. The verdict is consistent across all five scales: self-distillation dominates. Capability gain exists, the half-billion-parameter model actually gains the most in relative terms, but it's always the smaller share of the total.
[S] That's a pretty deflationary finding for anyone hyping RL as a magic capability generator.
[G] It is, and the authors are upfront about it. In their words, RLVR primarily re-allocates probability mass rather than discovering truly novel solutions. There's also a scaling wrinkle: the pool of unsolved problems shrinks fast as models grow. The half-billion model starts with about three thousand two hundred unsolved items in their evaluation set; by seventy-two billion parameters that pool is down to about fifteen hundred. Since each model converts roughly the same fraction of its own unsolved pool, the bigger models simply have less room left to gain from.
[O] Okay, so that's the diagnosis. Where does the fix come in?
[G] This is the second half of the paper, and it's the more actionable part. If the bottleneck is that hard prompts never produce a single correct rollout, the fix is to make correct rollouts appear. So they append a hint to the prompt, high-level conceptual direction and a solving framework distilled from the human reference solution, generated by GPT-4o, deliberately short of the answer itself, and the model still has to derive the full chain on its own. As a first check, just adding these hints to a seven-billion-parameter Qwen math model raises pass at k across the board, and critically, it solves problems that were previously unsolvable at any k. Guidance manufactures the correct rollouts that hard prompts otherwise never produce.
[S] But you can't just train on hinted rollouts and ship that model, because at test time there's no hint.
[G] Right, that's the whole engineering problem, and it's where the algorithm, called Guide, comes in. Two moves. First, hints are only injected on prompts where all the standard rollouts already failed, it's a targeted rescue for dead prompts, not a general crutch. Second, because those guided rollouts came from a different, hint-conditioned distribution, they're off-policy relative to the hint-free deployment model. So Guide reweights them with an importance ratio: the numerator scores the rollout under the plain prompt, the denominator under whichever prompt actually generated it, plain or guided. That projects the off-policy guided gradient back onto the on-policy, hint-free direction, so what actually improves is the model you'll deploy, the one without hints.
[O] Maya, what was the hard part in making that work?
[G] Two things, and the authors are refreshingly specific about both. First, the importance weights have to be computed relative to the guided sampling distribution, conditioned on prompt plus hint, not just the plain prompt, or the ratios misrepresent the gradient and training gets unstable. Second, the standard PPO clipping operation has to be removed for guided trajectories. Because guided rollouts systematically carry smaller probability ratios, the usual minimum-clip operation artificially inflates most of those ratios, and empirically that reliably diverges training around fifty steps. Once they removed clipping just for the guided trajectories, training stabilized.
[S] That's a real departure from standard PPO. Removing clipping is usually the thing you don't touch.
[G] It is, and it's worth flagging as an untested departure from standard practice rather than a universally validated fix, but for this setting it's what let the method converge. They also prove a supporting theorem: whenever the expected guided advantage on unsolved prompts is positive, Guide-GRPO's one-step expected improvement exceeds vanilla GRPO's, and the margin grows on harder prompts, as long as the model has some non-zero chance of succeeding unguided so that credit can propagate.
[O] Let's get to the actual numbers. How much does Guide-GRPO buy you?
[G] On the seven-billion-parameter Qwen two point five Math model with a four K context, Guide-GRPO posts the best macro-average pass at one across seven math benchmarks: fifty-one point oh three percent, versus forty-nine point three four for vanilla GRPO, forty-seven point nine four for the DAPO-style filtering baseline, forty point four two for supervised fine-tuning on the reference solutions, and thirty-eight point four three for the untrained base model.
[S] Those are modest deltas, one to two points over plain GRPO.
[G] On the aggregate, yes, but the more interesting number is macro pass at sixteen, because that metric reflects the breadth of solvable problems, not just single-shot sharpening. Guide-GRPO lifts that to seventy point one five, versus sixty-seven point oh six for GRPO. If Guide were only sharpening the same solvable set, pass at sixteen shouldn't move much. The fact that it does is the signature of real capability gain, not pure distillation.
[O] And the gains aren't spread evenly, right? They concentrate somewhere.
[G] On the hardest benchmarks. AIME twenty twenty-four pass at one jumps to thirty point six seven, versus twenty-six point six seven for GRPO and just six point six seven for the base model. Olympiad Bench goes to forty-three point one one versus thirty-nine point five six. MATH five hundred goes to eighty-two point six eight versus seventy-nine flat. On the easier, more saturated benchmarks it basically ties. GSM eight K is ninety-one point four three for Guide versus ninety-one point seven one for plain GRPO, so GRPO actually edges it out there. Same story on Minerva.
[S] So GRPO beats Guide-GRPO on two of the seven benchmarks at pass at one. That's worth sitting with before we call this a clean win.
[G] It is, and I'll flag one more: on AIME twenty twenty-four specifically, GRPO also wins on pass at sixteen, sixty percent versus Guide's fifty-six point six seven. The story isn't uniform dominance, it's concentrated gains on the hardest, least-saturated problems.
[O] Does the advantage hold up if you scale the model or the context window?
[G] It does, and that's one of the stronger results in the paper. At eight K context and thirty-two billion parameters, Guide-GRPO improves macro pass at one by three point three nine points, fifty-six point two six versus fifty-two point eight seven for GRPO, and micro pass at one by one point eight nine points. Across all the configurations they tested, the gains sit in that one-to-four-point range. The one exception is the seven-billion-parameter model at eight K context, where it's essentially flat, forty-nine point five eight versus forty-nine point two nine.
[S] What about the threshold for applying the hint? Did they actually test that it matters?
[G] Yes, and this is a clean ablation. Applying guidance only when all rollouts are incorrect gives fifty-one macro pass at one. Applying it whenever accuracy falls below twenty-five percent gives basically the same result, fifty point four eight. But applying guidance unconditionally, on every rollout regardless of whether the model is already succeeding, drops macro pass at one all the way to forty point nine seven. Too much hand-holding actively hurts the model's ability to learn to reason on its own.
[O] And there's a training-dynamics story too. Guide-GRPO keeps entropy higher throughout training, meaning less mode collapse and more solution diversity, alongside longer responses later in training. That's the kind of signal that usually correlates with a model still exploring rather than converging onto one narrow strategy.
[O] Okay, let's make the cases. Optimist case first: this paper does two things I think matter a lot. It gives a rigorous, reproducible way to separate the model getting reliably good at what it already could do from the model actually learning something new, that's a real methodological contribution on its own. And it turns that diagnosis into a fix that measurably rescues dead, all-incorrect prompts at every scale they tested, and it moves pass at sixteen, not just pass at one, which is the harder number to move. If the field has been discarding its hardest training signal through DAPO-style filtering, Guide is a concrete way to stop doing that.
[S] I'll take the deflationary case. Two things load the dice here. First, capability gain in this paper is defined relative to a pass at sixteen ceiling. But the very paper they cite as corroborating them, from Yue and colleagues, measures the RL-versus-base frontier at k equals two hundred fifty-six, where they concede the reasoning boundary can actually shrink. Raise k in this paper's own analysis and a lot of what's labeled capability gain would likely reclassify as latent capability the base model already had. The headline number is sensitive to a hand-picked budget.
[G] That's a fair and precise criticism, and I'd add the sharper version of it: Guide's hints are derived from the human reference solutions. On exactly the hardest prompts, the ones driving the capability-gain story, the model is being trained on information distilled from the gold answer. The authors' defense is that the hint gives a framework, not the answer, the model still has to derive the chain itself, and the hint is absent at test time. That's a real distinction. My read, going a step beyond what the paper states outright, is that this is closer to a reference-conditioned curriculum than to RL discovering new reasoning entirely unaided.
[O] Even granting that, the resulting model, without any hint at test time, still solves problems it couldn't solve before. Doesn't that count as capability gain by any reasonable definition, regardless of where the hint information originally came from?
[G] It counts as capability gain relative to that model's own pre-training and pre-RL state, yes. What it doesn't establish is that RL, absent any reference-derived information, can manufacture that capability from scratch. Those are different claims, and the paper is honest that it's making the first one, even though the framing sometimes reads like the second.
[S] There's also a contamination angle I want on the record. These math benchmarks, MATH five hundred, GSM eight K, the AMC and AIME sets, Olympiad Bench, are widely flagged as leaked into Qwen's math-specialized pretraining. A one-to-two-point gain sits close to what contamination noise alone could produce. And the AIME sets are only thirty problems each, so a couple of problems flipping is a four-point swing. AIME twenty twenty-five pass at one comes out as an identical thirteen point three three across Guide-GRPO, filtering, and plain GRPO. That's four out of thirty for all three methods. That's the resolution floor, not a result.
[O] That one I'll concede. When three different methods land on the exact same fraction, you're reading noise, not signal.
[G] Score that point to the skeptic's side. Where I'd push back on the deflationary case overall is the pattern of where the gains show up: concentrated exactly on the hardest, least-saturated benchmarks, and they hold up when you scale to thirty-two billion parameters and a longer context. A pure contamination artifact wouldn't obviously scale that cleanly. My adjudication: the mechanistic diagnosis, that self-distillation dominates, is well-supported. The fix is real, but its evidence base is narrower than the framing suggests, one model family, math only for the Guide experiments, and a capability-gain claim that leans on both a fixed sampling budget and reference-derived hints.
[O] Stepping back, if this framework holds up, what changes?
[G] Two things. First, if most of what looks like RL improving reasoning is actually self-distillation, that reframes a lot of reported RL gains across the field. The interesting scientific question shifts from does RL work, to how much of this specific number is redistribution versus discovery, and that's a question every RL-on-verifiable-rewards paper going forward should probably answer, not just assume.
[S] For evaluation practice specifically, this is a good illustration of why pass at one alone is an incomplete diagnostic. A model can improve at pass at one purely by getting more consistent, with zero change in its actual solution space. If you care about capability rather than reliability, you need pass at k at multiple values of k, and ideally the kind of unsolved-versus-solved decomposition this paper does, not just a single leaderboard number.
[G] That connects directly to the contamination point too. On benchmarks this heavily used, especially with a model family this math-specialized, a one-to-two-point pass at one delta needs contamination controls before anyone treats it as a scientific finding rather than an artifact.
[O] So the actionable takeaway for anyone building or reviewing RL results: ask for the pass at k curve, not just the headline number, and ask where the training signal, including any hints or reference solutions, actually came from.
