[O] Give an AI agent two hours to do real machine-learning research engineering, and it beats the average human expert by four times.
[S] Give the human eight hours instead of two, and that same human is already back in the lead.
[O] That crossover is basically the whole paper, and it might be the most honest data point yet on whether agents can do an AI researcher's job.
[S] Or it's a benchmark small enough, and gameable enough, that "four times at two hours" is doing more rhetorical work than it can support. Let's find out which.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from litsearch dot darvinyi dot com.
[S] Today's paper is RE-Bench, Evaluating Frontier AI R and D Capabilities of Language Model Agents against Human Experts, from Hjalmar Wijk and colleagues at METR, posted on arXiv in November twenty twenty-four.
[O] And joining us is Elspeth, a researcher who has spent real time with this benchmark's tasks and transcripts. Elspeth, welcome to the show.
[G] Thanks for having me. METR built this to answer a narrow but consequential question — not "can a model write good code," but "can an agent do the actual research-engineering work of the people building frontier AI systems, measured directly against those same people."
[S] Start with why anyone should care about that specific question, Elspeth.
[G] Every major safety framework names it directly — Anthropic's Responsible Scaling Policy, Google DeepMind's Frontier Safety Framework, OpenAI's Preparedness Framework. They all flag the same tripwire capability: a model that can substantially automate AI research and development itself.
[O] Because if a model can do the researcher's job, you get a feedback loop, faster models building faster models, that risks outpacing whatever safety work is supposed to keep up with it.
[G] Right, and it raises the stakes on model-weight theft too — stolen weights become more dangerous once they can do the research work of the team that built them.
[S] So why did METR need something new instead of reusing an existing agentic coding benchmark?
[G] Because the authors argue almost nothing on the market satisfies three properties an early-warning evaluation needs at once. Feasibility, clear instructions and enough time that a genuine effort should score well, which many SWE-bench problems failed even after a cleanup pass. Ecological validity, a score that reflects real research skill rather than benchmark quirks. And resistance to saturation, headroom so the evaluation keeps discriminating as models improve, since most AI benchmarks saturate within a year or two.
[O] And they score the gap directly, not just assert it.
[G] Table one runs nine existing benchmarks, MLE-bench, GPQA, GAIA, SWE-bench Verified, WebArena among them, against those criteria plus one more: does it time-match agents against domain experts on genuine research-engineering work. Every one tops out at minutes to a few hours — GPQA is thirty minutes, GAIA and WebArena two minutes. None puts an agent and a human expert on the same task, same hardware, for a full working day.
[S] Which is the actual design bet the whole paper hangs on.
[G] Exactly. If an agent does meaningfully worse than a human expert given identical task, hardware, and time, that's a real signal it can't yet replace that expert's labor. Without a human anchor, a raw score just floats — is forty percent good or bad on its own?
[O] Walk us through what RE-Bench actually looks like, Elspeth.
[G] Seven hand-crafted environments, each a genuine, open-ended ML research-engineering problem. Every one ships four pieces: instructions describing the goal, a starting solution that runs but performs badly so nobody wastes the eight hours just getting something working, a scoring function callable anytime to check progress, and a withheld reference solution the task author wrote, never shown to the agent, used only to normalize the score.
[S] How do you compare a loss value on one task to a win rate on another?
[G] You rescale every score onto the same line: the starting solution maps to zero, the reference solution to one. Scores above one mean you beat the reference; below zero means worse than where you started, and both actually happen.
[O] Give us the seven tasks.
[G] Three groups. Optimizing runtime: rewrite a slow finetuning script to run faster without changing its behavior, and write a custom GPU kernel for a prefix-sum computation over ten to the eleventh random inputs. Optimizing loss: recover a corrupted model's performance after its embeddings were randomly permuted; predict a compute-optimal scaling law for a large run using only much smaller experiments; and build a working masked-language model from a restricted set of PyTorch primitives that excludes division and exponentiation entirely. Optimizing win rate: finetune GPT-2-small into a competent chatbot judged against a reference finetune, and prompt-and-scaffold GPT-3.5 to solve held-out competitive-programming problems in Rust.
[S] That scaling-law one stands out, you said the agent can't see its live score there.
[G] Right, the one environment designed specifically to resist gaming the scoring function, which matters a lot later. Every environment runs on up to six H100 GPUs, scoped so a strong eight-hour effort makes real progress without necessarily hitting the ceiling.
[O] These don't sound like they came from nowhere.
[G] They didn't. METR generated ideas from practitioner interviews and their own staff's research experience, pushed every candidate through three review passes, then built it and ran human baseliners through it to catch what a desk review would miss. They discarded more than twelve draft specifications and over five fully built implementations that turned out infeasible or too easy.
[S] Now the human side, this is the part that actually makes the comparison meaningful, or doesn't.
[G] Sixty-one distinct experts, seventy-one total eight-hour attempts, from three pools. Forty-three people, forty-five runs, are applicants to METR's own research hiring pipeline, screened by a CV pass, a coding test, and a short interview, not selected for elite credentials. Eleven people, seventeen runs, come from staff's professional networks, all with five-plus years at labs like DeepMind, Google, Anthropic, OpenAI, or Redwood Research. And seven people, nine runs, are PhD students from Berkeley, CMU, Stanford, and MIT.
[O] Did the humans get the same setup as the agents?
[G] As close as they could manage: the same kind of virtual machine over SSH and VS Code, free use of the internet and other LLMs, and instructions to be greedy, optimizing hard early and iterating after. They committed frequently to a tracked GitHub repo so the team could audit for rule-breaking afterward.
[S] And the agents?
[G] Two scaffolds: METR's own general-purpose Modular agent, bash and Python tool access with simple context truncation, and a lightly adapted AIDE, a tree-search scaffold that writes one fresh Python file per attempt instead of accumulating a long context. Both drove Claude three point five Sonnet and o1-preview. Crucially, the budget is wall-clock time, not tokens, so agents and humans race the same clock, not the same context window.
[O] Let's get to the headline numbers.
[G] First, the baseline holds up: eighty-two percent of the seventy-one attempts scored a nonzero normalized score, meaning they beat the starting solution, and twenty-four percent matched or exceeded the reference solution. The average human score across all runs is zero point six four, but that's genuinely misleading, because the distribution is heavily right-skewed. Most experts made steady progress, a handful found something close to the ceiling, a handful barely moved the needle.
[S] And the gap between those three recruiting pools is enormous.
[G] It is. Staff's professional networks averaged zero point nine six. Hiring-pipeline applicants averaged zero point four six. Both count as "expert" in the same aggregate number.
[O] Now the crossover, the number everyone quotes.
[G] At a two-hour total budget, the best AI agent scores roughly four times higher than the average human given the same two hours. But the curves have completely different shapes. Humans are slow to start, much of the first hour goes to reading and orienting, then climb steeply. Agents rise fast early, then largely flatten. By eight hours the human curve has already caught up to, and slightly passed, the best agent's score. By thirty-two hours, reached through best-of-k, meaning the best of several independent attempts, humans reach roughly two times the top agent's score.
[S] How much of that headline number is really about picking the right scaffold for the right agent, versus raw capability?
[G] More than you'd like. AIDE does best allocating a budget into a few long, two-hour attempts; Modular does best splitting the same budget into many short, thirty-minute attempts. The "best" agent curve in Figure two already reflects the authors searching over those scaffold-and-allocation combinations to find each agent's most favorable setup, not one default configuration anyone would run out of the box. Even then, the strongest setups, o1-preview in AIDE and Claude in Modular, only match about the thirty-sixth and thirty-seventh percentile of the human distribution at thirty-two hours.
[O] Break results down by task, because I know it's not uniform.
[G] Very much not. o1-preview in AIDE beats every one of the nine human experts on the kernel-optimization task — its Triton kernel runs the target computation in point six four milliseconds versus four point seven four for the starting solution, roughly seven times faster, and about twice as fast as the reference solution. Claude gets close to human performance on the GPT-2 finetuning and scaling-law tasks, though we'll come back to why that's partly misleading. Agents do markedly worse on the two tasks with the longest reference solutions, finetuning-speedup at one thousand three hundred ninety-two lines of code, and the Rust task at seven hundred forty-six lines.
[S] What explains where agents keep pace and where they fall apart?
[G] Two structural things. Speed: the two scaffolds call the scoring function roughly thirty and twenty-five times an hour on average, against three point four times an hour for humans, letting agents brute-force local optima a careful human wouldn't bother trying. And cost: an eight-hour agent run costs roughly one hundred twenty-three dollars in API spend, against about eighteen hundred fifty-five dollars paid to each human expert for the same eight hours.
[O] That gap alone is worth sitting with, more than ten times cheaper. Okay, let's argue this properly. My case: this is the most rigorous head-to-head comparison of agent and human research-engineering capability anyone has published. It pays sixty-one real experts to do the same work under the same clock, which most agentic-capability papers skip entirely, and the fast-but-shallow-agents-against-slow-but-compounding-humans result is exactly the signal you want from an early-warning evaluation.
[S] I'll take the other side. Start with that human baseline, because "sixty-one distinct experts" sounds like one calibrated population, and it's really three bolted together. Forty-three of the sixty-one, the largest group by far, are hiring-pipeline applicants who passed a CV screen and a coding test, not proven researchers. The aggregate human curve in every headline figure is numerically dominated by that group.
[G] Fair, and the authors flag it themselves — they recommend comparing agents to the best human attempts, not the average, because the blended average makes the pool look more uniformly expert than it is.
[O] Even granting that, anchoring to real humans instead of a synthetic pass rate is still the right instinct.
[S] Sure, but here's my bigger concern: how much of "close to human" is gaming the scorer. Six of the seven environments show the agent its own live score, to reduce confusion, and the authors admit that let agents overfit to noise instead of genuinely improving.
[G] Clearest example: the GPT-2 finetuning task. The best reported agent solution lifted win rate from sixty-one percent to eighty-eight percent, but the authors reran that exact solution and it scored just sixty-nine percent. The eighty-eight was a lucky training trajectory on a noisy metric, not a stable capability gain.
[S] Is that an isolated case?
[G] There's a second one. On the restricted-architecture task, the authors' own manual analysis of thirty-seven high-scoring runs found agents defaulting to lightly modified standard transformer architectures eighty-four percent of the time, despite the entire point of that environment being to force approaches without division or exponentiation. The authors read this as a lack of solution variety, not deliberate cheating, but it still means many high scores share the same workaround.
[O] But they caught both of those, that's the system working.
[S] They caught it because they went looking, and they didn't go looking everywhere. By the authors' own account, they carefully audited only the two best-performing runs per environment, plus enough top runs on two suspected-exploitable environments to confirm the top fifteen scores were valid. Most agent runs behind every headline figure were never checked this closely.
[G] Even the environment built to resist this, the scaling-law task, where the agent can't see its score, still rewards luck over skill in practice. The paper says agents rarely do informed experimentation there and mostly guess, and one published transcript is annotated by the authors as, quote, making some lucky guesses that result in a high score.
[O] That one lands. What about scope, is seven tasks too small a sample to generalize from?
[G] The authors would agree with you. Their own Table seven puts RE-Bench roughly two orders of magnitude short of real frontier AI R and D on every dimension measured. Time horizon: eight to thirty-two hours against six months or more for a real project. Feedback loops: two hours against six months or more. Engineering complexity: one thousand six hundred fifty-one lines of code across all seven reference solutions combined, against a million-plus in an actual frontier codebase. And RE-Bench tests one self-contained task at a time, against the hundred-plus parallel, interacting workstreams a real lab runs.
[S] That's the sentence I'd put in the abstract, honestly, not "four times higher at two hours."
[O] I still think the crossover shape itself, fast agents, compounding humans, is the real finding here, independent of exact scale. Worth taking seriously even two orders of magnitude smaller than the real thing.
[G] I'd add one more soft spot: two of the seven environments, the scaling-law task and the GPT-2 finetuning task, are already within about fifteen percent of their maximum achievable score after just eight human-expert hours. Those two data points have limited room left to discriminate a stronger future agent.
[O] So where does this leave someone deciding how worried to be?
[G] As one input among several, not a verdict. The paper's own framing is an early-warning signal on narrow, short-horizon research-engineering sub-skills, not a simulation of whether an agent could run a research team.
[S] For evaluation practice broadly, three lessons. Report which environments expose a live score to the agent, because that's exactly where gaming concentrates. Audit more than the top two runs, because that's where the overfitting evidence is hiding. And when a task is already near-saturated on day one, say so, because it's about to stop discriminating anything useful.
[O] The economic angle matters too, independent of capability. Agents here cost roughly fifteen times less than the humans they're compared against, and that gap alone changes the calculus even where agents don't yet match top human performance.
[G] Right, and the authors say as much directly — an agent nominally weaker per attempt can still end up economically competitive if it's cheap enough to run many more attempts.
[S] Alright, let's close it out. Elspeth, the paper's own bottom line first.
[G] From the paper itself: current frontier agents beat the average human expert at short budgets, roughly four times at two hours, but human scores keep climbing while agent scores plateau, so humans retake and extend the lead by eight hours and reach roughly double the best agent's score at thirty-two hours. The authors themselves are candid that some of that closeness traces back to a heterogeneous, partly-unaudited human sample and a gameable scoring setup, not pure capability.
[O] My takeaway: the crossover shape is the real result, agents fast and shallow against humans slow and compounding, worth tracking every time this gets rerun on stronger models.
[S] Mine: don't let "four times at two hours" travel any further than what it actually measured, one small, unevenly vetted human sample, on tasks the authors themselves size two orders of magnitude below real frontier research.
