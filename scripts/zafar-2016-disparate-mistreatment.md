[S] Two classifiers can both pass every fairness test regulators were using in 2016, and one of them can still be wrong about black defendants at more than twice the rate it's wrong about white defendants.
[O] That's the exact tension this paper walks into. On ProPublica's COMPAS recidivism data, an unconstrained classifier gets a false positive rate of thirty-five percent for black defendants and seventeen percent for white defendants.
[S] And neither of the two legal fairness notions on the books at the time catches that gap. A classifier can treat identical people identically, produce proportional outcomes overall, and still be systematically wrong in one direction for one group.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a guest take one paper apart. Today's paper is "Fairness Beyond Disparate Treatment and Disparate Impact: Learning Classification without Disparate Mistreatment," by Muhammad Bilal Zafar, Isabel Valera, Manuel Gomez Rodriguez, and Krishna Gummadi, presented at the Web Conference in 2017.
[O] Joining us is Colin, a visiting scholar who's studied this line of work closely. Welcome, Colin.
[G] Glad to be here. What makes this paper worth an episode isn't a leaderboard number — it's that it cleanly separates a third failure mode from two everyone already used, in the same few months two other papers were proving hard limits on exactly this space.
[S] Let's build the two existing notions first, since the whole paper is defined by what they miss. Disparate treatment — what is it, formally?
[G] The paper writes it as a probability statement: the classifier's output shouldn't change when you reveal the sensitive attribute, holding the rest of the features fixed. Practically, it means two people with the same résumé, the same clothing description, the same criminal history, shouldn't get different decisions just because one is coded as a different race or gender than the other.
[O] That's the intuitive one — equal treatment for equal cases.
[G] Right, and disparate impact is the second, older idea, going back through employment discrimination law. It says the classifier's positive-outcome rate shouldn't differ between groups, full stop, regardless of why.
[S] Which sounds like the stronger fairness guarantee, honestly.
[G] Stronger in one sense, but wrong once you trust your ground truth. Disparate impact never asks whether a decision was correct, only whether outcomes were proportional — the right question when historical labels themselves might be biased, as in hiring or lending. But with trustworthy ground truth, forcing proportional outcomes penalizes accuracy for its own sake — the paper cites Ricci versus DeStefano, where courts ruled that kind of forced proportionality unlawful.
[O] So the paper's move is: once you trust the labels, disparate impact stops being the right question, and a third question opens up that neither existing test asks.
[G] Exactly, and they call it disparate mistreatment: do the misclassification rates themselves differ by group? Not the outcomes, not the treatment rule — the error rates.
[S] Walk us through the toy example the paper opens with, the three classifiers on stop-and-frisk decisions.
[G] A small constructed example: three fictitious classifiers deciding whether to stop a pedestrian for a suspected weapon, using clothing bulge and proximity to a recent crime, against ground truth on whether the person actually had one. C3 stops two people with identical non-sensitive features differently, purely because their gender differs — disparate treatment, full stop — yet has equal false positive and negative rates across genders, so no disparate mistreatment.
[O] So C3 fails the old test but passes the new one.
[G] C1 does the reverse: it treats every pair with identical features identically — no disparate treatment — but its false negative rate is zero for men and fifty percent for women. Neither existing notion flags that. The paper's point: these three notions are logically independent — you can satisfy any two while failing the third.
[S] And this isn't hypothetical — the paper cites a study by Goel and colleagues finding higher false-positive weapon-suspicion stops for black pedestrians in NYPD's stop-and-frisk data, landing right alongside ProPublica's COMPAS investigation making a structurally similar claim about recidivism scores.
[G] Right, both real-world cases are exactly the disparate-mistreatment pattern — equal or defensible treatment rules, but error rates that land unevenly.
[O] Okay, so we have the definition. Now, how do you actually train a classifier against it? That's where I want you to slow down, Colin, because this is the part that makes it a genuine method paper and not just a naming exercise.
[G] The paper defines five ways to measure it: overall misclassification rate, false positive rate, false negative rate, false discovery rate, and false omission rate. The first three condition on the true label — did we get this person's outcome wrong. The last two condition on the predicted label instead — of everyone flagged positive, how often were we wrong. The method targets overall rate, false positive, and false negative rate, since those are tractable; false discovery and omission rates are flagged as harder and left for future work.
[S] Tractable is doing a lot of work in that sentence. What's actually hard about it?
[G] The natural way to write the constraint — force the false positive rate for group zero to equal the false positive rate for group one, inside the training objective — is non-convex in the classifier's parameters. You can't just drop that into a standard solver and expect a global optimum.
[O] So what's the trick?
[G] A proxy. Define a function of each point — call it g of theta — the signed distance to the decision boundary, clipped to zero whenever correctly classified. So it's nonzero only where the boundary is wrong, and its magnitude tracks how wrong. Disparate mistreatment is then measured as the covariance between the sensitive attribute and that function, across the training set.
[S] Why covariance specifically?
[G] Because it cleanly asks whether being in one group correlates with how badly the boundary misclassifies you — and critically, that function is convex in the parameters, even though the original rate-equality constraint wasn't. Splitting the sum by group turns it into a difference of two convex functions, a disciplined convex-concave program, or DCCP — a well-studied class with efficient heuristic solvers, here one from Shen, Diamond, Gu, and Boyd, published the same year.
[O] So you're not solving the fairness constraint exactly, you're solving a convex relaxation of it that gets close.
[G] Correct, and the paper is explicit a near-zero covariance is neither necessary nor sufficient for exactly equal rates — an approximation, not an identity. Two variants of that clipping function let a practitioner target false positive or false negative rate specifically, whichever error matters most.
[S] There's one more piece I want on the record before we get to results — the disparate treatment story, because that's the paper's actual novelty claim over its closest competitor.
[G] Right. This constraint only needs the sensitive attribute during training, never inside the feature vector at decision time. Keep the sensitive feature and the rest disjoint, and the same run gives you no disparate treatment for free, alongside no disparate mistreatment. The paper contrasts this with a concurrent method by Hardt, Price, and Srebro, which post-processes an unfair classifier's outputs with per-group decision thresholds — that method needs the sensitive attribute at prediction time by construction, so it can never avoid disparate treatment.
[O] That's a real structural advantage, and it's a nice example of two papers landing on almost the same fairness target from different directions in the same few months.
[S] Let's get to whether it actually works. Start with the synthetic data, since that's where the mechanism is cleanest to see.
[G] The first setup generates ten thousand synthetic points where one group's negative-class features differ meaningfully. An unconstrained classifier hits eighty-five percent accuracy, with a false positive rate of twenty-five percent for one group and six for the other, and essentially no false-negative gap. Tightening the covariance threshold toward zero converges the false positive rates, and accuracy falls to about eighty percent — roughly a five-point cost to close that gap.
[O] That's a real trade-off, but a legible one — you're paying five points of accuracy for a big reduction in a specific, named unfairness.
[S] What happens once both false positive and false negative rates are unequal at the same time, which is the more realistic case?
[G] Two scenarios. When the gaps point opposite directions — one group over-flagged, the other under-flagged — constraining just the false positive rate rotates the boundary enough to also bring the false negative rate into near balance, almost for free, nearly matching what constraining both directly achieves. When the gaps point the same direction, meaning one group is just harder to classify overall, fixing one rate can worsen the other, and closing both costs far more — accuracy drops from eighty percent to sixty-nine for the paper's own method.
[O] So the geometry of the unfairness — same-direction gaps versus opposite-direction gaps — determines how expensive fixing it is.
[G] That's the finding, yes, and it's a genuinely useful piece of intuition, not just a numeric result.
[S] How does it stack up against the alternatives — the post-processing method and a naive baseline?
[G] A self-constructed reweighting baseline retrains the classifier, iteratively increasing the penalty on the worse-off group's misclassified points until the gap closes. On the hardest synthetic case, it collapses to thirty-eight percent accuracy — worse than a coin flip — under both constraints. The paper's own method gets sixty-nine percent there, Hardt and colleagues sixty-seven.
[O] Thirty-eight percent is a striking failure for the baseline — that's a strong point in favor of the covariance formulation being the right mechanism, not just one workable option among several.
[S] Now the real dataset — COMPAS. Set the scene, Colin.
[G] ProPublica compiled COMPAS recidivism-risk data from Broward County, Florida, restricted to the black and white subset — five thousand two hundred seventy-eight offenders. Two-year recidivism was fifty-two percent for black defendants, thirty-nine for white. Using five features — age category, gender, race, prior offense count, and charge degree — an unconstrained classifier reaches sixty-six point eight percent accuracy, with a false positive rate of thirty-five percent for black defendants versus seventeen for white, and a false negative rate of thirty-one versus sixty-one — black defendants hurt on false positives, white defendants on false negatives, simultaneously.
[O] And after applying the fairness constraints?
[G] Every constrained variant costs under one accuracy point, while cutting the false-positive gap from eighteen points to three or six, and the false-negative gap from thirty points to roughly ten to fourteen. Neither gap fully closes, which the paper attributes to the modest training set — about twenty-six hundred rows — limiting how precisely the covariance can be estimated.
[S] Near-free accuracy for a large fairness improvement — that's the headline number everyone will remember from this paper.
[O] Genuinely strong — under one point of accuracy for cutting the worst gap by two-thirds or more.
[S] I want to push on what that COMPAS result is actually testing, though, because I don't think it's the full method.
[G] You're right to flag that. Footnote seven states that for COMPAS, race is included as a learnable feature, and all methods are assumed to have access to the sensitive attribute at decision time. So the numbers we just walked through are the variant that permits disparate treatment — not the disjoint-features version the rest of the paper builds toward.
[O] So the "avoid both disparate treatment and disparate mistreatment at once" claim —
[G] — is validated only on the synthetic data, where the disjoint-features variant costs about five more accuracy points than the version allowed to see the sensitive attribute. On the one real dataset in the paper, that specific claim is never actually tested.
[S] That's exactly the kind of gap I look for — the headline claim and the flagship demonstration don't fully overlap.
[O] Fair, but I'd still call it a scope limitation rather than a broken result. The mistreatment reduction itself, on real data, at near-zero accuracy cost, stands on its own.
[G] Both of those things are true simultaneously, and that's a fair way to leave it.
[S] Let's do the debate properly. My strongest case: calibration. The paper cites two results — Chouldechova, and Kleinberg, Mullainathan, and Raghavan, both within weeks of this one — proving that when base rates differ across groups, as they do here, fifty-two percent versus thirty-nine, you cannot have both equalized false positive and negative rates and a well-calibrated classifier, except in a degenerate case. This paper cites those results and never reports whether its "fair" COMPAS classifiers are still calibrated. Given the base-rate gap, they mathematically cannot be — a cost it proves must exist but never measures.
[O] Let me make the strongest case the other way. This is the first paper to formalize "proportional mistakes" as its own fairness axis, distinct from proportional outcomes — a genuinely new conceptual contribution. And the mechanism is elegant: a convex-concave relaxation that plugs into any margin-based classifier, logistic regression or an SVM, without a specialized solver. That's the kind of method that gets reused for a decade, and it has — a direct ancestor of much of the constrained-fairness-optimization literature that followed.
[S] I don't dispute the conceptual contribution. I'm saying the evaluation doesn't complete the picture the paper itself sets up. There's also a label-bias question underneath all of this: both the COMPAS two-year-recidivism outcome and the NYPD "did they actually have a weapon" ground truth are treated as trustworthy targets, and the paper never audits whether those labels already bake in the disparities the method is built to remove.
[G] On calibration — the paper is candid its covariance proxy is an approximation and its DCCP solver has no global-optimality guarantee, but it's right that it stops short of reporting calibration or positive-predictive-value alongside the false-positive and negative gaps, while citing the papers that predict that cost exists. On label bias, the paper doesn't address it at all — outside its scope, but fair to hold against the empirical claim.
[O] Where does that leave the score, though? I still think the practical result — under one accuracy point for a two-thirds reduction in the worst COMPAS gap — is the more important fact for anyone deciding whether to use this.
[S] It's the more usable fact. It's not the more complete fact. Those are different claims, and the paper occasionally blurs them.
[G] That's about right. The central promise — measuring and reducing disparate mistreatment tractably — holds up. The dual promise, mistreatment and treatment removed together cheaply, is demonstrated only in simulation. And the calibration cost the paper's own citations guarantee is real is simply not on the page.
[O] Good, I'll take that as a split decision, not a knockout for either side.
[S] Agreed. Before we close, is there a connection here to how evaluation itself gets done, beyond fairness specifically?
[G] There is, and it's the part that ages best. Choosing which of the five misclassification measures to equalize — overall rate, false positive, false negative, false discovery, false omission — is a normative choice, not a technical detail. The paper's framing, that the right measure depends on which error costs more in your application, is the same discipline practitioners need whenever they collapse a metric into "accuracy" or "quality." Naming the specific error you're equalizing is most of the work.
[O] That's a good place to land. My one-line takeaway: this is the paper that gave the field a precise, trainable name for "wrong more often for one group," and that naming work outlasts any specific accuracy number in the paper.
[S] Mine: the headline COMPAS result is real and cheap, but it's the treatment-permitting variant, tested without a calibration check the paper's own citations say must be failing — read the full writeup before you treat this as a finished audit of the method.
[G] The paper's own bottom line: a flexible trade-off between disparate-mistreatment fairness and accuracy, false discovery and omission rates left for future work, and an explicit admission its solver carries no optimality guarantee. Read alongside Hardt and colleagues, Chouldechova, and Kleinberg and colleagues, this isn't an escape from the fairness-notion conflicts those papers were proving that same season — it's a new, trainable point inside it.
