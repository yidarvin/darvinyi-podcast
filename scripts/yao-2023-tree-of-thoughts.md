[S] Same GPT-4, same puzzle: take four numbers, reach twenty-four using plus, minus, times, and divide. All that changed between those two numbers is letting the model think in a tree instead of a straight line.
[O] That's not a rounding error, that's a model going from mostly failing to mostly succeeding, with zero new parameters and zero retraining.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the litsearch map. Today's paper is Tree of Thoughts, Deliberate Problem Solving with Large Language Models, by Shunyu Yao and colleagues at Princeton and Google DeepMind, published at NeurIPS in twenty twenty-three.
[S] It's on the docket because it quietly became load-bearing. A lot of what people now call test-time compute, or agents that plan and backtrack, traces straight back to this one.
[G] Thanks for having me. I've spent a lot of time with this paper, and it's a fun one — the mechanism is almost embarrassingly simple once you see it, which is probably why it spread so fast.
[O] Elena, before we get into the mechanics, what's actually broken in a plain language model that this paper is trying to fix?
[G] The authors frame it through dual-process theory from cognitive science. System One is fast and associative, System Two is slow and deliberate. A language model generating token by token, left to right, is pure System One. Every choice is locally greedy, and once a token is out, it's out. There's no mechanism to hold options open or revise course.
[S] Chain-of-thought was supposed to fix that though, wasn't it? Give the model room to reason step by step before answering.
[G] It helps, but it's still one path. Chain-of-thought samples a single chain of intermediate steps and commits to it. Self-consistency improves on that by sampling many independent chains and taking a majority vote on the final answer, but there's no exploration inside any one chain, and the majority-vote trick only works when there's a small set of possible final answers, like multiple choice.
[O] So the model can generate five different full attempts, but it can't stop halfway through one attempt, notice it's going nowhere, and branch from that exact point.
[G] Exactly, and the paper has a genuinely striking number for what that costs. On their Game of Twenty-Four task, around sixty percent of chain-of-thought runs are already doomed after the very first step, sometimes literally the first three words, something like "four plus nine," and the model just keeps reasoning anyway, because it has no way to look back.
[S] That's the kind of number that makes the case for you before you've even proposed the fix.
[G] It's their strongest motivating data point. They also reach back to nineteen-fifties AI, to Newell and Simon's idea that problem solving is search through a tree of partial solutions, where branches are operators that turn one partial solution into another. Tree of Thoughts is really an attempt to bolt that framing onto a modern language model without touching its weights.
[O] Okay, walk us through the mechanism. What actually counts as a "thought" here?
[G] A thought is a coherent chunk of language that counts as one intermediate step. A state is just the input plus the sequence of thoughts generated so far. What counts as a chunk is problem-dependent. In Game of Twenty-Four it's one arithmetic equation. In creative writing it's a whole paragraph-level plan. In crosswords it can be a single word. The rule of thumb the authors give is that a thought needs to be small enough that the model can generate several diverse candidates, but big enough that the model can meaningfully judge whether it's promising.
[S] So there's already a manual design choice baked in before any search happens. You have to decide the granularity by hand, per task.
[G] Right, and that's the first of four questions every version of Tree of Thoughts has to answer. Decomposition is the first. The second is the thought generator, how you propose the next candidates from a given state. They use two strategies. Sample several candidates independently from a chain-of-thought-style prompt, which works well when the space is rich, like writing. Or propose them sequentially within one prompt so the model doesn't repeat itself, which works better when the space is constrained, like arithmetic or word fills.
[O] And the third question is the one that's actually new here, the model judging its own partial answers?
[G] That's the state evaluator, and yes, that's the genuinely novel piece. Classical search has always needed a heuristic to know which branches are worth exploring. Deep Blue used hand-programmed evaluation functions, AlphaGo used a learned value network. This paper's move is to have the language model generate that heuristic itself, in plain language. Two ways to do it. Value each state on its own, for instance labeling a Game-of-Twenty-Four partial solution "sure," "maybe," or "impossible" after a few lookahead attempts, the model can reason that one, two, and three are simply too small to ever reach twenty-four. Or vote, where you show the model several candidate states side by side and have it argue for the most promising one, which works better when quality is hard to score in isolation, like whether a passage feels coherent.
[S] And you can sample either of those multiple times and aggregate, to make the heuristic less noisy, at the cost of more calls.
[G] Exactly, and that trade is explicit in the paper as a dial you turn.
[O] And the fourth piece is the actual search algorithm sitting on top of generate-and-evaluate.
[G] Right, and they keep it deliberately simple, just breadth-first and depth-first search, with a note that something like A-star or Monte Carlo tree search is left for future work. Breadth-first search keeps the b best states at each step, b equals five was their default, and they use it for the shallow trees, Game of Twenty-Four and creative writing, where the depth is at most three. Depth-first search dives into the single most promising state, and the moment the evaluator calls a state impossible, it prunes that whole subtree and backtracks to the parent to try the next branch instead. That's what they use for crosswords, where the tree goes ten steps deep.
[S] So conceptually the whole thing generalizes chain-of-thought and self-consistency as tiny special cases. A tree with breadth one and no lookahead is basically just chain-of-thought.
[G] That's literally how the authors describe it. Input-output prompting, chain-of-thought, self-consistency, even iterative self-refinement, all fall out as trees of limited depth and breadth. The pitch is genericity. Swap the base model, the generator, the evaluator, or the search algorithm independently, and you get a whole family of methods, with no fine-tuning at all. It's entirely prompting on a frozen model.
[O] Let's get to the numbers, because "seventy-four percent" is doing a lot of work in this paper's reputation.
[G] Start with Game of Twenty-Four, since it's the cleanest result. A hundred hard puzzles, scraped from a puzzle site and pre-sorted by how slowly humans solve them. Plain input-output prompting gets seven point three percent. Chain-of-thought actually gets worse, four percent. Self-consistency with a hundred sampled chains and majority vote only reaches nine percent.
[S] Wait, chain-of-thought is worse than plain prompting? That's an odd baseline to lead with.
[G] It's a real number from the paper, and it lines up with that sixty-percent-fail-at-step-one statistic. Once the first equation is wrong, chaining more reasoning on top of it doesn't help, it just walks further from twenty-four with more confidence. Tree of Thoughts with the smallest possible breadth, keeping only one state per step, already reaches forty-five percent. Widen it to keep the best five candidates at each step, and it's seventy-four percent.
[O] And crucially the comparison isn't just against ordinary chain-of-thought, they also built an oracle baseline.
[G] Right, and that's what makes this a fair fight rather than a trick. They computed what you'd get from best-of-one-hundred sampling, run chain-of-thought a hundred times and take the best attempt if any of them happens to be right. That oracle scores forty-nine percent. Tree of Thoughts beats that oracle by twenty-five points while visiting a comparable number of intermediate states, so the gain isn't "we sampled more," it's that guided search finds better states than blind resampling does.
[S] That's the number I actually trust, Elena — beating your own oracle is a legitimate signal.
[G] Creative writing is the softer of the three results, deliberately, since there's no ground-truth passage to check against. They score coherence two ways. A GPT-4 judge scoring one to ten, averaged over five samples, rates Tree of Thoughts seven point five six against six point nine three for chain-of-thought and six point one nine for plain prompting. Then a blind human pairwise comparison over a hundred passage pairs: raters preferred the Tree of Thoughts passage forty-one times, preferred chain-of-thought only twenty-one times, and called it similarly coherent the other thirty-eight.
[O] Two to one in actual human judgment, not just an automated score patting itself on the back.
[S] You said earlier we'd circle back to who did that human rating, and I want that now.
[G] Fair, it's worth being precise about. The comparison was blind and the passage order was randomized, but the raters were, quote, "a subset of the authors," not independent arms-length annotators. Forty-one to twenty-one is not a coin flip, but it's the softest piece of evidence in the paper.
[O] Which is a very different thing from a properly commissioned outside user study.
[G] Agreed, and it's the one result here I'd want replicated before leaning on it too hard. Then mini crosswords, five-by-five boards, twenty test games, the deepest search of the three, up to ten word-level steps with depth-first search. Plain prompting stalls badly, chain-of-thought tops out at sixteen percent of words filled correctly. Tree of Thoughts reaches seventy-eight percent of letters, sixty percent of words, and solves four of the twenty full boards outright, versus one board for chain-of-thought and zero for plain prompting.
[S] Four out of twenty games solved is not a huge number to hang seventy-four-percent-style headlines on.
[G] Agreed, and the authors run exactly the ablation that matters here. Strip out pruning, stop letting the evaluator kill off subtrees it judges impossible, and word accuracy drops from sixty percent down to forty-one and a half. Strip out backtracking entirely, so it's just a greedy fill with no recovery, and it collapses to twenty percent words, one board solved. So pruning and backtracking are each independently doing real work, not just adding compute for its own sake.
[O] And there was an oracle version for crosswords too?
[G] Yes. If instead of trusting the model's own pick of "best final state," you let an oracle read out the best state found anywhere in the whole search tree, it climbs to thirty-five percent of games solved, seven out of twenty. Which tells you something the authors are candid about, the final output heuristic is actually more of a bottleneck on this task than the search itself is.
[O] Okay, my optimist case. This is a genuine capability unlock with zero retraining, same frozen GPT-4, same weights, and you get roughly an eighteen-fold jump on a reasoning task just by changing how you sample from it. It's not a narrow trick either, input-output, chain-of-thought, and self-consistency all fall out as special cases, so this is strictly more general than everything that came before it.
[S] My skeptic case starts with the price tag the optimist case is conveniently skipping. Their own cost table says solving Game of Twenty-Four with Tree of Thoughts costs seventy-four cents a case, against forty-seven cents for best-of-one-hundred chain-of-thought and thirteen cents for best-of-one-hundred plain prompting. On creative writing it's roughly five times the completion tokens of the baselines, thirty-two cents against six or seven cents. Seventy-four percent isn't free, it's bought, and the paper says as much itself.
[G] Both of those are fair reads of the paper. I'd add the scope caveat: this is a single frozen GPT-4 snapshot from May twenty twenty-three, tested on three small, hand-built tasks with test sets of one hundred, one hundred, and twenty. And the tasks were chosen specifically because GPT-4 already fails them under standard prompting.
[O] Selected for failure feels like it stacks the deck a little.
[G] It does bias the headline number upward, and the authors' own appendix backs that up. On tasks GPT-4 is already good at, GSM8K math word problems and StrategyQA, Tree of Thoughts barely moves the needle, something like eighty-six to ninety percent on GSM8K and eighty-two to eighty-three on StrategyQA. So the honest claim isn't "Tree of Thoughts makes models smarter," it's "Tree of Thoughts recovers a lot of a model's latent capability specifically where greedy decoding was the actual bottleneck."
[S] That's a narrower and, frankly, more believable claim than the one the seventy-four percent number tends to get used for in citations.
[G] There's a second limitation worth equal airtime, the evaluator is only as good as the model doing the evaluating, and it visibly fails sometimes. There's a footnote where the crossword solver prunes a subtree that actually contained the correct answer, because GPT-4 judged a valid but obscure word, "agend," an old form of "agendum," as impossible. So the heuristic that makes the whole tree search work is itself imperfect, and that caps performance in ways that are hard to separate cleanly from the search algorithm's own limits.
[O] Which cuts against pure optimism, sure, but it also means there's headroom left. A better evaluator, or a fine-tuned one, should just make this better for free.
[G] Reasonable forecast, though it goes a step beyond what the paper tests. They explicitly leave fine-tuning the generator or evaluator for future work, and their own extension experiments show it doesn't transfer for free to a weaker model. Swap in GPT-3.5, and Tree of Thoughts on Game of Twenty-Four only reaches nineteen percent, nowhere near GPT-4's seventy-four. Mix and match, and GPT-4 generating with GPT-3.5 evaluating still gets sixty-four percent, but GPT-3.5 generating with GPT-4 evaluating only gets thirty-one. For a weaker model, the bottleneck is specifically its ability to generate good candidate thoughts, not the search wrapped around it.
[S] So the framework doesn't rescue a weak model, it amplifies a strong one that was already close.
[G] That's exactly the reading the paper's own ablation supports.
[O] So what does this actually change, if it holds up?
[G] Conceptually, more than the specific numbers do. Casting inference as tree search, with the model as its own generator, heuristic, and pruner, is the template that propagated. You can see its fingerprints in what followed: test-time compute scaling, agent frameworks that plan and backtrack over actions, and reasoning-focused reinforcement learning that's essentially trying to internalize what this paper does at inference time.
[S] Which raises the obvious next question, if you can train a model to do this search internally, do you still need the external tree at all?
[G] The paper gestures at exactly that in its own closing section, fine-tuning a model to make branching decisions directly, rather than always paying for an external search loop bolted onto a frozen model.
[O] For anyone doing evaluation work specifically, there's a methods lesson buried in here too, that oracle best-of-N baseline. Comparing against best-of-one-hundred rather than just single-sample chain-of-thought should be table stakes whenever a paper claims a search or sampling method beats a simpler one.
[S] Agreed, it's cheap and a lot of papers still skip it.
