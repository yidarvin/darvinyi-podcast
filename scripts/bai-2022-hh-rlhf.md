[S] Train a preference model on nothing but red-teaming data, then hand it a batch of ordinary helpful requests.
[O] And it does worse than useless?
[S] Worse than random guessing at picking the response a person would actually want. That's what the paper reports, in plain numbers.
[O] So helpfulness and harmlessness aren't just different skills in this system, they're actively fighting each other.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a guest scholar take one paper apart. Today it's "Training a Helpful and Harmless Assistant with Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback," from Yuntao Bai and colleagues at Anthropic, posted to arXiv in April of twenty twenty-two.
[S] It's on the docket because this is the paper that turned RLHF from a niche summarization trick into the default recipe for building an assistant — helpful and harmless, at the same time, without wrecking the underlying model in the process.
[O] Joining us to walk through it is Marcus, a researcher who's spent a lot of time with this one. Marcus, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. This paper earns the attention because it's less a single trick than a complete, studied system — two separate feedback datasets, a preference model, PPO on top of it, and an unusually honest look at exactly where the whole thing breaks.
[S] Set the table for us. What was actually missing before this?
[G] Three papers had pieces of the puzzle, but none had the full picture. InstructGPT showed reinforcement learning from human comparisons could make a model more helpful, but it leaned on a supervised demonstration stage first, capped its preference models at six billion parameters, and said almost nothing about harm. LaMDA trained for safety, but with absolute ratings and supervised learning rather than reinforcement learning, and never asked whether alignment costs capability. And Learning to Summarize had the reinforcement-learning-from-preferences machinery working cleanly, but only on one narrow task.
[O] So nobody had actually closed the loop — helpful and harmless, at once, at real scale.
[G] Right, and the authors frame the gap as three open questions. Does aligning a model make it dumber, an alignment tax you pay for safety? Can one model be both helpful and harmless at once? And how robust is reinforcement learning against a learned reward, before optimizing a preference model stops tracking what people actually want and starts gaming it instead?
[S] Those are exactly the three questions I'd want answered before trusting any assistant built this way.
[O] Walk us through the actual pipeline, Marcus.
[G] Two ingredients. First, crowdworkers hold open-ended chats with fifty-two billion parameter models. At each assistant turn they see two candidate replies. In helpfulness mode, they pick the more helpful and honest one. In red-teaming mode, they're asked to adversarially probe the model and pick whichever reply is more harmful.
[S] Wait, they're rewarding the worse response, on purpose?
[G] For data-collection purposes, yes, and it matters a lot later. The helpfulness dataset moves conversations upward, toward better behavior. The red-teaming dataset moves conversations downward, toward worse behavior, because the point is to fully map how the model can be tricked. The authors flag this asymmetry themselves as a design choice they'd reconsider.
[O] And no rubric handed to workers — they let "helpful" and "harmless" be whatever the crowd's own intuition says.
[G] Correct, and deliberate. They vetted workers by the sophistication of their dialogues, not agreement on individual labels. Roughly eighty percent of the data came from about twenty prolific MTurk workers, plus a separate pool hired on Upwork. When they later checked how often their own researchers agreed with crowdworker judgments, it came out around sixty-three percent.
[S] Sixty-three percent is basically "the ground truth has a lot of subjectivity baked into it."
[G] It's a real caveat, and we'll come back to it. From that comparison data they train a preference model — a language model with a scalar head on top, pretrained on comparisons first, then fine-tuned on the human labels. The score gap between two responses becomes a calibrated probability, through a Bradley-Terry-style logistic function, that a human would prefer one over the other.
[O] Then the actual reinforcement learning.
[G] PPO. The policy generates a full response — that's one timestep — and the preference model's score at the end is the reward. Following Learning to Summarize, they subtract a KL penalty that pins the policy near its starting point: reward equals preference score, minus a small lambda times the KL divergence from the initial policy. That lambda is point zero zero one, small enough that the authors admit it "might actually be wholly unnecessary," since the KL divergence typically stays under a hundred anyway.
[S] So the leash is mostly decorative.
[G] Mostly, here, yes. And the "initial policy" isn't a fresh checkpoint — it's a fifty-two billion parameter base model that's been context-distilled on a helpful, honest, and harmless prompt. Essentially, prompting baked permanently into the weights.
[O] What does the dataset actually look like, in numbers?
[G] Three tranches, gathered in waves from progressively better models. The base tranche: forty-four thousand helpfulness comparisons and forty-two thousand red-teaming comparisons, from the context-distilled model. Then a rejection-sampling tranche — generate sixteen candidates, keep the one the preference model likes best — fifty-two thousand helpfulness comparisons, only two thousand red-teaming. Then an "online" tranche: twenty-two thousand helpfulness comparisons and zero red-teaming, gathered by redeploying fresh RLHF models into the feedback interface roughly once a week, for about five weeks.
[S] Zero fresh red-teaming data in the online phase is a gap I want to flag right now.
[G] Fair, and it compounds a problem we'll get to shortly.
[O] You mentioned there's a robustness check baked into all this.
[G] Two of them, and they're the paper's most durable methodological contribution. First, they split the static dataset fifty-fifty and train independent preference models on each half: a train model the policy optimizes against, and a test model that only watches. If reward against the train model keeps climbing while the test model's score stalls, that's over-optimization, the reinforcement-learning analogue of a train-test gap.
[S] Does it actually happen?
[G] Yes, past roughly one hundred fifty thousand samples, the two start to diverge. Second, and this is the one people still cite years later: plot preference-model reward against the square root of the policy's KL divergence from its starting point, and you get an approximately straight line — and the lines for different model sizes run roughly parallel to each other.
[O] Why square root, mechanically?
[G] KL divergence grows at quadratic order in how far you perturb the policy, while reward grows roughly linearly in that same perturbation, at least while training stays in the small-step regime. Linear over the square root of quadratic gives you a square-root law. The useful consequence: a small model's full training curve, plus a large model's untrained starting point, can predict where the large model will land after reinforcement learning. It's also the mechanism behind why RLHF buys such a large effective jump in capability for a given model size.
[S] Let's get to what it actually bought them. Headline result?
[G] Figure one. The "online, helpful-and-harmless" model is preferred by crowdworkers far above the context-distilled baseline on helpfulness, and it stays near the top of the harmlessness ranking too. A model trained only for helpfulness gets even more helpful — but it's dramatically easier to red-team, right at the bottom of the harmlessness chart.
[O] And the alignment-tax question?
[G] This is the finding I think earns the paper its reputation. Averaged over six NLP evaluations — MMLU, Lambada, HellaSwag, OpenBookQA, ARC, and TriviaQA — RLHF hurts small models and helps large ones. Their thirteen billion and fifty-two billion parameter RLHF models come out ahead of the plain language model on zero-shot accuracy, and roughly even on few-shot. Above about ten billion parameters, it isn't a tax, it's a bonus.
[S] That's the claim I'd sanity-check hardest. What's the actual scan?
[G] Seven models, thirteen million parameters up to fifty-two billion, spaced roughly four times apart at each step — a genuine scaling study, not two convenient data points. And the authors flag their own caveat: some of the jumps look sudden and grok-like, which partly reflects a multiple-choice formatting quirk that happens to favor larger models. They surface that themselves rather than letting a reader catch it.
[O] Now, the tension you opened the show with.
[G] Quantified directly. A preference model trained one hundred percent on helpfulness data scores worse than chance on harmlessness comparisons, and the reverse holds too. During reinforcement learning, the policy's harmlessness score races to the upper tail of the harmlessness data, over-optimized, while its helpfulness score sits mid-distribution, still under-optimized. The cheap harmless behavior, refusing to engage at all, gets learned first. The expensive harmless behavior, explaining why a request is a bad idea and steering someone toward something better, what the authors informally call a "hostage negotiator," barely gets learned, because the red-teaming data structurally only ever shows what not to do, never a good example of what to do instead.
[S] That's a direct consequence of the "choose the worse response" data-collection design you flagged earlier.
[G] Exactly the same root cause. And it scales unevenly: reweighting the harmlessness loss by ten times costs the thirteen-million-parameter model seven point four percentage points of helpfulness accuracy, but costs the fifty-two billion parameter model only one point five points. Bigger preference models are simply far more robust to how you mix the data.
[O] Does redeploying fresh models weekly actually help, or is that just more data dressed up as a method?
[G] They checked that directly, because it's the obvious objection. A controlled experiment: one preference model and one reinforcement-learning run trained only on the forty-four thousand base comparisons, another trained on an equal-size mixture of base, rejection-sampled, and online data — same total example count, same hyperparameters. The online-mixture policy still wins on crowdworker Elo. So the online loop is buying something beyond raw volume, probably better coverage of the high-quality tail. On the preference-model side, test accuracy on the base, rejection-sampled, and online tranches comes in at seventy-four, seventy, and sixty-seven percent — getting harder to call as response quality goes up, which is exactly what you'd want to see.
[O] And none of this cost them on other skills?
[G] Two compatibility checks, both clean. Mixing helpful-and-harmless preference training with summarization training degrades neither skill. And running this same recipe on a model already fine-tuned for code actually improves its HumanEval pass rate, at every sampling budget tested — the instruction-following gains generalize to a skill the alignment data never touched.
[S] What about the professional-writer comparison? That's the number I've seen quoted the most, out of context.
[G] And the one the authors themselves are most careful about. Crowdworkers preferred the model's responses to Upwork-hired professional writers about fifty-six percent of the time for the helpful-and-harmless model, fifty-one percent for the helpful-only model. But the writers were answering non-adversarial, fairly easy prompts, off-distribution from the model's usual traffic, and the two RLHF models actually rank inconsistently against each other on this particular comparison. The paper reports it as a wide band on Figure one, not a point estimate, and calls it an existence proof, not a parity claim.
[S] Good, because "beats professional writers" is exactly the line that gets quoted without that asterisk attached.
[O] Alright, let's argue about what it means. I'll go first. This is the paper that showed alignment isn't a tax you begrudgingly pay, it's close to free, and above a certain scale it's a net win on capability too. It closed the loop between helpful and harmless in one system, named the reward-over-optimization problem instead of getting quietly burned by it later, and open-sourced the preference data. Nearly every serious assistant built since has either used this recipe or reacted against something it identified.
[S] I'll take the other side. Almost every number in this paper is generated by, and judged by, the same preference model. The reward is the preference model. The measure of whether RLHF helped is largely the preference model. Even "beats professional writers" is partly the policy learning to please the exact scorer evaluating it. The one signal that's genuinely independent, crowdworker Elo, comes from labelers who agree with the paper's own researchers only sixty-three percent of the time. That's a lot of noise under some fairly bold headline claims.
[G] You're both right, and the paper is honest enough to hand you the ammunition for both cases. The authors explicitly show reward and true quality diverging past roughly one hundred fifty thousand training samples, that's the train-test preference-model split doing exactly its job. They show a preference model getting fooled by a single well-chosen human example. They report that gender bias in the RLHF model correlates strongly with bias in the underlying base model, meaning the training barely touched it. None of that is buried in an appendix nobody reads, it's stated plainly in the main results.
[S] What I don't see anywhere is a fair external baseline. They compare against their own context-distilled and plain language models. InstructGPT and LaMDA get discussed at length, never benchmarked head to head.
[G] That's a fair gap, and the authors don't really dispute it, different labs, different scales, no shared evaluation harness existed at the time. What they'd say in their own defense is that this was never meant to be a leaderboard paper. It reads more like a mechanism catalogue: the preference model, the KL leash, what square-root-of-KL tells you, exactly where each piece breaks. Read as an engineering recipe with receipts, it holds up extremely well. Read as a calibrated benchmark result, almost every quantity shares the same measuring instrument.
[O] So the honest headline is "alignment is close to free, and here's a documented, if slightly circular, way to get there."
[S] With an asterisk about the size of the paper's own limitations section.
[G] Which, to their credit, is unusually candid. They say outright they're only scratching the surface of honesty as a problem, that they've studied average-case behavior and not worst-case robustness, and that they don't yet know whether the gender-bias result is a limitation of RLHF as a technique or just of their particular datasets. Very few papers this influential admit that much on the page.
[O] Given how much of the current eval landscape rests on exactly this pattern, one model scoring another model's outputs, where does that circularity concern actually go next?
[G] Straight into everything downstream that uses an LLM judge, a reward model, or a rubric grader. This paper is arguably the first clean demonstration that whatever you optimize against becomes both your reward and your evaluation, and the two need pulling apart if you want a real measurement. A lot of later reward-over-optimization and LLM-as-judge work is essentially this same lesson, generalized.
[S] It's also the direct ancestor of every "our data collection process introduced a subtle bias" postmortem you read in RLHF papers that came after it.
[G] Right down to the hostage-negotiator gap. Later work on refusal behavior and over-refusal traces straight back to this paper naming the problem first, even though it didn't solve it here.
[G] If there's one sentence, it's this: helpful and harmless are a real, measured tension, not a rounding error, and this is the paper that first quantified it, named reward over-optimization along the way, and showed that alignment training doesn't have to cost a large model anything at all.
[O] My takeaway: I came in skeptical that "alignment is essentially free" could hold up under scrutiny, and the seven-model scaling scan actually earned that claim for me, at least above ten billion parameters.
[S] Mine: trust the mechanism, discount the specific numbers. Nearly everything reported here is measured by the same instrument it's training against, and to their credit, the authors say so themselves. Go read the full writeup on the litsearch site for the figures.
