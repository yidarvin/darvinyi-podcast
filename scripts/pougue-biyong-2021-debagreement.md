[S] So set the stage, Edmund. Why did we need another stance or agreement dataset? There must be dozens already.
[G] The authors lean on a twenty twenty survey by Küçük and Can that catalogs twenty-four publicly available stance datasets, and when you look closely, the corpus is thin in exactly the ways that matter here. Only two of those twenty-four have more than seven thousand annotations, only two are annotated for agreement and disagreement specifically rather than general stance, and almost all of them provide text only — no author identity, no timing, no social graph.
[O] And the text itself is too clean, right? That's the part that jumped out at me.
[G] Exactly. Most of these datasets are claim-and-perspective pairs written in careful, structured prose. That's not representative of online debates on popular platforms, to use the authors' own phrasing, and it quietly makes the task easier than it is in the wild.
[S] What about the Twitter datasets? Those at least have a social graph.
[G] They do, and that's the closest prior work gets. But they come with two structural problems. First, they're distributed as tweet IDs, so as accounts and tweets get deleted, the dataset rots, and you can never fully reconstruct it. Second, they lean on retweets and hashtags as signal, which are Twitter-specific shortcuts that can let a model cheat the stance task rather than actually learn it.
[O] So the gap is a dataset that's simultaneously large, genuinely messy, rich in context — graph, authorship, timing — not dependent on those platform tricks, and permanently downloadable. Five properties, and apparently nothing hit all five at once.
[G] That's the pitch, and it's a fair one. None of the twenty-four datasets in that survey satisfy all five simultaneously.
[S] Walk us through how they actually built it.
[G] They pulled from Reddit using the open PushShift historical API, across five subreddits chosen to span two different kinds of online conflict: social movements and party politics. That's r slash BlackLivesMatter, r slash Brexit, r slash climate, r slash democrats, and r slash Republican. Brexit and climate are pulled from the subreddit's inception; the three US-focused forums are pulled starting January twenty twenty, to center on the George Floyd protests and the twenty twenty election.
[O] And the cleaning step is where I think this gets interesting, because they deliberately keep the ugly stuff.
[G] Right — empty, deleted, hidden, and hyperlink-only comments are dropped, but offensive content is kept on purpose, to, in their words, realistically capture the nature of online discussion. They filter out low-traffic posts, since most posts get fewer than five comments, remove comments shorter than ten or longer than one hundred words, and — this is the key structural choice — they only keep nest-level-one pairs, meaning a reply made directly to a top-level comment, as a tractable stand-in for a full thread.
[S] Why restrict to nest level one instead of modeling the whole thread?
[G] Tractability, mostly. A full thread tree is a much harder object to annotate and to model, and nest-level-one pairs still give you a clean edge for the graph: each surviving comment-reply pair becomes a directed edge between two users. Stack all those edges up per subreddit and you get a temporal, multi-edge user-interaction graph, which is really the dataset's distinguishing feature — it's not just labeled text, it's labeled text sitting on a real social network.
[O] How did they actually get the labels? This is the part I find most impressive, honestly.
[G] Labeling went through five hundred twenty-nine English-proficient annotators via Scale AI, trained on an instruction document, live webinars from an in-house subject-matter expert, and quizzes against a gold set. Each annotator carries a confidence score, calibrated on a seventy-four-pair gold standard the authors labeled themselves. Every comment-reply pair is then shown to somewhere between three and five annotators — the exact count set adaptively by the pool's average confidence against a threshold — and the majority label wins. Three-way ties get escalated to Scale's internal experts.
[S] And the annotators saw more than just the two comments?
[G] They saw the subreddit, the original post for context, the pair itself, and a glossary of subreddit-specific slang. Out of forty-nine thousand one hundred forty annotated pairs, they dropped six thousand two hundred forty-six that were labeled unsure or fell below two-thirds agreement, leaving a final forty-two thousand eight hundred ninety-four pairs — which, again, are exactly the edges of those five interaction graphs, spanning roughly twenty-three thousand six hundred users.
[O] That's a serious annotation operation for a datasets-track paper.
[G] It is, and it's worth flagging the number that undercuts the confidence a bit: only thirty-three percent of pairs reached full agreement across all annotators. That's not really a flaw, it's a measurement — it tells you how genuinely ambiguous short online exchanges are, even to trained humans looking at them carefully.
[S] What's the class balance look like?
[G] It varies by forum, which the authors treat as a feature, not noise. r slash Brexit and r slash climate skew negative — forty-two percent and forty percent disagree, respectively — while r slash BlackLivesMatter skews positive, at forty-five percent agree. On the modeling side, they fine-tune four families of pretrained encoders — BERT, RoBERTa, DeBERTa, and XLNet — feeding each the parent comment, a separator token, then the reply, and they split the data eighty-ten-ten by strict temporal order, so the test set is always the most recent data. Each configuration is trained four times with different seeds.
[O] I like the temporal split. That's the deployment-realistic choice — you're always testing on the future relative to training, which also guards against any label-era vocabulary leaking backward.
[S] Okay, numbers. Where does this land?
[G] The headline is a plateau. All nine fine-tuned model variants land in a tight window, roughly sixty-two to sixty-four percent accuracy on the three-way task. That's about twenty-two to twenty-four points above a majority-class baseline, so it's real signal, but nowhere close to solved. The two best variants, RoBERTa-large and DeBERTa-large, tie at sixty-four point one percent, and the full spread across all nine variants is only about two points, with no stable winner.
[O] So the dataset doesn't really separate one twenty twenty-one-era encoder from another.
[G] Correct — it mostly demonstrates they all hit the same ceiling. And if you drill into where BERT base uncased struggles, it's the neutral class specifically: sixty-eight point seven F1 on disagree, sixty-four point four on agree, but only fifty-two point one F1 on neutral, with just forty-four point one percent recall. The confusion matrix shows neutral pairs splitting almost evenly into false agree and false disagree predictions, twenty-eight percent each.
[S] Which tells you the model has no real handle on partial agreement or sarcasm.
[G] That's the authors' own read too — the errors cluster exactly where a human would call something a half-hearted concession or a sarcastic dig, and the model just can't hear that.
[O] Now, the transfer result. This is the one I actually find most convincing in the whole paper.
[G] It's the sharpest result here. They compare against Perspectrum, a clean, formal claim-and-perspective stance dataset, restricting both to the binary agree-or-disagree case. Train on Perspectrum, test on Perspectrum: ninety point five percent. Take that same model and test it on Reddit, and it collapses into the mid-fifties — and on the more class-imbalanced subreddits, BlackLivesMatter and climate, it doesn't even beat the most-frequent-class baseline.
[S] And it's not a one-way failure.
[G] No — train on all of DEBAGREEMENT and test on Perspectrum, and you only get fifty-seven point seven percent. These two tasks share a name, stance or agreement, but empirically there's very little transferable signal between formal and messy text.
[O] Which is the paper's real headline, I'd argue, more than the sixty-four percent ceiling.
[S] Fair. What about the context signals, the graph, the authorship, the timing? That's supposed to be the dataset's whole differentiator.
[G] This is where I'd flag something for listeners: they never actually build a context-aware model. What they have instead are two indirect probes. First, cross-subreddit training — train on the other four subreddits and test on the fifth, and on BlackLivesMatter specifically, that reaches seventy point eight percent, well above the fifty-eight point three percent you get training in-domain. Second, an ablation on r slash Brexit: reply text alone gives sixty point seven percent, both comments together give sixty-two point four percent, and the parent comment alone collapses to thirty-eight point one percent. The reply carries most of the signal, the parent nudges it upward.

[O] Stepping back, where does this actually matter for evaluation practice more broadly?
[G] Two places, I think. First, it's a clean instance of a broader eval-methodology lesson: benchmark difficulty and construct validity are not the same axis. A benchmark can look easy, like Perspectrum at ninety percent, purely because its text register makes the task easier, not because the underlying capability is solved. Second, there's a contamination clock running on this dataset that the paper couldn't have anticipated back in twenty twenty-one. The temporal train-test split genuinely guards against leakage for the encoders they tested. But this is public Reddit text from twenty fifteen through twenty twenty-one, and it's almost certainly sitting inside the pretraining corpus of any large language model released from twenty twenty-three onward. So a zero-shot score from a modern model on DEBAGREEMENT today would not be a clean comparison to the sixty-two-to-sixty-four percent this paper reports.
[S] Which is a trap I could see people falling into: look, some modern model gets seventy percent, progress, without realizing the comparison is already contaminated.
[G] Exactly the trap. Anyone reusing this benchmark against a modern model should treat it as compromised for that purpose, however useful it still is as a training resource or as the graph-plus-text object it was actually built to be.
