[G] By twenty twenty, open-domain question answering had split into two camps, and both had a ceiling. One camp is closed-book generative. Roberts and colleagues showed that a pretrained T five can just answer factoid questions from memory, no documents at all, purely recalling facts baked into its weights during pretraining.
[O] Which sounds great until you ask how big that model has to be.
[G] Eleven billion parameters, in their case, because literally every fact the model might ever need has to live somewhere in those weights. Expensive to train, and just as expensive to query, since you're running an eleven-billion-parameter model on every single question. The other camp is retrieve-then-extract: pull a handful of passages from Wikipedia, using something like Dense Passage Retrieval, or D P R, then have a BERT-style reader pull out a literal span of text as the answer.
[S] And that camp is cheap. What's the catch?
[G] A span classifier scores one passage, or a short list, at a time. It has no clean way to synthesize evidence spread across several passages. Empirically, accuracy for those systems tends to plateau once you retrieve much past ten or twenty passages, because there's no mechanism to make good use of anything beyond that.
[O] So you're stuck choosing between expensive and memorized, or cheap but structurally unable to add two facts together.
[G] That's the gap. There were also concurrent efforts already trying to fuse retrieval with generation instead of extraction — Retrieval-Augmented Generation, or RAG, and a system called SpanSeqGen. But both of those fuse a fairly small number of documents; RAG marginalizes over roughly its top five to ten. The question this paper actually asks is blunt: swap the extractive reader for a sequence-to-sequence one, and see how many retrieved passages you can feed it before it stops helping. Does it hit the same ten-to-twenty wall, or not?
[S] So what's actually different about the architecture, mechanically? Because "generative reader" by itself isn't new, RAG already did that.
[G] The mechanism here is almost aggressively simple, and that's part of the point. It's a two-stage pipeline, and only the second stage is the real contribution. Stage one just retrieves passages for the question, using either B M twenty five or a frozen D P R encoder, trained by somebody else, not fine-tuned here at all. Stage two is a sequence-to-sequence reader, initialized from a pretrained T five or BART, that turns those passages plus the question into an answer string.
[O] Okay, but that's still roughly what RAG does.
[G] Here's the actual difference. Take each retrieved passage, prepend special tokens, literally the words "question," "title," and "context," concatenate it with the question, and encode it with the T five encoder. Each passage is encoded completely independently of every other passage. Passage three's encoding never attends to a single token of passage seven.
[S] So the encoder is blind to everything except the one passage directly in front of it.
[G] Completely blind. Only after every passage has been separately encoded does anything join them together. Their token representations get concatenated into one long sequence, and it's the decoder, generating the answer autoregressively, that cross-attends over that full concatenation. That is the only point anywhere in the model where evidence from different passages can meet. Hence the name: fusion happens in the decoder. Once, late, and only there.
[O] And the reason to bother with that split, instead of just throwing every passage into one encoder together, is cost.
[G] Exactly, and it's a clean asymmetry. Encode all the passages jointly, concatenate everything and run one encoder over the whole thing, and self-attention cost scales quadratically with the total length. Encode each passage independently instead, and cost scales linearly with the number of passages, because each one only ever attends to itself. That is the design bet the whole paper rests on: give up any chance for the encoder to reason across documents, in exchange for cost that stays linear. And that is precisely what lets them push retrieval out to one hundred passages, roughly ten times what RAG or a typical extractive reader uses.
[S] Give me actual training numbers, not just "linear instead of quadratic."
[G] They fine-tune per dataset, starting from either T five base, two hundred twenty million parameters, or T five large, seven hundred seventy million. Adam optimizer, a constant learning rate of one times ten to the minus four, ten percent dropout, ten thousand gradient steps, batch size sixty four, on sixty four V one hundred GPUs. They evaluate every five hundred steps and keep whichever checkpoint has the best Exact Match on validation. Both training and testing retrieve one hundred passages by default, each truncated to two hundred fifty word pieces. NaturalQuestions and TriviaQA use D P R for retrieval; SQuAD uses B M twenty five instead, because Karpukhin and colleagues had already shown D P R specifically underperforms on SQuAD. And decoding is plain greedy decoding, no beam search, nothing forcing the output to literally be a span of the input text.
