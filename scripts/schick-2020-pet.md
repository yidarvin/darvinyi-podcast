[O] Today's paper is "Exploiting Cloze Questions for Few-Shot Text Classification and Natural Language Inference," by Timo Schick and Hinrich Schütze at LMU Munich, posted in January of twenty twenty and presented at EACL in twenty twenty-one. It introduces P-E-T, Pattern-Exploiting Training. Joining us is Julian, who's spent real time with this line of work. Julian, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. People cite this paper constantly and often misremember it slightly, so untangling what it does and doesn't show is worth doing carefully.
[S] Start with the actual problem, Julian. Why couldn't you just fine-tune normally on ten examples?
[G] Because ten examples isn't enough signal for a randomly initialized classification head to learn much of anything. The paper's own numbers make that concrete. With ten labeled examples, plain supervised fine-tuning of RoBERTa-large gets twenty-one point one percent on Yelp Reviews, a five-way task. Twenty-five percent on AG's News, a four-way task, which is exactly chance. Ten point one percent on Yahoo Questions, a ten-way task, again exactly chance. And around thirty-four percent on MNLI, a three-way task, also chance.
[O] So at ten examples, standard fine-tuning is doing nothing. It's a coin flip with extra steps.
[G] Right. There was also a separate, older line of work, starting with Radford and colleagues' GPT paper, showing you could hand a pretrained language model a natural-language hint and read off its next-token prediction to solve tasks with zero labeled examples at all. That works, but it clearly underperforms a properly supervised model once real training data exists. Nobody had combined the two ideas: a task description to help the model understand what's being asked, and actual training on the few examples you do have.
[S] What's the intuition for why a task description matters so much at this scale?
[G] The paper has a nice example. Take two labeled reviews: "This was the best pizza I've ever had," and "You can get better sushi for half the price." Now take a third, unlabeled one: "Pizza was average, not worth the price." From the two labeled examples alone, it's genuinely ambiguous which label the third one should get. But if you're told the task is specifically "does this text say anything about price," it resolves immediately. That's the whole thesis: with almost no labeled data, the task description does work that would otherwise take hundreds of examples to infer statistically.
[O] So how does PET actually operationalize "task description"? Walk us through the mechanism, Julian.
[G] Two pieces. A pattern rewrites your input as a phrase containing exactly one mask token, so it becomes a cloze question. A verbalizer maps each class label to a single vocabulary word. Together they're a pattern-verbalizer pair. Take natural language inference: does sentence A contradict sentence B? A pattern might turn "Mia likes pie" and "Mia hates pie" into "Mia likes pie? ___, Mia hates pie," with a verbalizer mapping "contradict" to "Yes" and "agree" to "No." Now the model's job isn't an arbitrary label assignment, it's just: is "Yes" or "No" the more likely word to fill that blank? That's exactly the objective a masked language model was already pretrained on.
[S] And you get an actual probability out of that, not just a guess?
[G] Yes. You take the model's unnormalized score for each verbalizer word at the mask position, softmax across the label set, and that's your class probability. Cross-entropy against the true label on the small training set is the fine-tuning loss for that one pattern's model.
[O] One pattern's model. So there are multiple.
[G] Right, the actual procedure has three steps. Step one, define several patterns that intuitively fit the task, sometimes two, sometimes six, and fine-tune a separate model per pattern on the tiny training set, three seeds each, cheap because the set is so small. Step two, use the whole ensemble to soft-label a much larger pool of unlabeled data, combining every model's scores with a distillation temperature of two, borrowed from Hinton and colleagues' knowledge-distillation recipe. Step three, fine-tune one final classifier, a standard sequence-classification head, on that soft-labeled set. That final model is what actually ships.
[S] Why not just keep the ensemble and average its predictions at inference time?
[G] Practically, a standard classifier is one model, not dozens, and distilling onto unlabeled data folds in far more than the ensemble ever saw labels for. There's also a training trick worth flagging: because the training set is so tiny, catastrophic forgetting is a real risk, so each pattern's model also trains with an auxiliary masked-language-modeling loss, weighted at just zero point zero zero zero one, vanishingly small, but it consistently helps.
[O] And the ensembling itself, is every pattern weighted equally?
[G] They test both, a uniform version where every pattern counts the same, and a weighted version scaled by each pattern's own accuracy before fine-tuning. Weighted is the default and does slightly better, though the gap is small.
[S] You mentioned an "iterative" version earlier. What does that add?
[G] That's iPET, iterative PET, and it exists because distillation has one failure mode: once the ensemble collapses into a single soft-labeled set, patterns can't correct each other, and some are much worse than others. So iPET trains several generations of models instead of one, each on a larger dataset labeled by a random quarter of the previous generation, preferring examples the ensemble is confident about. Each generation multiplies the training set by five, repeating until every model has seen at least a thousand examples, and only the final generation trains the classifier.
[O] So iPET is basically bootstrapping the training set itself before the final distillation.
[G] Exactly, and there's a genuinely striking extension: with a couple of adjustments, iPET works even with zero labeled examples, starting from untrained models and bootstrapping entirely from the ensemble's own early, low-confidence predictions.
[S] Let's get to whether any of this actually works. Julian, the headline numbers.
[G] At ten labeled examples, PET takes RoBERTa-large from that near-chance supervised baseline to fifty-two point nine percent on Yelp, eighty-seven point five percent on AG's News, sixty-three point eight percent on Yahoo, and forty-one point eight and forty-one point five percent on MNLI, for the matched and mismatched splits. iPET pushes further, to fifty-seven point six percent, eighty-nine point three percent, seventy point seven percent, and forty-three point two and forty-five point seven percent.
[O] That AG's News jump, twenty-five percent to eighty-seven point five percent, with ten examples, is the number that made me want to do this episode.
[S] It's a real number, but before we crown it, how much of that is PET's mechanism versus just having access to a big pile of unlabeled data that vanilla supervised fine-tuning never gets to see?
[G] That's the right question, and the paper has a control for it. They further-pretrain RoBERTa on Yelp's own unlabeled review text, a different way of exploiting the same extra data, and that supervised-plus-pretraining baseline still clearly trails PET at every training-set size, so unlabeled text alone doesn't explain the gap. But the ablation runs only on Yelp, because language-model pretraining is expensive, so whether it holds for AG's News, Yahoo, or MNLI is untested.
[S] So the strongest control they ran, they ran on one dataset out of four.
[G] Correct, and I think that's a fair thing to flag rather than wave away.
[O] What about zero labeled examples at all?
[G] Zero-shot iPET, no labels, just the bootstrapped ensemble, reaches fifty-six point seven, eighty-seven point five, seventy point seven, and fifty-three point six to fifty-four point two percent, across Yelp, AG's News, Yahoo, and MNLI. On AG's News, that zero-shot number actually beats standard supervised fine-tuning with a thousand labeled examples, which only reaches eighty-six point nine percent.
[S] That's the most surprising single result in the paper to me, and also the one I'd want replicated somewhere outside these four datasets before fully believing it.
[G] Worth adding, the gap doesn't vanish as you scale up. At a thousand examples PET still beats supervised fine-tuning on Yelp and Yahoo by a few points apiece, and by a wide margin on MNLI, eighty-five percent against around seventy-four. On AG's News specifically the two methods are essentially tied by then.
[S] Now, how does this stack up against actual competing methods, not just plain supervised fine-tuning?
[G] They compare against UDA and MixText, two semi-supervised methods that were state of the art at the time, both built on back-translation, which needs a machine translation model trained on thousands of parallel sentences. Using the smaller RoBERTa-base backbone, at ten examples, UDA averages roughly forty-three percent across the four tasks and MixText roughly thirty-nine, while PET averages around fifty-eight and iPET around sixty-two, with iPET's biggest margin on Yahoo Questions, sixty-seven against MixText's twenty point six. PET and iPET win clearly on every task.
[O] And that's despite PET getting no hyperparameter tuning at all, correct?
[G] Right, and this cuts in the paper's favor on fairness grounds: the authors tuned UDA's and MixText's hyperparameters directly on the test set, because there was no dev set to tune on either way, while PET used none of its own hyperparameter choices from that test set. If anything that favors the baselines, not PET.
