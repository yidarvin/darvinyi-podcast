[G] Thanks, glad to be here. This is one of those papers that quietly reshaped how a whole subfield talks about a problem, even though on the surface it's just a categorization exercise.
[O] So before we get into the four mechanisms, why did this need writing at all? "Goodhart's Law" already existed as a phrase.
[G] It did, but as the authors point out, it had become a catch-all. The original statement, from Charles Goodhart back in nineteen seventy-five, was about monetary policy specifically, any statistical regularity tends to collapse once you put pressure on it for control purposes. By twenty eighteen, people were using "Goodhart's Law" for almost any metric that got gamed, without distinguishing what actually broke.
[S] Which sounds harmless until you're the one debugging a reward model and you don't know whether you're looking at noisy tails, a metric breaking out of distribution, or an actual adversary gaming it. Those need completely different fixes.
[G] Right, and the paper is explicit that it's building on something informal, a twenty seventeen blog post by Scott Garrabrant called "Goodhart Taxonomy," which sketched, in his words, "at least four different mechanisms," but never formalized them. This paper's whole job is to take that sketch and make it precise enough to actually recognize in a real system.
[O] And they tie it directly to AI, which is why it's sitting in our safety and red-teaming pile and not a philosophy-of-measurement pile.
[G] Yes, the authors argue a Goodhart effect's importance scales with how much optimization power is pointed at the proxy. That's their reasoning for why this matters more for artificial intelligence than for a school district nudging test scores over years, because a training run can apply enormous optimization pressure very quickly.
[S] Which is basically a preview of the reward-hacking literature that shows up years later.
[G] Exactly, and you'll see this site's own citations pointing forward to that.
[O] Okay, let's get into the actual mechanics. What's the shared skeleton all four categories sit on top of?
[G] It's deliberately minimal. There's a goal function, the thing the regulator actually cares about, but can't observe directly. All they get is a metric, a proxy. The regulator picks a threshold and only keeps states where the metric clears that bar. Every failure mode is a different story for why that filtering stops tracking the goal.
[S] Start with the one you can't design your way out of.
[G] That's regressional Goodhart, and it's the simplest model in the paper, the metric equals the true goal plus some independent noise. Select for a high metric value, and you're selecting for high goal value and a lucky noise draw, both. So among the states that clear a high bar, the goal is systematically lower than the metric implies. The paper calls this "tails come apart," borrowing the phrase from an existing decision-theory discussion, and it's explicit that this one cannot be avoided. Any inexact metric collapses in the tail just from being conditioned on.
[O] So even a perfectly unbiased metric still lies to you at the extremes.
[G] Precisely, which is uncomfortable, because it means you can't fix regressional Goodhart just by being more careful about bias.
[S] What's the second one, extremal?
[G] Extremal Goodhart is about leaving the region where the metric was ever validated. The paper splits it two ways. The first is model insufficiency, a fitting problem, your metric is a simplified approximation that's fine near the data it was built on, and optimization pressure pushes you exactly where the approximation error is worst. Their machine-learning example is underfitting with a low-degree polynomial, where the higher-order terms only matter far from where you observed anything.
[O] That's basically every out-of-distribution failure story in machine learning.
[G] It is. The second flavor is different, a change in regime, and it's not a fitting problem. The metric-goal relationship is genuinely different once you're in the extreme region, no matter how well you learned the normal region. Their example is a wind-speed sensor that's systematically biased once the wind exceeds the instrument's design tolerance. The device isn't underfit, it's measuring a different physical regime entirely.
[S] So one is "you didn't model it well enough," and the other is "there was never a single relationship to model in the first place."
[G] That's a good way to put it, and the paper is careful to keep them separate even though they produce the same symptom on the surface.
[O] And the third category, causal, is where it stops being just about selecting states and starts involving an actual action.
[G] Right, and this is the part I think gets underappreciated. Causal Goodhart requires the regulator to intervene, to actually change something, not just filter which states survive. The paper works through three intervention points on a small causal diagram with a shared cause, a goal, and a metric. First, shared-cause intervention, their example is a teacher who trains general test-taking skill, a cause sitting behind two different test scores. Push that shared cause to its maximum, and you erase the causal path running through it, so whatever correlation is left is just noise. Second, intermediary intervention, the regulator fixes a variable on the chain between goal and metric, severing the link outright, the metric can move with the goal completely frozen. Third, metric manipulation, the bluntest one, the teacher just changes the recorded grade directly. That doesn't touch learning at all, it just breaks the metric as a measurement.
[S] The windmill example in the paper is the one that stuck with me, Julian, more than the classroom one.
[G] That's a good one, because it's not even about the regulator gaming anything, it's about forgetting their own actions are part of the system. Someone building a wind farm off a windspeed model has to remember that enough windmills change the local windspeed. Optimize hard enough on the original model, and you invalidate the very relationship you built the windmills to exploit.
[O] So causal Goodhart can happen with zero uncertainty and zero adversarial intent, just an intervention the model didn't account for.
[G] Exactly, and the paper flags a mirror-image failure too, where the regulator doesn't intervene but simply believes the wrong causal picture. Those "ignored cause" mistakes don't count as causal Goodhart, since nothing was intervened on, but they do make regressional or extremal Goodhart worse, because the assumed model is wrong.
[S] That's the seam I'd want pressure-tested later, because it means the boundary between causal and the other three isn't as clean in practice as the diagram suggests.
[O] And then the fourth category brings somebody else into the picture entirely.
[G] Adversarial Goodhart, yes, this is where a second actor with their own goals shows up. The paper splits it into two mechanisms. The first, adversarial misalignment, includes Campbell's Law as a named special case, and there's a clever toy model here, the agent picks its own metric, one that's uncorrelated with its true goal overall, but becomes strongly correlated exactly on the narrow subspace the regulator ends up selecting into. So the agent hijacks the regulator's own selection pressure to serve its own goal.
[S] That's a nasty result, because it means the agent doesn't even need its metric to be generally useful, just useful on the slice the regulator will actually filter down to.
[G] Exactly right. The second mechanism is the Cobra Effect, named for the probably-apocryphal story from colonial India, where a bounty on dead cobras led people to breed cobras just to kill them for the reward, increasing the cobra population the policy was meant to shrink. The paper splits this too, a normal Cobra effect, where the agent exploits a causal variable the regulator never modeled, and a non-causal Cobra effect, where the agent's incentive is aligned, but once it applies its own optimization pressure on top, you get regressional or extremal Goodhart anyway.
[O] So even a well-intentioned incentive scheme can Goodhart itself just from the agent optimizing hard against it.
[G] That's exactly the paper's point, and it's the one that should worry anyone designing an incentive for an agent smarter than the incentive designer expected.
[S] Okay, so that's eight-ish named failure modes. What's the actual evidence any of this carves reality at the joints?
[G] This is a conceptual paper, there are no experiments, so "results" here means something different, does the taxonomy actually subsume the concepts it claims to. The authors argue their split cleanly absorbs several things that used to be discussed as separate, informally related ideas, Goodhart's original monetary-economics statement, Campbell's Law from social-indicator research, the Lucas critique from macroeconomics, and the Cobra Effect from policy folklore. Their claim is that each is really naming one corner of the same phenomenon, proxy selection under increasing optimization pressure, not a genuinely separate effect.
[O] That's a real unification claim, even without a single number attached to it.
[G] It is, and to their credit the paper is upfront about what it isn't claiming. It restricts the formal treatment to the single-dimensional case, one goal, one metric, and says so directly, rather than pretending the multi-metric case just falls out for free. It also concedes its own categories, quote, "often occur together," rather than presenting as clean, separable buckets.
[S] So the honesty is good, but that's also the whole problem for me. If the categories overlap in practice, and there's no worked example running real data through the framework end to end, what stops "which Goodhart type is this" from being an unfalsifiable label you slap on after the fact?
[G] That's a fair challenge, and it isn't fully answered here. None of the modes is shown being distinguished from the others using actual measurements.
