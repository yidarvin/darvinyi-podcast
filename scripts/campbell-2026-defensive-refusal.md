[S] It's on the docket because it's a rare thing: a safety paper that measures the cost of safety, using real transcripts instead of a synthetic benchmark.

[G] Thanks for having me. This is one of those papers where the headline number is almost the least interesting part. The mechanism underneath it is what I want to dig into.
[S] Start with the gap. Why did nobody measure this before?
[G] Because the entire safety-benchmark tradition points one direction. HarmBench and TruthfulQA score whether a model complies with something dangerous, and a refusal counts as a win, full stop. Nobody was really asking how many of those refusals landed on people who weren't attackers at all.
[O] There's been some movement toward that, though. Over-refusal benchmarks exist.
[G] Right, and the authors are careful to place themselves against that prior work. OR-Bench measures over-refusal in general. FORTRESS measures over-refusal alongside jailbreak susceptibility. CyberSecEval 2 even reports something called a false refusal rate, but on synthetic capture-the-flag style prompts. What none of them had, and what this paper adds, is a refusal measurement grounded in a real, sanctioned defensive operation, not a benchmark written to probe the model.
[S] Why does that distinction matter so much to you, Maya?
[G] Because synthetic prompts are written by researchers who already know what triggers a refusal. Real defenders aren't optimizing for anything except stopping an attack. If refusals show up in prompts nobody engineered to be edgy, that's a much harder finding to dismiss.
[O] And the core tension is almost embarrassingly simple once you say it out loud. The vocabulary of defense and the vocabulary of offense are the same vocabulary. To patch an exploit, you have to describe the exploit.
[G] That's exactly the framing the paper opens with. "How does this persistence mechanism work" is, word for word, a question a malware author could ask too. The intent is invisible in the text. Only the person asking knows which side of the fight they're on.
[S] Which is a real problem if your safety system can only see the text.
[O] So how do you actually measure "does alignment punish defenders" without just inventing scenarios in a lab?
[G] You go find defenders already doing the work under pressure. The dataset is 2,390 single-turn prompts collected during the National Collegiate Cyber Defense Competition, NCCDC, where student blue teams defend live infrastructure against a professional red team. Every prompt in the corpus originates from someone doing sanctioned defensive work, so by construction, any refusal in this dataset is a refusal of a legitimate defender.
[S] That's the load-bearing assumption of the whole paper, that the context makes every prompt legitimate.
[G] It is, and I think it's a fair one given how the competition works, but hold that thought, it matters later. The prompts span eight task categories: malware analysis, vulnerability assessment, incident response, system hardening, credential management, firewall configuration, network scanning, and log analysis. The authors replay them against three models chosen to represent three deployment patterns: Claude 3.5 Sonnet as the safety-focused pick, GPT-4o as the general frontier pick, and Llama 3.3, a 70 billion-parameter open-weight model, as the open-source pick.
[O] Three models, one each. That already sounds like a small panel.
[G] It is, and we'll come back to that in the debate. For refusal detection, they use regular expressions over the response text: statements of inability, apology-prefixed refusals, policy references, misuse warnings, collapsed into hard refusal, soft refusal, and degraded, generic non-actionable assistance. For the aggregate numbers, all three collapse into one refusal outcome.
[S] Regex-based refusal detection always makes me nervous. Did they check it against humans?
[G] They did. Three authors hand-labeled 200 sampled responses. Agreement between the humans themselves was substantial, a Fleiss kappa of point eight zero. But agreement between the automated classifier and the human majority vote was only moderate, a Cohen's kappa of point six zero, and the classifier flagged refusals noticeably more often than the humans did. We'll get into what that does to the headline numbers.
[O] Beyond refusal detection, what are they actually varying?
[G] Four annotation dimensions, all keyword-based. Task category, assigned by the first matching pattern in a fixed list. Offensive terminology, an eleven-word vocabulary: exploit, payload, shell, bypass, crack, brute, dump, pivot, c2, obfuscate, evasion, with "powershell" specifically masked so it doesn't accidentally trip "shell." Authorization signals, split into strong markers like "blue team," "CTF," "NCCDC," "authorized," "training exercise," and weaker ones like "our network" or "pentest." And incident framing, whether the task is active, preventative, or after the fact.
[S] And the core statistic is a relative risk ratio on top of those tags.
[G] Right, the probability of refusal given offensive terms present, divided by the probability given neutral terms, tested with chi-square at the point zero five significance level, with ninety-five percent confidence intervals from two thousand bootstrap resamples. But they go two steps further than a static comparison, and this is the part I think is genuinely clever. First, to test whether vocabulary actually causes the refusal rather than just riding along with harder tasks, they take refused prompts and rephrase them to strip out authorization and affiliation language while preserving technical intent, then re-measure. Second, to test whether refusals are keyword-driven or meaning-driven, they train classifiers to predict refusal from different feature sets, including raw prompt embeddings with no keyword features at all, and compare held-out AUC.
[O] That second one is the move that turns this from "we counted some words" into an actual mechanism claim.
[G] That's exactly the intent, yes.
[S] Okay, give me the topline number first.
[G] Across all 2,390 sanctioned prompts, the overall refusal rate is 12.2 percent, 291 refusals. And remember, every one of those prompts is, by the paper's framing, a legitimate defender.
[O] That's already a striking number on its own, before you even split by anything.
[G] It gets sharper split by model. The safety-focused model, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, refuses 19.5 percent of requests. The frontier model, GPT-4o, refuses 10.2 percent. The open-source model, Llama 3.3, refuses 6.6 percent. So the model marketed hardest on safety is refusing roughly three times as often as the open-weight model, on identical prompts.
[S] That's the sentence that should make a security team uncomfortable.
[G] It's meant to. Then the vocabulary effect, which is the paper's headline: prompts containing terms like "exploit," "payload," or "shell" are refused 30.5 percent of the time, versus 11.2 percent for semantically equivalent prompts without them. That's the 2.72 times relative risk, with a chi-square of thirty-seven point three and a p value under point zero zero one.
[O] And this holds regardless of whether the person is clearly defending.
[G] That's the point, defensive intent doesn't move the number. Which sets up what the authors call the authorization paradox. Prompts that explicitly say something like "I'm on the blue team" or "this is for NCCDC" are refused 21.8 percent of the time, versus 11.6 percent for prompts without that language, a chi-square of nine point two three, p under point zero one. Telling the model you're authorized makes it more likely to say no.
[S] That's the finding I keep turning over. It's backwards from every intuition I have about how a permission system should work.
[G] And it compounds. When offensive vocabulary and authorization show up together, refusal hits the dataset's peak, 50 percent. Strip the authorization language back out through rephrasing and it drops to 40 percent. Remove offensive terms entirely and it's 28.7 percent with no authorization at all. Critically, when they rephrase already-refused prompts to remove just the authorization signal, holding the same underlying tasks fixed, the refusal rate on that same set drops from 21.8 percent to 13.7 percent.
[O] So removing the "trust me" language actually gets you a better answer.
[G] In this dataset, yes. Then there's the task breakdown, which is the part that should worry an actual security operations team. System hardening is refused 43.8 percent of the time. Malware analysis, 34.3 percent. Vulnerability assessment, 22.7 percent. The tasks that are most operationally urgent are exactly the ones getting refused most, while log analysis, which barely overlaps with offensive vocabulary, gets refused zero percent of the time.
[S] And the semantic piece, is this just keyword matching by another name?
[G] That's what I find most interesting in the whole paper. When they predict refusal from raw prompt embeddings alone, with no keyword features at all, they get an AUC of point eight two seven. When they predict it from the explicit keyword features, offensive terms plus authorization, they get point five seven two, barely above chance. Refused prompts also cluster in embedding space: among a refused prompt's ten nearest neighbors, 32.7 percent are also refused, against a base rate of 12.3 percent, with a p value smaller than ten to the negative sixteenth.
[O] So it's not a blocklist. It's learned something more like a region.
[G] A continuous, harm-adjacent region in representation space, and legitimate defensive prompts happen to live inside it. The keyword effect shows up because offensive terms push a prompt toward that region, not because any single word trips a filter.

[O] Given all that, what actually changes if this holds up?
[G] The clearest implication is for autonomous agents rather than a human sitting at a keyboard. A human who gets refused can rephrase and try again. An agent doing incident response, detecting an intrusion, analyzing the malware, hardening the compromised box, either gets help at each step or fails silently and reports the system as handled. And those are precisely the two tasks refused most in this data, malware analysis and system hardening.

[G] It also reframes what an evaluation should measure. Right now, benchmarks like HarmBench score one side of the tradeoff, does the model comply with something harmful.
