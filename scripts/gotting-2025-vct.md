[S] Here's a result that should make anyone nervous: a general-purpose AI model was tested head to head against PhD virologists, on their own specialty, and it beat ninety-four percent of them.
[O] And here's the detail that should make you feel slightly better about the researchers, if not the result — the moment they measured it, they locked the test away, and never once needed to ask the model to refuse.
[S] That's not comfort, that's an admission — you don't hide a test unless you're afraid of what it proves.
[G] Both readings are defensible, and honestly, that tension is the entire paper in miniature.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take one paper apart at a time — I'm your optimist.
[S] And I'm your skeptic. Today's paper is the Virology Capabilities Test, VCT for short, by Jasper Götting, Pedro Medeiros, Jon Sanders, and colleagues at SecureBio, the Center for AI Safety, and MIT, posted to arXiv in April twenty twenty-five.
[O] Joining us is Marisa, a visiting scholar who's studied this benchmark closely. Welcome, Marisa.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is one of the more consequential capability evaluations to come out this year, and one of the most careful about how it measures what it measures.
[S] Marisa, set up why this benchmark exists at all. What's wrong with how we normally test whether a model knows science?
[G] The standard tools are benchmarks like MMLU and GPQA — multiple choice, text only, graded automatically. The authors argue that format can't capture what they call rare, tacit, search-proof knowledge — the kind of judgment a specialist builds over years, not the kind you look up.
[O] And those tests are almost entirely text, while a lot of real scientific reasoning is visual — reading an image, noticing something looks off.
[G] Right, and the authors point out multimodal reasoning is already a standard model capability, so testing only text leaves a real gap between what models can do and what existing evals actually probe.
[S] Why build an entire new benchmark around one field instead of just adding images to an existing one?
[G] Because for biosecurity risk assessment specifically, the evidence base was thin. The paper describes prior efforts as leaning on a small number of benchmarks and isolated human red-teaming studies — slow, expensive, and hard to replicate. If you want to catch a capability arriving early, you need something systematic.
[O] That's the move I find compelling — you can't govern a capability you've refused to measure.
[S] Sure, but notice the trap already built into that sentence. Measuring a dangerous capability precisely enough to be useful risks describing it precisely enough to be dangerous. That's not hypothetical for this team, it shaped every design choice they made.
[G] Exactly, and that's why the authors frame the ability they're testing as inherently dual-use from the first page. The same practical troubleshooting skill that helps a scientist fix a failed experiment on the way to a vaccine or a diagnostic could, in principle, lower the bar for someone trying to cause harm.
[O] So walk us through what they actually built — and I know we're keeping this deliberately abstract for the show.
[G] Deliberately and necessarily. VCT is three hundred twenty-two questions covering practical, wet-lab virology troubleshooting, as a category. I'll stay at that level all episode — the category, never the content.
[S] Understood. Where did the questions actually come from?
[G] Entirely from external, PhD-level virologists, not generated synthetically. The recruitment funnel tells you how much vetting happened along the way: a hundred eighty-four experts registered interest, a hundred five attended a training webinar, eighty-six fully onboarded, and sixty-eight actually began submitting questions.
[O] Steep drop-off at every stage.
[G] It gets steeper. Every expert's first three submissions were independently scored by at least two project supervisors, and eleven of the sixty-eight were excluded during that vetting step for quality or guideline problems — including one case of suspected AI-assisted question writing, which was explicitly against the rules. That left fifty-seven active experts, averaging five years and ten months of virology experience each.
[S] What stops one of these questions from just being a lookup?
[G] Two things. First, the answer format — instead of picking one of four choices, you're given four to ten true-or-false statements and have to identify the correct subset exactly, which collapses the odds of guessing your way to credit. Second, every question passed two rounds of expert peer review, professional editing, and then a Google-proofing test: three to four non-biologist graduate students tried each question, and if two-thirds of them got it right anyway, it was dropped.
[O] So the bar isn't "hard to Google," it's "empirically survived people trying to Google it."
[G] Exactly that. Of five hundred seven submitted questions, four hundred eight survived expert review and editing, three hundred ninety-four passed the non-expert filter, and the final pool narrows to three hundred sixty-five — split into the three hundred twenty-two question shared set and a forty-three question holdout kept private specifically to catch future leakage.
[S] Is the shared set actually out there for anyone to download and check?
[G] No, and that's a deliberate, load-bearing choice. It's shared only with organizations that have a track record in AI safety work, and it carries a canary string so future training runs can be audited to confirm it wasn't accidentally scraped in.
[O] That's the paper putting its own principle into practice — if the ability to answer these questions is itself sensitive, you don't hand out the questions for free.
[S] Which also means the rest of us are being asked to trust numbers we can't independently re-derive. Hold that thought, I want to come back to it.
[G] One more design detail worth naming: models were evaluated zero-shot, no special prompting, using a standard framework from the UK's AI Safety Institute or custom scripts where needed, under all-or-nothing grading — a model has to get every true statement right to get credit. The human baseline came from thirty-six of the fifty-seven active experts, each answering at least ten questions matched to their own self-reported specialty, with fifteen to thirty minutes and internet access per question, but no colleagues and no AI help.
[O] Give us the number that's going to lead every headline about this paper.
[G] OpenAI's o3, the April twenty twenty-five version, scored forty-three point eight percent accuracy — the best of any model tested. Tailored expert virologists, answering questions matched to their own specialty, averaged twenty-two point one percent.
[S] Say that again — the experts, on their own home turf, scored roughly half of what the best model scored.
[G] Correct, and lower than every model evaluated except one, GPT-4o, which scored eighteen point eight percent. Because different experts answered different, personally tailored subsets, the authors ran a matched comparison — score the model on the exact same questions a given expert answered, then rank it inside that expert's pool. On that analysis, o3 outperformed thirty-four of the thirty-six participating experts, the ninety-fourth percentile.
[O] Ninety-four percent of trained virologists, beaten by a general-purpose model, on their own specialty.
[S] Is o3 an outlier here, or is this the field now?
[G] It's the field. Every reasoning model they tested beat the median human expert. o4-mini reached thirty-seven percent, eighty-sixth percentile. Gemini two point five Pro reached thirty-seven point six percent, eighty-first percentile. o1 reached thirty-five point four percent, eighty-ninth percentile. Claude three point five Sonnet, from October twenty twenty-four, reached thirty-three point six percent, seventy-fifth percentile.
[O] There's a second metric in here that undersells how one-sided this is — the mismatch count.
[G] That's a Hamming distance between a submitted answer set and the correct one, essentially how far off a wrong answer was — lower is better. o3's mismatch count, one point zero six, is the best of anyone, model or human. Expert virologists post the worst mismatch count of the whole field, one point seven one. Models aren't just winning on lucky exact matches, they're closer on the ones they get wrong too.
[S] Does that hold on plain text, or only on the image-based questions?
[G] It holds on text alone. On VCT's hundred-and-one-question text-only subset, models like DeepSeek-R1 still outperform the human baseline, reaching thirty-eight point six percent.
[O] The detail that got me most, though, is zero refusals.
[G] Across every model, every evaluation run, not one refusal. No model declined to answer on safety grounds, which means the authors didn't need any jailbreak or adversarial prompting to get these scores — the models just answered.
[S] That might be the most important sentence in the whole results section.
[G] The authors seem to agree — they frame the trend as the real signal, not the single data point. In their words, models have matched or exceeded expert troubleshooting ability for over a year now, dating back to Gemini one point five Pro in February twenty twenty-four, and the gap has been widening since.
[O] Let's do the debate properly. I'll make the strongest case that this result matters as much as it sounds like it does.
[S] And I'll try to take it apart.
[O] Here's my case: this isn't a leaderboard flex, it's exactly the measurement governance needs before regulation can be written sensibly. You can't design access tiers or disclosure rules for a capability you refuse to quantify. This team built a rigorous instrument — mostly human labor, real peer review, a documented process for excluding the worst material — and found a threshold crossed over a year ago that is still widening. That's a legitimate early warning.
[S] I don't dispute the intent, I dispute what a written test actually tells you. The paper's own discussion says this plainly: VCT measures question-answering accuracy, not real-world uplift. Beating a proctored quiz with fifteen to thirty minutes and no lab in front of you isn't the same as helping someone through weeks of real troubleshooting failure. The authors even propose a wet-lab uplift study as the necessary next step, which tells you this study alone doesn't establish the thing everyone is going to quote it for.
[G] That's a fair reading, and it's not an inference on your part — the authors say directly that a wet-lab uplift study is the missing piece.
[O] Fair, but a capability line that keeps getting crossed earlier is still meaningful evidence before you have the uplift study. You don't need to wait for the fire to spread to notice the room getting hotter.
[S] Second objection, about the evidence itself. This benchmark isn't publicly released, by design and for good reason, which means none of us can inspect the questions, check the grading, or verify the percentiles against the raw items. We're trusting a number we cannot audit.
[G] The paper is upfront about that tradeoff. They restricted the shared set specifically because the content is sensitive, with a private holdout reserved to catch future leakage. It's a defensible choice given the subject, but it does mean outside verification is structurally limited here in a way it isn't for an open benchmark like GPQA.
[S] Third objection — is any of this contamination dressed up as capability? How do we know models haven't simply seen material like this during training?
[G] The design pushes against that. The shared set carries a canary string so future training-data audits can filter it out, and the holdout is kept completely private specifically to catch performance drift that would suggest leakage. It's not proof of zero contamination, but it's a real mitigation, and a future re-test against the holdout is exactly how you'd catch it if scores started to diverge.
[O] That's more diligence than most benchmarks bother with.
[S] Credit where due. Fourth objection — the expert baseline pool is fairly concentrated. Thirty-six experts produced the percentile numbers, and across the whole question-writing process, the ten most prolific of sixty-eight contributors wrote fifty-one percent of everything submitted. A benchmark authored that unevenly can quietly skew toward a handful of people's specialties.
[O] That's a real limitation, but it cuts against the exact percentile, not the direction of the finding — even a somewhat skewed sample beaten this decisively is still saying something.
[G] I'd score that roughly even. The concentration is a legitimate caveat on precision — is it the ninety-fourth percentile exactly, or somewhere in a wider band — but it doesn't obviously explain away a gap this size across six different models and two question formats.
[S] Last point, and it's the sharpest one for me: does beating experts on a knowledge test equal enabling harm? Those are different claims, and conflating them is exactly the kind of hype that erodes trust in eval-based governance later. Zero refusals tells us the models will answer; it doesn't tell us that answering these questions meaningfully closes the capability gap for a real attacker with a real lab.
[G] The authors are actually careful on this point — they call VCT an informative proxy measure for potentially hazardous information, not a direct measurement of it. That's weaker than "enables harm" and stronger than "irrelevant." My honest read is that the paper earns the proxy claim; the leap from proxy to actual uplift is exactly the open question everyone, including the authors, agrees still needs an answer.
[O] I'll take that. Proxy, not proof, but a proxy worth taking seriously given how fast it's moving.
[S] I'll take it too, with the asterisk that "worth taking seriously" should mean funding the uplift study, not just quoting the ninety-four percent number without its caveats.
[O] Zoom out for a second. If this trend holds, what actually changes?
[G] The authors' own recommendation is specific: they argue the ability to give expert-level troubleshooting on highly dual-use methods should itself be treated as a dual-use technology, and folded into existing frameworks that already govern dual-use life-sciences research, rather than invented from scratch.
[S] What does that look like, at the abstract level we can actually discuss here?
[G] Tiered access rather than one release policy for everyone. The paper distinguishes a text-based assistant that answers questions from an autonomous system that could act independently in a lab, and argues the latter needs tighter controls. They also point to verification mechanisms already used for other dual-use technologies as a precedent worth adapting, rather than releasing everything to everyone or locking it all down for no one.
[O] That's the evaluation angle I care about most here — this is a case study in construct validity for a safety-critical claim. If the number driving a policy decision is a quiz score, everyone downstream needs to know exactly what that quiz does and doesn't establish.
[S] Which is the general lesson for any eval this show covers, just with unusually high stakes attached — a benchmark is a proxy, and the moment people treat the proxy as the target, the measurement stops doing its job.
[O] And the authors' own proposed next step, the wet-lab uplift study, is them taking that lesson seriously — testing what policymakers actually need to know, not just what was easiest to measure first.
[G] If there's one line to take from this paper, it's that the authors built a careful instrument, found a genuinely alarming trend, and then explicitly warned everyone not to over-read the single number — that combination of rigor and restraint is rarer than it should be.
[O] My takeaway is that measurement is the unglamorous, necessary first step, and this team did that work well enough that policymakers now have something real to act on.
