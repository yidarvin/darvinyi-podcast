[S] Here's an uncomfortable number to start with. Out of four hundred forty-five benchmarks that claim to measure things like reasoning, alignment, and safety in language models, only fifty-three percent even bother to justify why their score means what they say it means.
[O] And barely one in six run a statistical test before declaring one model better than another. That's not a rounding error, that's most of the field grading on vibes with a leaderboard attached.
[S] Vibes with citations. And the paper we're covering today didn't just complain about that gap, twenty-nine expert reviewers actually read the methodology of four hundred forty-five papers to measure it.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the frontier of AI research. Today's paper is "Measuring what Matters: Construct Validity in Large Language Model Benchmarks," from Andrew Bean, Adam Mahdi, and forty other colleagues across Oxford, EPFL, Yale, and a dozen more institutions, published at NeurIPS twenty twenty-five's Datasets and Benchmarks track.
[S] Forty-two authors is basically its own case study in construct validity. What exactly is being measured by an author list that long?
[O] Joining us to walk through it is Crispian, who's spent real time in this literature. Crispian, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. This paper matters because it takes a concept from nineteen-fifties psychology, Cronbach and Meehl's original work on validity in psychological testing, and turns it into an actual audit instrument, then points that instrument at four hundred forty-five of the benchmarks the field currently relies on.
[S] Give us the concept in one breath before we go further.
[G] Construct validity is the degree to which a benchmark score provides real evidence for a claim about some abstract phenomenon, reasoning, safety, alignment, that you can't measure directly. You build a task and a metric as a proxy, and construct validity is whether that proxy actually tracks the thing you say it tracks.
[O] So if a benchmark has strong construct validity for reasoning, a model that scores well really is, in some meaningful sense, reasoning better. If construct validity is weak, a high score can be irrelevant, or actively misleading.
[S] Which is the exact failure mode this field has watched happen over and over. A benchmark saturates, someone finds it was gameable, and it turns out the score was never tracking the thing it claimed to.
[G] Right, and the paper is explicit that nobody had checked this systematically at scale before. Two adjacent audits get named directly. One from McIntosh and colleagues reviewed twenty-three benchmarks for what they call functionality and integrity issues, with no grounding in the validity literature at all. The other, BetterBench, from Reuel and colleagues, is a forty-six criteria audit of twenty-four benchmarks covering the whole lifecycle, design, documentation, maintenance, and it explicitly writes construct validity out of scope, saying assessing it would require, in their words, in-depth analysis by domain experts.
[O] So this paper is the team that decided to actually do that in-depth analysis.
[S] Crispian, isn't there something almost circular here, though? "Construct validity" assumes there's a true construct to converge on. Who decides that "reasoning" has one correct definition in the first place?
[G] That tension is real, and to the authors' credit they don't pretend it away. Their own data shows it: of the benchmarks that define their phenomenon at all, forty-seven point eight percent use a definition the reviewers coded as contested, no field-wide consensus exists. The paper's answer isn't "here is the correct definition of reasoning." It's "state your definition explicitly, and make sure your task and metric actually follow from it." The validity comes from internal fit, not from some outside authority certifying the concept.
[S] Which is a more modest goal than the name implies, but fine, I'll take modest and honest over grand and vague.
[G] The corpus itself starts enormous: forty-six thousand, one hundred fourteen articles, every paper from ICML, ICLR, and NeurIPS between twenty eighteen and twenty twenty-four, plus ACL, NAACL, and EMNLP from twenty twenty to twenty twenty-four. A keyword filter, title or abstract containing "benchmark" and either "LLM" or "language model," narrows that to two thousand, one hundred eighty-nine candidates.
[O] Still far too many for twenty-nine humans to read closely.
[G] Which is why they didn't start with humans. They ran GPT-four-oh-mini as an automated screener against three of their four inclusion criteria: does the paper concern LLM capabilities, does it introduce an empirical benchmark with reported performance, is it compatible with text or vision modalities. They validated the screener against fifty human-labeled articles and reported an F one of eighty-four percent, which breaks down as eighty percent precision and eighty-nine percent recall.
[S] Eighty-nine percent recall on a fifty-article sample means roughly one in nine genuinely qualifying papers likely got dropped before any human reviewer ever saw it, out of two thousand, one hundred eighty-nine candidates. That's a real gap for an automated filter that only runs in one direction.
[G] It's a fair read, and it's the authors' own number, reported in an appendix rather than the abstract. After the screener, five hundred twenty-two articles went to the twenty-nine reviewers, matched by area of expertise, who triaged by hand against all four criteria, adding a fourth: is this benchmark genuinely novel, not a repackaging of something that already exists. That manual pass landed on four hundred forty-five included benchmark articles.
[O] And then the actual coding starts.
[G] The codebook is built around four elements, following a definition of a benchmark from Raji and colleagues: a phenomenon, the abstract thing you're measuring, say multi-step mathematical reasoning; a task that operationalizes it, grade-school math word problems; a metric that scores it, exact match; and the claims authors make from the resulting score. The individual codebook items are drawn from classical validity theory, face validity, does the test look plausible on its face, content validity, does the task cover the important parts of the phenomenon, ecological and predictive validity, does it resemble real use and predict future performance, and convergent and discriminant validity, does it correlate with what it should and not with what it shouldn't.
[S] And how do you know two reviewers looking at the same paper actually agree on what counts as, say, "well defined"?
[G] Every one of the four hundred forty-five was coded by a primary reviewer, then a second reviewer mapped those answers onto a simplified category set for the statistics, verified by the primary reviewer. To check reliability, forty-six of the four hundred forty-five, about ten percent, were independently coded by two reviewers. The mean agreement across all thirty categorical questions was a Brennan-Prediger Kappa of point five two four, moderate on the standard scale, with sixty-eight point one percent raw agreement.
[O] Moderate is honest. I'd trust a paper reporting point five two four far more than one that skips inter-rater reliability entirely, which, by this paper's own findings, describes most of the field.
[G] On the findings themselves: reasoning was the single most common target phenomenon, eighteen point five percent of all four hundred forty-five benchmarks, followed by alignment at eight point one percent and code generation at five point seven percent. Seventy-eight point two percent of benchmarks defined their phenomenon at all, but of those definitions, only fifty-two point two percent were coded as widely agreed upon, the rest contested or lacking real consensus.
[S] Multiply that out and well under half of all benchmarks rest on a definition the field actually agrees on.
[G] About forty-one percent, correct. And sixty-one point two percent described their phenomenon as a composite of sub-elements, "agentic capability" spanning intent recognition, alignment, and structured output, rather than one unified construct, which raises the question of whether a single score can represent something that's actually several bundled abilities.
[O] That part doesn't bother me on its own. Capabilities genuinely are compositional. The problem is reporting one number for a composite and letting readers assume it's clean.
[S] Sure, but then show sub-scores. What about sampling?
[G] Only seventeen point one percent of benchmarks used random or stratified sampling to build their task set. Twelve point three percent relied exclusively on convenience sampling, whatever data was easiest to obtain, and another twenty-seven percent used some convenience sampling as part of the mix. Thirty-eight point two percent reused data from an existing benchmark or human exam rather than building fresh material, and seven point seven percent sourced their tasks solely from another benchmark.
[O] Reuse isn't automatically bad, it saves enormous effort, and if the original was carefully built, some validity should carry over.
[S] Should. The paper's point is you also inherit the original's hidden flaws, and reused material has had more time to leak into training data. Scoring?
[G] Exact match dominates, used at least partially by eighty-one point three percent of benchmarks, exclusively by forty point seven percent. LLM-as-judge shows up in seventeen point one percent, human ratings in thirteen percent. And the number that should worry anyone reading a leaderboard: only sixteen percent of all four hundred forty-five benchmarks used any uncertainty estimate or statistical test when comparing models.
[O] Sixteen percent. So the vast majority of the time, when a paper says model A beats model B, there's no test behind that beyond one number being bigger than another.
[G] Correct. And on claims: fifty-three point four percent of articles presented some evidence for their own benchmark's construct validity, comparing to other benchmarks of similar phenomena, to a human baseline, or to a more realistic setting. Which also means close to half offer no such justification at all.
[S] Give me the worked example, Crispian, you always save the best part.
[G] It's GSM8K, the grade-school math benchmark, used in the main text as a demonstration, not a takedown. It scores well on several items: a fresh, from-scratch dataset rather than reused data, a large, justified sample size, and model comparisons reported over multiple runs with standard deviations, exactly the statistical rigor sixteen percent of the field bothers with. But it's marked down for not isolating arithmetic-execution error from reading-comprehension and reasoning error, so a model can fail a problem for reasons that have nothing to do with math reasoning, and for having no formal error-taxonomy of its own failure modes, despite that kind of analysis being exactly what later work used to guide improvements in prompting strategies.
[O] So even the benchmark half the field treats as a gold standard for reasoning fails a few items on this exact checklist.
[G] That's precisely why they chose it. Widespread adoption doesn't imply construct validity, and the authors want that visible even in a benchmark everyone already trusts.
[O] Here's my case for why this paper matters beyond one more audit: it's the first time anyone has operationalized construct validity for LLM benchmarks at this scale and made it actionable. Not "benchmarks are bad," which everyone already knew, but eight specific recommendations and a checklist you can run through while building your next benchmark. That could become a reviewer checklist item at NeurIPS or ACL.
[S] I'll grant the ambition. My problem is with the paper's own evidence for its own reliability. The headline number is point five two four, moderate, across thirty questions. But the appendix breaks that down field by field, and the two questions closest to the paper's central claims are its least reliable items in the entire codebook. Whether a benchmark defines its phenomenon at all, the question behind "seventy-eight percent provide a definition," has a kappa of point zero seven three, barely above chance. Whether authors present their own construct-validity evidence, behind "fifty-three percent justify construct validity," comes in at point three zero five, still well under the mean.
[G] Both numbers are real, from Table nine in Appendix D, and Sam's right to flag them. Raw percent agreement on phenomenon_defined was fifty-three point seven percent, barely better than a coin flip on a binary question. That doesn't invalidate the paper's overall pattern, dozens of other fields agree well above chance, but it does mean the two statistics closest to the thesis are the shakiest in the dataset, folded into an unweighted mean rather than flagged in the abstract.
[O] Crispian, did the authors explain why they used an unweighted mean instead of downweighting the noisiest fields?
[G] Not that I found in the text. That's exactly the kind of choice a critic would push on, and the paper doesn't answer it.
[O] Even so, a noisy signal in the right direction beats no signal, which is what the field had before this paper existed.
[S] There's a second, structural problem. The corpus is six peer-reviewed venues, ICML, ICLR, NeurIPS, ACL, NAACL, EMNLP. That excludes, by construction, every benchmark released by an industry lab without peer review.
[G] The authors name this limitation themselves in the discussion. It's sharper than it sounds in isolation, because a companion critique in this same batch of research, from McIntosh and colleagues, argues the benchmarks practitioners actually reach for day to day are mostly unrefereed preprints. So this audit, however rigorous, measures the more disciplined half of the ecosystem and skips the half that arguably needs the scrutiny most.
[O] Still, auditing the peer-reviewed half first is a reasonable place to start, you can't review everything in one paper.
[S] Just don't let the framing imply this is a survey of "LLM benchmarks" broadly. It's a survey of academic conference benchmarks specifically.
[G] Agreed, that's worth stating plainly. Last flag: the checklist's payoff is demonstrated, not measured. GSM8K is one worked example showing how the checklist applies, it isn't evidence that a benchmark scoring well on the checklist actually produces more stable or less gameable leaderboard results than one scoring poorly. Plausible, given the theory behind it, but untested here.
[O] So the diagnosis is rigorous, and the remedy is well reasoned but unproven.
[G] That's a fair summary. This paper diagnoses more than it solves, but it's the most careful diagnosis this literature has gotten.
[O] If this takes hold, I think the realistic outcome is cultural before it's technical. Reviewers start asking "did you justify construct validity" the way they now ask for a confidence interval. That alone raises the floor.
[S] I'd add a specific prediction: pushback on multiple-choice formats, since the paper flags them as easy to score but easy to game and rarely representative of real use, and more papers reporting standard deviations across runs, since that's cheap and directly answerable from this checklist.
[G] The paper states its own stakes plainly: unrecognized weak links between a benchmark's tasks and the phenomenon they claim to measure lead to poorly supported scientific claims, misdirected research effort, and policy decisions resting on evidence that was never as solid as the leaderboard implied. That's the throughline connecting this back to the rest of evaluation science, contamination, judge reliability, agentic evals, they're all versions of the same question: does the number actually mean what we're treating it as meaning.
[O] My takeaway: this is the first checklist I'd actually want stapled to a benchmark submission, because it turns a vague complaint into specific, checkable items.
[S] Mine: trust the diagnosis, read the appendix before you trust the exact percentages, and remember this audit covers only the peer-reviewed half of a much messier ecosystem.
[G] And the paper's own closing line is the best summary: measuring what matters requires a conscious, sustained effort from the research community to prioritize construct validity, a cultural shift, not a one-time fix.
