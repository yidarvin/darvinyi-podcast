[O] Here's the claim that should stop you: change nothing about the model, only how you read out its answer, and GSM8K jumps seventeen points.
[S] And here's the catch: that free lunch costs forty times the inference compute, and the paper never once compares against a single pass at that same budget.
[O] Forty passes to fix one greedy path that had no way to recover from its own first mistake — that trade looks obviously worth it to me.
[S] Obviously worth it, or obviously the paper choosing not to ask the harder question. Let's find out which.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio. Today's paper is "Self-Consistency Improves Chain of Thought Reasoning in Language Models," by Xuezhi Wang, Jason Wei, Dale Schuurmans, Quoc Le, Ed Chi, Sharan Narang, Aakanksha Chowdhery, and Denny Zhou, out of Google Research's Brain team, published at ICLR in 2023.
[S] It's past seven thousand citations, and depending who you ask, it's either the paper that quietly invented test-time compute scaling, or a clever trick that got there by spending compute nobody priced.
[O] Either way, everything from majority-vote sampling to today's reasoning models leans on the idea inside it. Joining us to referee is Marcus, who's spent real time with this paper's tables.
[G] Glad to be here. Same ground rule as always: I'm not one of the authors, so "the authors show" means exactly that, never "we."
[S] Set the table for us, Marcus. What's broken about chain-of-thought before this paper touches it?
[G] Chain-of-thought prompting, from Wei and colleagues earlier that same year, had just shown a few worked examples unlock real multi-step reasoning. But it still decodes greedily — one path, token by token, no way back. One arithmetic slip early on, and the whole chain is wrong with nothing to catch it.
[O] So the model reasons like someone who refuses to double-check their own work.
[G] That's exactly it. And the existing fixes all bolt something heavy on: Cobbe and colleagues trained a separate verifier to re-rank candidate solutions; the team behind LaMDA collected human annotations to train a re-ranker. Both need extra training, extra labels, sometimes a second model — and neither touches the actual moment of decoding.
[S] Which is the gap this paper is aiming at.
[G] Precisely — can you make chain-of-thought more reliable without training anything? No verifier, no new labels, no fine-tuning, just a smarter way to read the answer back out. The intuition is almost anthropological: the authors point to work on human reasoning showing a genuinely hard problem usually has more than one route to the right answer, and wrong answers tend to be wrong in their own idiosyncratic, disagreeing ways.
[O] Walk us through the mechanism itself.
[G] Three steps, and it replaces exactly one thing: the decoder. Step one, prompt with the same chain-of-thought exemplars as before. Step two, instead of greedily decoding the single most likely path, sample a diverse set of reasoning paths — ordinary temperature sampling, top-k, or nucleus sampling. Step three, marginalize: each sample gives a reasoning path and a final answer, you discard the path, and take a majority vote over just the answers. Whichever answer the most paths agree on wins.
[S] So the reasoning text is disposable — scaffolding to reach an answer you then throw away.
[G] Right, that's the "marginalize out the reasoning path" part, literally summing over a latent variable. Formally: pick the answer that the largest share of your sampled paths agree on.
[O] Did they try weighting by the model's own confidence in each path, instead of a flat vote?
[G] They did, and it's one of the more interesting negative results here. Table one compares plain majority vote against weighting by the model's probability of generating each path. Unweighted vote scores seventy-four point four on PaLM and GSM8K; the properly normalized weighted version scores seventy-four point one — indistinguishable. The model treats its own sampled paths as roughly equally likely regardless of correctness, so plain voting loses you nothing.
[S] That's actually a quietly damning finding about the model's self-knowledge, not just a footnote about voting schemes.
[G] It is — and it's the same gap that motivated verifiers in the first place. The model can't separate its own good reasoning from bad by likelihood alone.
[O] What's the actual budget behind the headline numbers?
[G] Forty sampled paths per question, averaged over ten runs. Sampling parameters vary a little by model — temperature zero point five, top-k forty for UL2 and LaMDA; temperature zero point seven, top-k forty for PaLM; temperature zero point seven, no top-k cutoff for GPT-3. And critically, this only works with a fixed answer set to vote over — open-ended generation has no clean way to tally votes, which the authors flag directly as the scope limit.
[S] You called it a "self-ensemble" earlier. Why does the word choice matter?
[G] Because it's a different claim than ensembling. A normal ensemble trains multiple separate models and aggregates them — expensive, and rare in practice at this scale. This is one frozen model, sampled many times. No new parameters, no new training run, just a different decoding loop.
[S] Give us the headline numbers, unrounded.
[G] Best model per task, self-consistency against greedy chain-of-thought: GSM8K up plus seventeen point nine points, AQuA plus twelve point two, SVAMP plus eleven point zero, StrategyQA plus six point four, ARC-challenge plus three point nine. On PaLM, five hundred forty billion parameters, GSM8K climbs fifty-six point five to seventy-four point four. On GPT-3's code-davinci-002 engine, sixty point one up to seventy-eight point zero.
[O] Both already strong baselines, pushed nearly twenty points higher.
[G] And the part that surprised even the authors: the gains grow with scale rather than shrink. On the smallest model, UL2 at twenty billion parameters, the lift is three to six points. On LaMDA at one hundred thirty-seven billion and GPT-3 at one hundred seventy-five billion, it's nine to twenty-three points. MultiArith on LaMDA alone goes fifty-one point eight to seventy-five point seven.
[S] That's the opposite of what I'd guess — bigger models are already more accurate, I'd expect less headroom for a decoding trick to find.
[G] The authors' read is that multi-step reasoning is itself emergent with scale, so a bigger model isn't just more accurate on any one path — it generates a genuinely more diverse set of correct paths to sample from. A small model doesn't have that diversity to exploit.
[O] What about tasks where chain-of-thought was actually known to hurt?
[G] Good catch — the paper has a section built for exactly this. Prior work had shown chain-of-thought sometimes underperforms plain standard prompting, on tasks like ANLI and e-SNLI. Self-consistency recovers it and then some: ANLI-R1 goes from sixty-eight point eight under greedy chain-of-thought — actually below the sixty-nine point one standard-prompting baseline — up to seventy-eight point five.
[S] So it's not just amplifying what chain-of-thought was already good at. It patches an actual failure mode.
[O] And against the obvious alternatives — sample-and-rank, beam search?
[G] Both lose, at matched sample budgets, which is the right test. Sample-and-rank — same forty samples, keep the one with the highest log-probability — improves over greedy, but by much less than voting; on GSM8K it barely moves. Beam search does worse still, because beam search actively suppresses diversity, and diversity is the whole mechanism. Prompt-order and multi-prompt ensembling underperform too: on LaMDA and GSM8K, forty prompt permutations gets nineteen point two, three prompt sets gets eighteen point six, against self-consistency's twenty-seven point seven at the same sample count.
[O] So diversity in the reasoning path is doing something diversity in the prompt just isn't.
[G] That's the paper's own conclusion, and the numbers back it cleanly.
[S] Is the story uniform across tasks, or are there places this barely moves the needle?
[G] Worth flagging: on tasks already near-solved, the gains shrink a lot. CommonsenseQA on PaLM goes seventy-nine to eighty point seven, plus one point seven. ARC-easy goes ninety-five point three to ninety-six point four, plus one point one. Self-consistency is repairing genuine reasoning failures, and once a task is mostly not failing, there's less left to repair.
[O] Which is actually reassuring — it's not inflating numbers everywhere indiscriminately, it tracks where the model was actually struggling.
[S] Give me a concrete example of that repair, then — what does one actually look like?
[G] The paper includes a good one. A GSM8K question about a bike trip with two stops, correct answer twenty-five miles between them. Greedy decoding subtracts the wrong pair of numbers and lands on forty. Two of the sampled paths each do a different, correct subtraction, both independently arrive at twenty-five, and the vote overrides the greedy path's forty with the majority's twenty-five.
[O] So the greedy path wasn't obviously broken, it just made one clean arithmetic misstep nothing downstream could catch.
[G] Exactly the mechanism in miniature: not "the model can't do the problem," but "any single decode has some chance of one bad step, and voting statistically cancels that out."
[O] My strongest case: this is the first clean demonstration that you can trade inference compute for accuracy, no training required, and it works. That's the direct ancestor of every majority-vote-at-k or consistency-at-k number quoted for reasoning models today — DeepSeek-R1's consistency-at-sixteen included. Accept "sample more, vote," and you've accepted the test-time-compute paradigm in miniature.
[S] My deflation: every gain here is bought at forty times the forward passes of the baseline it's beating, and the paper never runs the comparison that would settle it — does a single pass with that same extra compute, a bigger model or better single-path decoding, get you just as far? Without that number, "plus seventeen point nine" is a compute-adjusted claim wearing an unadjusted headline.
[G] You're both onto something real. On the scale sweep, that's genuinely convincing evidence this isn't a fluke of one model — four architectures, twenty billion to five hundred forty billion parameters, the effect holds and grows. That earns its confidence. On the missing compute-matched baseline, you're right, it's a real gap, the first question I'd ask reviewing this today, and what partially rescues it is Figure two: most of the lift arrives within the first five to ten samples and saturates by forty, so the real price is tunable and cheaper than the full-budget headline implies — but the paper reports that headline at full budget regardless.
[O] Did the authors themselves suggest any way to get the accuracy without paying full price every time?
[G] They gesture at it in the conclusion, though they don't run it: use self-consistency once to generate better supervised data, then fine-tune a model on the winning answers so a single future pass can approximate what forty passes used to buy. That's explicitly flagged as future work, not something this paper demonstrates.
[S] Which is a tell in itself — if compute-matching were trivial to win, I'd expect them to have shown it rather than deferred it.
[G] Fair inference, though it's also just how far one paper's stated scope goes.
[S] Second problem, for the record: this only repairs errors the model makes idiosyncratically. What happens when it's systematically wrong?
[G] Then voting entrenches the error instead of fixing it. The out-of-distribution symbolic task shows it plainly — on the four-letter version of last-letter concatenation, UL2 at twenty billion sits at zero accuracy under greedy decoding, and self-consistency doesn't move it, plus zero point zero. If every sampled path shares the same blind spot, the vote just confirms it with more confidence, not less.
[O] That's real, but it also reads like an honest description of what voting can and can't do, not a strike against this specific paper.
[G] Fair — the authors scope the method to a fixed answer set for exactly this reason, and their own conclusion flags better-grounded rationales as future work, not something this paper claims to solve.
[S] Last thing — contamination. These are 2022 models running on GSM8K and CommonsenseQA. Any pretraining exposure there?
[G] Unmeasured, and the paper doesn't address it directly. But the defense holds up reasonably: the headline claim is the relative lift over greedy decoding, same model, same prompt, same test set. Whatever contamination exists sits equally in both arms of that comparison, so the delta itself is close to contamination-neutral even where the absolute numbers aren't fully trustworthy alone.
[O] Zoom out for a second — what's the more lasting idea here, beyond the specific numbers?
[G] Two things outlive the leaderboard positions. First, "percent consistent" turns out to correlate with accuracy — Figure five, over GSM8K — so the vote's agreement rate doubles as a free, label-free uncertainty estimate. High agreement, trust the answer; low agreement, the model is telling you it doesn't know. Second, the paper explicitly tests this against imperfect prompts, prompts reduced to bare equations, and zero-shot chain-of-thought, and self-consistency helps in every one of those degraded settings, not just the clean one — on zero-shot GSM8K with PaLM it goes forty-three up to sixty-nine point two.
[S] That robustness result is underrated compared to the headline table, honestly.
[G] Agreed. It's the difference between "this works in a curated setup" and "this works even when your prompt has a typo in it."
[O] And for anyone building evals: an uncertainty signal you get for free out of sampling, no separate calibration step, no confidence head to train — that's a genuinely useful primitive beyond just squeezing out accuracy.
[G] If I leave you with the paper's own framing: reasoning tasks have fixed answers, but the road to them doesn't have to be, and sampling that diversity and voting on it beats trusting any single path.
[O] Mine: this is the paper that made "spend more compute at inference time" a real, working lever instead of a hopeful gesture — everything from majority-vote-at-k tables to consistency-at-sixteen in today's reasoning models traces back to this decoding swap.
[S] Mine: believe the effect, price the compute honestly — a seventeen-point jump on a frozen model is real evidence, but it's evidence bought at forty times the forward passes, and the paper that gets to claim full victory here is the one that eventually runs the compute-matched baseline.
