[O] So set the scene for us. Why does post-training even matter this much?
[G] Because it's the difference between a raw base model and something you'd actually want to talk to. Instruction tuning, preference tuning, reinforcement learning from human feedback — that whole finetuning stack is what turns next-token prediction into an assistant. And the paper's framing is that this is simultaneously the most important part of building a frontier model and the least transparent. Closed labs publish model cards with a paragraph on alignment. They don't publish the data.
[S] And open efforts existed already — Tulu 2, Zephyr-beta.
[G] They did, but the paper is blunt about it — those pipelines were simpler and cheaper, and by late twenty twenty-four they'd fallen behind specifically on the skills that are hardest to fake: math, precise instruction following, grade-school arithmetic. Not chat vibes. Verifiable skills.
[O] So Tulu 3's pitch isn't "here's a better model." It's "here's the entire apparatus."
[G] Exactly. Weights, yes, but also the datasets, the decontamination tooling, the training code, a full evaluation framework called Tulu 3 Eval, and a section near the end that catalogs the methods that didn't work.
[S] I'll admit, publishing negative results is rare enough that it's worth crediting on its own.
[G] It is. Most papers bury a failed ablation in a footnote. This one gives it a full section.
[O] Let's get into how it actually works. Walk us through the pipeline.
[G] Four stages, though the paper frames it as three training stages on top of data curation. You start from Llama 3.1 base models — eight billion, seventy billion, and four-oh-five billion parameters. Stage zero identifies core skills — knowledge, reasoning, math, coding, precise instruction following, chat, and safety — and sources or synthesizes prompts for each. Stage one is supervised finetuning, S-F-T. Stage two is D-P-O, direct preference optimization. Stage three is the new piece, Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards, which they call R-L-V-R.
[S] Before we get to R-L-V-R, the data stage is worth pausing on, because "synthetic data" usually makes me nervous.
[G] Reasonable instinct, and they address it directly. Many of the synthetic prompts come from persona-driven generation — you condition a generator, GPT-4o in their case, on a persona, something like "a nurse working night shifts," to get diverse, skill-specific instructions. But the part I'd flag as the paper's quiet contribution is decontamination. Every training source goes through eight-gram matching against their own eval suite. Not full-string matching, not embedding similarity — eight-gram overlap, because that's what caught near-duplicates, like a math problem where only the numbers were swapped.
[O] Give us a number on that.
[G] They strip eleven point three percent of NuminaMath-TIR, five point four percent of WildChat, three point five percent of Evol-CodeAlpaca — all for benchmark overlap. And they release the decontamination tool itself, not just the cleaned datasets.
[S] Okay, I'll grant that's a genuinely useful artifact regardless of how the model performs.
[O] Now S-F-T into D-P-O.
[G] S-F-T produces the Tulu3-S-F-T checkpoint after data-mixture ablations. D-P-O then trains on a preference mix blending off-policy pairs with on-policy generations sampled from the model itself, compared against outputs from other models. The infrastructure detail worth knowing: to scale D-P-O to seventy billion and four-oh-five billion parameters, they cache the reference policy's log-probabilities and run separate forward passes for the chosen and rejected completions instead of concatenating them. That roughly halves activation memory for near-identical loss.
[S] A systems trick, not a science trick — but it's what decides whether a smaller lab can even attempt this.
[G] Right, and that's very much in the spirit of the paper — as much engineering detail as algorithmic detail.
[O] Now the headline piece. R-L-V-R.
[G] The framing is elegant. Standard R-L-H-F trains a learned reward model on human preferences, then optimizes against it. R-L-V-R keeps the same K-L-constrained objective — maximize verifier score minus a K-L penalty back to the reference policy — but throws out the learned reward model entirely. In its place: a deterministic verifier. The completion either passes a check or it doesn't. Reward is a constant, alpha equals ten, if correct, zero otherwise.
[S] So no reward hacking against a learned model's blind spots, because there's no learned model to hack.
[G] That's the pitch, yes — provided the domain actually has a verifier. Math answers, exact numeric match. Instruction-following constraints, like "answer in exactly three bullet points," checked with per-constraint verifier templates drawn from the IFEval taxonomy. They optimize this with P-P-O over roughly thirty thousand verifiable prompts — seven thousand four hundred seventy-three from GSM8K, seven thousand five hundred from MATH, and about fifteen thousand verifiable instruction-following prompts.
[O] Any implementation details that actually mattered?
[G] A handful, and they're candid these were trial and error. Initialize the value model from a general reward model rather than from scratch. Disable dropout during R-L training so the K-L estimate is clean. Apply a minus-ten penalty when a response fails to end with an end-of-sequence token. And whiten the advantage estimates. None of these are novel alone, but few papers write down which of them mattered together.
[S] And the evaluation framework — is that just "we ran some benchmarks," or is there a real methodological idea in there?
[G] There's a real idea, and I think it's underrated relative to R-L-V-R. They split Tulu 3 Eval into a development suite they iterate against, and an unseen suite they deliberately did not inspect until the very end. It's a guard against overfitting your own data and hyperparameter choices to the benchmarks you're optimizing against — a discipline most papers rarely impose on themselves.
[O] Let's get to results. What actually happened?

R-L-V-R adds four tenths of a point at eight billion and one tenth of a point at seventy billion, on the overall average.
