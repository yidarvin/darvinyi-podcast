[O] So let's set the stage. Before this paper, what was the field's operating assumption about how to spend a compute budget?
[G] The dominant reference point was Kaplan and colleagues' twenty twenty scaling laws paper. It found a power-law relationship between model size and loss, and it's genuinely foundational work, it's the reason the field started taking scaling seriously as a science rather than folklore. But one specific recommendation from that paper became gospel: given a tenfold increase in compute, you should grow the model about five and a half times, and grow your training data only about one point eight times. In the paper's own notation, an exponent of zero point seven three on parameters and zero point two seven on tokens.
[S] So almost all of the extra compute goes into making the model bigger, and only a sliver goes into feeding it more text.
[G] Exactly, and that recommendation is what produced the table this paper opens with. GPT-three, one hundred seventy-five billion parameters, three hundred billion tokens. Jurassic-one, one hundred seventy-eight billion parameters, also around three hundred billion tokens. Gopher, DeepMind's own model, two hundred eighty billion parameters, three hundred billion tokens. Megatron-Turing NLG, five hundred thirty billion parameters, two hundred seventy billion tokens. Only LaMDA breaks the pattern, and even that one's an outlier by size, not by design.
[O] So the whole field converged on roughly the same training-token budget almost by accident, because everyone was reading the same scaling law the same way.
[G] That's the authors' argument, yes. And it matters because training a frontier model happens essentially once. If your allocation between size and data is wrong, you don't get a cheap do-over.
[S] Which is exactly why I want to know how they think Kaplan and colleagues went wrong. That's a specific, falsifiable methodological claim, not just a difference of opinion.
[G] It comes down to two choices. First, Kaplan and colleagues held the learning rate schedule fixed in length across all their runs, rather than matching it to how long each model actually trained. This paper finds that setting the cosine decay schedule to match the token horizon gives a noticeably better final loss, regardless of model size. If you use a schedule built for a much longer run, your loss estimates for a shorter run come out worse than they should, which makes training on fewer tokens look like a worse deal than it actually is. Second, most of Kaplan and colleagues' models were quite small, many well under one hundred million parameters. This paper deliberately trains models up to sixteen billion parameters, and at that scale they start to see the frontier curve slightly, which the smaller-scale study simply couldn't detect.
[O] So it's not that Kaplan and colleagues did sloppy work, it's that a specific methodological choice, plus a narrower range of model sizes, both nudged the conclusion in the same direction.
[G] That's a fair read, and I'd add that the authors are careful to credit the earlier paper directly rather than just claiming to beat it.
[S] Okay, so what's the actual method here? How do you re-derive an optimal allocation empirically?
[G] They train over four hundred language models, ranging from seventy million to more than sixteen billion parameters, on between five billion and five hundred billion tokens, and then they estimate the optimal frontier three separate ways, deliberately, as a hedge against any one fitting method being wrong. Approach one fixes a family of model sizes, trains each one for four different token horizons, sixteen times apart in length, and reads off the lower envelope of loss against compute. Fitting power laws to that envelope gives an exponent of zero point five oh on parameters and zero point five oh on tokens.
[O] Which already looks nothing like Kaplan's zero point seven three and zero point two seven.
[G] Right. Approach two is IsoFLOP profiles: pick nine fixed compute budgets, spanning roughly three orders of magnitude, and for each one sweep the model size while holding total FLOPs constant. Every one of those nine curves traces a clear valley, a single best model size for that compute budget. Projecting those valleys outward gives zero point four nine and zero point five one. Approach three fits a full parametric loss surface across every run, an entropy floor term plus a finite-model penalty and a finite-data penalty, minimized under the same compute constraint. That gives zero point four six and zero point five four.
[S] Three different methods, three different sets of assumptions, and they land within a few points of each other, all clustered near fifty-fifty.
[G] Which is the headline finding. For every doubling of model size, you should also double the training tokens, not grow the model five times faster than the data the way Kaplan's law implied.
[O] And then they didn't just publish the curves, they went and spent the compute to test the prediction directly.
[G] That's the part I find most disciplined about this paper. Gopher's training budget was five point seven six times ten to the twenty-third FLOPs. Their analysis says the compute-optimal model at that exact budget should be somewhere between forty and seventy billion parameters. They take the upper end and train a seventy billion parameter model, called Chinchilla, on one point four trillion tokens. That's about four times more data than Gopher saw, and a quarter of the parameters, at the identical compute cost.
[S] Same architecture family as Gopher otherwise?
[G] Very close. Same MassiveText training corpus, a different subset mix to support the longer token count. They switch from Adam to AdamW, which improves both the language-modeling loss and downstream fine-tuning performance, and use a slightly modified SentencePiece tokenizer that particularly helps with representing mathematics and chemistry notation.
[O] Small architectural choices, but this is really a data-versus-size experiment at its core.
[G] Right, everything else is held as close to constant as an engineering team reasonably can across a training run this large.
[O] Let's get to the results, because this is where the paper earns its reputation. Walk us through it.
[G] Chinchilla outperforms Gopher on essentially every evaluation they run, despite being a quarter of the size. On the Pile, it has lower bits-per-byte than Gopher on every single subset they test. On MMLU, the fifty-seven subject exam benchmark, Chinchilla reaches an average five-shot accuracy of sixty-seven point six percent, against Gopher's sixty percent. For context, random guessing scores twenty-five percent, the average human test-taker in their comparison scores thirty-four point five percent, and the average human expert ceiling is eighty-nine point eight percent. [O] What does it mean going forward, though? If model size and data should scale together, what changes about how the field builds datasets?
[G] Their own projection table is the clearest way to see it. At Gopher's compute budget, they estimate the optimal model needs about one point five trillion tokens. At two hundred eighty billion parameters, Gopher's own size, it's about five point nine trillion.
