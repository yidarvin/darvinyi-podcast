[O] And here's the twist. When Patronus AI ran that exact test on GPT-four-Turbo with a realistic retrieval setup, it got the number wrong, or refused outright, on eighty-one percent of questions.
[S] Eighty-one percent. That's not a model with a rough edge. That's a model you would not let near a live desk.
[O] Or it's a model exposing exactly where the industry's retrieval pipelines were in twenty twenty-three, which is a very different headline than "the model can't do finance."
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an AI optimist, an AI skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the litsearch atlas. Today it's FinanceBench, A New Benchmark for Financial Question Answering, from Pranab Islam, Anand Kannappan, Douwe Kiela, Rebecca Qian, Nino Scherrer, and Bertie Vidgen, authors ordered alphabetically, at Patronus AI, Contextual AI, and Stanford, posted to arXiv in November twenty twenty-three.
[S] Joining us to walk through it is Rhys, a researcher who has studied this benchmark closely. Rhys, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. This is one of the papers that tried to settle an argument that was mostly being had on vibes at the time, whether large language models were actually ready for financial question answering, and it settled it with a very specific, very unflattering number.
[O] Let's start with the gap, Rhys. Why did finance need its own benchmark in late twenty twenty-three, when general QA benchmarks already existed?
[G] Because a financial analyst's actual job doesn't look like a reading-comprehension quiz. They spend their day finding a figure buried somewhere in a two-hundred-fifty-page ten-K, summarizing it, and reasoning about it to support a decision. General benchmarks like SQuAD, NarrativeQA, and HellaSwag contain few or no financial questions, and the authors are careful to note that strong performance on an open-domain benchmark doesn't reliably transfer to a specialist domain like finance.
[S] And the finance-specific benchmarks that did exist before this, FinQA, TAT-QA, FiQA, what was wrong with them?
[G] They handed the model the exact passage it needed. FinQA and TAT-QA are genuinely good at testing numerical reasoning over curated tables, but by giving the model the relevant page up front, they collapse the task into closed-book reading comprehension. That erases the part analysts actually find hardest, finding the right information in the first place. FiQA, meanwhile, is mostly sentiment analysis, a small slice of what analysts actually ask.
[O] So the bet here is open-book, realistic retrieval, real documents.
[G] Exactly, what the authors call ecological validity. Real questions an analyst would actually ask, over the real filings they'd actually read, graded with the model having to locate its own evidence rather than being handed it. And finance is a genuinely unforgiving domain for that test: it needs up-to-date company knowledge, it mixes free text with dense tables, questions often span multiple long filings, and it leans heavily on numerical reasoning, exactly where language models are known to slip.
[S] Which matters more here than in most domains, because a wrong number delivered with a confident explanation isn't a harmless miss. It's a hallucination that can move a real position.
[G] That's the framing the authors lead with directly, and it's why the grading scheme they built treats a wrong answer very differently from a refusal, but we'll get to that.
[O] Let's get into how the dataset itself was built.
[G] FinanceBench is ten thousand, two hundred thirty-one question, answer, and evidence triplets, covering forty US publicly-traded companies across three hundred sixty-one public filings, ten-Ks, ten-Qs, eight-Ks, and earnings reports, released between twenty fifteen and twenty twenty-three. Every entry has the question, the gold answer, an evidence string with the exact text that justifies it, and a page number, and many also include a human justification showing how a number was actually computed.
[S] There are three flavors of question, right, not just one big undifferentiated pile?
[G] Right, and they were built by a vetted team of finance annotators. Domain-relevant questions, nine hundred twenty-five of them, are twenty-five hand-built questions any analyst asks of any company, things like dividend history or margin consistency, posed across thirty-seven of the forty companies. Novel-generated questions, one thousand, three hundred twenty-three, are analyst-written and company-specific, deliberately kept reasoning-heavy rather than purely extractive, about thirty-six per company. And then metrics-generated questions, seven thousand, nine hundred eighty-three of them, are the bulk of the dataset. Annotators extracted eighteen base metrics from the three main financial statements across eight years of filings, and templates derived further metrics from those, like net income margin from net income divided by revenue.
[O] So the metrics-generated set is templated, but the underlying arithmetic is real.
[G] Genuinely real, some purely extractive, some requiring multi-statement arithmetic. And there's a secondary taxonomy over eight thousand, nine hundred eight of these questions labeling the skill involved: twenty-eight percent are pure information extraction, sixty-six percent involve numerical reasoning, and six percent involve logical reasoning. This is a benchmark dominated by getting numbers right, not by reading comprehension in the abstract.
[S] Who actually wrote these, and how much can we trust the labels?
[G] Twenty finance-credentialed annotators were recruited, many treasury analysts, finance MBAs, junior analysts. Five were dropped early for quality issues and their work discarded, leaving fifteen. A senior analyst reviewed roughly ten to twenty percent of each annotator's work every week, with corrections and feedback. It's a real quality pipeline, not a one-shot crowd task.
[O] And then, because manually grading every model response against ten thousand questions would be impossibly expensive, they carved out a smaller evaluation slice.
[G] A hundred fifty cases, fifty from each of the three question types, and only those hundred fifty are open-sourced. Everything you're about to hear as a headline number comes from grading model answers against that hundred-fifty-case slice, sixteen model configurations tested, which works out to two thousand, four hundred individual responses, manually labeled by the research team.
[S] Sixteen configurations from how many actual models?
[G] Four models across three providers, GPT-four and GPT-four-Turbo, Claude two, and Llama two, tested across five setups. Closed book is the most naive, the model gets the question and nothing else. Oracle hands the model the exact evidence page, removing retrieval entirely, an unrealistic upper bound. Single vector store builds one retrieval index per document, easy but unrealistic at scale. Shared vector store indexes across the whole shared corpus of filings at once, which is the setup that actually resembles how a firm would deploy this. And long context just dumps the whole filing straight into the prompt, up to a hundred thousand tokens for Claude two, a hundred twenty-eight thousand for GPT-four-Turbo, truncated around ninety-five thousand tokens when a filing runs longer.
[O] And the grading itself is three-way, not just right or wrong.
[G] Correct answer, incorrect answer, or failure to answer, and that third category is deliberate. A model explicitly saying it can't find the information is scored differently from a model confidently inventing a wrong one, because in a live setting those failure modes carry very different risk.
[S] Give me the actual numbers, starting with the worst case.
[G] Closed book GPT-four-Turbo, no documents at all, gets nine percent of questions correct, three percent incorrect, and refuses eighty-eight percent of the time. That's actually the safest kind of failure, a model that mostly knows what it doesn't know.
[O] And then you add retrieval, the setup that actually matters for a real deployment.
[G] Shared vector store, the realistic one, GPT-four-Turbo answers nineteen percent correctly, incorrect on thirteen percent, and fails to answer sixty-eight percent of the time. That's the number the abstract leads with, eighty-one percent wrong or refused.
[S] Nineteen percent correct on the setup that's actually supposed to resemble production use. That's the headline everyone quotes, and it's brutal.
[G] It is, but the more interesting story is what happens as you make retrieval easier. Give each document its own vector store instead of sharing one across all the filings, and GPT-four-Turbo jumps from nineteen percent to fifty percent. Llama two makes almost the same jump, nineteen percent to forty-one percent. Drop the whole filing into a long-context window and skip retrieval entirely, and GPT-four-Turbo reaches seventy-nine percent, Claude two reaches seventy-six percent. And the unrealistic oracle setting, evidence page handed over directly, tops out at eighty-five percent.
[O] So the closer you get the right page in front of the model, the more the gap closes.
[G] That's exactly the authors' conclusion, and it's the paper's strongest methodological contribution. The single-store-versus-shared-store-versus-oracle ladder is a clean ablation that pins most of the failure on retrieval, not on the model's arithmetic once it actually has the right evidence in front of it.
[S] But the failure mode isn't the same across models, right? That's the part I find genuinely useful here.
[G] Very different. GPT-four-Turbo tends to refuse when it can't find the evidence, sixty-eight percent failures against only thirteen percent wrong answers on the shared store. Llama two does the opposite, it confidently hallucinates, seventy percent incorrect on the shared store and fifty-four percent incorrect on the single store, with refusal rates in the single digits.
[O] So in a live setting, Llama two is the more dangerous model, not because it's less accurate in some abstract sense, but because when it's wrong, it doesn't tell you.
[G] Right, and there's a concrete example in the paper worth having in your head. Asked for Best Buy's fiscal-year-twenty-nineteen total inventories, two retrieval-augmented responses gave different wrong figures, eleven point three nine billion and nine point five billion. The correct answer, five point four one billion, was sitting right there in the filing. The failure isn't arithmetic. It's confidently pulling the wrong number off the wrong line and reporting it fluently.
[S] Two more findings before we get to the debate. Prompt order actually swings accuracy by double digits?
[G] By more than double digits. In the long-context setting, putting the filing before the question, versus after it, swings GPT-four-Turbo from seventy-eight percent down to twenty-five percent, and Claude two from seventy-six percent down to thirty-seven percent. The authors think the model loses track of the question after several thousand tokens of evidence when the question comes last. Interestingly, that pattern reverses in the oracle setting, where the context is much shorter, question-last actually does slightly better there, eighty-nine versus eighty-five percent.
[O] And the harder question category?
[G] Metrics-generated questions, the numeric, multi-statement ones, are the hardest category across nearly every realistic setup, which tracks with everything else we've just walked through. The gap mostly closes only once you move to long-context or oracle, where retrieval stops being the bottleneck.
[O] Let me make the optimist case, because I think there's a real one buried under that eighty-one percent headline. This paper didn't just show models fail at finance, it isolated exactly why: retrieval, not reasoning. Give a model the right page, and accuracy climbs steadily toward the mid-eighties. That's an engineering problem, not a capability ceiling, and engineering problems get solved fast. The retrieval stack here is a twenty twenty-three vintage Chroma database with ada-double-oh-two embeddings and naive chunking. That's not what a serious deployment would use even a year later.
[S] Here's my deflationary case, and it's really the same point turned around. If the eighty-one percent number is mostly a statement about a dated, naive retrieval pipeline, then it was never really telling you what GPT-four-Turbo can do at financial QA. It was telling you what a specific, already-outdated retrieval stack could do in front of it. That's a much weaker claim than the headline implies, and it's the headline everyone actually quotes.
[G] You're both circling the same asterisk, and it's the right one to flag. The models tested are a late-twenty-twenty-three cast, GPT-four, GPT-four-Turbo, Claude two, Llama two, on a twenty twenty-three retrieval stack. The shared-store result is genuinely as much an indictment of that retrieval pipeline as of the models themselves. Read the eighty-one percent as a twenty twenty-three watermark, not a standing fact about frontier capability today. That said, O, I'd push back a little on "engineering problem, easily solved." The paper's own contribution, isolating retrieval as the bottleneck, is durable. Whether that bottleneck is actually cheap to fix at production scale, across thousands of documents, with acceptable latency, is a separate, harder question the paper doesn't answer.
[S] There's a second issue that worries me more than the dated baselines, and it's about the number itself surviving into the present. Only a hundred fifty of the ten thousand, two hundred thirty-one cases are open-sourced. That hundred-fifty-case slice has since become one of the most widely used finance evaluations out there.
[G] Which means the exact numbers everyone quotes come from a hundred fifty cases that are now a real contamination candidate for any model trained after this paper was published. That's a sharper problem than most benchmark contamination stories, because it's not the underlying filings leaking, those are public SEC documents anyway and the open-book framing blunts that risk. It's the specific evaluation slice, question, answer, and grading label together, potentially leaking whole.
[O] Is there anything to say in the paper's defense there?
[G] Only that they were transparent about releasing just the hundred fifty and kept the other ten thousand withheld, which at least limits how much of the benchmark's substance is exposed. But it doesn't change that the exact cases people benchmark against today are the exposed ones.
[S] And the grading itself. I want to push on this, because it's the part that would bother me most if I were reviewing this paper cold. Two thousand, four hundred model responses, labeled by a single researcher on the team, spot-checked, complex cases discussed, with explicit allowance for minor deviations and rounding. No inter-annotator agreement is reported for those response labels.
[G] Correct, and that's a real gap, especially because sixty-six percent of the underlying questions are numerical, which is exactly where a correct-versus-incorrect judgment call gets fuzzy, a rounding difference, a units mismatch, a slightly different but defensible interpretation. A single grader working alone, however careful, leaves that judgment unverified.
[O] So where does that leave the eighty-one percent, honestly?
[G] As a genuinely useful, directionally correct finding, wrapped around three real asterisks. It's a late-twenty-twenty-three model-and-retrieval snapshot, not a permanent capability score. It's now measured almost entirely on a hundred-fifty-case public slice with meaningful contamination risk for anything trained since. And it rests on single-rater grading with no reported agreement, on a task that's two-thirds numerical, exactly where graders disagree most.
[S] Does any of this connect to how evaluation gets done more broadly, or is it a finance-specific story?
[G] It connects directly. The single-store, shared-store, oracle ladder is a template other domains have started borrowing, deliberately separating a retrieval failure from a reasoning failure instead of reporting one blended accuracy number that hides which one is actually broken. And the three-way correct, incorrect, refusal labeling is exactly the right instinct for any high-stakes domain, because it surfaces the hallucination-versus-honesty split that a single accuracy score would flatten out completely.
[O] So the lasting contribution isn't really the eighty-one percent.
[G] It's the framing. Ground the questions in real documents, grade open-book, and separate where the failure actually comes from. The number will keep aging. The methodology is what other benchmark builders, in finance and well outside it, kept.
