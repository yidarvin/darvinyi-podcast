[S] Here's an uncomfortable fact about the paper we're covering today: in the summer of twenty twenty three, it warned in writing that static benchmarks would get memorized by the very models being tested on them.
[O] And it was right. That warning turned into the founding argument behind half the dynamic-benchmark work that came after it.
[S] Sure, but the paper making that warning hasn't been substantively revised since December of that same year, while the exact problem it named kept mutating underneath it.
[O] Which is either a survey that aged badly, or a survey honest enough to print its own expiration date up front. I think that's worth the next half hour of arguing.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar dig into one paper from the literature until nothing shaky is left standing. Today's paper is "A Survey on Evaluation of Large Language Models," by Yupeng Chang, Xu Wang, and fourteen colleagues spanning Jilin University, Microsoft Research Asia, Westlake University, and several other institutions, first posted to arXiv in July of twenty twenty three.
[S] It's on the docket because it's one of the most cited framing documents in the entire evaluation literature, and framing documents are exactly the kind of paper that's easy to cite and hard to actually interrogate.
[O] Joining us to do that interrogating is Priya, who has spent real time with this survey. Welcome, Priya.
[G] Thanks for having me. I'd push back a little on "just a framing document" though. The authors call it the first comprehensive survey of large language model evaluation, and before this, if you wanted to know how the field was testing these models, you were reading dozens of individual papers with no shared vocabulary connecting any of them.
[S] Set the scene for me. Why did evaluation need its own survey specifically in the middle of twenty twenty three?
[G] Because the volume had exploded past what anyone could track by hand. The authors ran their own count of the literature: essentially zero LLM evaluation papers per month before twenty twenty one, climbing to thirty-three papers in the single final month they measured. Every new model paper ran its own grab-bag of benchmarks, every capability claim about reasoning or factuality or safety got checked against a different, ad hoc slice of prior work, and there was no common frame for comparing one evaluation effort against another.
[S] Give me a concrete example of that grab-bag problem actually causing trouble.
[G] Take the "sparks of artificial general intelligence" paper on GPT four, from right around the same time. It ran the model through a set of hand-crafted qualitative tests and argued for real generality. Critics pushed back specifically on the human-crafted nature of that evaluation: no shared protocol, no comparison benchmark, just one team's chosen probes. The survey we're discussing exists partly to stop exactly that kind of dispute from being unresolvable.
[O] That's the growth curve in Figure two, right? It practically doubles every couple of months through twenty twenty three.
[G] Close to it. Two papers in January, seven in February, twelve in March, fifteen in April, twenty-two in May, thirty-three by June. The authors use that exact chart as their justification for writing the survey in the first place.
[S] Which is a little bit funny, because that's also the chart that predicts the survey's own shelf life. If evaluation research is compounding that fast, a snapshot survey is racing a treadmill it cannot win.
[G] The authors aren't blind to that irony, to their credit, and we'll get to how they hedge it. But let's do the actual contribution first, because I think it's more substantial than "snapshot."
[O] Go ahead, what's the actual structural idea here?
[G] Three questions asked together for what the authors argue is the first time: what to evaluate, where to evaluate, and how to evaluate. Not one taxonomy, three orthogonal ones, each its own section, built out of two hundred sixty-nine references the authors read and organized by hand.
[O] Break down "what" for me first.
[G] Seven top-level capability branches. General natural language processing tasks, which itself splits into understanding, reasoning, generation, multilingual ability, and factuality. Then robustness, ethics, bias, and trustworthiness as one cluster. Social science. Natural science and engineering. Medical applications. Agent applications. And a catch-all "other applications" bucket covering education, search and recommendation, and personality testing. Reasoning alone pulls in well over a dozen separately cited studies.
[S] Seven branches for the entire space of things you might ask a language model to do feels either impressively compact or suspiciously convenient.
[G] It's a real tension, and I'll flag it now because we come back to it later: agent applications gets exactly one branch, with seven citations under it. That's the branch that, within about two months of this paper posting, exploded into its own subfield.
[O] Hold that thought. What about "where"?
[G] Table seven: forty-six benchmarks, split into three families. General-task benchmarks like MMLU, HELM, BIG-bench, C-Eval, and Chatbot Arena. Specific-downstream-task benchmarks like APPS, MATH, CUAD, ToolBench, and MultiMedQA. And multi-modal benchmarks like MME, MMBench, and SEED-Bench. I hadn't seen that many benchmarks catalogued and typed against each other in one table before this.
[S] And "how"?
[G] Two paradigms, split cleanly by whether a human has to be in the loop. Automatic evaluation covers four metric families: accuracy metrics like exact match, F one, and ROUGE; calibration metrics like expected calibration error; fairness metrics like demographic parity difference; and robustness metrics like attack success rate. Human evaluation gets its own table too, six criteria expanded from the earlier three H rule of helpfulness, honesty, and harmlessness: accuracy, relevance, fluency, transparency, safety, and human alignment.
[S] Six criteria sounds rigorous on paper, but who checks that different evaluators applying "transparency" or "human alignment" actually agree with each other?
[G] The authors flag that too, to their credit. Even human evaluation carries high variance and instability, partly from cultural and individual differences between evaluators. It's one of the more candid moments in the paper: adding more human judgment doesn't automatically buy you more reliable judgment.
[O] And this is where the seed of LLM as judge shows up, isn't it?
[G] Exactly one paragraph's worth. The paper mentions PandaLM, which trains a model specifically to judge other models' outputs, and LLM-Eval, a unified scoring method for open-domain conversation. Both get filed as a single bullet inside automatic evaluation, not flagged as a coming shift in how the entire field would score itself. Within about a year, GPT four as judge protocols like AlpacaEval and MT-Bench would become close to the default way people evaluate instruction-tuned models.
[S] So the taxonomy's own bones already show where the field was about to bend, and the paper just didn't know it yet.
[G] That's a fair read, and it's not really a knock. Nobody in July of twenty twenty three had that foresight. It's just the clearest evidence of how fast the ground moved right underneath this particular paper.
[O] Give me the actual findings then. What does two hundred sixty-nine papers' worth of synthesis conclude?
[G] Section six splits it into what LLMs do well and where they fail, based entirely on what the cited literature reported. On the "do well" side: fluent generation, sentiment analysis, text classification, arithmetic and logical reasoning, machine translation, question answering, and strong contextual comprehension. On the "fail" side: natural language inference, especially representing genuine human disagreement, abstract reasoning, non-Latin and low-resource languages, visual-modal grounding, and a documented tendency to absorb and amplify toxicity and bias present in training data.
[S] None of that is the paper's own experiment though. It's aggregating other people's numbers off other people's benchmarks, with no attempt to check whether those benchmarks even agree with each other or measure the same underlying thing.
[G] Correct, and the authors are explicit about that limit too. They say directly that no single benchmark or protocol comes out as "the most useful," precisely because the underlying studies don't converge cleanly. Section seven then closes with seven grand challenges: designing genuine AGI benchmarks, complete behavioral evaluation beyond static tasks, robustness evaluation, dynamic and evolving evaluation, principled and trustworthy evaluation systems, one unified framework spanning all LLM tasks, and treating evaluation as a lever for improving models rather than just scoring them.
[O] Dynamic and evolving evaluation is the one that ages best, right? That's the contamination warning from our cold open.
[G] Nearly word for word, yes. The authors write that static, public benchmarks are likely to be memorized by LLMs, resulting in training data contamination, and they call for dynamic and evolving evaluation systems as the fix. That's a genuinely correct prediction of the exact problem that motivated things like LiveCodeBench and LiveBench a year or two later.
[O] Okay, let me make the strongest case for this paper, because I think it's underrated even sitting at three thousand five hundred plus citations. This gave a scattered, ad hoc literature a shared vocabulary. What, where, how, that's not a trivial organizing move, it's the kind of frame later papers now use to justify their own existence without even citing it directly anymore. And it named its own biggest blind spot in advance. Section seven point four isn't hedging, it's a direct prediction that came true.
[S] I'll grant the vocabulary point, genuinely. But here's my deflationary case: a survey can't be falsified the way a claimed state of the art result can, so "advancing the field" and "cataloguing the field" are doing very different amounts of work, and this paper is almost entirely the second one. The agent applications branch has seven citations total. Within two months, SWE-bench arrived, then AgentBench, then WebArena, GAIA, and OSWorld, and agent evaluation became its own subfield with dozens of dedicated benchmarks this taxonomy has no branch for.
[O] That's not a flaw in the survey though, that's just what surveys are. A photograph, not a video.
[S] Sure, but a photograph you're still citing as current three years later stops being an honest photograph. The GitHub repository the authors maintain is a reading list, not a revised taxonomy. Figure one hasn't been redrawn since the ninth arXiv revision back in December of twenty twenty three.

That's a real form of influence, even if it isn't falsifiable the way a benchmark result is.
[O] Which is my whole point. You don't get cited past three thousand five hundred times by cataloguing quietly in a corner. People built on this.

That argument doesn't go stale even when the content underneath it does.
[O] So what does this actually change for someone doing evaluation work today?
[G] Two honest things. First, the what-where-how frame is still a genuinely useful checklist when you're scoping a new evaluation effort: have you covered the right capability, the right benchmark family, the right protocol. Second, you cannot treat this paper's content as current. Anyone citing its list of benchmarks, or its account of what state of the art LLMs could and couldn't do, needs to know it's a photograph of mid twenty twenty three, not a live index.
[S] And that's a real cost for a field moving this fast. HELM, one of the benchmark suites this survey catalogues as a single entry, updates continuously and re-runs live leaderboards. A static taxonomy paper competing for the same shelf space just cannot do that.
[O] Different tools for different jobs though. You don't need a leaderboard's freshness for conceptual scaffolding, you need that once, well, and this paper delivered it.
[G] The disclaimer section actually backs that up. The authors acknowledge up front that the field will keep moving and that some of what they describe will be outdated by the time anyone reads it, which is an unusually honest thing for a survey to put in writing.
[S] Honest, but it doesn't buy back three years of silence on the actual taxonomy tree.
[G] My one line: this is the right paper to cite for how the field learned to organize the question of evaluation, and the wrong paper to cite for what you should evaluate today. Read it as a map of the terrain circa twenty twenty three, not a current index.
