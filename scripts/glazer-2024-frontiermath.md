[S] Here's a stat that should worry anyone who grades AI systems on math: the best model in this paper solved under two percent of the problems, full stop.
[O] Six weeks after that number went public, a model from the lab that helped fund the benchmark scored twenty-five point two percent on it.
[S] That's either a genuine reasoning leap, or evidence the fox had a key to part of the henhouse — and the paper itself never tells you the second part happened.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the frontier of AI evaluation. Today's paper is FrontierMath, A Benchmark for Evaluating Advanced Mathematical Reasoning in AI, from Elliot Glazer and more than forty colleagues at Epoch AI, posted in November of twenty twenty-four.
[S] Forty-plus co-authors on a benchmark paper is itself a tell — most of them are the mathematicians who wrote the problems, not machine learning researchers.
[O] Joining us is Alaric, a visiting scholar who's spent real time with this benchmark and the mathematics underneath it. Alaric, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. This is one of the more interesting evaluation designs to come out of twenty twenty-four, for reasons that go well beyond the headline number.
[S] Alaric, start with why anyone needed a new math benchmark at all — what was wrong with the old ones?
[G] By late twenty twenty-four the standard testbeds were saturating. On the authors' own numbers, OpenAI's o1 was hitting ninety-four point eight percent on the MATH dataset, Claude three point five Sonnet was at ninety-six point four percent on GSM eight K, and o1 was at ninety-eight point one percent on the college-math slice of MMLU.
[O] Once everyone clusters near the ceiling, the benchmark stops discriminating between models, and it stops tracking whether the next model release is actually better at math.
[G] Right, and the paper is candid about why the obvious fixes don't scale. Hand-written harder problems already existed — the Advanced Reasoning Benchmark, the GHOSTS dataset with around seven hundred graduate-level problems — but those either lean on course material a large enough model may have absorbed, or need a human to grade a free-form proof, which kills scalability.
[S] And the other obvious fix — pull harder problems from real competitions — runs into contamination.
[G] Exactly the second failure mode. OlympiadBench, PutnamBench, Omni-Math — these draw thousands of real olympiad and competition problems, but that text is public and old, so a large pretraining run may well have seen it already. Putnam-AXIOM tested that directly by renaming the variables in existing Putnam problems, and o1-preview's accuracy fell from fifty percent on the originals to thirty-four percent on the renamed versions.
[O] Which is itself the tell — if renaming a variable costs sixteen points of accuracy, a chunk of the original score was memorization, not math.
[G] There's a third lane too: formal-proof benchmarks like MiniF2F, which sidestep contamination by demanding a Lean or Isabelle proof instead of a scraped answer. That trades one problem for another — it measures competition math in a proof language most models were barely trained on, and the authors note Lean's own math library doesn't even cover the full undergraduate curriculum.
[S] So the wishlist is genuinely new problems, genuinely hard problems, and grading that doesn't need a human reading a proof.
[G] That's precisely the gap FrontierMath is built to fill.
[O] Walk us through how they actually built it.
[G] The sourcing effort is unusually large for a benchmark paper — over sixty mathematicians, spread across more than a dozen countries, from graduate students up to faculty, collectively holding fourteen International Mathematical Olympiad gold medals, and one contributor holds a Fields Medal.
[S] That's a lot of expensive talent to spend writing exam questions.
[G] It's why the coverage is so wide: number theory, algebraic geometry, algebraic topology, combinatorics, category theory, representation theory, differential geometry, logic. The five released sample problems span that range — a density estimate on Artin's primitive-root conjecture, a degree-nineteen polynomial via monodromy and the Riemann existence theorem, a p-adic problem solved with the Skolem-Mahler-Lech theorem, a representation-theory orbit count, and a point count on a curve over a finite field via the Weil conjectures.
[O] None of that sounds like something you shotgun an answer to.
[G] Which is by design. Every problem had to clear four requirements, two of them the clever part. Originality means it can't be a known result lightly disguised. Guessproofness is sharper — the authors target no more than a one percent chance of guessing right without doing the real work, which is why answers tend to be large, structureless integers or exact symbolic expressions rather than small round numbers.
[S] And the other two requirements are what make automated grading possible at all.
[G] Automated verifiability and computational tractability. The answer has to be a Python object using SymPy — an integer, a symbolic expression, a matrix — and any reference solution has to run in under a minute on ordinary hardware. Where the answer is unique, grading is an exact match or a symbolic zero-check; where it isn't, say a Diophantine tuple, a bespoke script checks the submitted object against the real constraints directly, rather than one hard-coded answer.
[O] So a closed model behind an API gets graded the same way as an open one, because nobody's reading a proof.
[G] That's the whole point of the pipeline. On top of it, every problem gets at least one blind peer review for correctness, ambiguity, guessproofness, and difficulty tagging. The authors then ran a deeper audit on thirty-five problems — thirty random, plus the five released publicly — and found two had an author-provided answer that was simply wrong, missed by the first reviewer.
[S] Two out of thirty-five is basically six percent, right there in the audit sample.
[G] The authors compute it properly — a Jeffreys-prior posterior estimate on that trial gives about six point nine percent, and they round up to roughly ten percent once you account for errors even a second reviewer might miss. The same audit caught six problems with missing hypotheses and two that failed guessproofness on a closer look — all revised, not dropped.
[O] Ten percent is worth sitting with, but finish the method first — how do they even rate how hard these are?
[G] Three axes, self-reported by the problem's own author: Background, one to five, high school to research level; Creativity, estimated expert-hours to find the key idea, uncapped; and Execution, estimated expert-hours to grind out the computation once you have the idea. The authors admit first- and second-reviewer ratings often disagreed substantially, so a specific tier is one mathematician's impression, not a calibrated score.
[S] And contamination prevention — how do you keep sixty-plus people from leaking a few hundred hard problems onto the internet?
[G] Contributors worked over encrypted channels, kept materials in password-protected archives, and the finished set was screened with two plagiarism tools, Quetext and Copyscape — deliberately not Turnitin, since Turnitin stores submitted text in a database that could end up training a future model. No significant matches turned up. By subject, the set leans on number theory and combinatorics, seventeen point eight and fifteen point eight percent of classification tags respectively, drawing on over two hundred distinct solution techniques so nothing pattern-matches easily.
[O] Let's get to the number everyone quotes. How did the models actually do?
[G] Six contemporaneous frontier models ran through an experiment-enabled harness, not a single prompt — the model writes and executes Python in a loop, sees the output fed back into its context, and keeps going up to a ten-thousand-token budget, then submits a best guess through a specific pickle-dump convention. The six were o1-preview, o1-mini, GPT-4o, Claude three point five Sonnet, Grok two Beta, and Gemini one point five Pro.
[S] And the headline.
[G] On a single pass over the full benchmark, no model reached two percent accuracy. Compare that to the same models elsewhere — o1 at seventy-four percent on AIME twenty twenty-four, ninety-four point eight on MATH, ninety-eight point one on MMLU college math; Claude at ninety-six point four on GSM eight K; o1-mini at sixty point five four on Omni-Math.
[O] That's not a small gap — that's a benchmark reading near zero while every other math benchmark reads near-saturated.
[S] Which is exactly why I want to know how solid that zero actually is. Was this one clean run per model?
[G] For five of the six models, yes — a single pass over the full set. Because the raw signal sits so close to the noise floor, the authors flag the single-pass ranking as unreliable themselves, and instead dig into just the four problems any model solved at all, rerunning each model five times on those four.
[S] So "under two percent" is solid — that's the full-set single pass — but any claim about which model is best rests on four problems and five reruns.
[G] That's a fair read, and the data backs it up. Only one cell in the whole grid hit five for five — o1-preview, on the algebraic topology problem. Everything else is noisy: Grok two Beta solved one problem sixty percent of the time and zero percent on the other three; Gemini did the mirror image, sixty percent on a different problem and zero everywhere else.
[O] Overall though, o1-preview came out ahead, and in an interesting way.
[G] It averaged one point two nine responses per problem before committing, versus three point eight one for Grok two Beta — mostly one-shotting rather than iterating. Token use tells the same story: Gemini hit the ten-thousand-token ceiling on only sixteen point eight percent of questions, using around six thousand tokens on average, while Claude, GPT-4o, and Grok blew past the limit on over forty-five percent of attempts, averaging twelve to seventeen thousand tokens.
[S] Meaning the other three were still grinding when the clock ran out.
[G] The cleaner summary number across all this is pass at eight — the chance at least one of eight attempts succeeds — and o1-preview leads there too, at roughly six percent.
[O] Six percent is still a wall, not a crack.
[G] It is. And one honesty note worth naming: the authors say that on at least one solved problem, a few quick simulations were enough to guess the right answer without real mathematical understanding — a correct answer, not necessarily correct reasoning. Given the overall solve rate is under two percent, they conclude that shortcut isn't generalizing across the set, but it's a real crack in guessproofness.
[S] Alaric, bring in the humans now. What did the actual mathematicians say when they looked at this?
[G] The authors interviewed four prominent mathematicians — Fields medalists Terence Tao, Timothy Gowers, and Richard Borcherds, plus Evan Chen, an IMO coach and co-author who contributed problems alongside Tao. All four called the research-level problems exceptionally hard. Tao's read on a sample set was blunt: short of a real domain expert, the only way to solve them now is a graduate student paired with an AI and some algebra packages.
[O] And he had a number for the gap too, right?
[G] He estimated that guiding an AI to a correct solution today takes about five times the effort of just solving it yourself — though he expects that to fall below one within a few years. He also ventured the benchmark would resist AIs for several years at least.
[S] What about the pushback? Not everyone can have loved the format.
[G] Borcherds was the dissenting voice — the problems, he said, aren't quite the same as coming up with original proofs, and forcing a single numerical answer feels somewhat contrived next to how research actually proceeds. Gowers offered a narrower, more constructive framing: such a system could be a nice time-saving assistant for the boring parts of research, checking whether a suspected conjecture is actually true.
[O] Okay, optimist case. This is careful eval engineering — guessproofness as an explicit design target, a grading pipeline needing zero human graders, an actual Bayesian error-rate estimate instead of "we checked it, trust us." That's the bar the field should hold benchmarks to. And it did its job: a real, hard number in November twenty twenty-four, exactly when GSM eight K and MATH had nothing left to say.
[S] I'll take the deflationary side — three reasons, only one of which the paper admits to. First: a ten percent estimated error rate under a headline solve rate under two percent isn't a footnote, it's potentially half the story. If the true error rate is near ten percent, a meaningful chunk of "the model failed to reason" could really be "the reference answer is wrong," and nothing in the released numbers tells an outside reader which.
[G] That's fair, and it's the authors' own number — they round two out of thirty-five up to roughly ten percent themselves. To their credit they say so plainly; the issue is that a ten-percent error band next to a two-percent solve rate leaves very little room to trust any individual "unsolved" verdict.
[S] Second, the difficulty tags are self-reported by whoever wrote the problem, and the paper's own second reviewers frequently disagreed with the first reviewer's rating. So "this is a fifteen-hour research problem" is one mathematician's gut feel, not a measurement.
[O] Both fair, but neither changes the headline direction — even generously discounting for a ten percent error rate, models are still solving something like one in twenty problems at best, not one in two.
[S] Sure, the direction survives. My third point isn't in the paper at all, and it changes how you should read the whole thing. The only mention of OpenAI here is one acknowledgments line thanking them for their support. What that hides is that OpenAI funded and had early access to three hundred of the problems, statements and solutions both, except for a fifty-question holdout where they got statements only.
[G] That detail didn't come from this paper. It surfaced afterward, in a separate blog post from Epoch AI, and by their own account they needed OpenAI's sign-off before disclosing the relationship at all, and hadn't even nailed down the data-access terms when they finally did disclose it.
[O] And it isn't abstract — six weeks after this paper posted its under-two-percent headline, OpenAI announced o3 scoring twenty-five point two percent on FrontierMath.
[S] An order-of-magnitude jump, on a benchmark whose own authors call the ranking unreliable at these low numbers, right after the lab that funded a third of the question set released a model with a suspicious amount of daylight over everyone else.
[G] To be precise about what we can and can't conclude: nothing in the public record proves the o3 number reflects leaked answers rather than a genuine capability jump. But the paper's silence on the funding relationship is exactly why a reader can't rule that out either way, and that's a real cost, independent of what actually happened.
[O] Which is the frustrating part. The science underneath this benchmark is good. The disclosure practice around it is what let the air out of the headline number.
[S] Zoom out, then — what does this mean for how the field should build and disclose evals going forward?
[G] Two structural points the authors admit themselves, separate from the funding issue. Every problem must reduce to something a script can check, so FrontierMath can't include proof-writing or open-ended exploration — arguably most of a research mathematician's week. And because problems are scoped to expert-hours rather than the weeks or months a real open problem demands, a high score would show a narrower capability: fast, concentrated technical problem-solving, not sustained research.
[O] Which is a useful caution for anyone tempted to read a future high FrontierMath score as "AI can now do math research."
[G] It would mean something. Just not that.
[S] And the bigger lesson for the field is the funding story — if a benchmark is going to be used to make claims about a specific lab's model, any funding or early-access relationship with that lab belongs in the paper itself, not in a follow-up blog post six weeks later.
[G] If there's one line to take from the paper on its own terms, it's this: the automated, guessproof verification pipeline is a real fix for how math benchmarks contaminate and saturate, and the under-two-percent number was a legitimate, useful signal at the moment it published.
[O] My takeaway is that this is what a well-engineered benchmark looks like when it's honest about its own error bars, and the gap it exposed is still one of the most important results of the year.
[S] Mine is simpler: treat any FrontierMath score you hear quoted as incomplete until you know which subset it was run against, the public sample, the OpenAI-funded slice, or the independent holdout, and go read the full writeup on the litsearch site for the figures and the rest of the numbers.
