[S] So the worry isn't exotic. It's the most boring possible failure mode. The test leaked into the textbook.
[G] Right, and by mid twenty twenty-four it wasn't a fringe worry anymore, it was everywhere. That's exactly the regime where a handful of memorized points quietly decide bragging rights.
[O] But nobody could actually prove it, because you can't inspect somebody else's training set.
[G] Exactly, and the usual defenses don't help much either. N-gram overlap filters and embedding-similarity decontamination are both leaky, and neither is auditable from outside the lab that ran it. So the authors borrow a trick from computer vision. Back in twenty nineteen, Recht and colleagues rebuilt new test sets for CIFAR-ten and ImageNet from scratch and measured how much accuracy evaporated on the new set. This paper runs that exact experiment on language model reasoning.
[S] Build a fresh twin of the benchmark, guaranteed unseen, and measure the gap.
[G] That's the whole design. What would a model actually score if GSM8K's test set had simply never been published? You can't observe that directly, but a difficulty-matched, provably unseen twin is the closest anyone can get.
[O] So walk us through GSM1K itself. Felix, how do you build a fair twin of a benchmark without just generating more of the same questions with a model?
[G] You don't let a model anywhere near it. GSM1K is one thousand two hundred and five problems, and every single one was written by a human annotator, with zero LLM or synthetic data anywhere in the pipeline. Annotators were shown three real GSM8K problems at a target difficulty level and asked to write a genuinely new problem at that same level.
[S] Why does that constraint matter so much? Generating candidates with a model and filtering them would've been so much cheaper.
[G] Because a synthetically generated twin could itself be contaminated, by the very model that wrote it. Human-only construction closes that loophole completely.
[O] Writing new problems is the easy part conceptually. Matching them to GSM8K closely enough that a score drop can't just be blamed on GSM1K being harder, that's the actual engineering.
[G] Right, and the authors check that on several axes. First, solution-step distribution, they count the calculator-tag operations in each solution and match GSM1K's step-count histogram to GSM8K's. Second, answer magnitude, since models are known to struggle more with problems that produce large numbers, the final winnowing step discards GSM1K candidates until the answer-magnitude distribution overlays GSM8K's almost exactly.
[S] Those are design controls. Did they actually check whether it worked, with real people?
[G] Two independent human checks. For distinguishability, nineteen fresh annotators were shown five problems, four from GSM8K and one from GSM1K, and paid to spot the odd one out. Correct identification rate, twenty-one point eight three percent, against a twenty percent chance baseline. Humans basically can't tell the difference.
[O] And for difficulty itself?
[G] Fourteen annotators, fifteen minutes on the clock, solve as many as you can. On GSM8K they averaged four point oh seven problems, standard deviation point nine three. On GSM1K, four point three six, standard deviation one point one one. So GSM1K is, if anything, marginally easier, not harder. That matters a lot for interpreting later results, because it means any accuracy drop can't be waved away as "the new test was just tougher."
[S] What about quality control on the writing itself? Thousands of human-written arithmetic problems is a recipe for typos and ambiguous phrasing.
[G] Three review layers. Trusted annotators check correctness and formatting first. Then an independent annotator blind-resolves each problem without seeing the intended answer, and anything that doesn't match gets discarded. Then Scale's general quality-audit team reviews what's left. Out of two thousand one hundred and eight initial problems, one thousand four hundred nineteen survived the blind resolve, one thousand three hundred seventy-five passed the audit, and after the final magnitude-matching winnow, one thousand two hundred and five made the final cut.
[O] That's a serious amount of human labor for a benchmark nobody's even allowed to download.
[G] Which is deliberate, and it's the other half of the design. They're not releasing GSM1K publicly, because releasing it would recreate exactly the contamination problem it exists to diagnose. They've precommitted to open-sourcing it once either three open-source models from distinct lineages clear ninety-five percent, or June twenty twenty-five arrives, whichever comes first. There's also a held-out tripwire set, extra vetted problems that never made the public dataset, kept in reserve to catch leaks if a proprietary provider's API access ever quietly turns into training data.
[S] How do you even evaluate closed models on a benchmark you refuse to release?
[G] You send the questions over the API and trust the provider not to log them into a future training run. That's a real residual risk, and the authors say so plainly instead of pretending it away.
[O] And the evaluation harness itself, is that held constant too?
[G] A fork of EleutherAI's LM Evaluation Harness, identical settings across every model, five-shot prompting drawn from the GSM8K training set, temperature zero, and they raised the maximum generation length from two hundred fifty-six tokens to one thousand, because the shorter cap was cutting off chains of thought mid-reasoning. Because the harness is uniform rather than tuned per model, some of their GSM8K numbers land below what a model's official leaderboard entry claims. That's fine, the object of study isn't the absolute score, it's the GSM8K-minus-GSM1K gap.
[S] And then there's the memorization probe, which is really the clever part.
[G] For open-weight models only, since you need access to the model's own token probabilities. They compute the per-character-normalized log-likelihood of the model generating the actual GSM8K test set, essentially, how natural does this exact sequence feel to the model. Higher log-likelihood is read as evidence that the sequence, or something very close to it, showed up during training.
