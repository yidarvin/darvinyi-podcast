[O] Every field has one instrument that everybody uses and almost nobody can defend from first principles. In economics, that's the Bartik instrument.
[S] Three decades of papers ran the same recipe. Interact local industry shares with national growth rates, call it an instrument, publish. Nobody had actually agreed on why it was valid.
[O] And when someone finally forced the question, the answer wasn't reassuring. On the single most famous use of this trick, the industries actually driving the estimate weren't the ones the original authors pointed to.
[S] Which means a huge slice of applied economics has been leaning on an assumption nobody had ever written down.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an AI optimist, an AI skeptic, and a visiting scholar take one paper apart at a time.
[S] I'm the skeptic, and today's paper isn't machine learning at all. It's an econometrics paper from twenty twenty, and it earns its spot here because two of this graph's own most cited nodes are quietly built on the exact design it interrogates.
[O] We're talking about Bartik Instruments, What, When, Why, and How, by Paul Goldsmith-Pinkham, Isaac Sorkin, and Henry Swift, published in the American Economic Review. Joining us is Marcus, who's spent real time with this paper's proofs and its replications. Welcome, Marcus.
[G] Happy to be here. This is one of those papers where the math is almost secondary to the discipline it introduces, so I'd rather spend our time on what it actually forces researchers to report.
[S] Start with the basics, Marcus. What is a Bartik instrument, in plain terms?
[G] Take a local labor market, a city or a region, and ask how exposed it is to a national trend. You take that place's existing employment shares across industries in some baseline year, multiply each one by how fast that industry is growing nationally, and add it up. A place with a lot of workers in a fast-growing industry gets a high score, a place concentrated in a shrinking industry gets a low one. That combined number is the instrument, named for Bartik's nineteen ninety-one work and popularized by Blanchard and Katz the following year.
[O] And it's been everywhere, not just labor economics.
[G] Right. By the time this paper came out, some version of it had been used across labor, public finance, development, trade, macroeconomics, and finance for three decades, estimating the elasticity of labor supply, the local effects of Chinese import competition, wage effects of immigration, the effects of credit-supply shocks. This graph already has two of the biggest examples sitting in it, the China-shock paper by Autor, Dorn, and Hanson, and the robots-and-jobs paper by Acemoglu and Restrepo. Both instrument local exposure with exactly this shares-times-growth recipe.
[S] So what's the actual problem? That sounds like a design people understood well enough to use for thirty years.
[G] They understood how to build it. What was missing was agreement on why it's valid. There are two very different stories you could tell. One says the local shares are the exogenous piece, which makes this a differential-exposure design, close cousin to a difference-in-differences setup, comparing places with more of an industry to places with less. The other says the exogenous thing is the national growth rate, the "shock," which requires many independent industry shocks that average out across the whole bundle. Practitioners motivated their papers with a two-industry toy example and moved on without ever settling which story was actually doing the work.
[O] And with real data you'd have dozens or hundreds of industries bundled into one number.
[G] Exactly, which meant a reader had no way to open that number up. You couldn't tell from the coefficient alone whether a result rested on broad, diffuse variation across the whole economy, or on one or two idiosyncratic industries carrying the entire estimate.
[S] So how do the authors actually resolve which story is true?
[G] Two moves. First, an equivalence result. Employment growth in a place is just the sum, across industries, of that industry's local share times its growth rate. The Bartik instrument keeps the local shares but replaces each industry's growth rate with only its national component. Written that way, two-stage least squares using the Bartik instrument turns out to be numerically identical to a generalized method of moments estimator that instead uses every industry's local share as its own separate instrument, weighted by a matrix built from the growth rates.
[O] Numerically identical, not just similar?
[G] Identical, same coefficient every time, by construction. It's an algebraic identity, not an empirical claim, so there's nothing to argue with on that part. The consequence is what matters for practice: the assumption you're actually leaning on is exogeneity of the shares, not of the national growth rates. Whatever story a paper tells about "shocks," the design is really a pooled comparison across places with more or less of specific industries.
[S] Okay, but isn't that just relabeling something people already suspected? What's the actual contribution beyond a cleaner name for the assumption?
[G] Fair pushback, and the authors anticipate it. The relabeling isn't the contribution by itself, it's what the relabeling makes possible. Because a Bartik instrument is a weighted average of these individual share-instruments, you can ask exactly how much weight each one carries. That weight is called a Rotemberg weight, building on earlier work by Andrews, Gentzkow, and Shapiro. It's a sensitivity elasticity, how much a violation in one industry's exogeneity assumption would bias the overall estimate. The weights sum to one across all industries, though individual weights can be negative, and in every application the authors run, a small number of industries end up carrying most of the total.
[O] So instead of trusting the aggregate coefficient, you can ask which handful of industries are actually behind it.
[G] Right, and then check those specific industries the way you'd check any comparison group. Do they have clean pre-trends? Do they match the story the paper's own narrative is telling? The authors turn this into a short checklist. Report the shares with the largest weights and see if they match the paper's emphasis. Check how much of the variation in weights traces back to the growth-rate side versus the share side. Test for pre-trends specifically among the high-weight shares rather than the aggregate instrument. And compare the headline two-stage least squares estimate against alternative estimators, an overidentified version, a maximum-likelihood variant, jackknife and heteroskedasticity-robust versions, plus a formal overidentification test.
[S] Show me it actually finds something. Don't just tell me it could.
[G] Their own canonical example is a labor-supply design run on Census data from nineteen eighty to twenty ten. Three hundred ninety-seven industries go into the instrument, but the top five, oil and gas extraction, motor vehicles, an "other" category, guided missiles, and blast furnaces, carry over forty percent of the total positive weight. And the national growth-rate component explains under one percent of the variation in which industries get the big weights. The "shocks are averaging out" story is doing basically no work in their own baseline example.
[O] Let's get to the paper everyone actually cares about, the China-shock replication.
[G] Right, this is Autor, Dorn, and Hanson's twenty-thirteen paper, instrumenting local labor markets' import exposure with industry shares interacted with import growth from China to other high-income countries. The authors reproduce the original result closely, a decline of point six of a percentage point in manufacturing employment share, matching Autor, Dorn, and Hanson's own preferred estimate.
[S] So the number replicates. Where's the catch?
[G] The catch is in the weights. Despite three hundred ninety-seven underlying industries, the top five instruments, electronic computers, games and toys, household audio and video, telephone apparatus, and computer equipment, carry over half the total weight. And apart from games and toys, those are higher-skill, technologically advanced industries. They are not the low-skill, labor-intensive industries, apparel, footwear, furniture, textiles, that the original paper's own narrative is built around.
[O] That's a real mismatch between the story and what's actually driving the number.
[G] It is. The growth-rate side, import growth to other high-income countries, explains less than twenty percent of the variation in weights, so the "these industries got hit by an exogenous China shock" framing isn't a great guide to what the estimate depends on either. When the authors run alternative estimators, they diverge sharply, from about half the size of the baseline estimate on one jackknife-style version to several times larger on a maximum-likelihood version. And a formal overidentification test rejects the idea that all three hundred ninety-seven instruments are jointly estimating the same thing.
[S] That's not a small caveat. That's the field's most cited shift-share design showing real disagreement across estimators that are all supposed to be measuring the same effect.
[G] It's a genuine flag, and the authors are careful to describe it as exactly that, a flag, not a refutation. They don't re-estimate a corrected China-shock effect. They show you where to look.
[O] Was every application this uncomfortable? You mentioned three designs.
[G] No, and the third one is the useful contrast. It's a re-analysis in the spirit of Card's two thousand nine immigrant-enclave design, estimating substitution between immigrants and native workers. For high-school-equivalent workers, the weights there are almost entirely explained by the immigrant inflows themselves, and the share of Mexican immigrants living in a city back in nineteen eighty alone carries almost half the total weight. Card had already flagged that concentration himself, in the original paper. But here, most overidentification tests fail to reject, and the alternative estimators agree closely with each other. It's the paper's clean example, where running the diagnostic comes back reassuring instead of alarming.
[O] So the tool isn't a doom machine. Sometimes it tells you the design is fine.
[G] Exactly, and that matters. The paper isn't arguing every shift-share design is broken, it's arguing you can't know which ones are without actually running the check.
[O] Give me the optimist case, because I think it's a strong one here. This turns a hand-wavy identification story into an auditable object. Any reader can now ask "which five industries" instead of taking a paragraph of prose on faith. And it isn't hypothetical, it's already becoming standard practice, report a Rotemberg-weight table, report pre-trends for the high-weight shares. That's a real, lasting improvement to how a whole subfield does empirical work.
[S] Here's my deflation. The diagnostic is descriptive. It cannot confirm or reject that a share is actually exogenous, it can only tell you where to look. And it doesn't resolve the debate it sits on top of. Borusyak, Hull, and Jaravel put out a competing framework the same year, arguing inference should rest on a smaller number of "important" shocks rather than shares, and under their assumptions you can still get valid, if less precise, inference. Those two frameworks can disagree about which designs are credible. So "run the diagnostic" doesn't hand you one clean verdict, it hands you a number that still needs interpreting.
[G] Both of you are right, and they're not actually in tension. What isn't up for debate is the equivalence result itself, that part is an exact algebraic identity, verified by construction, worked by hand in the two-industry and two-period special cases before the general proof. Nobody contests that. What is a judgment call is everything downstream of it, whether a flagged industry's pre-trend means you should discount the headline estimate, and by how much. The paper is honest that it doesn't answer that. It hands you the telephone-apparatus pre-trend and stops.
[S] Which is exactly what happened with the robots-and-jobs paper on this graph. Same design, same kind of finding.
[G] Right, that one ran this exact diagnostic on its own robot-exposure instrument and found automotive manufacturing alone explains sixty-seven percent of the cross-commuting-zone variation, with Rotemberg weights running fifty to ninety percent in its main specifications. That paper only had that number to report because this framework existed. So a point for the optimist case, the tool changed what counts as due diligence, and a point for the skeptic too, because in both papers, finding the concentrated weight didn't produce a revised, corrected estimate. It produced a flag the reader has to sit with.
[O] I'll take that trade. A field that flags its own weak spots is in better shape than one that doesn't know it has them.
[S] I'd just add, the flag is only useful if people keep raising it. A diagnostic that becomes a box to check without anyone reading what it says is worse than no diagnostic at all.
[O] Does this generalize past economics? It feels like it should.
[G] The structural idea travels well. Any time an aggregate measure is built by summing many components, some benchmark score across many tasks, some influence measure across many training examples, you can ask the same question, which components are actually carrying the number, and do those specific components hold up under scrutiny. The Rotemberg weight is a formal version of an instinct evaluation researchers already have, that a headline score can hide a handful of examples doing all the discriminating work.
[S] It's basically a leakage check dressed up in economics notation. A "three hundred ninety-seven industry" instrument that's functionally five industries is the same shape of problem as a benchmark with thousands of examples that a model's score really tracks on a few hundred of them.
[O] And the fix is the same too. You don't throw out the aggregate number, you report what's underneath it.
[G] That's a fair extension, though it goes a bit beyond what the paper itself claims, the authors stay inside shift-share designs. But the discipline, decompose the aggregate, audit the pieces that matter most, report that audit alongside the headline number, isn't specific to labor economics.
[S] It's also a discipline that only works if the audit gets published alongside the headline, not filed away in an appendix nobody opens.
[G] If there's one line to take from the paper, it's the authors' own: this doesn't overturn any specific published estimate, and they're careful to say so. What it gives every reader of a shift-share paper is a two-line check, which handful of shares is actually carrying the number, and do those shares have clean pre-trends, instead of trusting the author's framing of which industries matter.
[O] My takeaway is that this is what real methodological progress looks like in applied economics. Not a flashier estimator, a cheap habit that became mandatory because it kept finding real problems.
[S] Mine is more cautious. The habit is only as good as how skeptically people read what it turns up, and on the field's most famous example, the industries doing the work still weren't the ones the story was built around. That's worth remembering every time someone cites that six-tenths of a percentage point number.
