[O] Ask GPT-4 to move five blocks around a table into a new arrangement, and it hands you a fluent six-step plan, confident verbs, correct-looking block names, and by step four it's physically impossible, because two steps earlier the plan itself covered up the very block it's now trying to stack something on.
[S] So the fix in this paper isn't a smarter model. It's no model doing the actual thinking. Hand the search off to a decades-old style of algorithm that's been provably correct since before either of us was born, and let the language model do the one thing it's actually reliable at, which is translating.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist and a skeptic argue about a paper until someone who actually knows it settles the score.
[S] Today's paper is L-L-M plus P: Empowering Large Language Models with Optimal Planning Proficiency, by Bo Liu, Yuqian Jiang, Xiaohan Zhang, Qiang Liu, Shiqi Zhang, Joydeep Biswas, and Peter Stone, out of U T Austin and SUNY Binghamton, posted in the spring of twenty twenty-three.
[O] Joining us is Elena, who's spent real time with this paper and the planning literature it sits inside. Welcome, Elena.
[G] Glad to be here. Quick disclosure up front: I wasn't one of the seven authors, so when I say "they" or "the paper shows," that's exactly what I mean. I'm reporting what's on the page, not defending my own work.
[S] For context: six hundred fifty-three citations at last count, and it's never gone through peer review anywhere, it's stayed a preprint the whole time.
[G] Right, and it's become close to a standard citation anytime someone connects language models to classical planners.
[S] Elena, start with the failure mode that opens the paper, because I think it's the whole argument compressed into one paragraph.
[G] Take the paper's own example. Five blocks, a specific goal stacking, GPT-4, zero examples given. It produces a six-step plan that reads perfectly, but it's wrong. Step four tries to move block two onto block five, except two steps earlier the plan itself put block three onto block five. Block five isn't clear anymore, and the model never tracked that its own third step changed the world it was reasoning about.
[O] That's such a specific kind of failure, though. It's not that the model doesn't understand English, it's that it can't hold a changing world state in its head across six steps.
[G] Exactly the distinction the paper leans on, borrowed from a cognitive-science paper by Mahowald and colleagues: linguistic competence, knowing how to say things, versus functional competence, knowing what to say. GPT-4 has plenty of the first. This kind of long-horizon state tracking needs the second.
[S] Meanwhile classical planning has had a working answer to exactly this problem for decades.
[G] Right, that's the other half of the setup. Feed a sound and complete symbolic planner a formal problem description, a starting state, a goal, actions with explicit preconditions and effects, and it's guaranteed to find a correct plan if one exists, often the cheapest one. The catch is "formal." Nobody wants to hand-write that specification for every new task, which is exactly why classical planning stayed a research tool instead of a natural-language interface.
[O] So the gap isn't "planning is unsolved." It's "translating a sentence into something a planner can read is unsolved."
[G] That's the paper's whole bet. It's not the first attempt to connect language models to planners, a dozen concurrent papers do versions of this, but it's the one that specifically isolates translation as the model's job and leaves the actual reasoning to something already solved.
[S] Walk us through the pipeline itself, Elena. What are the moving parts?
[G] Three stages, sitting on one big assumption. The assumption: a human or a domain expert has already written what the paper calls the domain P-D-D-L file, the fixed vocabulary of actions for this whole family of problems, things like pick-up or stack, each with explicit preconditions and effects. That file is written once per domain, not once per problem.
[O] So all the per-problem work happens downstream of that one fixed setup file.
[G] Right. Stage one: the language model reads the new problem in plain English, plus one worked example, a prior problem paired with its correct P-D-D-L, and writes the problem P-D-D-L: the objects, the starting state, the goal. Stage two: that file, plus the fixed domain file, goes to a classical planner called Fast Downward, which searches with zero risk of an illegal move, since it only considers actions whose preconditions provably hold. Stage three: the planner returns a bare action sequence, and the language model translates it back into natural language, or a robot's function calls.
[S] So the model never plans. It's a translator going in, a translator coming out, and the middle is somebody else's already-solved problem.
[G] That's the entire pitch, and the paper is explicit that this isn't incidental. They frame writing P-D-D-L as a machine-translation task the model is already good at, not a reasoning task it has to perform.
[O] Does the translation actually work without help, though?
[G] No, and this is the most honest part of the paper. Ask GPT-4 cold, zero examples, for the P-D-D-L file on that same five-block problem, and it invents a predicate that doesn't exist, literally a made-up condition called "empty," and drops the fact that one block starts on the table. Syntactically it looks like real P-D-D-L. Semantically it's garbage, and no planner downstream can rescue that.
[S] And one example fixes it?
[G] One. A single worked pair, a different five-block problem plus its correct P-D-D-L, as context, same GPT-4, same target problem, and the file that comes back is syntactically and semantically correct, and Fast Downward solves it immediately. The paper's own words: without that context, the language models fail to produce correct problem files. That's not a marginal bump, that's zero to working.
[O] So the in-context example is doing an enormous amount of load-bearing work.
[S] And it's hand-picked by a human, not learned by the system. Worth flagging before we get to the numbers, because it means every domain's performance is gated on somebody choosing a good example.
[G] Agreed, and the paper doesn't study how sensitive results are to which example gets picked. One example, chosen by the authors, per domain.
[O] Let's get to the actual numbers, because I've been waiting.
[G] Seven domains, borrowed from past International Planning Competitions: Barman, a robot bartender mixing drinks; Blocksworld, the classic stacking problem; Floortile, robots painting a checkerboard floor without stepping on wet tiles; Grippers, moving objects between rooms; Storage, hoists lifting crates; Termes, a robot building structures by stacking and climbing blocks; and Tyreworld, changing a flat tire in order. Twenty auto-generated problems per domain, one hundred forty total, all solved with GPT-4 at temperature zero, so every run is deterministic.
[S] Deterministic is good for reproducibility, less good for telling you anything about variance.
[G] Hold that thought, it's where I'd push back myself later. They run Fast Downward with a provably optimal search first, and if that times out after two hundred seconds, fall back to a faster, not-guaranteed-optimal search called LAMA. The optimal-search rate is the headline number, the LAMA fallback rate is reported alongside it where it matters.
[O] And the baselines?
[G] Four of them, plus the main result. The language model as planner with no example, the language model as planner with one example, a Tree of Thoughts style baseline that turns the language model into its own breadth-first search over candidate actions, and then L-L-M plus P itself, with and without its in-context example.
[S] Give me the actual table.
[G] Barman: every direct baseline scores zero percent, L-L-M plus P gets twenty percent optimal, one hundred with the fallback counted. Blocksworld: baselines top out around thirty percent with fallback, L-L-M plus P hits ninety. Grippers: baselines climb to sixty percent with fallback, L-L-M plus P reaches ninety-five, one hundred with fallback. Storage: baselines sit at zero or twenty-five with fallback, L-L-M plus P is at eighty-five.
[O] That's not a close race in any of those domains.
[G] It isn't. Tyreworld: baselines top out at fifteen percent, L-L-M plus P gets ten percent optimal, ninety percent with the fallback. And without its own in-context example, L-L-M plus P falls straight to zero percent everywhere, the same finding as the translation test, just showing up again across the whole benchmark.
[S] You skipped two domains.
[G] On purpose, because they're the interesting exceptions. Termes: every baseline is zero percent, and L-L-M plus P only reaches twenty. Floortile: everyone, including L-L-M plus P, scores zero percent flat.
[O] Zero for the method that's supposed to win? What happens there?
[G] The planner works fine in both cases, the failures are upstream, in translation. On Floortile specifically, the generated problem files omit initial conditions, leaving floor tiles disconnected in the model's representation, which makes the derived problem literally unsolvable no matter how good the planner is. It's not that the planner tried and failed. It's that the model handed it a broken map.
[S] So the one domain where the story breaks is the cleanest evidence for my worry: this system is exactly as good as GPT-4's P-D-D-L writing, full stop.
[G] That's a fair read, and it's basically what the paper itself concludes too. Most L-L-M plus P failures across the whole suite trace back to mis-specified problem files, not planner limitations.
[O] There's also a real robot in this paper, right? Not just benchmark numbers.
[G] Yes, a home-service robot with one gripper, given a tidy-up request: move a mustard bottle from the coffee table to the pantry, and throw out an empty soup can on the side table along the way. The side table and recycling bin happen to be on the route, so the genuinely optimal plan re-grasps the bottle partway through rather than two separate trips.
[S] And does it find that?
[G] It does. L-L-M plus P produces the plan at total cost twenty-two, using the paper's own travel-cost numbers, coffee table ten units from the side table, twenty from the pantry, while the direct baseline goes to the pantry first and backtracks for the soup can, total cost thirty-one. It's a single demonstration, not a benchmark, but it's a real robot executing the plan, which the tables never show you.
[O] That's the part that sells it to me, honestly. Watching cost twenty-two beat cost thirty-one on an actual robot arm is more convincing than another percentage in a chart.
