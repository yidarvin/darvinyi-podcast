[S] Here's a sentence that should make any safety researcher nervous: to teach a model when to refuse, OpenAI barely used any human labels at all.
[O] And the result is safer than the human-labeled version by their own numbers, while refusing far fewer requests it should have answered.
[S] Fewer human eyes on the safety signal, and the outcome improves. That's the part I want to poke at today.
[O] Poke away, because I think this is one of the more practical ideas to come out of the safety literature in a while.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a guest scholar take one paper apart. Today it's "Rule Based Rewards for Language Model Safety," from Tong Mu, Alec Helyar, and colleagues at OpenAI, posted to arXiv in November of twenty twenty-four and later presented at NeurIPS.
[S] It's on the docket because it's a genuinely different recipe for safety training. Write the policy as rules, grade a response against those rules with an LLM, and feed that straight into reinforcement learning. No giant human preference dataset required.
[O] Joining us to walk through it is Daniel, a researcher who's spent real time with this paper. Daniel, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. It's a clever piece of engineering, and the authors are unusually candid about where the evidence is thin. We should get into both sides of that.
[S] Start with the problem. Why wasn't standard R-L-H-F good enough for safety on its own?
[G] The standard recipe is: collect human comparisons of responses to unsafe requests, train a reward model on them, then optimize the policy against that reward model with P-P-O. It works, but the human layer is the weak point. Safety judgments are nuanced, whether and how to respond to a borderline request, and if the labeling instructions are underspecified, annotators fall back on personal bias.
[O] Do they give a concrete example of that going wrong?
[G] A good one. On self-harm prompts, some annotators preferred completions that pointed the user to a US suicide hotline number. A reasonable instinct, but useless, even harmful, for a user anywhere else in the world. Multiply that kind of underspecification across a whole safety policy and you get a model that's either overly cautious, refusing things it shouldn't, or refusing in the wrong tone, judgmental and preachy.
[S] And fixing that means re-collecting or re-labeling data.
[G] Right, which is slow and expensive, and it goes stale again the moment the policy changes or a new failure mode turns up.
[O] This isn't the first attempt to have AI do some of that labeling instead of humans, though. Where does Constitutional AI fit in?
[G] Constitutional AI and the broader R-L-A-I-F line replace human comparisons with an AI grader, cutting cost. But the constitution is a handful of broad principles, something like "choose the less harmful response," which hands the grading model enormous discretion over what "harmful" even means.
[S] Fine for a research demo, less fine for a production deployment.
[G] Exactly the authors' point. For a real deployment you need to enforce a specific policy, which categories to refuse and in what style, not a vibe the model infers on its own.
[O] There's also Sparrow, from DeepMind, which I know also leans on rules.
[G] Sparrow is the closest prior work, and it's worth naming the differences. Sparrow trains a second, rule-conditioned reward model, but it still relies on human data, more than fourteen thousand annotated conversations, and it doesn't fit weights to guarantee the reward actually ranks completions the way the rules intend. This paper does both of those differently: AI feedback instead of human data, and a reward that's explicitly fit to enforce the ranking, injected straight into reinforcement learning rather than distilled into a reward-model dataset first.
[O] Okay, walk us through the mechanism itself. What is a rule-based reward, concretely?
[G] Four pieces. First, propositions, narrow, binary statements about a response given a prompt. Does it refuse? Is there a short apology? Is the refusal judgmental? Does it comply, and is the compliance actually helpful? Each one is a yes-or-no question about the completion.
[S] Why not just ask a grader to score the whole response against the policy directly, the way Constitutional AI does?
[G] Because the authors found LLMs are much more accurate on one narrow yes-or-no question than on a sprawling, multi-part judgment call. So instead of asking a grader to hold the whole policy in its head at once, they shatter the policy into many small classification tasks and let the model be good at each one individually.
[O] And the rules stitch those back together.
[G] Right. A rule ranks completions for a target response type. The paper uses three: Hard Refusal, a brief apology plus a clear statement of inability; Soft Refusal, a more empathetic decline for sensitive cases, like self-harm; and Comply. For each response type, a rule says which combination of propositions counts as ideal, which counts as less good, and which is unacceptable.
[S] Give me the shape of that, not the specifics.
[G] For a hard refusal: refusing, not judgmental, not complying, that's ideal. Refusing but judgmental is less good. Actually complying is unacceptable. It's a simple decision table, but it's explicit. No discretion left to the grader about what counts as good.
[O] So each proposition becomes a feature the reward can use.
[G] Yes. A few-shot classification prompt asks a grader model to output only "yes" or "no," and the probability assigned to "yes" becomes the feature value. Propositions also get bundled into class features, like "ideal" or "less good," by multiplying the relevant proposition probabilities together and normalizing. Across the three response types that's twenty features for Hard Refusal, twenty-three for Soft Refusal, and eighteen for Comply.
[S] What model do they actually use as the grader?
[G] A helpful-only S-F-T model, one that's never seen safety training, because they found it had higher precision at labeling disallowed content than a safety-tuned model would.
[O] And the reward itself, once you have all those features?
[G] The R-B-R is just a linear model over them. The total training reward is the existing helpful-only reward model's score, plus a weighted sum of the proposition and class features. Nothing exotic. The sophistication is entirely in how the features are defined, not in the model that combines them.
[S] How do they set the weights?
[G] They fit them with a hinge loss over synthetic comparison data. For pairs of completions where the policy says one should outrank the other, the loss penalizes the total reward whenever it doesn't put a large enough margin between them. Because there are only a few dozen parameters, the propositions plus five class probabilities, fitting the whole thing runs on a laptop in a couple of minutes.
[O] Compare that to retraining a reward model from scratch.
[G] No comparison. A standard R-M has millions of parameters and needs a real training run. This is closer to fitting a small regression.
[S] Where does the training data for that come from? You still need labeled comparisons somewhere.
[G] This is the other clever piece, it's almost entirely synthetic. They prompt a helpful-only model to generate completions with targeted traits: one perfect refusal, a couple of deliberately bad ones, judgmental, or a nonsensical non-sequitur, and one that actually complies with the disallowed request. That gives exactly the ranked set the rules need, across about six thousand seven hundred prompts, four completions each.
[O] So almost no humans in the loop at all.
[G] Almost. There's one small human step, a Gold set of five hundred eighteen hand-labeled completions, split across the three response types, used only to tune the wording of the classification prompts themselves. That's roughly one percent of the reward-model data and three percent of the supervised fine-tuning data, once you combine it with the helpful-only baseline data.
[O] Let's get to the payoff. What's the headline number?
[G] The metric they build the comparison around is an F one score between two things: Not-Unsafe, does the completion avoid disallowed content, and Not-Overrefuse, does it avoid refusing a safe request. On human evaluation of their largest models, R-B-R-P-P-O scores an F one of ninety-seven point one. The human-safety-data baseline scores ninety-one point seven. The helpful-only baseline, with no safety training at all, scores ninety-five point eight.
[S] Break that F one apart for me. Where's the human baseline actually losing?
[G] Not on safety. Human-P-P-O hits a perfect one hundred percent Not-Unsafe. It loses on usefulness, its Not-Overrefuse score drops to eighty-four point seven percent, almost fourteen points worse than the helpful baseline. R-B-R-P-P-O gets ninety-seven point three percent Not-Unsafe and ninety-seven percent Not-Overrefuse, nearly matching the human baseline's safety while giving back almost none of the helpful baseline's usefulness.
[O] That's the whole pitch in one table, honestly. Matching safety without the over-refusal tax.
[S] Is that internal metric graded by the same R-B-R classifiers that generated the training signal?
[G] Yes, and that's worth sitting with, I'm sure we'll come back to it. But the same pattern shows up on external benchmarks too. On X-S-Test, a public over-refusal test set, R-B-R-P-P-O matches the helpful baseline at ninety-nine point five percent Not-Overrefuse, versus ninety-five point five for the human baseline. On WildChat, a public set of real unsafe chat prompts, R-B-R-P-P-O reaches ninety-six percent Not-Unsafe by their own metric, and similar numbers, ninety-five point nine, ninety-five point two, by an independent moderation classifier and by Llama Guard.
[O] What about capability? Does any of this cost you general model quality?
[G] Barely measurable. On M-M-L-U, Lambada, HellaSwag, and G-P-Q-A, R-B-R-P-P-O sits within a point or two of both baselines, seventy-four point four versus seventy-five point nine on M-M-L-U, for instance. All the differences are inside the reported noise.
[S] Does the R-B-R generalize beyond the one reward model they built it for?
[G] They test that directly. Bolting the R-B-R onto an over-cautious human-trained reward model cuts its over-refusals by sixteen points. Bolting it onto a reward model trained on outdated safety data improves both safety and over-refusal by about ten points each. It isn't fixing one reward model's quirks, it generalizes as a correction term.
[O] And the efficiency claim, is it really the data doing the work, or something else?
[G] They isolate that too. Subsample the human-data baseline down to the same five hundred eighteen completions the R-B-R's Gold set uses, and it performs worse than either the full human baseline or the R-B-R on both axes. Their read is that a reward model can't learn a refusal boundary from that little comparison data, while the R-B-R enforces the boundary directly, by construction, regardless of data volume.
[O] Let me make the strongest case for this. You get comparable-or-better safety, a large usefulness win, at roughly one percent of the human labeling cost, with a reward you can refit in minutes instead of re-running a whole data collection and R-M training cycle. That's not a marginal tweak, it's a different cost curve for keeping a safety policy current.
[S] I'll grant the cost curve. My problem is the evaluation. Daniel already flagged it, the primary internal metric is scored by the same R-B-R propositions that generated the training reward, just thresholded instead of weighted. That's close to grading your own homework.
[G] The authors are candid about exactly that risk, they use the phrase "correlated errors" themselves. Their mitigations: zero prompt overlap between train and eval, reporting the grader's own accuracy against the human Gold set, and layering in genuinely external checks, GPT-4 grading on X-S-Test, an independent moderation classifier, Llama Guard, and a blinded human evaluation.
[S] Good practice, but look at what the cleanest external number shows. On WildChat, the real unsafe-prompt benchmark, Human-P-P-O beats R-B-R-P-P-O on safety, high nineties versus ninety-six percent. The one external check not shaped by the R-B-R's own propositions favors the baseline this paper is trying to beat.
[O] Fair, but that's also the frontier trade-off working as intended. Human-P-P-O buys that extra safety margin by refusing far more often. Look only at the safety axis and you're ignoring the usefulness collapse that comes with it.
[G] Both things are true at once, and the paper doesn't hide it, that's exactly the trade-off the F one is designed to summarize. I'd add a second limitation, though. This is demonstrated on a simplified four-area policy, erotic content, hate speech, criminal advice, self-harm, not a comprehensive production policy, and there's no adversarial or jailbreak testing anywhere in the paper. Every evaluation is on the benign distribution, over-refusal and under-refusal on ordinary requests, not on someone actively trying to break the model.
[S] So we don't actually know if a rule-based reward holds up against an adversary, only against a well-behaved user who happens to phrase something ambiguously.
[G] Correct, and the authors say as much. The scope here is well-specified, decomposable safety behavior, not adversarial robustness.
[O] There's also a grader-quality dependency worth naming as a real limit, not just a caveat.
[G] Right. Their own Gold-set accuracy numbers run from ninety-three point six percent at their largest grader size down to forty-three point eight percent at their smallest. The whole method inherits whatever the grader gets wrong, and a weak grader collapses fast.
[S] So the labor doesn't disappear, it just moves. Instead of writing labeling instructions for annotators, someone has to write and iterate on classification prompts for every proposition, and confirm a capable-enough grader even exists.
[G] That's a real cost, and the authors don't pretend otherwise. Their argument is that the loop is faster, not that the labor disappears. A classification-prompt fix takes effect on all data immediately, while a human-data problem might not surface until after a full P-P-O run finishes.
[O] Which actually happened to them.
[G] It did, and it's the best anecdote in the paper. Their first human-data baseline over-refused every Comply prompt in evaluation. They traced it to an imbalance in the comparison data, only a third of the safe-prompt comparisons contained a negative, refusal, example, so the model saw three times more positive refusal examples than negative ones. They fixed it by manually splicing about fifty hand-written refusals into the data. It's a clean illustration of the paper's central thesis, distilling a policy into reward-model data is lossy, and the R-B-R skips that step entirely.
[S] I'll concede that one. A silent reward-model bug that only shows up after a full training run is a genuinely worse failure mode than a classification prompt you can validate up front.
[O] Zoom out, what does this connect to beyond safety specifically?
[G] The authors flag this themselves. The same recipe should work anywhere a behavior decomposes into explicit, judgeable rules, say "don't use slang" in an essay, even outside safety. Where it struggles is genuinely subjective quality, like judging whether an essay is well-argued, where writing an explicit rule is hard. Their suggested fix is a hybrid: rules for the checkable parts, human-labeled preference data for the parts that resist decomposition.
[S] It also says something for anyone building an LLM-as-judge evaluation, not just a training reward. The self-grading concern we raised isn't unique to R-B-R, any pipeline that uses one model's judgments both to train and to evaluate a system inherits the same correlated-error risk. The template here, external graders, held-out accuracy checks, a blinded human eval, is a reasonable answer to that broader problem.
[O] And there's an ethical dimension worth naming, the authors raise it themselves.
[G] They do. Moving the safety signal from humans to an LLM grader reduces direct human supervision and risks magnifying whatever biases that grader already carries. Their stated mitigation is to evaluate the R-B-R's accuracy carefully and to keep some human data in the loop rather than removing it entirely.
[G] If there's one sentence to take from this, it's that a safety policy doesn't have to be compressed into a preference dataset before it can train a model. You can write it as rules and put it directly in the reward.
[O] My takeaway is that the usefulness win is the real story here. Matching safety while giving back almost all of the over-refusal cost is the part production teams should actually care about.
[S] Mine is the caution flag. The cleanest external safety check in the paper doesn't clearly favor this method, and nobody has stress-tested it against an adversary yet. Read the full writeup on the litsearch site for the figures and the complete numbers.
