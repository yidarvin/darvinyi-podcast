[S] Here's a sentence that should worry anyone who trusts a multi-hop leaderboard: a model that cannot look at more than one paragraph at a time ties the state of the art on a benchmark built to require two.
[O] Or here's the flip read: maybe that just proved something we all secretly hoped was true, that the "multi-hop" label was doing less work than the leaderboard implied, and the paper that showed it made every dataset built after it better.
[S] "Better" is generous. Thirty-five percent of the so-called two-hop questions in HotpotQA turn out to be answerable off one paragraph, because the distractors were too easy to spot.
[O] Thirty-five percent that were fixable, once someone showed the field exactly where to look. That's the paper we're doing today.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an AI optimist, an AI skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the litsearch dot darvinyi dot com map. Today it's "Compositional Questions Do Not Necessitate Multi-hop Reasoning," from Sewon Min, Eric Wallace, Sameer Singh, Matt Gardner, Hannaneh Hajishirzi, and Luke Zettlemoyer, published at ACL twenty nineteen.
[S] It's on the docket because it's close to the founding document of "your multi-hop benchmark has a shortcut problem" — the kind of paper that later work like MuSiQue and the DiRe diagnostic cite as the reason those datasets exist at all.
[O] Joining us is Gideon, a researcher who's spent a lot of time with this paper and the shortcut-learning literature it kicked off. Gideon, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. This is one of those papers where the method is almost deliberately unglamorous — a BERT model with the reasoning capacity switched off — and that's exactly the point.
[O] Let's set up the target. HotpotQA came out in twenty eighteen from Yang and colleagues, and it was a big deal because it asked crowd workers to write questions that needed two linked Wikipedia paragraphs, not one.
[S] Right, the pitch was genuine multi-hop reasoning — find fact A in paragraph one, use it to locate paragraph two, extract the answer. Gideon, what's the actual setup a model sees?
[G] Ten paragraphs total. Two are the gold paragraphs the question was written from, and eight are distractors selected by TF-IDF similarity to the question. The model has to find the answer somewhere in that pool of ten.
[O] And the core argument of this paper is that "the question requires two hops" and "the model is shown ten paragraphs it has to search" are two different claims, even though HotpotQA's design treated them as the same claim.
[S] Give me the concrete example, because I think this is where it clicks for people.
[G] The paper's Figure one: what is the former name of the animal whose habitat the Réserve Naturelle Lomako Yokokala was established to protect? Read cold, that's genuinely two-hop — find what the reserve protects, then find that animal's old name.
[G] But of the ten paragraphs shown, only one is about an animal at all. You don't have to identify the reserve's purpose first. You can just scan for the one paragraph that mentions an animal and read the answer straight off it.
[S] So the compositional structure is real in the question text, but it isn't enforced by the evidence the model actually sees.
[O] Which is a subtle but important distinction — multi-hop-ness isn't a property of the question alone, it's a property of the question paired with a specific evidence bundle. Change the bundle, and the same question can become single-hop or stay multi-hop.
[G] That's exactly the authors' framing. Sewon Min and colleagues put it directly: even highly compositional questions can be answered with a single hop if they target specific entity types, or if the facts needed to answer them are redundant.
[S] So how do you actually test that claim, instead of just asserting it?
[G] You build a model that's structurally incapable of multi-hop reasoning, and see how far it gets. That's single-paragraph BERT — the paper's central instrument, and it's explicitly a diagnostic probe, not a proposed system.
[O] Walk us through it.
[G] It takes the question and exactly one paragraph, concatenates them with a separator token, and runs that through BERT-base. A pooling layer produces four scores, indicating whether the answer is a span in this paragraph, a yes, a no, or simply absent.
[G] Critically, it does this independently for each of the ten paragraphs, in parallel. There's no attention across paragraphs, no shared representation between them, nothing that lets the model combine a fact from paragraph one with a fact from paragraph five.
[S] So it literally cannot chain evidence. If two separate facts in two separate paragraphs are both required, this model has no mechanism to notice that.
[G] Correct. The final answer comes from whichever paragraph got the lowest no-answer score across all ten independent runs. If that paragraph happens to contain the actual gold answer, the model wins, and it never had to reason about the other nine.
[O] So a strong score from this model is basically a lower bound on how much of the dataset is solvable without any cross-document reasoning at all.
[G] Exactly the logic. And they don't stop at the model. There's a human study too: on two hundred bridge-type validation questions, one group of graduate-student annotators sees all ten paragraphs, and a second group sees only nine — the eight distractors plus just one of the two gold paragraphs, with the other gold paragraph deliberately withheld.
[S] That's a genuine control condition, which I appreciate. If the question really needs both gold paragraphs, single-gold-paragraph humans should fall apart.
[G] And third, they hand-label a hundred bridge-type validation examples, reading the actual distractor paragraphs each question was shown with, into categories that explain why a single-hop shortcut exists or doesn't. Three independent measurement approaches: a capacity-limited model, a controlled human study, and manual annotation.
[O] That triangulation is really the paper's methodological backbone. If the model result stood alone, you could dismiss it as a BERT quirk. Stacked with the human ablation and the taxonomy, it's much harder to wave away.
[S] Fair, though I'll flag now and come back to it: a hundred hand-labeled examples with no reported inter-annotator agreement number is thinner evidence than the model result. We'll get there.
[O] Let's get to numbers. What does single-paragraph BERT actually score?
[G] On HotpotQA's distractor setting, the standard ten-paragraph setup, it gets an F one of sixty-seven point oh eight.
[S] Compared to what?
[G] Against the official leaderboard as of March fourth, twenty nineteen: BiDAF scored fifty-eight point nine nine, GRN sixty-six point seven one, QFE sixty-eight point oh six, DecompRC sixty-nine point six three, and the strongest system on the board, BERT Plus, sixty-nine point seven six.
[O] So a model with zero cross-paragraph reasoning lands within about two and a half points of the best purpose-built multi-hop system, and clears most of the field entirely.
[S] That's the headline, and it's a real result. But before I concede the whole point — the harder open-domain setting, where the model has to search all of Wikipedia instead of a curated ten-paragraph pool. How does it do there?
[G] Much worse, relatively speaking — thirty-eight point four F one. It still beats BiDAF and GRN, and roughly matches QFE, but it's well behind Cognitive Graph's forty-eight point eight seven, and behind DecompRC's forty point six five.
[S] So the shortcut is specific to the curated distractor setting. Once you widen the haystack, single-hop guessing stops working as well.
[G] Right, and the paper explains why later. But stay with the distractor setting a moment, because there's a striking human result too. On those two hundred bridge questions: with all ten paragraphs, human annotators scored eighty-seven point three seven F one. With one gold paragraph withheld, so genuinely nine paragraphs and one hop's worth of information, eighty-two point oh six.
[O] A five-point drop for removing half the intended evidence. That's barely a scratch.
[S] And there's an even sharper version of that same point with the model, right? The question-truncation experiment.
[G] Yes. They cut every question down to its first five tokens starting from the wh-word, stripping out most of the actual compositional content. Single-paragraph BERT's score drops from sixty-seven point oh eight to fifty-two point one three. Losing most of the question only costs about fifteen points.
[O] Which tells you the model, and arguably the dataset, isn't leaning much on the compositional structure of the question at all.
[S] Now give me the taxonomy, because that's where I want the receipts on why this happens.
[G] A hundred bridge-type examples, hand-categorized. Twenty-seven percent genuinely require multi-hop reasoning, where both paragraphs are load-bearing. Thirty-five percent are single-hop-solvable because of weak distractors, entity-type matching picks out the one relevant paragraph, like the animal example. Twenty-six percent are compositional but answerable from redundant evidence, where part of the question can just be ignored. Eight percent are non-compositional single-hop questions outright, and four percent were excluded as ambiguous.
[O] So less than a third of the bridge questions are actually doing what the dataset claims all of them do.
[S] What about comparison questions, the other HotpotQA question type?
[G] Those hold up better for the multi-hop claim. Using rule-based categorization, forty-five percent are genuinely multi-hop, where single-paragraph BERT scores near chance, fifty-four point four six F one. Thirty-six percent are context-dependent, sometimes multi-hop depending on content, at fifty-six point one six. And seventeen percent are single-hop, where the model does much better, seventy point five four.
[O] So comparison questions are the part of HotpotQA that's actually resistant to this shortcut.
[G] Right, and that's an important nuance. The paper's critique lands specifically on bridge questions, not the whole dataset.
[S] Last thing before we argue about what it means. Did they try to fix it?
[G] Yes, two ways. First, adversarial distractors: instead of TF-IDF similarity, they use single-paragraph BERT itself to pick the eight distractors most likely to fool it, out of the top fifty TF-IDF candidates, with only about nine point eight two percent overlap with the original distractors. That alone drops the score from sixty-seven point oh eight to forty-six point eight four. Filtering those candidates further by entity type drops it to forty point seven three.
[O] That sounds like the fix working.
[G] Until you retrain. Retrain single-paragraph BERT on the new, harder distractor distribution, and it recovers to sixty point one oh on the adversarial set, and fifty-eight point four two on the type-filtered set. Most of the original shortcut comes right back.
[S] So the fix is shallow. It patches this specific model against this specific distractor distribution, not the underlying vulnerability.
[G] That's close to the paper's own conclusion, stated almost verbatim: these distractors are still insufficient. And on the open-domain side, they show retrieval is the real bottleneck there, not reasoning — with five hundred retrieved paragraphs the model only reaches thirty-nine point one two F one, but manually adding the two true gold paragraphs into that pool jumps it to fifty-three point one two.
[O] Okay, let me make the optimist case, because I don't think this paper is bad news for the field. I think it's the opposite. This is exactly the kind of self-correction good science is supposed to produce. Someone built a benchmark, someone else stress-tested the load-bearing claim, found it partly false, and published the taxonomy of why. That's the field working.
[S] I'll take the deflationary side, then. For years, HotpotQA leaderboard numbers were reported and cited as evidence of multi-hop reasoning progress. This paper shows a chunk of that progress was actually measuring something closer to "can you find the one relevant paragraph out of ten." Every paper that cited a distractor-setting number as a multi-hop result before twenty nineteen was, to some degree, over-claiming, and most of them didn't know it.
[G] Both of those are defensible, and the paper actually supports pieces of each. On the optimist side, the triangulated evidence is genuinely strong methodology — model, human ablation, and taxonomy all pointing the same way is hard to dismiss as noise. On the skeptic side, the numbers are stark: thirty-five percent of bridge questions solvable through weak distractors alone is not a rounding error, it's over a third of a major question type.
[O] Gideon, does the paper let us say how bad it was in aggregate, like what fraction of the entire dataset, comparison questions included, is affected?
[G] Not directly. The twenty-seven, thirty-five, twenty-six, eight split is bridge questions only, and comparison questions get a separate, less severe breakdown. The paper doesn't collapse those into one dataset-wide contamination percentage, so it's worth resisting the urge to invent one on air.
[S] Fair, and that's actually a limitation worth naming. The closest thing to a headline number, sixty-seven F one with zero multi-hop capacity, is doing a lot of the paper's rhetorical work, more than the fragmented category breakdown does on its own.
[O] Here's where I push back on the deflationary read, though. Look at what happened after: 2WikiMultihopQA and MuSiQue were both built explicitly to close this gap, with harder, more carefully controlled distractor and support-fact structure. The shortcut didn't kill multi-hop QA as a research direction, it made the next generation of benchmarks better.
[S] Sure, but notice what this paper's own remediation attempt shows. The adversarial distractor fix didn't actually hold. Sixty-seven down to forty-seven, then right back up to sixty once you retrain on the new distribution. If the authors of this paper couldn't patch it with a serious attempt, that's a warning that "just build harder distractors" isn't a complete answer, and it's worth knowing whether MuSiQue and the rest actually solved it or just moved the shortcut somewhere less visible.
[G] That's a fair open question, and it's outside what this paper can answer. It doesn't test any post-twenty-nineteen benchmark, and it doesn't test whether modern retrieval-augmented or long-context language models have a version of the same vulnerability. That's a live question the field picked up later, not something settled here.
[O] Which, honestly, is itself points for the paper. It correctly identified the axis that later work needed to fix, even if it didn't fix it itself.
[S] I'll give it that much. The diagnosis is sound even where the proposed treatment falls short.
[O] So what actually changes because of this paper, if you're building or grading a benchmark today?
[G] The concrete methodological export is: don't trust "the question requires N hops" as a property of the question text alone. Test it against the actual evidence pool a model or annotator sees, the way the human ablation and the adversarial-distractor experiment do here.
[S] And practically, that means either much larger, more diverse evidence pools, open-domain retrieval instead of a curated ten paragraphs, or adversarially constructed distractors, and the paper shows neither is a free lunch. Open-domain retrieval turns into a retrieval problem, and adversarial distractors get partially defeated by retraining.
[G] Downstream, this is close to the origin point of a broader habit in eval design — treating "does this benchmark actually require the skill it claims to require" as an empirical question to test, not a design intention to assume holds. The same instinct shows up later in contamination audits and in construct-validity critiques of large language model benchmarks generally.
[O] It's a good reminder that a benchmark's name and its intended difficulty aren't the same thing as what it's actually measuring. You have to go check.
[G] If there's one sentence to take from the paper, it's that compositionality in a question doesn't guarantee multi-hop reasoning is required to answer it — that has to be verified against the evidence a model actually sees.
[O] My takeaway: this is science doing its job. Someone found the load-bearing crack in a widely used benchmark and showed exactly where it was, which made everything built after it stronger.
[S] Mine is more cautious: a sixty-seven F one from a model that literally cannot combine two paragraphs should make you re-read every multi-hop leaderboard number you've ever cited, and ask what shortcut you haven't found yet.
