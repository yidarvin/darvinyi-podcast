[S] Today's paper is Language Models as Zero-Shot Planners: Extracting Actionable Knowledge for Embodied Agents, by Wenlong Huang, Pieter Abbeel, Deepak Pathak, and Igor Mordatch, out of UC Berkeley, Carnegie Mellon, and Google. It was posted in January of twenty twenty-two and presented at ICML that same year.
[O] Joining us is Renata, who's spent real time with this paper. Renata, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. One housekeeping note up front: I'm not one of the four authors, so when I say "they found" or "the paper shows," that's me reporting what's on the page, not claiming credit for it.
[S] Fair enough. Renata, start with the actual question they're asking, because the framing is sharper than "can an LLM plan."
[G] It really is a specific question: can the world knowledge already sitting inside a pre-trained language model, knowledge that's normally probed with trivia questions, be turned into a sequence of actions an embodied agent can execute? Not train a new model on labeled demonstrations for one environment. Extract planning knowledge that's already there, with no gradient updates, no environment-specific data, just a prompt.
[O] That's a genuinely different bet than what came before.
[G] It is. Prior systems in this space learned from explicit step-by-step demonstrations collected for one specific environment, supervised sequence models that are only as good as the labeled data they were trained on. This paper asks whether a big enough frozen model already skips that step.
[S] And the testbed is a simulator, not a real house.
[G] Right, VirtualHome, a household simulator where every activity is a program: a sequence of steps like "walk to kitchen" or "open fridge," built from forty-two atomic actions. The task pool comes from a knowledge base of two thousand eight hundred twenty-one human-annotated activities covering two hundred ninety-two distinct high-level tasks. They hold out eighty-eight of those tasks for evaluation and keep the other two hundred four as a pool to draw prompting examples from.
[O] And the surprising part is the plans it writes just... read well.
[G] Genuinely well. Prompted with one example task and its plan, GPT-3 and Codex generate plans that ten Mechanical Turk annotators, answering a simple yes-or-no "does this complete the task," rate as correct at a rate matching or beating the human-written reference plans for those same tasks.
[S] Which sounds like the story's over. It isn't, obviously, or we wouldn't be doing an episode.
[G] It isn't, and this is where the paper earns its second half. They define two separate axes. Correctness is that human yes-or-no judgment. Executability is mechanical: does the plan parse into valid syntax, using only real actions and real objects, and does every step satisfy the environment's pre- and post-conditions, things like the fridge has to be open before you can grab milk from it. A plan can ace one axis and fail the other completely.
[O] Give me the failure, concretely.
[G] The model will confidently write "microwave the chocolate milk," and VirtualHome has no "microwave" verb and no "chocolate milk" object anywhere in its vocabulary. Or it'll write something syntactically fine but lexically wrong, like "open TV" when the environment only recognizes "switch on TV." Fluent, plausible, completely unusable as a program.
[S] So the raw language model is basically hallucinating an API it doesn't have access to.
[G] That's a fair way to put it, and it's exactly the gap the rest of the paper is built to close.
[O] Walk us through how they close it, because I think this is the elegant part.
[G] Three techniques, all inference-time, none of them touching model weights. First, admissible action parsing by semantic translation. They enumerate every valid action-object combination the environment supports, forty-seven thousand five hundred twenty-two of them, and embed all of it once with a second frozen model, a BERT-style model trained with the Sentence-BERT objective, which they call the Translation LM. Whatever free-form phrase the planning model generates gets embedded the same way, and they pick the admissible action with the highest cosine similarity. "Microwave the chocolate milk" gets pulled toward whatever's actually closest in the environment's real vocabulary.
[S] That only works if the phrase and the true action end up close in embedding space. What guarantees that?
[G] Nothing guarantees it, and the paper is honest that translation errors are a real source of failure, they specifically call out compounded instructions like "brush teeth with toothbrush and toothpaste" as cases the Translation LM maps poorly. But empirically, for single-concept steps, it works well enough to matter a lot, which the ablations bear out.
[O] What's technique two?
[G] Autoregressive trajectory correction. If you translate an entire plan only after it's fully generated, one bad early step can send everything downstream off the rails, errors compound. So instead they interleave generation and translation one step at a time: sample a few candidate next-step phrases, translate the best one to an admissible action immediately, and feed that grounded action back into the prompt before generating the next step. Every step is conditioned on something the environment can actually run, never on free-form drift. It also gives them a natural stopping rule, if the best candidate's combined score falls under a threshold, or most samples come back empty, the plan just ends there instead of continuing to hallucinate.
[S] And technique three?
[G] Dynamic example selection. Up to this point every query task got the exact same fixed example in the prompt. But if your one fixed example is "order pizza" and the query is "apply lotion," you're giving the model a wildly irrelevant template. So they use that same Translation LM to find whichever task in the demonstration pool is most semantically similar to the query, and use that as the in-context example instead. Their own figure shows "shave" getting picked as the example for "apply lotion," which actually makes sense, grab something, apply it to your face, put it away.
[O] Three cheap tricks, one frozen language model doing double duty as both the planner and its own translator's backbone.
[G] Two frozen models technically, a causal planning model and a separately frozen embedding model for translation, but yes, nothing retrained. Combine all three and that's their final method, which they call "Translated," as in Translated GPT-3, Translated Codex.
[S] Renata, give us the actual numbers, because "significantly improves executability" is doing a lot of work in that sentence.
[G] Concretely: Codex twelve B goes from eighteen point oh seven percent executable, vanilla, to seventy-eight point five seven percent translated. GPT-3 one seventy-five B goes from seven point seven nine percent to seventy-three point oh five percent. That's the headline the abstract itself leads with, eighteen to seventy-nine.
[O] That's a massive jump for three inference-time tricks and zero retraining.
[S] It is, but what happened to correctness while that was happening?
[G] It drops. GPT-3 one seventy-five B goes from seventy-seven point eight six percent correct, vanilla, down to sixty-six point one three percent translated. Codex twelve B goes from sixty-four point eight seven down to fifty-four point eight eight. The paper states this trade-off explicitly, right in the abstract, it doesn't bury it in a results table.
[O] I actually want credit given for that. A lot of papers would just report the seventy-nine and stop.
[S] Credit granted, but I want the metric that actually predicts whether a deployed agent succeeds, not two metrics you have to average in your head.
[G] They give you exactly that in section five point two: the percentage of plans judged both executable and correct together. Human-written plans hit that bar sixty-five point nine one percent of the time. The best zero-shot method, Translated GPT-3 one seventy-five B, reaches thirty-five point two three percent. Translated Codex trails at twenty-seven point two seven.
[S] So on the number that actually matters for deployment, the gap to human performance is still roughly two to one, not closed.
[G] That's the honest read, and it's the paper's own framing, they put that comparison in the paper themselves rather than leaving it for a critic to compute.
[O] Before we get to the debate, I want the ablation, because I think it tells you which of the three tricks is actually load-bearing.
[G] It's decisive. Starting from Translated Codex at seventy-eight point five seven percent executable: remove action translation entirely and it collapses to thirty-one point four nine percent. Remove dynamic example selection and it's fifty point eight six. Remove trajectory correction and it's fifty-five point one nine. Translation alone is worth more than the other two combined.
[S] And I assume smaller models don't get a free pass just because they post decent executability numbers.
[G] They explicitly catch this themselves. GPT-2 gets surprisingly high raw executability, but it's a mirage, the model is often just repeating the one demonstration example verbatim or truncating "go to sleep" down to a single step, "go to bed." Executable because it's trivial, not because it solved anything. Their own LCS metric, longest common subsequence against the human reference plan, exposes it: high executability, terrible LCS. They flag that failure mode in the text rather than let the raw number stand uncritically.
[O] One more result I think undersells itself: give the model actual how-to instructions instead of just a task name, and Translated Codex hits thirty-two point eight seven percent LCS, within about one point of a supervised LSTM trained from scratch on VirtualHome's own annotated data, at thirty-four percent.

[G] Honestly, not fully. The paper calls it a "crude reference" itself. I'd read it as suggestive, zero-shot with instructions gets you into the neighborhood of a trained baseline, not as a controlled head-to-head.

[S] I'm less charitable about the annotator setup. No kappa statistic, no repeated raters, ten people per task judging something as loosely defined as "does this plan complete the task."
[G] That's a real gap. It's also worth saying the paper predates contamination auditing as a standard practice, this is January twenty twenty-two, so there's no check for whether GPT-3's pretraining data overlapped with VirtualHome's own task templates. Not a flaw unique to this paper, just era-typical, and worth flagging on air anyway.
[O] What about the environment itself? Is VirtualHome doing the model any favors?
[G] Somewhat, and the authors say so directly in their limitations section. Everything here is what they call mid-level grounding, plans expressed as symbolic actions like "grab cup," assuming some low-level controller exists to actually execute the grabbing. They explicitly don't touch low-level sensorimotor control or perception. And there's no observation loop at all, the model plans by imagination, the same way the human annotators who wrote the reference plans did, never seeing what's actually in the room. One consequence they name themselves: a task like "stack two plates on the right side of a cup" isn't solvable, because the plans only ever refer to one instance of each object class.
[S] No environment feedback is the part I keep coming back to. You generate a whole plan blind and just hope it holds up.
[G] Agreed, and the authors list that as their own open problem, not something a critic had to discover.
[O] I think that's actually the most interesting forward-looking thread here, and I want to be clear this is my read, going past what the paper itself claims. The idea of grounding a language model's free-form output against a second, smaller model that knows what's actually achievable, that's the shape of a lot of what came after in language-driven robotics and agents.
[G] I'd sign onto that as a fair extrapolation, with the caveat that this specific paper never runs feedback or a real robot. It stays entirely in one simulated house, sampled by a text environment.
[S] Which is exactly why I'd resist treating a mid-level, feedback-free household simulator as a stand-in for the harder embodiment problem, real perception, real physics, a model that can't see what its last action actually did.
[O] Fair, that's a genuinely different, harder problem this paper doesn't attempt.
[S] Does any of this actually connect back to evaluation practice, though, beyond robotics?
[G] I think the strongest connection is almost meta: this paper is a good model for how to report a trade-off honestly. It doesn't hide the correctness drop behind the executability win, it runs the harder combined metric itself in section five point two instead of leaving that computation for readers to reconstruct.
