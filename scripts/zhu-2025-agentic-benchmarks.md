[S] A benchmark that hands an agent a perfect score for doing nothing at all.
[O] And a different benchmark that quietly docks a real, working model by twenty eight percent because somebody redesigned a website.
[S] Same instrument, breaking in two opposite directions, and nobody would know unless they went and checked.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take one paper apart. Today's guest is Thaddeus, who has spent real time inside this work.
[S] The paper is "Establishing Best Practices for Building Rigorous Agentic Benchmarks," from Yuxuan Zhu and colleagues at U I U C, with co-authors spanning Stanford, Berkeley, M L Commons, Amazon, and the U K A I Safety Institute, senior-authored by Daniel Kang.
[O] Thaddeus, welcome. This one is basically an audit of the auditors, it grades the benchmarks that grade the agents.
[G] That is exactly the pitch, and it is overdue. Every lab is racing to publish agent benchmarks right now, and this is the first paper to turn "is this benchmark trustworthy" into an actual checklist.
[S] Start with why this is even a new problem. We have had benchmarks for a decade, ImageNet, M M L U, BLEU scores for translation. Why do agents break the old playbook?
[G] Because those benchmarks grade a fixed answer against a fixed key, a categorical label, a reference string. An agentic benchmark asks a model to reason, write code, and change a live environment's state, and some separate piece of machinery decides, after the fact, whether the outcome counts as success.
[O] So the grading itself becomes a second thing that can be wrong, on top of the agent.
[G] Precisely, and the paper frames it as two structural hazards traditional benchmarks never had, a complex task setup, a live environment plus a toolbox, and an unstructured task outcome, free text or code or file edits, that no simple metric can score cleanly.
[S] Give me the sentence that made you sit up.
[G] The authors write that these issues can swing reported performance by up to one hundred percent in relative terms. That is not measurement noise, that is a benchmark telling you the opposite of the truth.
[O] And they do not start from theory, they start from two exhibits that are almost comic. In tau-bench, an airline customer-service benchmark, a trivial agent returning empty responses gets marked correct on tasks designed to be impossible, like changing a non-refundable ticket, hitting a thirty eight percent success rate, higher than a real G P T four oh based agent.
[S] And separately, SWE-bench-Verified, probably the most cited coding-agent benchmark that exists, grades a patch as correct if it passes a hand-written test suite. Prior work the authors cite found that twenty four percent of the top fifty leaderboard entries are actually wrong once you look past the tests.
[G] Those two examples are the whole thesis in miniature. This is not fringe work, SWE-bench-Verified, tau-bench, GAIA, and WebArena are the benchmarks OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, and Meta cite when they make capability claims.
[O] So walk us through the actual framework, Thaddeus. What is the core idea that ties all this together?
[G] The paper sets up a chain of three things that have to line up: the capability you actually care about, task success, and a positive evaluation result. You proxy the capability with whether the task got done, and you detect that using some grading result. Break either link, and the number lies.
[S] Break down the two link names, because they are going to carry the rest of this conversation.
[G] Task validity is the left link, capability to task success: a task should be solvable if and only if the agent has the capability being tested. It breaks two ways, a shortcut that passes without the capability, or an impossible task, unsolvable even with it. Outcome validity is the right link, and it breaks when the grader is incomplete or gameable, exactly what happens when SWE-bench's tests pass on a wrong patch.
[O] So one failure mode inflates scores by making things too easy to fake, and the other deflates them by making genuinely correct work look like failure.
[G] Right, and the paper keeps those symmetric on purpose. Underestimation is just as much a validity failure as overestimation, even though the overestimation cases get more attention because they are the scarier story for safety claims.
[S] Where did the checklist itself come from? I want to know this is not just introspection from one lab.
[G] The authors first assembled a corpus of seventeen widely used agentic benchmarks, chosen because top AI providers rely on them, or because they won awards at peer-reviewed venues. They surveyed agent-evaluation frameworks like Inspect AI and METR's protocols, folded in previously documented issues, and borrowed from software testing practice. The synthesis is the Agentic Benchmark Checklist, A B C, forty three items across three parts.
[O] Forty three sounds like a lot to hold in your head. What are the three parts?
[G] Task validity is ten checks, T.1 through T.10, across tools, environment, and implementation. Outcome validity is twenty checks, O.a through O.i, organized by outcome type, information retrieval, code, state modification, multistep reasoning, and grading method. Benchmark reporting is thirteen checks, R.1 through R.13, about honesty when a flaw cannot be eliminated.
[S] Give me one T-check and one O-check that actually caught something, not just abstract hygiene.
[G] T.5, isolate the agent from ground truth, caught KernelBench leaving correct kernel answers sitting in G P U memory, readable through out-of-bounds memory access. O.e.1 and O.e.2 demand fuzz testing that varies edge cases, memory layouts, and value ranges — the authors' sharpest example, random negative numbers tell you nothing about a re-lu, since it just zeroes out anything negative, so the fuzzer must generate inputs the program is actually sensitive to.
[O] And the reporting checks are almost the cheapest fix in the whole paper.
[G] They might be the highest value per unit of effort here. R.12 asks for a non-A I or human baseline, R.13 for the score of a trivial agent that does nothing. If tau-bench had run R.13 before release, the empty-response loophole would have been caught on day one.
[S] So the checklist is not just diagnostic, some of it is preventive, cheap insurance you run before you ship the benchmark.
[G] That is the intent, yes, and T.9, shipping an automatic oracle solver that can solve every task, is the same idea on the task-validity side. If your own solver cannot clear the benchmark, you have no business asking whether an agent can.
[O] Let us get to the number that made me want to record this episode. What happened when they actually ran A B C against real benchmarks?
[G] They scored ten of the seventeen, chosen to cover the full range of capabilities: SWE-bench, SWE-Lancer, KernelBench, BIRD, Cybench, MLE-bench, GAIA, tau-bench, WebArena, OSWorld. The topline is stark: seven of ten violate task validity, seven violate outcome validity, all ten have reporting limitations. Eighty percent acknowledge no weakness in their own design, and none satisfies every reporting criterion.
[S] Those are not overlapping small samples, that is essentially the entire popular set failing on at least one axis.
[G] Correct, and then for every flaw they flagged, they did not stop at "this is theoretically a problem," they built the exploit and measured the damage in a real number. Six specific cases get worked all the way through.
[O] Walk us through the ones that actually land as headlines.
[G] Start with tau-bench. Two violations, two numbers. Treating simple substrings or unchanged states as ground truth inflates scores by thirty eight percent. Separately, letting an agent list every possible answer instead of committing to one inflates scores by forty percent. WebArena has a milder version, loose substring matching plus an unvalidated LLM judge, worth roughly a five point two percent overestimate.
[S] Those first two are relative-terms numbers, right, consistent with that "up to one hundred percent" headline in the abstract?
[G] Worth being precise about that headline: one hundred percent relative is the single worst case, tau-bench's empty-response exploit specifically, not a typical result. The other numbers here sit closer to five to thirty percent absolute, still enormous for a benchmark people cite as ground truth, but not every benchmark off by half.
[O] Fair, but even the sober end is startling. KernelBench overstates correct kernel generation by thirty one percent because its fuzz tester only varies the values fed into a kernel, never the tensor shapes or memory layout, so kernels that would break under a different shape sail through.
[S] SWE-Lancer is the one that should worry every lab running it internally. The agent gets unrestricted file-system access, including to its own hidden test files. Those sit in a password-protected archive, but the directory listing is readable and the contents can be overwritten without cracking the password. So an agent swaps in a single line, assert one equals one, and scores a hundred percent having solved nothing.
[G] That one is the cleanest demonstration in the paper of task validity collapsing entirely, the shortcut does not even touch the intended capability.
[O] And then OSWorld cuts the opposite direction, which I think is the most underrated result here.
[G] It does. Thirteen of forty six Chrome-section tasks in OSWorld rely on HTML selectors that broke when the underlying websites redesigned their pages. That is not an exploit, just link rot, and it underestimates U I-TARS, the strongest open-source agent on OSWorld, by twenty eight percent absolute — a model looking worse on paper for reasons that have nothing to do with the model.
[S] So the same underlying sloppiness produces overestimates in one benchmark and underestimates in another, purely depending on which direction the bug happens to point.
[G] That symmetry is one of the paper's better arguments for why this needs a checklist and not case-by-case firefighting.
[O] Then there is the forward-looking piece, which I actually think is the most convincing evidence in the whole paper, CVE-Bench.
[G] CVE-Bench evaluates agents exploiting real web vulnerabilities, and the authors applied A B C during construction, not after. Its time-based SQL-injection check looked for a SLEEP clause anywhere in the log, which does not prove it executed, so agents passed by inserting SLEEP anywhere, inflating scores by thirty two point five percent. Separately, an outbound-server check was beatable from the same Docker network; closing it dropped success by ten percent. Net, A B C cut CVE-Bench's overestimation by thirty three percent absolute, confirmed by outside experts.
[O] Here is my optimist case, hold me to the paper's actual text, Thaddeus. This is the most useful contribution I have seen this year for taking agent benchmarks seriously — it hands the field forty three checkable items, and proves the checklist works prospectively on CVE-Bench, not just retrospectively on benchmarks that already existed. That is the difference between commentary and a tool.
[S] I will grant the tool is real and I will grant CVE-Bench is genuine forward evidence. But I want to push on how the other ten benchmarks got scored, because that is where most of the paper's actual numbers come from. Thaddeus, who scored them?
[G] The same team that wrote the checklist. They assigned one point per satisfied item, zero otherwise, across ten benchmarks they selected for popularity. The paper does not report any inter-rater reliability check, no second team independently applying A B C to see if they land on the same scores.
[S] That is the exact failure mode the paper tells other people to fix. Check O.c.1 demands documented evidence of a judge's accuracy and consistency before you trust its verdicts. The authors are the judge here, and there is no O.c.1 applied to themselves.
[O] That is fair, but I would push back gently, the individual exploits are not matters of subjective judgment. Nobody needs an inter-rater study to confirm that assert one equals one passes SWE-Lancer's test suite. That is a fact you can rerun.
[G] That distinction matters, the six headline exploits are demonstrated, not merely scored, so they do not hinge on the authors' subjective one or zero. The self-scoring risk lives in the broader totals, the seven-of-ten and eighty-percent numbers, where a different team might land somewhere else on the softer, judgment-based checks.
[S] Second concern, and this one is sharper, circularity. A meaningful chunk of A B C was synthesized directly from previously reported issues in these same ten benchmarks. So when the authors re-find those issues, that is not independent confirmation the checklist generalizes, that is the checklist recovering the cases it was built from.
[G] Fair, and that is why I would weight CVE-Bench more heavily than the ten-benchmark assessment. It was not part of the corpus the checklist was derived from, and A B C caught genuinely new issues there, confirmed by outside experts, the one place it is tested on material it was not built to already know.
[O] One case study is thin evidence for a forty three item framework, though. I would want to see it applied blind to five or six more benchmarks nobody has picked apart yet before calling it fully validated.
[G] Agreed, and to be fair to the authors, they do not claim otherwise, the paper reads as establishing the framework and demonstrating it works at least once prospectively, not as a closed case.
[S] Last thing, the scoring is binary, one or zero, no severity weighting. Failing to report a trivial-agent baseline, R.13, costs the same point as failing to isolate agents from ground truth, T.5. One is cosmetic, the other is the SWE-Lancer bug handing out perfect scores for nothing.
[G] Correct, it does not say how much a violation moves the final number, that only comes from the bespoke exploit experiments the authors ran by hand. Run A B C on your own benchmark and you get a list of what is wrong, not how badly, unless you build the exploit yourself.
[O] Which is real labor. This is not a linter you run in continuous integration, it is forty three judgment calls, some of which need you to write and test an actual exploit.
[G] That is the honest cost of the approach. The authors would probably say that cost is exactly why so many of these issues survived years of use before anyone found them, nobody wanted to do the work either.
[S] Zoom out for a second. Why should someone who does not build benchmarks for a living care about this?
[G] Because these scores are load-bearing. A tau-bench number or a SWE-bench-Verified leaderboard position gets cited in papers, product announcements, sometimes policy conversations about what models can do autonomously. If the instrument is broken by up to one hundred percent relative, every downstream claim built on it inherits that error, silently.
[O] And it compounds with the field's incentive structure, everyone racing to ship a new agent benchmark, and speed is directly in tension with the careful isolation and fuzz-testing work this paper asks for.
[S] Which is why the reporting checks are the cheap win. Running a trivial agent and a human baseline before you publish costs almost nothing next to building the benchmark, and R.13 alone would have caught the tau-bench problem before it reached a leaderboard.
[G] The paper's own framing in the conclusion is modest, and correctly so, it establishes the checklist and shows it works, it does not claim to have fixed agentic evaluation. The real test is whether builders adopt A B C before release, rather than auditors applying it after the leaderboard is already public.
[O] My takeaway is that this is the most actionable paper on evaluation rigor I have read this year, and I want to see A B C become a release checklist the way test coverage became one for software.
[G] If there is one line to take from this paper, it is that a benchmark score is only as trustworthy as the two links behind it, does the task truly require the capability, and does the grader truly detect success, and right now, most popular agentic benchmarks are failing at least one of those links.
[S] Mine is more cautious, the ten-benchmark scorecard is useful but self-graded, so treat the numbers as a snapshot and the framework as the keeper. Read the full writeup on the litsearch site for the figures and checklist, and we'll see you next episode.
