[S] Marcus, set the table. Why does a game about punching trees need its own planning paper?
[G] Because the authors ran an experiment that punctures a comfortable assumption. Hierarchical LLM planning, an LLM breaks a task into sub-goals, a low-level controller executes each one, already worked well on tabletop robot arms and text-based environments. So they took an existing planner called Inner Monologue and ran it, unchanged, on three environments side by side: a tabletop manipulation task, a text-based home simulator called ALFWorld, and Minecraft.
[O] And it just didn't transfer?
[G] Not remotely evenly. With a perfect, oracle low-level controller, Inner Monologue hits ninety nine percent on Tabletop, forty two percent on ALFWorld, but only twenty three percent in Minecraft. Swap the oracle controller for a realistic, learned one, and Tabletop only dips to eighty percent, while Minecraft collapses to nine percent.
[S] So open worlds aren't just harder, they break something specific.
[G] Two specific things, and the paper names both. First, complex sub-task dependency: mining a diamond from scratch takes at least thirteen sub-goals executed in exact order, chop wood, craft planks, build a table, craft tools, mine stone, smelt iron, and so on. The odds an LLM nails all thirteen in one shot fall fast as the chain gets longer. Second, state-dependent feasibility: even a logically correct plan can be badly inefficient given where the agent actually stands. Their example, crafting a bed is normally fastest by killing a sheep for wool, but if no sheep or village is reachable within three minutes of gameplay, the efficient move is killing a spider for string instead.
[O] So the plan can be right on paper and still wrong for the world you're standing in.
[S] Which is exactly the kind of gap that "the LLM already knows the recipe" papers tend to gloss over.
[O] Walk us through the fix, Marcus. What do the four letters actually stand for?
[G] Describe, Explain, Plan, and Select, and it's one LLM playing two of those roles. When the low-level controller fails a sub-goal, a rule-based Descriptor reads Minecraft's own symbolic game state, inventory, biome, GPS, and writes a short text description of what just happened. That description goes to the same LLM, now acting as an Explainer, prompted with a handful of chain-of-thought examples to pinpoint the actual error, say, that smelting an iron ingot needs a furnace the agent never crafted. Only then does the LLM switch back to Planner and rewrite the plan, conditioned on both the description and its own explanation of the failure.
[S] That's the loop closing. Inner Monologue already fed back pass or fail. What's actually different here?
[G] The self-explanation step. Just telling the model it failed doesn't tell it why, and the paper's argument is that forcing an explicit diagnosis before re-planning is what fixes the plan, instead of just retrying it blind. Describe, then explain, then re-plan, repeated until the task finishes or the step budget runs out.
[O] And Select is the fourth letter, the part that isn't just re-planning.
[G] Right, and it answers the second challenge, the bed example. When a plan has parallel options with no strict order, chop an oak, a birch, or an acacia tree, any of them satisfies "get wood," a plain planner has no way to prefer whichever is actually closer. So the authors train a small horizon-predictive Selector, a goal-conditioned convolutional network sharing its backbone with the controller, to predict how many steps remain to finish each candidate goal from the current state. It's trained with a cross-entropy loss against ground-truth horizons pulled from offline trajectories, and at each choice point it just picks whichever candidate it predicts is closest.
[S] Why not just use CLIP, or MineCLIP, and measure visual similarity to the goal? Cheaper, no extra training required.
[G] The authors anticipated that objection directly. An oak tree sitting right in the agent's camera frame can look visually close to a CLIP model even with a canyon between the agent and the tree. Visual similarity isn't traversal cost. The learned Selector is trained on how long things actually take, not how close they look.
[O] So it's not "describe your failure" doing all the work here, it's also learning what closeness really means in this world.
[S] Let's get to numbers, Marcus. How's this actually evaluated?

Every task runs thirty times, same fixed controller, same underlying OpenAI models across every method compared: text-davinci-zero-zero-three for the GPT-style baselines, code-davinci-zero-zero-two for the code-style ones, including D-E-P-S itself.
[O] Seventy one tasks is a lot of ground to cover in one number.
[G] It is, and the paper doesn't let easy and hard tasks share a budget. Difficulty scaling is built into the evaluation itself, not just implied by which tasks happen to fail.
[O] And the headline?
[G] Averaged across all eight groups, D-E-P-S reaches forty eight point five six percent success. The strongest prior baseline, Code as Policies, gets twenty five point seven seven percent. [S] Thirty nine to forty eight, that's still a real jump from one small trained module. Does any of it generalize past Minecraft?
[G] Yes. The paper reruns the describe-explain-replan piece, without the Minecraft-specific Selector, on ALFWorld. [S] The selector ablation is worth a beat too, not just the headline jump on the hardest group.

[O] Beyond Minecraft, does any of this change how people should build agents more broadly?
