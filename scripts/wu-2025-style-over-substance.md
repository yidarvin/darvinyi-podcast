[O] And before anyone feels smug about that, the humans grading the same answers weren't much better at catching it.
[S] That's the deflating part. This isn't really a GPT-4 problem, it's an "anyone judging text without fact-checking it line by line" problem.
[O] Which is also the useful part. If you can isolate exactly where the bias lives, you can design around it instead of just complaining about it.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where we take one paper off the map and actually argue about it. Today's paper is "Style Over Substance: Evaluation Biases for Large Language Models," by Minghao Wu and Alham Fikri Aji, out of the Mohamed bin Zayed University of Artificial Intelligence and Monash University, published at COLING twenty twenty-five.
[S] Joining us is Nola, a visiting scholar who's spent real time in this corner of eval research. Welcome, Nola.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is one of those papers that's easy to summarize in a sentence and hard to fully sit with, because the sentence is basically: the leaderboards a lot of the field trusts might be measuring confidence and length, not correctness.
[O] Let's set the stage first. By twenty twenty-three, Elo-style head-to-head judging had basically become the default way to rank chatbots — show a judge two answers to the same question, they pick a winner, run enough of these and back out an Elo rating, like a chess ladder.
[S] It started with the Vicuna blog post from Chiang and colleagues, and it fed straight into the LMSYS Chatbot Arena, which plenty of people still treat as close to ground truth.
[G] It's popular for good reasons — it's fast, it's cheap next to hiring domain experts, and it produces one clean leaderboard number people can screenshot. The problem the authors zero in on is a confound. If you show a judge two different models' real answers and one wins, you genuinely can't tell whether the judge preferred the more correct answer or just the longer, more confidently worded one.
[S] There'd already been a partial flag on this — Wang and colleagues had shown these judges carry a position bias, favoring whichever answer is shown first. But nobody had isolated which surface property an Elo judge actually rewards, independent of real quality.
[O] So the contribution isn't really the finding on its own, it's the experimental trick that gets you a clean read on it.
[G] Exactly right. Instead of comparing different models' natural outputs, they take one verified-correct answer per question and have GPT-4 systematically inject a single controlled defect into copies of it. Any preference a judge shows among those copies is now directly attributable to that one flaw, not to some tangle of real capability differences.
[S] Walk us through exactly what they built, Nola.
[G] Forty general-purpose questions, pulled from the eighty-question Vicuna evaluation set Chiang and colleagues released. They keep only the "generic," "knowledge," "common sense," and "counterfactual" categories, and drop "fermi," "coding," "math," "roleplay," and "writing" — anything that needs specialized expertise or is inherently subjective to grade.
[S] Which already tells you something about the scope. This is deliberately the easy end of the question distribution.
[G] It is, and it's worth flagging early, we'll come back to it. For each of the forty questions, GPT-4 writes a correct baseline answer, then five deliberately flawed variants: one minor factual error, several minor factual errors, several major factual errors, an "advanced learner" version with occasional spelling slips, and an "intermediate learner" version with grammatical mistakes. Each of those six variants is generated at two lengths, roughly a hundred words and roughly fifty words. That's twelve answer "models" per question, every one traceable back to the same underlying correct answer.
[O] And they didn't just trust GPT-4 to plant exactly one clean defect and leave it at that.
[G] No, every single output is manually reviewed and edited to make sure it contains exactly the intended flaw and nothing else. Then all twelve are compared pairwise, in both answer orders, which gives five thousand two hundred eighty unique comparisons across the forty questions.
[S] Who's actually doing the judging?
[G] Four judge types. Crowd workers on Amazon Mechanical Turk — three hundred seventy-seven unique annotators, each required to have completed over five thousand prior assignments at a ninety-nine percent-plus approval rate, capped at twenty annotations each so no single worker dominates the data, averaging fourteen annotations apiece, with a mandatory twenty-second delay before they're allowed to submit, specifically to discourage skimming. Then a dedicated expert team of twenty people, each holding at least a master's degree from an English-speaking country, who annotate a two-hundred-comparison sample of the full five thousand two hundred eighty. They justify that sample size with a U.S. Chess Federation rule of thumb that twenty-five games are enough to rate a player, and every one of the twelve models shows up in at least twenty-eight of those two hundred sampled games. And then two LLM judges: GPT-4 and Claude-1.
[O] Why both LLM judges, and not just GPT-4?
[G] Self-preference risk. Every answer being judged, including the flawed ones, started as GPT-4 output that was hand-edited afterward. Judging with GPT-4 alone risks a "prefer your own voice" confound, so Claude-1 is brought in as an independent check. Elo itself is the standard chess formula, with a K-factor of thirty-two, following Chiang and colleagues, and to cancel out any effect of comparison order, they average the rating over ten thousand random orderings of the games.
[S] Okay. Give us the number that actually made people sit up.
[G] On the single unified Elo scale, GPT-4 rates "Several Minor Factual Errors" at twelve-oh-six, and "Correct plus Short" at ten ninety-six. A longer answer with several fabricated facts beats a shorter, entirely correct one. When the authors went back and manually read GPT-4's own written justifications, they found cases where GPT-4 ranks "Several Major Factual Errors" above "Correct plus Short" even after explicitly noting the factual errors in its own reasoning.
[O] So it's not that GPT-4 fails to notice the errors, it notices, and still prefers the flawed answer.
[G] Sometimes, yes. And that sits on top of a length bias that shows up in every judge type. When two similarly-scored answers differ mainly in length, the longer one wins sixty-six point eight percent of the time for Claude-1, sixty-seven point nine percent for GPT-4, fifty-seven point one percent for crowd workers, and fifty-one point nine percent for experts, the least biased group, but still tilted.
[S] And there's a position bias stacked on top of that.
[G] There is. Crowd workers, GPT-4, and Claude-1 all default toward whichever answer is shown first. Crowd workers pick Assistant One forty-five point nine percent of the time versus forty-four point eight for Assistant Two, with nine point three percent ties. GPT-4 goes fifty point four versus forty-four point four, with five point two percent ties. Claude-1 is the most order-biased of all, fifty-two point six versus forty-four point six, with just two point eight percent ties. Experts actually break the pattern — they favor the second answer, thirty-five point five versus forty-four point five, and call a tie twenty percent of the time, far more often than anyone else.
[O] That expert tie rate is interesting by itself, it suggests they're the only group actually willing to say "I can't call this."
[G] Which connects to the next finding. Humans, both crowd and expert, are strikingly indecisive across the board. Crowd-sourced Elo scores across all twelve models span only nine twenty-six to ten eighty-six. Expert scores span a bit wider, eight thirty-two to eleven sixty-two, but both ranges are far narrower than GPT-4's or Claude-1's spreads, even though the real quality gap between "Correct" and "Several Major Factual Errors" is large and deliberately engineered to be obvious.
[S] Which either means humans are calibrated skeptics who refuse to overreact to twelve near-identical-looking paragraphs, or it means they're not reading closely enough to tell them apart. Which is it?
[G] The paper leans toward the second read, and the inter-annotator agreement numbers back that up. Cohen's kappa between GPT-4 and Claude-1 is point five one, moderate agreement. Every pairing involving a human comes out "slight": expert-Claude-1 at point one four, crowd-Claude-1 at point one oh, crowd-GPT-4 at point one one, expert-GPT-4 at point oh nine, and crowd-expert at just point oh eight. The two LLMs agree with each other more than any human pairing agrees with anything.
[O] Okay, so that's the diagnosis. What's the proposed fix?
[G] The Multi-Elo Rating System, MERS. Instead of one holistic win-or-lose call, judges score each answer independently on three axes: Accuracy, meaning factual correctness and logical consistency, Helpfulness, meaning relevance and depth, and Language, meaning grammar, clarity, and tone. Each axis gets its own Elo. It's borrowed from the Multidimensional Quality Metrics framework used in machine translation, which splits fluency from adequacy instead of blending them into one number.
[S] Does it actually work?
[G] For GPT-4, yes, quite a bit. Under MERS, GPT-4's Accuracy Elo for "Correct" is twelve hundred, and for "Several Major Factual Errors plus Short" it's seven forty-six, a wide, clean separation. That's a real improvement over the single-score Elo, where those categories blurred together.
[O] So the fix works.
[G] For GPT-4 specifically. It doesn't rescue crowd judgment at all. On that same isolated Accuracy axis, crowd workers rate "Correct plus Short" at nine sixty-three, and "Several Major Factual Errors plus Short" at nine fifty-five, eight points apart, nearly indistinguishable. That's exactly the failure MERS was supposed to fix, still showing up under the fix.
[S] So the abstract's line about MERS "significantly enhancing the quality of LLM-based evaluations" is doing some quiet cherry-picking.
[G] I'd put it more carefully. It's true for GPT-4-as-judge, and the abstract does hedge in the very next sentence, saying there's no significant improvement for crowd-sourced evaluation. But it's easy to skim the headline and walk away thinking the fix generalizes across judge types, when the paper's own results table shows it plainly doesn't for the judge type that's cheapest and most widely used in practice.
[O] Let me make the strongest case for why this paper matters in a good way. It doesn't just wave hands about "LLM judges have biases," it isolates the bias with a genuinely clean design, one verified-correct answer, one injected defect at a time. And the fix is cheap: you're not building a new judge, you're asking the same judge three narrower questions instead of one broad one. That's a change anyone running a Chatbot-Arena-style pipeline could adopt by next week.

[G] The paper doesn't tell you, and that's a legitimate gap. I'd also point out there's no real baseline being beaten here — there's no competing bias-detection method MERS is compared against. It establishes a phenomenon against the status quo of single-score Elo, then evaluates its own fix against that same status quo. That's reasonable for a paper naming a new problem, but it means how much you trust "does it hold up" partly comes down to trusting the effect sizes at face value.
[O] Where I'd push back on the skeptic case is contamination, actually, in the optimistic direction. The forty questions come from the already-public Vicuna set, which means today's judges may already be tuned somewhat against exactly this failure mode, precisely because papers like this one got attention.
[S] Which cuts the other way too. It means the bias magnitudes reported here are probably an upper bound specific to twenty twenty-three-era judges, not some timeless constant about how language models judge text. We don't actually know how much of this has already been patched by instruction-tuning done since.

What it does show cleanly, and I don't think either of you disputes this, is the mechanism: length correlates with the "level of detail" criterion baked directly into the judging prompt itself. A judge rewarding length isn't even doing something obviously wrong by its own instructions, it's doing exactly what it was asked to do, and that instruction just happens to correlate badly with correctness.

If your judging prompt asks for "level of detail," don't be surprised when you get length instead of accuracy.
