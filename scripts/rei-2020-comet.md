[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a guest scholar take one paper apart.
[S] Today's paper is "COMET: A Neural Framework for MT Evaluation," by Ricardo Rei, Craig Stewart, Ana Farinha, and Alon Lavie at Unbabel, published at EMNLP twenty twenty.
[O] It's the paper that unseated BLEU as the metric people actually trust, and we've got a guest who knows its internals cold. Elena, welcome to the show.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is one of those papers where the framing matters almost as much as the architecture — it's really an argument that MT evaluation had quietly fallen behind the systems it was supposed to be judging.
[S] Elena, set the stage. What was actually broken before this paper showed up?
[G] For nearly two decades, machine translation quality was judged mostly by BLEU and close relatives like METEOR, NIST, and chrF. All of them work the same way: count matching n-grams, or edit operations, between the system's output and a human reference translation. None of them ever look at the source sentence at all.
[O] Which was fine back when MT output was clunky and lexically constrained.
[G] Right, that approximation held up while neural MT was still rough. But modern neural systems produce fluent, semantically faithful translations that paraphrase the reference instead of matching it word for word — exactly the behavior an n-gram metric can't see.
[S] And there's a specific shared task that made this concrete, not just a hunch.
[G] The WMT nineteen Metrics Shared Task. Two findings mattered most. Segment-level correlation with human judgment was frustratingly low across every submitted metric. And every metric's ability to rank the strongest systems against each other collapsed once you narrowed the field to the top performers — exactly the regime where a metric matters most, because that's when you're actually deciding which system to ship.
[O] There's also a telling headcount buried in that same shared task. MT itself drew one hundred fifty-three system submissions that year. The metrics track drew twenty-four, and almost half of those were quality-estimation entrants just repurposed as metrics.
[S] So evaluation was falling behind the very thing it was supposed to evaluate.
[G] That's the authors' framing exactly, and it's what COMET sets out to fix on two fronts at once: segment-level fidelity, and the ability to still tell strong systems apart. It's also among the first mainstream MT metrics to feed the source sentence into the score, not just the hypothesis and the reference.
[O] Why does that matter so much?
[G] Because a translation can be unfaithful to the source in ways no reference-only comparison will ever catch. You can match the reference's words closely and still have mistranslated the meaning, if the reference itself happens to permit that overlap.
[S] Walk us through the architecture, Elena. What is COMET actually made of?
[G] It's a framework, not one single model — three trained variants, all sharing one backbone: a pretrained cross-lingual encoder, XLM-RoBERTa base in this paper's experiments, though the authors note multilingual BERT or XLM would plug in the same way. That encoder independently embeds the source, the hypothesis, and the reference, token by token, and a pooling layer compresses each into one sentence embedding using a layer-wise attention mechanism, so the model draws on whichever transformer layer actually carries the useful signal instead of just defaulting to the last one.
[O] And on top of that shared backbone, two different architectures.
[G] The Estimator model concatenates the hypothesis and reference embeddings with four interaction features: the element-wise product of hypothesis and source, the element-wise product of hypothesis and reference, and the absolute difference of each of those pairs. That combined vector feeds a regressor trained to predict a quality score by minimizing mean squared error against a human judgment.
[S] Wait — hypothesis interacting with source, and with reference, but the raw source embedding itself isn't in there?
[G] Right, and there's a specific reason it's left out entirely. The authors tried adding the raw source embedding back in, as an ablation, and it only improved eight of eighteen tested language-pair settings, for an average gain under one one-thousandth of a Kendall's tau point. Essentially nothing. So the source only enters through those interaction terms, never as a raw feature on its own.
[O] Interesting — so the source matters, but only in a specific, indirect way.
[S] And the second architecture?
[G] Translation Ranking is a different training objective entirely. It takes a four-way input — source, reference, a "better" hypothesis, and a "worse" one — and optimizes what's called a triplet margin loss, the same loss FaceNet used for face recognition, which pulls the better hypothesis closer than the worse one to both anchors, the source and the reference, in embedding space.
[O] But at inference you only ever have one hypothesis to score, not a pair.
[G] Right, so it computes the harmonic mean of the hypothesis's distance to the source and its distance to the reference, then converts that distance into a bounded similarity score between zero and one. That's the model they call COMET-RANK.
[S] You said three trained variants total. Where does the third come from?
[G] The framework is judgment-agnostic by design, so the authors train it on three different flavors of human judgment to prove the point. COMET-HTER is an Estimator regressed on human-mediated translation edit rate, trained on the QT21 corpus — a hundred seventy-three thousand tuples, but narrow: information-technology and life-sciences text only, across four language pairs. COMET-MQM is another Estimator, regressed on a proprietary Multidimensional Quality Metrics score built from minor, major, and critical error counts, trained on a twelve-thousand-segment internal Unbabel corpus across twelve language pairs, always with English as the source. And COMET-RANK is the Translation Ranking model, trained on WMT's relative-ranking data from twenty seventeen and twenty eighteen.
[O] Twelve thousand segments is tiny next to the other two.
[G] It is, roughly a tenth the size of the ranking data. That turns out to be one of the more interesting results in the paper — it holds up anyway.
[S] Let's get to the numbers, then, because this is where the paper has to earn the claim.
[G] They evaluate on the WMT nineteen Metrics DARR corpus — the same benchmark whose failures motivated the whole paper — against BLEU, chrF, the twenty-nineteen shared-task winner YiSi-one, and the concurrently published BERTScore and BLEURT. On English-into-other-language pairs, COMET-RANK roughly doubles BLEU's correlation on several of them: point six nine three versus BLEU's point three six three on English-to-Kazakh, point six six four versus point three nine five on English-to-Finnish, point six six five versus point three three three on English-to-Lithuanian.
[O] Doubling a correlation coefficient is dramatic. Where doesn't it win?
[G] English-to-Russian is the one exception in that direction. COMET-RANK gets point five eight zero, and BERTScore with an XLM-R backbone edges it out at point five nine nine. Though notably, the MQM Estimator wins that same pair outright, at point six one five, ahead of every other metric tested.
[S] What about translating into English, which is BLEURT's home turf?
[G] COMET-RANK still beats BLEURT in five of seven into-English pairs. Chinese-to-English: point four four five versus BLEURT-large's point four three six. German-to-English: point two zero two versus point one seven four. That matters because BLEURT is built directly on English BERT, specialized for exactly this direction, and COMET still comes out ahead more often than not.
[O] And the MQM Estimator — the one trained on only twelve thousand segments, and never on English as a target language?
[G] Stays competitive throughout, and wins outright on English-to-Russian. The paper calls this a "zero-shot" result, and hypothesizes the source-conditioning is doing the work, since MQM never saw English-as-target training data but did see the source sentence at inference.
[S] Now the headline result — the one the whole paper is actually chasing. Does COMET fix the top-system collapse?
[G] That's Figure three. As you prune the evaluated pool down from all submitted systems to just the top four, BLEU's correlation collapses toward zero — under point zero five in the into-English direction. All three COMET variants stay well above it, COMET-RANK generally highest, meaning COMET keeps discriminating between the best systems long after BLEU has essentially nothing left to say.
[O] That's the number from our cold open.
[S] Directly answering both of WMT nineteen's headline complaints, in one figure.
[G] There's one more result worth naming, because it isolates the paper's central design bet. A separate table trains two versions of the ranking model on WMT seventeen — one reference-only, one with the source added — then tests both on WMT eighteen. Adding the source improves Kendall's tau on every one of the eight tested pairs, and the gain is much larger going into English than out of it: up to plus point one five five on German-to-English, plus point one three two on Turkish-to-English, versus only plus point zero two four to plus point zero five one in the English-to-other direction.
[O] Which tells you what, exactly?
[G] That source-conditioning helps most precisely where the reference alone is least informative — when English is the target and there's less redundancy in the reference to lean on. It's direct evidence for the paper's founding idea, not just a plausible story bolted on afterward.
[S] Let's do the debate, then, because there's real tension here.
[O] I'll make the optimist case plainly. This is a framework that swaps a hand-designed formula for a model trained directly on what humans actually said, and the numbers back it up almost everywhere, across three different kinds of human judgment, including a language-pair set with barely any training data behind it. That's not incremental. That's the whole approach to MT evaluation changing.
[S] I'll grant the numbers. What I won't grant yet is the interpretability trade. A BLEU score decomposes into exactly which n-grams matched — you can see why a system scored low. COMET's score is the output of an XLM-R regressor, or a triplet-embedding distance. There's no per-segment explanation. If a leaderboard ranking hinges on a COMET score, you can't audit why.
[O] That's a real cost, but it's the same cost every field paid moving from hand-crafted features to learned representations. Nobody's going back to n-gram counting because it's more legible.
[S] Legibility isn't nostalgia, though, it's accountability. Here's the sharper version of my worry. COMET-MQM, the variant the paper highlights as its surprising strong result, is trained on a proprietary, unreleased twelve-thousand-segment corpus, annotated under Unbabel's own internal typology. Nobody outside Unbabel can check the annotators' agreement rates, or which error types they systematically over- or under-penalized.
[G] That's a fair characterization, and it's worth being precise about what's public and what isn't. The QT21 corpus behind COMET-HTER is public, but it's narrow — industry text, IT and life sciences, four language pairs — so how well its post-editing-effort target generalizes past those domains is genuinely untested here. The MQM corpus is the one that stays fully proprietary.
[S] There's a second, quieter issue too. COMET-RANK trains on WMT DARR from twenty seventeen and twenty eighteen, and it's tested on WMT DARR from twenty nineteen. No literal segment leaks across that split. But it's the same annotation protocol, largely the same crowd-sourcing pipeline, the same twenty-five-point exclusion threshold for building relative rankings. A strong showing there partly demonstrates COMET learned that specific pipeline's quirks, not some pipeline-independent notion of translation quality.
[O] Is that actually distinguishable from just learning what quality is? Every learned metric has to train on somebody's judgments, from somewhere.
[G] It's a real open question, and it's not unique to COMET — it's the meta-evaluation problem sitting underneath every learned scorer. This paper is an early, fairly clean instance of it, and it's the same problem later large-language-model-as-judge systems run into in a much more acute form. A metric that learns to imitate annotators inherits whatever those annotators got systematically wrong, and because the scorer is a black box, that inherited bias is much harder to spot than a BLEU miscount would ever be.
[S] So my scorecard: real win on correlation, real and mostly unaddressed risk on what that correlation is actually measuring.
[O] My scorecard: the paper does exactly what it said it would do, on its own terms — and its own terms happen to be the same terms the whole field uses to decide what counts as a good MT system. That's not nothing.
[G] Both of those are defensible reads. What the paper doesn't give you is a test against a differently-collected pool of human judgments — an independent expert MQM panel, say, outside the WMT crowd-sourcing pipeline entirely — which is exactly the experiment that would separate "correlates with these annotators" from "measures translation quality" as an independent thing.
[O] Zoom out for a second, Elena. Why does this matter beyond one metrics leaderboard from twenty twenty?
[G] Because COMET is essentially what MT evaluation looks like now. It went on to become a dominant metric cited across later WMT metrics tasks and system papers, in more or less the way the authors hoped it would.
[S] Which means the meta-evaluation problem we just walked through isn't hypothetical anymore. It's baked into how the field currently decides which translation systems are actually good.
[O] And the pattern generalizes well past machine translation. Any time you swap a transparent, rule-based check for a learned model trained on human preference data — reward models, or large-language-model judges scoring open-ended answers — you're making the same trade this paper made back in twenty twenty. Better correlation with the judgments you trained on, at the cost of knowing exactly why any single score came out the way it did.
[G] One more thing worth naming, because it cuts partly against the skeptic case. The paper commits to releasing the framework and the trained models, and it did. That openness is genuinely part of why COMET could be scrutinized and adopted at scale, rather than staying a black box only Unbabel could inspect. It doesn't resolve the annotator-bias question, since the MQM training data itself stays closed, but it does mean outside researchers can at least probe the encoder and the architecture directly.
[G] If there's one line to take from this paper, it's that COMET proved a learned, source-aware metric can track human judgment far better than counting shared words, and it genuinely did become the field's new default. Just don't mistake "correlates with the judgments we trained it on" for "measures quality" — those are not the same claim, and this paper's own evaluation can't fully separate them.
[O] My takeaway: this is what real progress in evaluation looks like — messy, contested, clearly better than what it replaced, and still worth pushing further rather than trusting blindly.
