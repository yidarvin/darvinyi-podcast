[O] Here's the number that started this whole project. Frontier models were clearing over ninety percent on MMLU, and up to ninety-six percent on MATH. The yardstick had basically run out of runway.
[S] And here's the fix, which is almost brutal in its simplicity. Go find a thousand experts, ask each of them for the hardest question they can write, and only keep the ones that already beat every frontier model.
[O] The result is a benchmark where, at release, the best model in the whole paper scored thirteen point four percent.
[S] Which either means the field finally got an honest yardstick again, or it means somebody built a test specifically engineered to make headlines out of models failing.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist and a skeptic argue about a paper until someone who actually knows it settles the score.
[S] Today's paper is Humanity's Last Exam, from the Center for AI Safety and Scale AI, led by Long Phan, Alice Gatti, Ziwen Han, and Nathaniel Li as co-first authors, with about a thousand more people who submitted a question good enough to make the cut.
[O] And joining us is Edmund, who has spent real time with this benchmark and its aftermath. Edmund, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. And the standard caveat — I'm not one of the roughly one thousand authors here, so when I say "the authors show" or "they report," I mean exactly that, not "we."
[S] Edmund, start with the gap. Why did the field need this at all?
[G] Because saturation quietly breaks measurement. Once a benchmark is pinned near its ceiling, a two-point gain on it is indistinguishable from noise, prompt luck, or the model having simply seen the test set somewhere in pretraining. MMLU above ninety percent, MATH near ninety-six percent, even GPQA — a benchmark explicitly designed to be Google-proof — cracking around seventy-five percent. The authors' own Figure 1 makes the point visually. Take those same models that clear fifty to ninety-six percent on GPQA, MATH, and MMLU, and put them on Humanity's Last Exam, and they collapse into the low single digits.
[O] So the point isn't that the models got worse. It's that the ruler stopped being able to tell strong from state-of-the-art.
[G] Exactly, and that's the framing the paper leads with. The whole design brief is three constraints: every question needs one unambiguous, verifiable answer; it can't be solved by a quick web search; and it has to be a question current frontier models actually get wrong. Satisfy all three, and you've rebuilt headroom.
[S] Which is a very different goal from "test broad human knowledge," despite the name.
[G] We'll get to the name. It's worth sitting with for a minute.
[O] Let's go to the method, because the collection process is honestly the most unusual part of this paper. Edmund, walk us through it.
[G] It's a global, incentivized crowd-sourcing effort. Close to a thousand subject-matter experts, across more than five hundred institutions in fifty countries, mostly professors, researchers, and people with graduate degrees. The authors backed it with a five hundred thousand dollar prize pool — five thousand dollars each for the top fifty questions, five hundred dollars each for the next five hundred — plus the offer of paper co-authorship for anyone with an accepted question. That's actually why the author list on this paper is enormous.
[S] So the incentive structure is doing real work. People aren't submitting for free.
[G] Right, and the submission bar is strict. Every question is closed-ended and machine-gradable. Twenty-four percent are multiple-choice with five or more options, the rest are exact-match, where the model has to produce a specific string. About fourteen percent are multimodal, requiring the model to read an image alongside the text. Every submission needs a known, unambiguous solution that isn't quickly recoverable by search.
[O] And then the filtering. This is the part I find genuinely clever.
[G] The funnel is the heart of the method. Every submitted question first runs an automated difficulty check against several frontier models — GPT-4o, Gemini 1.5 Pro, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, and o1 for multimodal questions, plus o1-mini and o1-preview for text-only ones. If a model solves it, or for multiple-choice, if the models on average beat random guessing, the question is rejected before a human ever sees it. Across the whole drive, the authors logged over seventy thousand model attempts, which surfaced roughly thirteen thousand questions that actually stumped the models.
[S] Thirteen thousand out of how many total submissions?
[G] The paper doesn't give the raw submission count, only what survived each stage. Those thirteen thousand went through two rounds of expert peer review — reviewers with graduate degrees in the relevant field, using standardized rubrics — narrowing down to about six thousand candidates, and finally to organizer and expert approval. What's released is two thousand five hundred public questions, plus a private held-out set kept back specifically to catch overfitting on the public split.
[O] And the subject mix skews hard toward math.
[G] Mathematics alone is forty-one percent of the public set. Then biology and medicine at eleven percent, computer science and AI at ten, physics at nine, humanities and social science at nine, chemistry at seven, engineering at four, and an "other" bucket at nine. So this is, structurally, a quantitative-reasoning-heavy exam wearing a "humanity's knowledge" name.
[S] Talk about grading, because closed-ended doesn't mean grading is trivial once you allow exact-match strings.
[G] Grading is automated with an LLM judge — o3-mini, given the question, the model's response, and the reference answer, and told to allow equivalent formats, a decimal versus a fraction, an estimate within a stated tolerance. Every model is also asked to state a confidence from zero to one hundred percent, and that confidence feeds a calibration metric, the root-mean-square calibration error. Bin the questions by stated confidence, compare each bin's actual accuracy to its stated confidence, and take the root-mean-square gap. A perfectly calibrated model scores zero. Every model in this paper scored above seventy percent.
[O] Hold that number, because I think it's the real headline. Let's get the raw accuracy numbers on the table first.
[G] On the full benchmark: GPT-4o at two point seven percent. Grok 2 at three percent. Claude 3.5 Sonnet at four point one. Gemini 1.5 Pro at four point six. Gemini 2.0 Flash Thinking at six point six. o1 at eight percent. Then, on the text-only subset only, DeepSeek-R1 at eight point five percent, and o3-mini on high effort at thirteen point four percent — the top score in the paper.
[S] Say that caveat again, because it matters. DeepSeek-R1 and o3-mini aren't evaluated on the same test as everyone else.
[G] Correct. Neither model is multimodal, so they were run only on the text-only questions, which is a strictly easier slice than the full multimodal set the other six models faced. The paper is transparent about this in a footnote, but the number everyone quotes — thirteen point four percent — is not apples to apples with the two point seven percent sitting above it in the same table.
[O] Now give me the calibration numbers, because that's the part that actually made me sit up.
[G] GPT-4o, eighty-nine percent calibration error. Gemini 1.5 Pro, eighty-eight. Grok 2, eighty-seven. Claude 3.5 Sonnet, eighty-four. o1, eighty-three. Gemini 2.0 Flash Thinking, eighty-two. o3-mini, eighty. DeepSeek-R1, seventy-three, the best of the group and still catastrophic. On questions these models get wrong the overwhelming majority of the time, they answer with high confidence instead of hedging. The authors read that as direct evidence of confabulation.
[S] And the reasoning models pay a real cost for their few extra points.
[G] They do. Average completion tokens for the reasoning models run into the thousands per question, highest on mathematics, where the difficulty is concentrated. The authors' own line on this is that future models should be judged on being compute-optimal, not just on accuracy.
[O] Alright, let's argue about what this means. I'll make the optimist case first. This is the field doing exactly the right thing at exactly the right moment. MMLU and its peers were dead as instruments. This paper rebuilds headroom with a rigorous, expert-vetted, incentive-aligned collection process, and it surfaces a finding — the calibration gap — that's arguably more important than the accuracy number itself. A model that's wrong and confident is more dangerous than a model that's wrong and says so. HLE puts a number on that gap for the first time at this scale.
[S] I'll grant the calibration finding — that's real and it's durable regardless of what happens to the accuracy number. But the accuracy headline itself has three problems. First, the one we just covered: the "best" score of thirteen point four percent is on an easier, text-only test than the rest of the table, so the leaderboard as printed isn't a fair ranking. Second, the grader is a model — o3-mini — which also appears as a system being graded. Exact-match grading is fairly constrained, but the judge's equivalence calls, fractions versus decimals, estimates within tolerance, inject noise the reported accuracies silently absorb, and the paper doesn't headline a judge-versus-human agreement rate to bound that noise. Third, a public, static, gradeable benchmark depreciates the moment it's released, because it's exactly the kind of artifact that flows into training data and tool pipelines.
[G] Both of those last two points, the paper actually engages with, just not in the main text — it's in an appendix most people skip. On grading noise, they don't report a judge-versus-human agreement number, so that criticism stands as written. But on depreciation and quality, there's a real fight happening in the appendix that's worth putting on the table.
[O] Go on.
[G] After release, a group called FutureHouse published a critique arguing that roughly thirty percent of HLE's chemistry and biology answers are likely wrong. The authors respond directly. They ran their own targeted audit of two hundred questions, twice, with a rebuttal phase for the original question authors, and landed on an overall expert disagreement rate of fifteen point four percent for the public set. On the biology, chemistry, and health subset specifically — the same slice FutureHouse targeted — they found about eighteen percent disagreement, not thirty. But then they show something honest: if you use a single-dissenting-reviewer standard instead of their multi-reviewer, rebuttal-included process, that same subset's disagreement rate jumps to twenty-five percent, which is close to FutureHouse's number. So a good chunk of the gap between eighteen and thirty percent is a methodology difference in how you define "wrong," not a clean contradiction.
[S] That's a genuinely useful piece of context, and it cuts both ways. Fifteen to eighteen percent expert disagreement is still not nothing — that's roughly one in six or one in seven questions where a domain expert would push back on the official answer, sitting quietly under every headline accuracy number in that table.
[O] To be fair, the authors themselves say that error rate is in line with other expert-grade benchmarks, and they cite physician disagreement on complex health questions as a comparable baseline.
[S] Sure, and I'd want that comparison spelled out more precisely rather than asserted. But I'll take the concession that this isn't uniquely sloppy — it's the normal cost of writing genuinely hard, expert-level questions at this scale.
[G] There's one more structural point worth flagging, which is the paper's own prediction about itself. In the discussion, the authors say it's plausible models could exceed fifty percent accuracy on HLE by the end of 2025, given how fast prior benchmarks have saturated. That's their own forecast, written into the paper, about how depreciating this specific number would be.
[O] And if that happened roughly on schedule, that's actually a point in the optimist's favor — it means the field kept moving fast enough that a hard exam went from single digits to genuinely competitive within a year. That's the trajectory HLE was built to measure.
[S] Or it's a point in my favor — it means the headline "under fourteen percent" that everyone quoted at release was already a snapshot with an expiration date stamped on it by the authors themselves, and the private held-out set that's supposed to be the durable check on gaming isn't tracked against the public number anywhere in this paper.
[G] I think that's the fairest way to leave it, actually. The paper doesn't report a public-versus-private gap over time, so neither of you can fully close that argument from what's on the page — it's a real gap in the paper, not just a rhetorical one.
[O] Edmund, adjudicate. Score the two of us.
[G] On "does this restore genuine measurement headroom" — optimist wins clearly, and it's not close. The construction pipeline is rigorous, and the calibration finding stands on its own regardless of the accuracy debate. On "is the top-line number trustworthy as printed" — skeptic wins. The text-only asymmetry for the best score is a real apples-to-oranges problem in the paper's own table. On "is the dataset riddled with errors" — I'd call that one closer to a draw leaning optimist. Fifteen to eighteen percent disagreement is real, but the FutureHouse thirty percent framing overstates it once you account for methodology, and the authors show their work on that comparison.
[S] I'll take that. One more thing I want on record before we move on: the name. "Humanity's Last Exam" is a closed-ended, math-heavy quiz. It rewards crisp, verifiable derivations, not the open-ended judgment, synthesis, or long-horizon research work that actually defines most expert labor.
[G] The authors are honest about that limit, to their credit. Their own discussion section says high performance here would demonstrate expert-level closed-ended knowledge, but wouldn't by itself demonstrate autonomous research ability or general intelligence. Their framing is that this might be the last closed-ended academic exam worth giving a model, but it is nowhere near the last benchmark AI will need.
[O] So what would raise everyone's confidence here, Edmund?
[G] A reported judge-versus-human grading agreement rate, so the noise in that o3-mini judge is bounded rather than assumed. A tracked gap between the public and private held-out scores over time, so we can actually see depreciation happening rather than inferring it. And ideally a human-expert accuracy baseline on the same test, since right now the "expert frontier" in the title is implied by the question design, not directly measured against real human test-takers.
[O] Let's close with implications. Does any of this change how people should actually use HLE day to day?
[G] I'd say two things carry over into general evaluation practice, beyond this one benchmark. First, calibration deserves to be reported alongside accuracy far more often than it is — a model that's wrong and confident is a worse deployment risk than a model that's wrong and uncertain, and this paper is a clean demonstration of how to measure that gap cheaply. Second, the authors' own response is a model for handling post-release criticism — they didn't just defend the number, they opened a bug-bounty-style feedback program, launched a dynamic fork called HLE-Rolling to absorb corrections and new questions, and ran a second late-contribution round that produced a second private held-out set for future evaluations.
[S] That second point matters more than it sounds. A benchmark that can visibly correct itself after release is a lot more trustworthy long-term than one that just gets cited forever at its launch number.
[O] Alright. Takeaways. Edmund, give us the paper's.
[G] Humanity's Last Exam rebuilt measurement headroom after MMLU-class benchmarks saturated, using two thousand five hundred expert-written, machine-graded questions filtered specifically to beat frontier models — and at release, every model scored under fourteen percent. Its most durable finding isn't the accuracy number; it's that every single model was catastrophically overconfident on the questions it got wrong.
[O] Mine: this is exactly the kind of benchmark the field needs more of — rigorously built, honestly incentivized, and self-correcting after release through HLE-Rolling. The headroom is the whole point, and it bought the field real time.
[S] And mine: treat the headline accuracy number as a depreciating asset, not a scoreboard. Read the top score against the right subset, ask for the judge-agreement rate that isn't in the paper, and remember that the authors themselves predicted this number wouldn't survive the year.
