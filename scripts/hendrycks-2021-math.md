[G] Glad to be here. This is a paper whose central contribution and its central prediction had almost opposite fates. The dataset is still a default reasoning eval five years later, and the forecast about scaling is about as wrong as a forecast gets.
[S] Set the stage. Why did anyone need a new math benchmark in twenty twenty-one? We've covered GSM8K on this show, grade-school word problems, and that already existed.
[G] GSM8K is a good contrast. Both papers target the same underlying weakness, that autoregressive models can't self-correct once they stumble mid-derivation. But GSM8K stays at two-step to eight-step grade-school arithmetic and algebra. MATH goes straight at competition mathematics, the kind of problem on the AMC and the AIME, the American Invitational Mathematics Examination, contests for the strongest high-school mathematicians in the country.
[O] So it's not just harder numbers, it's a different category of hard.
[G] Right, and the authors are explicit about what was missing before this. One camp was formal theorem-proving benchmarks, HOList, HOLStep, the Metamath-based work from Polu and Sutskever, which give you automatic grading but force the model into a proof language nobody actually writes mathematics in. The other camp was plug-and-chug: DeepMind's Mathematics dataset, symbolic integration, arithmetic, sorting. Transformers were already crushing those. Lample and Charton report over ninety-five percent on symbolic integration alone.
[S] So every existing benchmark was either divorced from how math is actually written, or already solved.
[G] That's their argument, and their own comparison figure backs it up. State of the art on HOList was around seventy-three percent, HOLStep ninety-two, DeepMind Math about ninety-five, symbolic integration ninety-nine. Their own benchmark opened at around seven percent for the best model they tried. That gap is the entire pitch.
[O] Walk us through what's actually in the dataset, Julian.
[G] Twelve thousand five hundred problems, seven thousand five hundred for training, five thousand held out for test. They're pulled from American math competitions, largely collected off the Art of Problem Solving forums, AoPS, where contest archives get posted and discussed. Every problem ships with a full step-by-step solution in LaTeX, not just a final answer, plus a difficulty rating from one to five within each of seven subjects: prealgebra, algebra, number theory, counting and probability, geometry, intermediate algebra, and precalculus.
[S] How do you grade free-form LaTeX automatically? That's usually where these things collapse into BLEU-score heuristics.
[G] That's the actual engineering contribution, more than people credit it for. Every solution's final answer sits inside a boxed command, so the grader always knows exactly where to look. Then there's a normalization layer: fractions get canonicalized so slash notation and frac and dfrac all match, multi-variable expressions get alphabetized, polynomials get ordered by descending degree, units can be present or absent, zero point five equals one half, factor orderings are interchangeable. That turns free text into something scoreable with plain exact match, over a combinatorially large answer space, with no human in the loop.
[O] And geometry, you can't just describe a triangle in prose and expect useful reasoning.
[G] They handle that with Asymptote, a vector graphics language, so a diagram becomes source code the model can read as text. Clean trick. It sidesteps needing a multimodal model just to cover figure-bearing problems.
[S] What about the second dataset, the pretraining one, AMPS?
[G] Auxiliary Mathematics Problems and Solutions. The idea is a model shouldn't meet competition math cold, it should learn the fundamentals first. AMPS has two pieces. One is Khan Academy: six hundred ninety-three exercise types, over one hundred thousand problems with full worked solutions, everything from basic addition through Stokes' theorem. The other is their own Mathematica-generated corpus, a hundred hand-designed scripts across algebra, calculus, statistics, geometry, linear algebra, and number theory, thirty-seven of which emit full step-by-step LaTeX. Around fifty thousand exercises per script, which comes out to roughly five million problems, twenty-three gigabytes total, bigger than the sixteen gigabytes of text BERT was trained on.
[O] What models did they actually run this on?
[G] GPT-2, from point one billion up to one point five billion parameters, pretrained on AMPS and then fine-tuned on MATH. And GPT-3, Curie, roughly thirteen billion parameters, and Davinci, roughly one hundred seventy-five billion, through the OpenAI API, fine-tuned where the API allowed it, or run few-shot otherwise. They also tried T5, but its tokenizer strips out LaTeX symbols, so after a two-week hyperparameter sweep it wasn't competitive, and a BART run in the appendix landed slightly worse than their smallest GPT-2.
[S] Give us the actual numbers, because "low accuracy" has been doing a lot of work so far.
[G] The headline range is three percent to six point nine percent. Their best model, GPT-2 at one point five billion parameters, pretrained on AMPS and fine-tuned, tops out at six point nine percent average accuracy. Few-shot GPT-3 at one hundred seventy-five billion only manages five point two percent. Few-shot GPT-3 at thirteen billion is worse still, three percent flat.
[O] Wait, the biggest model does worse than the small fine-tuned one?
[G] Considerably worse. And within the GPT-2 family, scaling barely moves the needle. Point one billion gets five point four percent, one point five billion, fifteen times the parameters, gets six point nine, a twenty-eight percent relative gain for a fifteen-times parameter increase. Fit a log-linear curve through that and extrapolate to forty percent accuracy, and you land on the number from our cold open, something on the order of ten to the thirty-fifth parameters.
[S] Which is the paper's headline claim, that scale alone won't solve this.
[G] Their words, more or less: while scaling is automatically solving most other text-based tasks, scaling is not currently solving MATH. And they back it with a comparison figure. Other benchmarks of the era, SuperGLUE, code generation, LogiQA, were all falling to raw scale. MATH visibly wasn't.
[O] But AMPS pretraining did something scale alone couldn't.
[G] Probably the most durable empirical result in the paper. A zero point one billion model pretrained on AMPS and fine-tuned hits five point four percent, matching a thirteen billion parameter model fine-tuned without AMPS, at five point two. That's roughly a hundred thirty times the parameter-efficiency of scaling alone. They even tried pretraining on real Math StackExchange data instead, about three gigabytes of it, and it did slightly worse than AMPS alone, six percent versus six point two. Their synthetic, curated corpus beat authentic internet math text.
[S] And the human baseline, that's the number everyone quotes.
[G] Six university students, twenty held-out problems, one hour, no calculator. A computer science student who dislikes math got eight out of twenty, forty percent. Someone ambivalent about math got thirteen out of twenty. A three-time International Mathematical Olympiad gold medalist got eighteen out of twenty, ninety percent, with the misses being pure arithmetic slips. So the human spread is forty to ninety percent, against three to seven percent for the best models of the day.
[S] There's a quieter problem with that number, though. Four of the six participants are the paper's own authors.
[G] True, and they disclose it themselves. They couldn't crowdsource this given the math background required, so it's six university students, four of whom wrote the paper, plus two acquaintances. Real, honestly-flagged limitation. Treat the forty-to-ninety range as a rough sense of human variance, not a blind, independent baseline.
[O] Which doesn't undercut the basic point, that humans clear this by a wide margin models of the day didn't, but you shouldn't cite ninety percent as an independently verified ceiling.
[O] There's also that scratch-space finding, which I think is the most interesting result in the whole paper.
[G] It's genuinely counterintuitive. You'd expect letting a model write out its reasoning before the final answer to help, the way it helps a person. Instead, forcing GPT-2 one point five billion to generate the full step-by-step solution before its boxed answer dropped accuracy from six point nine percent down to five point three. They call it a snowballing effect: one wrong step early derails everything after it, so the model talks itself into a worse answer than it would've guessed directly.
[S] So scratch space hurt at generation time. Did it help anywhere?
[G] Yes, at training time, which is the subtler half of the result. Stripping step-by-step solutions out of fine-tuning cost them zero point six percent, down to six point three. And when they handed a zero point seven billion model an increasing fraction of the actual ground-truth solution as a hint, accuracy barely moved until they revealed ninety-nine percent of it, and even then it topped out around forty percent. The model can clearly benefit from someone else's reasoning trace. It just can't yet trust its own.

It gave the field an automatically gradable target that the whole chain-of-thought-to-Minerva-to-reinforcement-learning line optimized directly against. You don't get that lineage without a clean, boxed-answer grader sitting at the end of it.
[S] I'll take the other side, starting with the forecast, because it's not just wrong, it's instructively wrong. Scaling wasn't a wall. The paper actually names the exact capability gap, models can't use their own generated reasoning, that chain-of-thought at scale, plus reinforcement learning on verifiable answers, closed within about eighteen months. So, point scored on the forecast, but the paper diagnosed its own blind spot pretty precisely, it just didn't have the tool yet to fix it.
[S] There's a second problem, and it's not hindsight, it's about what the number even measures. Their own figure three shows a model landing on the correct boxed answer through an invalid derivation. Exact match on the final answer can't distinguish a sound proof from a lucky guess, a real gap for a benchmark whose whole pitch is measuring problem-solving, not answer-guessing.
[O] I'll grant that one without a fight. It's the same complaint you'd level at any final-answer-only benchmark, and it's real.
[G] The paper is upfront about it too, figure three is presented as a caveat, not swept under the rug. Where I'd push back slightly is that exact-match-on-boxed-answer is also the thing that made large-scale reinforcement learning on this benchmark's descendants tractable at all, you need a reward signal computable without a human or a second model in the loop. So the design choice that limits construct validity is the same one that enabled the scaling line which made the forecast obsolete. It's not a clean story.
[S] One more, and I think it outweighs the others: contamination. This dataset is scraped largely off a public forum, for competition problems that are themselves public and widely mirrored. Any model trained on a recent web crawl has plausibly seen these exact problems and their exact solutions. So when a twenty twenty-five system reports ninety percent plus, how much of that is reasoning and how much is memorization?
[G] The paper can't answer that, obviously, it predates the concern entirely. But it's exactly why later work treats static MATH scores with caution, and pairs the five-hundred-problem MATH subset with genuinely fresh, held-out contest sets like AIME twenty twenty-four and twenty twenty-five, where a new problem set gets written every year specifically so it can't have leaked into training data.
[O] So the old scoreboard is basically retired as a trustworthy number for frontier models.
[G] Largely, yes, for frontier systems specifically. It still functions fine as an eval for smaller or weaker models that plausibly haven't memorized the whole test set.
[O] So what does this actually change for how we build and read benchmarks now, Julian?
[G] A few things, mostly about learning from where this paper's confidence outran its evidence. First: be suspicious of any capability forecast built by extrapolating a scaling curve from models that lack the mechanism you're trying to measure, chain-of-thought here, but the lesson generalizes. Second: pair a static benchmark with a refreshed or held-out companion once it's old and public enough to plausibly sit in someone's training data, exactly what AIME twenty twenty-four and twenty twenty-five now do for MATH. Third: final-answer exact match is a fine, cheap, scalable signal, but it's not a substitute for grading the reasoning itself once you actually care about validity and not just a leaderboard number.
[S] Their own calibration result is a good closing data point for that last one. GPT-2 one point five billion was badly overconfident, near-certain probabilities on both right and wrong answers, and the area under the ROC curve for separating correct from incorrect was sixty-eight point eight percent, well above chance but far from reliable. A model that can't tell you when it's wrong isn't a model you should trust to self-report.
[O] Which is, again, a twenty twenty-one-scale-model finding, it might not hold at frontier scale today. But it's a useful reminder that accuracy and calibration are different axes, and this paper measured both, even though the second one got a lot less attention.
