[O] Let's start with the problem, because I think a lot of listeners feel this in their bones. What's actually broken about benchmark scores as a way to track AI progress?
[G] The paper lays out three structural issues. First, a lot of benchmark tasks are artificial rather than economically meaningful, so a score doesn't map cleanly onto anything you'd care about in the real world. Second, benchmarks are frequently built adversarially against current models. The authors point to HellaSwag and Humanity's Last Exam, both constructed by throwing out questions the best models of the day could already answer.
[S] Which quietly biases every human comparison, because you've hand-picked the problems machines are bad at.
[G] Exactly. And third, the one the paper treats as most damaging: individual benchmarks saturate faster and faster. A score climbs, plateaus near ceiling, and then it's dead weight. Worse, there's no shared scale to compare a benchmark from 2019 against one from 2025.
[O] So you can't even say whether GPT-2 and a 2025 frontier model are one benchmark generation apart or ten.
[G] Right, every new benchmark is a fresh ruler with no marked units. METR's move is to borrow a unit everyone already understands: time. Instead of asking what fraction of a fixed test a model passes, ask how long a task, measured by how long it takes a skilled human, the model can complete.
[S] I'll grant that's an elegant reframing. My question is always whether an elegant reframing survives contact with the actual data collection, so let's get into the method.
[G] The core object is what they call the X percent task-completion time horizon: the human-time length of tasks a model completes with X percent success. To estimate the 50 percent version, they assembled 170 tasks spanning five orders of magnitude of difficulty, from three sources.
[G] HCAST contributes 97 diverse software and general-agency tasks running from one minute up to 30 hours. RE-Bench adds 7 genuinely hard machine-learning research-engineering tasks, each about 8 hours long. And then there's a new suite they built specifically for this paper, Software Atomic Actions, 66 single-step tasks lasting one to 30 seconds, added so that weak, pre-2023 models, down to GPT-2, register any signal at all instead of just flatlining at zero.
[O] That last piece feels important. Without those tiny tasks you'd have no floor on the curve.
[G] Right, and every task gets two independently measured numbers. A human time rating, from skilled baseliners, professionals in software, machine learning, or cybersecurity with around five years of experience, most from top-100 universities, attempting the task without special context. The rating is the geometric mean time of the successful human runs. In total that's over 800 baselines, more than 2,500 hours of human time, covering 148 of the 169 tasks directly, with researcher estimates filling the rest.
[S] And the agent side?
[G] Twelve frontier models and four near-frontier models, spanning 2019 to 2025, each run 8 times per task inside an agent scaffold, scored success or failure. Then, per model, they fit a logistic regression mapping human task length to the model's success probability. The location parameter where that curve crosses 50 percent is the time horizon.
[O] It's basically Item Response Theory, but the difficulty axis is set by how long a human takes, not learned from how models perform.
[G] That's the analogy the authors draw themselves, and it matters. It means task difficulty is anchored outside the model population, so it doesn't quietly drift as models get better.
[S] Here's my first probe. "Skilled baseliner working without task-specific context" sounds a lot like a specific, narrow kind of human. Does the paper interrogate that choice?
[G] It does, extensively, and we'll get to a result later that shows exactly how much that choice matters. But hold that thought, because the headline number is worth stating cleanly first.
[O] Please, give us the number.
[G] Plotting each model's 50 percent time horizon against its release date, on a log axis, the fit is a clean exponential. The horizon has doubled every 207 days, call it seven months, from 2019 through 2025. R-squared of point nine seven. The bootstrapped 95 percent confidence interval on the doubling time is 166 to 240 days, roughly plus or minus 19 percent.
[O] That's a tight fit for something this messy.
[G] It is, and the range it spans is enormous. GPT-2 sits at roughly a two-second horizon. o3 reaches around 110 minutes, and it succeeds at some individual tasks that take skilled humans over four hours. Worth flagging for anyone who read an earlier version of this paper: the original March 2025 draft put the frontier at Claude 3.7 Sonnet, around 50 minutes. The authors are explicit that the trend, not any single model's exact placement, is the durable finding.
[S] I actually appreciate that they say that out loud instead of quietly revising the number and hoping nobody notices.
[G] They go further. They report that error bars on any one model's horizon are wide, but highly correlated across models, because sampling an easier or harder set of tasks shifts every model's estimate together in the same direction. And the fit barely moves if you change the regularization, the task weighting, or switch from weighted to ordinary least squares.
[O] So the slope is robust even where any single point isn't.
[G] Precisely, and there's a wrinkle worth noting. o3 sits significantly above the long-run trend line, with a p-value of point zero zero six, and the 2023 to 2025 growth rate looks about 20 percent faster than the full 2019 to 2025 average. There's a live question of whether that's acceleration or noise.
[S] Before we go further, is the metric even internally coherent? Does difficulty for models actually track difficulty for humans?
[G] That's tested directly. Model success rate falls steadily as human completion time rises, R-squared around point eight zero. And the set of tasks different models can do is strongly correlated across models, about point seven three. On the diversity-weighted suite, recent frontier models clear roughly 55 to 60 percent average success; GPT-2 manages about 10 percent.
[O] Now here's the part that I think is genuinely underrated: they didn't just measure the trend, they diagnosed where the gains are coming from.
[G] Right, there's a failure analysis comparing GPT-4, the November 2023 version, against o1. Across sampled failed runs, the single biggest shift is that o1 almost stopped repeating failed actions, 12 such failures for GPT-4 down to 2 for o1. It also got better at tool use and logical reasoning.
[S] But?
[G] But it abandoned tasks prematurely more often, 8 cases up to 16. And both models still struggle badly in messy environments without clean feedback loops. So this isn't "the model got smarter" in some vague sense, it's specifically reliability and error recovery doing most of the work.
[O] That's a much more falsifiable claim than "AI is getting more capable," and I like that they went and checked it instead of just asserting it.
[S] Agreed, that's a real strength. Now let's talk about the extrapolation, because that's where this stops being descriptive and starts being a forecast people will act on.
[G] The authors pick one month, about 167 working hours, as the target horizon, partly because Ngo has argued that a one-month AGI, one that can do a month of expert work, could plausibly onboard and acquire context the way a new human employee does, and would by construction exceed humans at things like writing large applications or founding a company. Perturbing the data across bootstraps and methodology choices, the central estimate for when that horizon is reached lands around mid-2029, with an 80 percent interval spanning roughly two years, mid-2028 to mid-2031.
[O] And if the faster 2024 to 2025 trend is the real signal rather than noise?
[G] Then it arrives much sooner, with about half the probability mass landing before 2027.

[O] I'll make the optimist case first, because I think it's stronger than skeptics usually credit. This is a genuinely new instrument. It's the first capability metric I've seen that doesn't saturate, that puts a 2019 model and a 2025 model on literally the same axis, and that's been stress-tested against a wall of ablations, regularization choices, weighting schemes, and it barely moves. A seven-month doubling held for six straight years is not a small pattern. And they didn't cherry-pick the story to be flattering. Reliability improving faster than raw reasoning is a more interesting, more mechanistic finding than "line goes up."
[S] Here's my strongest deflationary case. Fifty percent success is a coin flip. A model with a 110-minute horizon fails that task half the time. [G] The 80 percent horizon doubles at almost the same rate, 204 days versus 207, so the growth rate itself isn't the issue. But at any given point in time, the 80 percent horizons are four to six times shorter than the 50 percent horizons for the same model. And the paper is honest that their dataset can't even pin down a 95 percent horizon with any precision.
[O] So deployable, reliable capability is trailing the headline curve by years, not doubling periods.
[S] Which means the "2029" number is really "2029 for a coin flip on a curated task," and reliability, not raw horizon length, is the actual bottleneck the metric underweights. [G] It is. The tasks are self-contained, automatically scorable, with clear feedback loops. Real software work is rarely any of those things. The authors score their own tasks against 16 hand-picked messiness factors, and models do measurably worse on messier tasks. To their credit, the trend over time looks similar for the messy and clean subsets, no visible plateau specific to messy work, but the absolute gap is real.
[S] And then there's the human-reference problem, which I think is the single most damaging result in the paper for the headline framing.
[G] It probably is. In an internal pull-request probe, professional repo maintainers completed the same fixes five to eighteen times faster than the contract baseliners METR used for timing. And agent performance tracked contractor time, not maintainer time. Choose the more experienced human as your yardstick, and the model's effective horizon shrinks by an order of magnitude.
[O] That's a real gut check on the whole framing. If "human time" means a contractor without repository context rather than the person who actually owns the code, the horizon you're measuring is specifically "time for a newcomer," not "time for an expert."
[G] There's a second independent check pointing the same direction. When they apply the identical methodology to SWE-bench Verified, an industry-standard software benchmark, they still get an exponential trend, but a much steeper one, doubling roughly every 70 days instead of 207. The authors trace that to SWE-bench's own time annotations underestimating how long the easiest tasks really take relative to their contractor baselines, which artificially compresses the apparent doubling time.
[S] So even the shape of the trend is sensitive to exactly whose clock you use and how those times were collected. That's not a minor footnote, that's a load-bearing assumption for a forecast people will cite as "2029."
[O] I'll concede that point fully. Where I push back is contamination. Ottilie, is leakage actually a live threat here?
[G] The authors argue it's not the central threat, because the tasks are agentic and execution-scored rather than multiple choice, and the 66 new SWAA tasks were built specifically to anchor pre-2023 models with fresh material. But HCAST, RE-Bench, and SWE-bench Verified are public, so some leakage into training data is plausible. The authors' own position is that the slope, not any single model's level, is the claim doing the work, and slope should be far less sensitive to any one model having seen a task before.
[S] I'll accept that as a reasonable mitigation, not a full answer.
[O] So where does that leave the headline claim, honestly?
[G] My read, staying close to what the paper actually shows: the doubling trend on this specific suite is real and unusually well-supported for a capability claim in this field. Whether that trend transfers to messy real-world work, and how far behind deployable, high-reliability performance trails the 50 percent number, are exactly the two questions the paper cannot yet answer, and the authors say so themselves rather than papering over it.
[O] Which is honestly rare enough in this literature to be worth praising on its own.
[S] Agreed, and it's why I take the trend seriously even while I distrust the specific date. So what should a working evaluation researcher actually do with this?
[G] Two things, going slightly beyond the paper's own recommendations. First, if you're building or reporting a benchmark, report success at multiple reliability thresholds, not just one, because a single 50 percent number quietly buries the reliability gap we just discussed. Second, be explicit about whose completion time you're using as the human reference, because this paper just demonstrated that choice alone can move a headline result by an order of magnitude.
[O] And more broadly, this connects directly to AI governance conversations happening right now.
