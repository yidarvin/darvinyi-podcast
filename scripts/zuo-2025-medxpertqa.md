[G] Two problems. On the text side, the standard benchmarks, MedQA, MedMCQA, PubMedQA, and the medical slice of MMLU, had simply been solved. A test where every frontier model already tops out can't tell you which model is actually better.
[O] Which is the saturation problem in a nutshell. Once everyone's at the ceiling, you're measuring noise.
[G] Right. The second problem is on the multimodal side, and it's a different failure mode. Benchmarks like VQA-Rad, Path-VQA, Slake, and PMC-VQA pair a medical image with a question that was automatically generated from its caption. So the questions end up being things like "what organ is this," basic visual perception, not the multi-step differential-diagnosis reasoning a physician actually does. And none of them wrap the image in a patient history, lab values, or exam findings, which is what an image means in a real clinical encounter.
[S] What about MMMU, the big multimodal reasoning benchmark? It has a health and medicine slice.
[G] It does, but the authors argue it isn't medicine-specific, it tops out at university-course difficulty, and it still lacks that clinical context around the image. So their design target was a benchmark hard enough to not already be solved, sourced from real licensing and specialty-board exams rather than generated from captions, and defended against contamination, since scraped licensing-exam questions are exactly what a web-scale training run would have absorbed.
[O] Three honest design goals. Let's get into how they actually built it.
[G] They start from thirty seven thousand, five hundred forty three candidate questions. Sources are the USMLE and COMLEX-USA general licensing exams, seventeen American specialty-board exams covering fields like internal medicine, psychiatry and neurology, surgery, and family medicine, and three image-rich sources, the American College of Radiology's exams, the European Board of Radiology's exams, and the New England Journal of Medicine's Image Challenge. That splits into roughly twenty six thousand, seven hundred candidates for text and just under eleven thousand for multimodal.
[S] Thirty seven thousand candidates down to four thousand four hundred sixty final questions. Walk me through the filtering.
[G] Five stages. First, Human Expert Filtering. They use a Brier score, a proper scoring rule over how a pool of real human test-takers actually answered each question, with an adaptive threshold calibrated to expert-annotated difficulty tiers. Anything the humans found too easy gets cut, taking the pool down to a bit over ten thousand.
[O] So step one is literally "would this stump a person," before any model ever sees it.
[G] Exactly. Step two, AI Expert Filtering, runs eight models, smaller seven-to-eleven-billion-parameter models as "basic" experts plus GPT-4o and Claude 3.5 Sonnet as "advanced" experts, four times each per question. Anything the basic models always get right is discarded as too easy; anything every model gets wrong is kept. That cuts the pool to under five thousand.
[S] And then?
[G] Edit-distance and semantic-similarity filtering, using a medical sentence encoder plus outlier detection, strips near-duplicate questions a model could exploit as template shortcuts, a small trim to around four thousand seven hundred. Then the interesting step: Question and Option Augmentation. GPT-4o and Claude 3.5 Sonnet rephrase every surviving question and expand the option count, to ten for text and five for multimodal, to lower the odds of memorization and raise the pure guessing floor.
[O] And a final human pass on top of all that machine filtering.
[G] Right, multi-round review by licensed physicians, who correct rather than just delete flagged errors. That lands at two thousand four hundred fifty five text questions and two thousand five multimodal questions, then a small slice of each becomes a few-shot development split, leaving four thousand four hundred sixty test questions total, across seventeen specialties and eleven body systems.
[S] That's a more careful pipeline than most benchmark papers bother with. I'll say that up front.
