[S] Every major lab claims its model is multilingual, and here is a benchmark built specifically to make them prove it with knowledge, not vocabulary.
[O] And when you actually run that test, the results are wild. Swap out translated English trivia for questions written from scratch inside each language, and a single language's score can swing by more than twenty points.
[S] Which sounds like proof multilinguality is fake. Read it the other way, though, and it means every leaderboard built on translated benchmarks like MMMLU or MGSM might be quietly misjudging exactly which languages a model is weak in.
[O] Either way, no model in this paper clears thirty-five percent average, on a test built to be fair to all thirty-one of its languages at once.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper at a time. I'm the optimist.
[S] I'm the skeptic, and today we're grading multilingual benchmarks on whether they're actually multilingual.
[O] The paper is MultiLoKo, a multilingual local knowledge benchmark for LLMs spanning thirty-one languages, by Dieuwke Hupkes and Nikolay Bogoychev at Meta. Joining us is Emrys, who has spent real time inside this benchmark. Emrys, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. MultiLoKo is one of the more careful attempts yet to ask whether a multilingual model actually knows things locally, rather than just being able to translate.
[S] Emrys, start with the gap. What's wrong with how multilinguality gets tested today?
[G] Most benchmarks in wide use are English content translated into other languages. MGSM is translated GSM8K, MMMLU is translated MMLU, Belebele too. They scale to dozens of languages cheaply, but the content itself never left English.
[O] Sounds fine on the surface — same questions, just a different language.
[G] Translated text differs systematically from native text, and more importantly, an English-authored question probes knowledge relevant to an English-speaking worldview. A Swedish question about a singer nobody outside Sweden has heard of will never get generated by a translation pipeline in the first place.
[S] So it's multilingual the way Google Translate is multilingual.
[G] The paper's own phrase is multilingual on the surface, not in content. Older benchmarks like PAWS-X, XNLI, and XCOPA at least weren't translation-based, but they're classification-format, built for a pre-autoregressive era, and cover far fewer languages than modern deployment needs.
[O] There's precedent for doing this properly, though. TyDiQA and MLQA source data separately per language.
[G] Right, but neither deliberately selects locally relevant content, and their coverage is small. One benchmark, EXAMS, did source exam questions separately per language years ago — the authors flag, almost bemused, that it was essentially never adopted for any major model release.
[S] So the gap is: nobody had built something both wide-coverage and genuinely locally sourced, with a controlled way to measure how much the translation shortcut distorts the picture.
[G] That's exactly the two goals MultiLoKo sets for itself.
[O] Walk us through how you build a locally relevant question for thirty-one languages without thirty-one in-house expert teams.
[G] For each language they take the six thousand most-visited Wikipedia pages from twenty sixteen to twenty twenty-one, sample roughly three-thousand-character paragraphs, and have annotators rate each topic's local relevance on a one-to-five scale. One means specific to the language, their example is a Swedish singer nobody outside Sweden knows. Five means globally famous, their example is YouTube. Anything scoring above three gets discarded.
[S] So you're explicitly filtering out the globally famous material a translated benchmark would have picked anyway.
[G] Exactly the point. What's left is locally salient by construction. Native speakers then write closed-form questions with one short answer, five hundred per language, after a fifty-question pilot per locale to sanity-check the instructions.
[O] And the quality control after that?
[G] Two more passes. A separate reviewer checks each question against the guidelines and flags or fixes issues. Then two more annotators, different from the writer, independently try to answer it — if either is wrong, or lands on a different answer because the question is ambiguous, it's dropped. The only allowed edits add semantically equivalent correct answers, like "four" for "4."
[S] That's a genuinely careful pipeline. The translation arms are what I actually want to hear about.
[G] Here's the clever part. Every non-English question gets translated into English, and every English question into all thirty other languages, both by human translators and by Google Translate — a main, locally-sourced partition of fifteen thousand five hundred questions, plus four translated partitions, human and machine, in each direction, fifteen thousand questions apiece.
[O] So for any given question you can ask: was this easier because the model actually knows the fact, or because English training data is just bigger?
[G] That's precisely what the design lets you separate. One more piece: instead of a random dev-test split, they split by page-visit frequency — most-visited topics land in dev, the long tail in a blind test set only an external provider can score, specifically to resist contamination and overfitting.
[S] Which models actually got run through this?
[G] Eleven, all marketed as multilingual. Four base models: Llama 3.1 at seventy billion and four hundred five billion, Mixtral 8x22B, and Qwen2.5 seventy-two billion. Seven chat models: Gemini 2.0 Flash, GPT-4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, and chat variants of the same Llama, Mixtral, and Qwen families. Base models got five-shot prompts, chat models zero-shot, temperature zero, prompts written separately per language by linguistic experts.
[O] And the metrics. I know there are four.
[G] Two headline ones: Exact Match, whether the answer verbatim matches a gold answer, and Gap, the spread between best and worst language. Then two diagnostic metrics from the parallel design. Mother Tongue Effect compares a question asked natively versus in English; positive means the knowledge is more accessible natively. Locality Effect compares locally-sourced questions to English-translated questions; positive means translation underestimated it.
[S] So Mother Tongue Effect is about the model's ability to move knowledge between languages, and Locality Effect is about how much the sourcing method itself misleads you.
[G] Exactly that distinction, and it's the spine of the paper's second contribution.
[O] Give me the headline numbers.
[G] Best average Exact Match is Gemini 2.0 Flash at thirty-four point four, essentially tied with Llama 3.1 four-oh-five B at thirty-four point three, and GPT-4o at thirty-four. At the bottom, base Qwen2.5 seventy-two B lands at nineteen point seven, and the chat models collapse further, Mixtral 8x22B instruct at ten point one, Qwen2.5 seventy-two B instruct at two point five.
[S] Wait. The base Qwen model beats its own instruction-tuned version by a factor of eight?
[G] Base models beat their chat counterparts almost across the board, and the authors attribute that partly to false refusals and poor instruction-following under a strict exact-match parser, though sometimes the chat model is simply giving a qualitatively different, harder-to-score answer.
[O] Even the best models are barely a third correct.
[G] And even the easiest languages cap below fifty percent. Parity is worse. The gap between best and worst language runs from thirty-four point eight for Gemini up to forty-eight point eight for GPT-4o across the full thirty-one languages. Restrict to just the five best non-English languages, and top models already lose more than five points relative to English.
[S] So multilingual here means consistently mediocre, unevenly.
[G] A fair read of that section.
[O] Does knowledge in one language actually transfer to another?
[G] Mostly not. For most languages, Mother Tongue Effect is positive — models do better asked natively than asked the identical question in English, meaning local knowledge isn't moving into English. Exceptions are mostly languages floored in both settings anyway, Bengali, Khmer, Urdu, Farsi, no room left to drop. Hindi's the interesting exception: reasonable native performance, yet still better in English.
[S] And Claude 3.5 Sonnet?
[G] Excluded from all the transfer analyses. The prompt couldn't get it to produce a parsable English answer, so its English score was an artefactual four point eight, which inflated its apparent Mother Tongue Effect to twenty-four. That's a measurement artefact, not a transfer finding.
[O] Is there a sharper signal than the average effect?
[G] Consistency. Of the questions a model gets right in either language, what fraction does it get right in both. Even the best models sit under fifty percent. An Exact Match of thirty in English and thirty in some other language does not mean it's the same thirty questions.
[S] Now the meta-finding, the one I actually want to argue about. What happens when you swap locally-sourced questions for the English-translated version?
[G] Two separate comparisons here. First, take a language's own locally-sourced questions and ask them in English instead — that isolates proficiency from raw question difficulty. The difficulty ranking only correlates point seven nine between the two settings. Bengali jumps from twenty-sixth hardest to fourth easiest in English, Urdu twenty-sixth to twelfth, Hindi fourteenth to fifth. Russian moves the other way, seventh to twenty-first.
[O] So for Bengali and Urdu, it's not that the model lacks the knowledge, it just can't operate well enough in that language to surface it.
[G] That's the read. The second, more practical comparison: write questions in English and translate them, versus sourcing locally. That's the Locality Effect, swinging a single language's score by up to twenty-two points — GPT-4o ranges minus twenty-two to plus fourteen, Gemini minus twenty-one to plus fifteen. Roughly fifteen points on average, reshuffling which languages look hard, rank correlation only point seven eight.
[S] Does it change which model looks best, though?
[G] Barely at all. Model rankings stay almost identical whether you use locally-sourced or translated data. So translated benchmarks are fine for ranking models against each other, but actively misleading if you're deciding which languages a model needs work on.
[O] And machine translation stacked on top of human?
[G] Every model scores lower with Google Translate than with human translation, drops ranging two to thirty-four percent of the human-translated score. Model ranking correlation stays high, point nine seven, with only three local swaps among models not statistically distinguishable anyway. But per-language difficulty shifts up to twelve points, and for Russian, Swedish, and Urdu specifically, that holds consistently across models.
[S] Did the held-out test split actually work as a contamination check?
[G] Reasonably. Average score drops from twenty-four on dev to twenty-one on test, confirming the frequency-based split is genuinely harder, meaning more of that knowledge sits in the tail. Which incidentally suggests Wikipedia itself might not be the primary place these models learned it from.
[O] Let me make the strongest case here. This is the first benchmark I've seen that operationalizes multilingual on the surface versus multilingual in content: a real four-way parallel design, multi-pass human quality control, an out-of-distribution split held by an external provider. It should worry every lab leaning on MMMLU or MGSM: a translated benchmark can misjudge a language's difficulty by twenty points while still ranking models correctly.
[S] The construction is careful, genuinely one of the better-built multilingual benchmarks I've read. But the headline scores fold together three things: does the model know the fact, can it operate in that language, can it follow the format. Look at the floor: Qwen2.5 seventy-two B instruct at two point five, Claude's English score at four point eight — format failures, not knowledge failures.
[G] Fair, and the authors are candid about it. They found a post-processing bug that mis-scored Urdu for GPT-4o and Gemini, had to hand-patch English and Japanese, and say plainly that evaluating chatty models independently of their instruction-following remains unsolved. So the base-beating-chat pattern, and the very lowest scores, read better as lower bounds than a clean leaderboard.
[O] That's an argument for reading the absolute scores cautiously, not for dismissing the meta-findings, which are the actual contribution.
[S] My second point: the meta-findings have their own asterisks. The twenty-two-point swing is a per-language extreme; the average Locality Effect across languages is close to zero, because positive and negative swings cancel out. So "rankings are stable" and "a single language can swing wildly" are both true at once, and easy to quote one without the other.
[G] Both are literally true in the paper, and both need saying together. That's exactly why Gap exists alongside average Exact Match as a metric.
[S] A third issue: uncontrolled question difficulty. The authors flag that Tagalog lands as the second-easiest language in the whole benchmark, and their best guess is that its annotators simply wrote easier questions. If difficulty isn't controlled across thirty-one independently-run pipelines, some of what looks like a language effect might just be an annotator effect.
[G] Acknowledged directly in the paper, and it means the language-difficulty rankings specifically, not the model rankings, deserve some skepticism too.
[S] There's a coverage wrinkle underneath that. Locale isn't the same as language — the authors admit English, Spanish, Portuguese, French, and Chinese each span multiple very different cultures, and didn't control for that, so the "local" questions for those languages are probably a grab bag of whichever culture the annotators drew from.
[G] True, and it cuts into the annotator-pool question too. The authors say plainly they're less likely to catch mistakes in languages they don't personally understand, which risks a quality bias toward whichever handful of languages the authors and their closest reviewers actually read.
[O] What about contamination, Emrys? Everything here comes from Wikipedia, which sits in basically every one of these models' pretraining data.
[G] About as well as you can without a private data source: a blind test set held externally, split by page-visit frequency so the tail lands in test. It behaves like a real out-of-distribution split — dev averages twenty-four, test twenty-one. But every item is from Wikipedia, the dev set is public, and a three-point gap is a modest signal, not strong.
[S] And that risk isn't even across languages. English Wikipedia dwarfs Khmer or Marathi Wikipedia, so contamination for a popular English page is a different animal than for a long-tail question in a low-resource language. The paper doesn't break that down, but it follows directly from how uneven Wikipedia itself is.
[G] Fair, and the authors concede the harder version of that worry themselves. The benchmark could, in their words, be hacked relatively easily just by oversampling multilingual Wikipedia during training.
[O] Even granting every one of those caveats, the direction of the finding survives: translated benchmarks distort language-level conclusions. That's the part I'd bet holds up under replication.
[S] I'd bet on that too, honestly. I just wouldn't bet on the thirty-four percent headline meaning what it looks like it means.
[G] If I'm scoring it: on whether locally-sourced, parallel benchmark construction is worth doing, the optimist wins outright — the design answers a question the field needed answered. On whether the absolute scores are a trustworthy leaderboard today, the skeptic is right. Trust the comparisons this paper is built to make; be far more careful with the raw numbers.
[O] So what changes if labs actually take this seriously?
[G] Practically, it argues against treating MGSM- or MMMLU-style translated benchmarks as the last word on which languages a model is weak in — you'd want a locally-sourced check before deciding where to invest data or fine-tuning effort. It also argues for reporting Gap and consistency alongside average accuracy, not accuracy alone.
[S] For evaluation practice more broadly, it's another data point that generative, exact-match scoring is running out of road. Free-form answers eventually need a more forgiving matcher, or a judge, and a judge model brings its own biases into a benchmark specifically trying to be fair across languages.
[G] The paper tried BLEU, ChrF, a "contains" check, and judges, and stuck with Exact Match anyway — the least-biased option available, not the most accurate one. An honest admission nobody has actually solved this yet.
[O] And the deeper implication: closing this gap doesn't look like a scaling problem, it looks like a data problem — more locally-sourced, non-English-native training data, not just a bigger, more English-centric model.
[G] Consistent with the Mother Tongue Effect results. Knowledge sits in the language it was learned in, and being bigger or more capable doesn't automatically move it into English, or out of it.
[G] The paper's own takeaway: none of the eleven models evaluated are evenly multilingual, translated benchmarks can distort which languages look hard by twenty points or more while still ranking the models correctly, and multilingual evaluation needs to test knowledge, not just language proficiency.
