[S] And I'm the skeptic. Today's paper is MMBench: Is Your Multi-modal Model an All-around Player?, by Yuan Liu, Haodong Duan, Yuanhan Zhang, Bo Li, Songyang Zhang, and colleagues at Shanghai AI Laboratory and collaborating universities. Published at ECCV in 2024, first posted as a preprint back in 2023 and revised since.
[O] Joining us is Elena, who has spent real time in this paper's tables and its evaluation code. Elena, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. This is a paper that's easy to undersell as "yet another VLM benchmark," but the evaluation protocol underneath it is the actual contribution, and it's had a long shelf life because of that.
[S] We picked it because it's still a default in VLMEvalKit, and because it's a clean case study in how much an evaluation methodology, not just a dataset, can change what a leaderboard number means.
[G] Right, and the authors are explicit about the gap they were closing. By early-to-mid 2023, vision-language models like GPT-4v, LLaVA, and MiniGPT-4 were multiplying fast, and the two existing ways to compare them were both broken in complementary ways.
[O] Walk us through that gap, Elena, because it sets up everything else.
[G] Objective datasets, VQAv2, COCO Caption, GQA, OK-VQA, give you a number, but that number is corrupted two ways. First, exact-match scoring produces false negatives constantly, if a model says "bicycle" and the reference says "bike," that's scored wrong. Second, those datasets are coarse. One accuracy score tells you nothing about whether a model is bad at OCR specifically, or spatial reasoning specifically, or celebrity recognition specifically.
[S] And the alternative was human evaluation.
[G] Subjective protocols like OwlEval, eighty-two hand-built open-ended questions, and LVLM-eHub's pairwise arena, fix the matching problem, a human knows "bike" means "bicycle." But they don't scale, they're hard to reproduce with a different annotator pool, and small sets are statistically noisy.
[O] So the paper's premise is that you need fine-grained ability coverage and an objective, reproducible metric at the same time.
[G] That's the pitch. And there's a second, less obvious problem underneath both approaches. Many 2023-era VLMs had weak instruction-following. Ask a four-choice question, and a lot of them won't output a clean "A," they'll restate the answer in prose, or refuse, or wander. Score that with rule-based string matching and you're penalizing a formatting failure, not a reasoning failure, and conflating the two.
[S] That's a fair problem to name. Let's get into how they actually built the thing.
[G] Two layers: a taxonomy for coverage, and an evaluation protocol for robustness. Start with the taxonomy. At the top sit two level-one abilities every model needs: Perception, gathering information from sensory input, and Reasoning, drawing conclusions from it. Those split into six level-two groups, Coarse Perception, Fine-grained Perception split into single-instance and cross-instance, Attribute Reasoning, Logical Reasoning, and Relation Reasoning. And those split again into twenty level-three leaf abilities, things like Object Localization, OCR, Celebrity Recognition, Image Quality, Spatial Relationship, and Structuralized Image-Text Understanding.
[O] Twenty is a lot of categories for what's fundamentally a multiple-choice test.
[G] It is, and every leaf ability gets at least a hundred twenty-five questions, hand-collected by undergraduate and graduate volunteers, over eighty percent sourced from the internet, the rest reusing public dataset images with self-written questions. Total is three thousand two hundred seventeen multiple-choice questions, each one a question, two to four choices, an image, and an answer. They also translated the whole set into Chinese, MMBench-CN, GPT-4-assisted with human verification, so you get an apples-to-apples bilingual comparison. Split is four to six, dev to test, dev ships with public answers, test answers stay server-side.
[S] Elena, before we move on, how much quality control is actually behind that eighty percent internet-sourced figure? Volunteer-collected data from the internet is exactly where I'd expect junk to creep in.
[G] Good instinct, and they do run two automatic filters before any human reviews anything. A text-only majority-vote filter feeds each question, image withheld, to a panel of state-of-the-art LLMs, GPT-4, Gemini-Pro, and so on. If most of them answer correctly from text alone, the question isn't actually testing multimodal understanding, and it gets flagged for manual review. A separate wrong-sample filter runs the question past a panel of state-of-the-art VLMs with the image included, and if every one of them fails, the question gets flagged as potentially broken, bad choices or a wrong labeled answer, and manually checked.
[O] Okay, so that's the coverage half. What's the actual methodological contribution?
[G] Two pieces on the evaluation-protocol side. First, LLM-assisted choice extraction. A heuristic pass tries to pull a bare label, A, B, C, or D, out of the model's raw output. If that fails, GPT-4, specifically gpt-4-0125, gets shown the question, the choices, and the model's prediction, and is asked to output the matching label, or a pseudo-label "Z" if nothing matches.
[S] And how do we know that's trustworthy, rather than just outsourcing the grading to another black box?
[G] They validated it against about four hundred twenty human-annotated hard cases, ten percent of the cases the heuristic pass couldn't resolve. GPT-4 hit ninety-one point five percent alignment with human choice extraction, the best of everything they tested, GPT-3.5-Turbo and Qwen-Max both landed around eighty-five percent, and the strongest open-source option, InternLM2 7B, reached eighty-seven. And the effect size is real. VisualGLM-6B's heuristic match rate, meaning how often the bare string parser worked at all, is only sixty-four point eight percent. Adding LLM-based extraction adds twenty-three point two points to its measured accuracy, purely by rescuing answers the string matcher couldn't parse.
[O] Twenty-three points from a scoring fix alone. That's the false-negative problem made concrete.
[G] Exactly. And second, the paper's real contribution: CircularEval. Instead of asking a question once, MMBench asks it N times, N being the number of choices, circularly shifting the choice-and-answer mapping each pass, and only counts the model correct if it gets every single pass right.
[S] Say that mechanism again, slowly, because it's the whole ballgame.
[G] Take a four-choice question. Pass one, choices in original order, model answers. Pass two, the same choices rotated by one position, so what was labeled "D" is now labeled "A," and so on, model answers again. Four passes total for a four-choice question. If the model gets even one pass wrong, the whole question counts as failed, and in practice you can stop early once it fails, so the real inference cost is less than four times, not exactly four times.
[O] Why does that catch something a single pass doesn't?
[G] Because the paper's own analysis shows VLMs have real positional bias. One model in their study predicts the letter "B" forty-two point one percent of the time, against a near-uniform twenty-five percent per letter in the ground truth. A model with that habit can rack up correct answers on a single pass just by favoring a letter, independent of whether it understood the image. Circularly shifting the labels breaks that shortcut, because the "right" letter keeps moving.

[O] Let's get to results, because the cold-open number deserves the full table now.
[G] The CircularEval-versus-VanillaEval comparison on MMBench-dev is the paper's headline finding. OpenFlamingo v2 collapses from thirty-six point seven percent under vanilla single-pass scoring to two point six percent under CircularEval, a drop of thirty-four point one points, down to essentially random. And it's not just the weak models. GPT-4v drops ten point eight points to seventy-four point three percent. Qwen-VL-Max drops eight point seven points to seventy-six point four. InternLM-XComposer2, the smallest drop among the strong models, still falls four point seven points to seventy-nine point one.
[S] So even the best model in the study loses almost five points just from being asked the same question with the letters shuffled.
[G] Right, and CircularEval also widens the gaps that actually separate models. LLaVA-v1.5-13B beats its own 7B version by two point one points under vanilla scoring, but by four point seven points under circular scoring. The stricter protocol makes real capability differences more visible, not less.
[O] What's the actual leaderboard, once you apply CircularEval and GPT-4-assisted extraction together, on the held-out test split?
[G] A text-only GPT-4-Turbo control, no image at all, scores two point nine percent overall, close to the random floor, which is the paper's contamination sanity check, confirming the quality-control filters removed most of the text-answerable leakage. Among open-source models, InternLM-XComposer2 tops the board at seventy-eight point one percent, ahead of every other open-source model and every proprietary model by what the paper calls a large margin. Among proprietary models, Qwen-VL-Max scores seventy-five point four, GPT-4v seventy-four point three, Gemini-Pro-V seventy point two. And MiniCPM-V, at three billion parameters or fewer, still clears sixty-one point four percent, which is the paper's evidence that scale isn't the only lever that matters.
[S] What about the weak end?
[G] MiniGPT-4, IDEFICS, VisualGLM, and InstructBLIP all sit in the thirties to forties percent, and OpenFlamingo v2 is essentially at random, two point three percent on the test split. There's also a clean ablation on backbone choice: holding the LLaVA vision stack and training data fixed, swapping the language model from Vicuna-v1.5 to the larger InternLM2-20B lifts every ability category, reasoning most of all. And going from seven billion to thirteen billion parameters within the same language-model family adds eight point three points for MiniGPT, one point five for InstructBLIP, and three point five for LLaVA-v1.5.
[O] Two more results worth naming before the debate. What did they find on the bilingual side, and on content moderation?
[G] Bilingual gap first. Most models score lower on MMBench-CN than on the English version, but the strong bilingual models barely notice, InternLM-XComposer2's English-to-Chinese drop is under one point, and the largest gap among every model scoring above seventy percent is only two points. On content moderation, all three proprietary VLMs refuse some questions outright. GPT-4v refuses one point eight percent of CircularEval passes, and seventy-four percent of its refusals are celebrity-recognition questions specifically. Gemini-Pro-V refuses one point six percent, Qwen-VL-Max just zero point one percent. The paper computes an upper bound assuming perfect answers on every refusal, and scores move by at most two point four points, a real but modest effect.
[S] And the fine-grained breakdown, where does the proprietary-versus-open-source gap actually concentrate?
[G] It's not uniform, which is itself a finding. It concentrates almost entirely in Structuralized Image-Text Understanding, tables, diagrams, layouts, and in knowledge-heavy leaves like celebrity recognition and physical property. Elsewhere, open models are competitive. And every model in the study plateaus somewhere. Max accuracy across every evaluated VLM is only sixty-one point three percent on Image Quality, sixty-one point five on Structuralized Image-Text Understanding, sixty-four on Physical Relation Reasoning, and sixty-eight on Spatial Relationship. Low-level visual judgments, structured-document parsing, and spatial reasoning are where everyone still struggles.

[O] Okay, time for the actual argument. I'll make the strongest case for CircularEval first. This is a real methodological advance, not a cosmetic one. It suppressed the single biggest source of noise in multiple-choice VLM evaluation, letter-position bias, and it did it with a mechanism you can verify from first principles, not a black-box adjustment. The OpenFlamingo v2 collapse from thirty-seven to under three percent is proof the old numbers were partly fiction. And the cost story is honest too, they note that once a model fails one pass, you skip the rest, so the real inference cost is well under N times, which makes it practical to actually deploy, and it has been, it's a default in VLMEvalKit today.
[S] Here's my deflationary read. CircularEval is a bar-raiser, not a guessing-immune protocol, and the paper doesn't fully earn the stronger claim it implies. Requiring all N passes to succeed does suppress lucky single-shot guessing and random positional bias, that OpenFlamingo v2 number proves it. But a model with a consistent, non-random wrong prior can still pass. If a model has learned some shift-invariant shortcut correlated with the answer's content rather than its label position, circular shifting won't catch it, because the shortcut moves with the content, not the letter.
[O] That's a real distinction, but is it a practical one, or a theoretical worry?
[S] It's practical because the paper never tests it. There's no adversarial baseline, something explicitly designed to exploit content-level shortcuts rather than position, run through CircularEval to establish a true guessing floor. They show the floor is much lower than vanilla scoring's twenty-five to thirty-seven percent. That's a weaker claim than "guessing is now impossible," and the paper's own framing sometimes reads as the stronger one.
[G] I'll adjudicate that one. The skeptic is right on the specific claim: the paper demonstrates CircularEval suppresses positional guessing, which is real and well-evidenced, but it does not demonstrate immunity to a more sophisticated adversarial shortcut, because it never constructs one to test against. That's a genuine gap, not a nitpick. But I'd push back on calling the whole protocol a bar-raiser and nothing more. The magnitude of the collapse on genuinely weak models, and the fact that it widens real capability gaps like the LLaVA 7B-versus-13B comparison, tells you it's measuring something structurally different from vanilla scoring, not just applying a stricter threshold on the same measurement.
[O] Second front. The choice extractor. I actually think this is the underrated piece. Before this, evaluating a model with poor instruction-following meant either penalizing it for formatting instead of reasoning, or hand-checking thousands of free-form answers. GPT-4 at ninety-one point five percent human alignment is a genuinely good automated stand-in, and it rescued twenty-three points of real accuracy for VisualGLM-6B alone, which would otherwise have looked far worse than it actually is.
[S] Ninety-one point five percent sounds solid until you look at what it's measured on. Four hundred twenty samples, ten percent of only the hardest, heuristic-match-failed cases. The paper doesn't report whether the remaining eight point five percent of misalignment is randomly distributed across models, or whether it systematically favors verbose models over terse ones, or hedging models over blunt ones. On a leaderboard that separates twenty-plus models by single-digit point differences, an uneven eight-point-five-percent error rate is not obviously negligible, and we're not told it isn't a problem.
[O] Fair, but that's a call for more analysis, not evidence the number is actually wrong.
[S] It's both. And there's a durability problem layered on top. They fixed the extractor to a single frozen checkpoint, gpt-4-0125. The entire leaderboard's comparability depends on that specific model staying available and behaving consistently. The paper doesn't discuss what happens to reproducibility once that checkpoint is deprecated, which, given how fast these APIs turn over, is not a hypothetical.
[G] Both points land, and I'd add one more, that cuts in the same direction. MMBench's own quality-control loop uses the same class of state-of-the-art LLMs and VLMs, GPT-4, Gemini-Pro, contemporaneous VLM families, to decide which questions survive filtering. That risks a subtle circularity, tautologically favoring whatever those particular models are already good at, and flagging genuinely hard questions that trip every current model as "potentially wrong" rather than "hard." The paper reports this loop transparently, which is to its credit, but it doesn't stress-test whether that circularity introduces a systematic skew in which abilities end up over- or under-represented.
[S] So score that one to me too.
[G] On evidence, yes, that's an unexamined risk, not a refuted one.

[O] Where does this land for evaluation practice more broadly, Elena?
[G] I think the durable lesson is that multiple-choice VLM evaluation needed exactly two fixes at once, a way to stop rewarding format compliance over reasoning, and a way to stop rewarding lucky or biased guessing, and this paper is one of the first to ship both together and prove the effect size. That combination is why it became a default eval rather than a one-off dataset. But the paper is also a good case study in a broader eval-practice risk, that self-referential quality control, using today's strongest models to curate a benchmark that will then rank those same model families, is a pattern worth watching for in other benchmarks too, not just this one.
[S] And there's a contamination angle the paper touches but doesn't fully close. Over eighty percent of the images are internet-sourced, the benchmark has become popular enough to be widely discussed and cited, and the dev split's answers are public. That's exactly the profile of a benchmark that risks leaking into future training data over time, independent of anything the authors did wrong.
[O] Which argues for periodic re-audits, not a one-time contamination check.
[G] Agreed, and that's consistent with what I'd want to see next: a published breakdown of extractor agreement by model family, not just an aggregate number, an explicit adversarial baseline run through CircularEval, and a recurring contamination check as the field keeps training on data that increasingly includes benchmark discussions like this one.

[G] If I leave the listener with one line: MMBench correctly diagnosed that 2023-era VLM evaluation was contaminated by guessing, positional bias, and instruction-following noise, and CircularEval plus LLM-assisted extraction measurably fixed real problems, that's earned, not hype. But the paper's own validation of its extractor and its quality-control loop is thinner than the confidence the resulting leaderboard numbers project.
