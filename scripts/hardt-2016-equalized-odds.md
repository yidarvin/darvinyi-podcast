[S] Good, we'll hold you to that. Callum, set the scene. What was the accepted answer before this paper, and why did it break?
[G] The default notion in twenty sixteen was demographic parity: the accept rate has to be equal across a protected group. Formally, the probability of a positive prediction given group zero equals the probability of a positive prediction given group one. The authors are building directly on a critique from Dwork, Hardt, Pitassi, Reingold, and Zemel's twenty twelve paper "Fairness through Awareness," which had already flagged that this is a shaky notion.
[O] Shaky how? On paper it sounds like the obvious fair thing to ask for.
[G] Two failure modes, and the paper leads with both. First, it doesn't actually ensure fairness. You can satisfy demographic parity by accepting qualified people in one group and unqualified people in another, as long as the percentages match. That happens naturally whenever training data is thin for one group. Second, and this is the sharper problem, it can forbid the perfectly accurate predictor. If the true outcome is itself correlated with the protected attribute, which is the ordinary case in credit or admissions data, demographic parity rules out predicting the ground truth exactly, and calls that unfair.
[S] So the criterion can punish you for being right.
[G] Exactly the authors' framing. And enforcing parity anyway can be expensive, which the FICO case study later quantifies precisely.
[O] So what were they actually asking for in a replacement criterion?
[G] Three things, stated explicitly in the introduction. It has to be checkable directly from data, without interpreting individual features or making causal assumptions — they call this property "oblivious." It has to never brand the perfectly accurate classifier as discriminatory. And it needs a constructive way to fix an already-trained score, without re-engineering the whole training pipeline. Nothing on the table in twenty sixteen had all three, with a matching optimality guarantee.
[S] That's a well-posed wish list. Let's hear the definition.
[G] Definition two point one: a predictor Y-hat satisfies equalized odds with respect to protected attribute A and outcome Y if Y-hat and A are independent conditional on Y. In the binary case that unpacks to something very concrete: equal true positive rates across the two groups, and equal false positive rates across the two groups.
[O] So it's letting the prediction depend on the protected attribute, just only through the actual outcome.
[G] That's the elegant part. It doesn't forbid using A as information, it forbids using A as a proxy for Y once you already know Y. And unlike demographic parity, Y-hat equals Y, the perfect predictor, always satisfies it. The criterion gets easier to satisfy as your classifier gets more accurate, not harder.
[S] There's a relaxation too, equal opportunity. What's the actual difference in practice?
[G] Equal opportunity only requires equal true positive rates within the advantaged outcome — people who'd actually pay back the loan get an equal shot at approval, full stop, with no constraint on the false positive side. It's a weaker ask than equalized odds, and as we'll see in the numbers, weaker turns out to be a lot cheaper.
[O] Now the part I actually find clever: how do you get from "here's a criterion" to "here's an algorithm," without retraining anything?
[G] This is section four, and it's genuinely elegant. For a fixed group, every achievable false-positive, true-positive pair you could reach by randomizing on top of your existing classifier forms a convex region — the convex hull of the origin, your classifier's own operating point, its mirror image, and the point one-one. Figure one draws two of these regions, one per group. An equalized-odds classifier has to land in a single point that's in both regions at once — the overlap. Equal opportunity is weaker: the two groups just need to share the same true positive rate, the same horizontal line, and each can otherwise sit on its own more favorable point.
[S] And finding that point isn't some heuristic search.
[G] No, and this is Proposition four point four: it's a linear program in four variables, solvable exactly from nothing but the joint distribution of prediction, group, and outcome. No access to the raw features, no retraining. The paper even notes it could be computed in a privacy-preserving way, using only aggregate statistics.
[O] That's the part that makes this deployable rather than theoretical. What does the theory actually guarantee about how good the result is?
[G] Theorem five point six, and this is the result I'd put right next to the FICO numbers as the paper's strongest contribution. If your starting score is exactly Bayes optimal, meaning it's the best possible accuracy-maximizing predictor, then deriving an equalized-odds classifier from it via this post-processing step gives you the Bayes-optimal classifier among everything satisfying that constraint. And if your starting score only approximates Bayes optimality, within some distance measured by what they call the conditional Kolmogorov distance, the fair classifier's excess loss is bounded by two times the square root of two, times that distance. It's a quantified degradation, not a leap of faith.
[S] Practically, that means you don't need a bespoke fair-training algorithm.
[G] Right — take a good ordinary classifier, run this post-processing step, and you're most of the way to the fairness-constrained optimum. That's the whole selling point of doing this as post-processing rather than retraining.
[O] Okay, theory's beautiful. Show me it surviving contact with real data.
[G] That's section seven, the FICO case study, and it's the part most people remember. The data is just over three hundred thousand TransUnion TransRisk credit scores from two thousand three, ranging from three hundred to eight hundred fifty, with race as the protected attribute across four groups: Asian, white non-Hispanic, Hispanic, and black. The outcome is default: failing to pay a debt for at least ninety days on at least one account within the following eighteen to twenty-four months.
[S] And there's a real lender objective behind the numbers, not an arbitrary accuracy target.
[G] Right. A commonly used FICO cutoff of six hundred twenty corresponds to an aggregate non-default rate of eighty two percent, and the paper studies a lender who's indifferent at exactly that rate — meaning a false positive, giving a loan to someone who defaults, costs about eighty two eighteenths as much as a false negative, denying a loan to someone who wouldn't have defaulted. Against that objective, they compare five policies: max profit, which is unconstrained; race-blind, a single threshold for everyone; demographic parity; equal opportunity; and equalized odds.
[O] Give me the headline numbers.
[G] As a fraction of the maximum achievable profit: race-blind gets ninety nine point three percent. Equal opportunity gets ninety two point eight percent. Equalized odds gets eighty point two percent. Demographic parity gets sixty nine point eight percent. The paper's own line is that equal opportunity fairness costs less than a quarter of what demographic parity costs, in forgone profit, at this operating point.
[S] That's a real number, and it's the strongest empirical argument in the paper. But I want to flag what's doing the work underneath it — the ROC curves.
[G] Right, that's Figure ten, and the authors are careful to call this out themselves. FICO scores are measurably less predictive for black and Hispanic applicants than for white and Asian ones — a lower ROC curve within those groups. The authors attribute that to differences in prediction accuracy in the underlying score, not to differences in actual default behavior. And because equalized odds forces every group down to the worst group's ROC ceiling, that's exactly why it costs so much more than equal opportunity, which lets each group use its own curve.
[O] So the cost of equalized odds is really the cost of the underlying score being unequally accurate across groups, not a cost of fairness itself.
[G] That's a fair reading, and the paper says as much — the gap between equal opportunity and demographic parity would shrink further with a more accurate underlying classifier, since both approach the unconstrained optimum as prediction quality improves.

[O] What's the actual takeaway for someone doing evaluation work today, not fairness research specifically?
[G] The disaggregation habit is the transferable lesson. This paper's whole method is: don't just report an aggregate accuracy number, report it broken out by subgroup, and treat a gap between subgroups as a finding, not noise. That habit generalizes well past race and credit — it's the same instinct behind checking whether an evaluation's headline metric holds up when you slice by language, by demographic, by any axis a system might be quietly worse on. The oblivious framing is also a useful discipline: what can you actually conclude from outputs alone, without pretending you understand the internals? And the honest limitations section is worth imitating too — a good evaluation paper should say plainly what its own metric can't tell you, the way this one does about oblivious tests in general.
