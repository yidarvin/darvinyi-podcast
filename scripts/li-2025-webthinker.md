[O] Take an open thirty-two billion parameter reasoning model, let it decide for itself when to search the web and what to click on next, and its score on G-A-I-A jumps from twenty-two point three to forty-eight point five. That's not a tweak, that's more than doubling.
[S] Sure, but the paper's other headline is that it beats Gemini and Grok's Deep Research systems — and that win only shows up on the one task graded by another language model, not on any benchmark with a hard, checkable answer.
[O] Even so, on the hard, checkable benchmarks, the same open model still nearly doubled its own baseline just by being allowed to click a link instead of stopping at the first page of search results.
[S] Which is a real result. I just want to know how much of that doubling survives once you ask who's doing the grading, and whether a model searching the live web might be finding the benchmark's own answer key along the way.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the litsearch dot darvinyi dot com map every episode.
[S] Today's paper is Web-Thinker: Empowering Large Reasoning Models with Deep Research Capability, by Xiaoxi Li and colleagues at Renmin University of China, the Beijing Academy of Artificial Intelligence, and Huawei Poisson Lab, published at NeurIPS twenty twenty-five.
[O] And joining us to walk through it is Priya, who's spent real time in this one. Priya, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is a fun paper — it's basically an argument that deep research agents have been built backwards, and the fix is refreshingly simple to describe, even if it wasn't simple to build.
[S] Set the stage for us. What's broken with the existing deep research agents that this paper is responding to?
[G] The diagnosis is that large reasoning models like OpenAI's o1, DeepSeek-R1, and Qwen's QwQ are excellent at long chains of thought, but that thinking runs entirely on whatever got baked into the weights during training. Ask one to write a report that needs synthesizing many current web sources, and it just stalls — there's no mechanism for gathering evidence it's never seen.
[O] Which is exactly what R-A-G, retrieval-augmented generation, exists to fix, isn't it?
[G] That's the obvious next move, and it's exactly where the paper says existing open-source systems get stuck. They wrap the reasoning model in a fixed R-A-G workflow — either retrieve once for the query, or decompose it into sub-questions, retrieve for each, and merge. The paper calls out three specific failures there: the search itself is shallow, usually one round of top results with no ability to click through and read further; reasoning and retrieval are decoupled, so the model reasons, then retrieves, instead of searching the instant it hits a gap; and report writing happens all at once at the very end, after retrieval has already stopped, so a half-written section can't go ask for the one more fact it turns out to need.
[S] Meanwhile the systems that actually handle this well — OpenAI's Deep Research, Grok3's DeeperSearch, Gemini's Deep Research — are all closed.
[G] Right, and that's the gap Web-Thinker aims at: a fully open framework where the reasoning model drives the whole process itself, instead of being one component in someone else's fixed pipeline.
[O] Walk us through what Web-Thinker actually does differently, mechanically.
[G] It runs in two modes. The first is Problem-Solving Mode, built around what the authors call the Deep Web Explorer. When the model's main reasoning chain hits a knowledge gap, it spins up the explorer as its own sub-agent, with two primitive actions: a search engine, and a navigation tool that can click links or buttons to go deeper into a page. The explorer runs its own internal reasoning loop — after every result it decides whether to search again or click deeper — and it can recurse like that until it has what it needs. Then, critically, it doesn't hand the main chain a pile of raw pages back. It returns one concise, distilled answer, and the main chain picks up exactly where it left off.
[S] So the delegation is the trick — the main model stays focused on the actual problem, and a sub-process absorbs all the messy back-and-forth of searching.
[G] Exactly, and that's what turns one shallow search into genuine multi-hop web exploration.
[O] And the second mode is for the report-writing side?
[G] Right, Report Generation Mode, using what they call an Autonomous Think-Search-and-Draft strategy. Every page the explorer reads gets accumulated into a document memory. On top of the explorer, the model gets three more tools — draft a section, check the current report, edit the report — and each of those is actually executed by a separate assistant language model, so the main reasoning model just orchestrates at a high level while the assistant handles the detailed text manipulation. When the main model decides it's ready to write, it emits an instruction, the assistant pulls the most relevant documents out of memory, and produces the updated report. Because drafting happens as evidence arrives, instead of all at once after retrieval stops, a half-finished section can trigger exactly the search it needs next.
[S] That's the part I find genuinely clever — a report that can go "wait, I need one more source for this paragraph" while it's still being written.
[G] It is, and the ablations later back up that it's the part doing the most work.
[O] What about the reinforcement learning piece — is that what teaches the model to use all of this well?
[G] That's the third piece, and it's iterative online D-P-O. They sample many reasoning trajectories per query from a mix of complex datasets, and build preference pairs using three rules applied strictly in priority order. First, correctness: a trajectory with the right final answer, or the higher-quality report, always beats one that doesn't, full stop. Second, among two correct trajectories, fewer total tool calls wins — that rewards efficiency. Third, only if both are correct and use the same number of tool calls, the shorter one wins, once its length ratio against the other crosses a threshold. Those pairs train the model with the standard D-P-O objective, and then — this is the "iterative online" part — the updated model re-samples fresh trajectories, builds a new preference set from those, resets the reference policy to itself, and repeats. The main results use two rounds of that loop.
[S] What's the actual backbone model doing all this?
[G] QwQ-32B for the main reasoning, Qwen2.5-Instruct at the same size for the assistant model doing the writing. Search runs over Bing's web search A-P-I, ten results per query, with page content pulled by a crawler called Crawl4AI. Generation runs up to eighty-one thousand, nine hundred twenty tokens, so there's a lot of room for a long chain of searching, clicking, and drafting before the model has to commit to an answer.
[O] Let's get to the headline numbers.
[G] Four complex-reasoning benchmarks: G-P-Q-A, PhD-level science questions; G-A-I-A, general assistant tasks; WebWalker Q-A, deep web navigation; and Humanity's Last Exam, H-L-E, extremely hard cross-discipline questions. On every one, the training-free version of Web-Thinker already beats Search-o-one, the strongest prior open agentic-search system, and the R-L-trained version pushes further still. The two biggest jumps are exactly where you'd expect live retrieval to matter most: G-A-I-A goes from QwQ's own twenty-two point three up to forty-eight point five with the full R-L system, and WebWalker Q-A goes from four point three all the way to forty-six point five.
[S] Those numbers only mean something next to a baseline, though. Where does Web-Thinker land against the closed systems it says it's competing with?
[G] That's where it gets more interesting. On H-L-E, Web-Thinker's R-L version scores fifteen point eight, which does edge out o3-mini on high reasoning effort, at fourteen point zero. But OpenAI's own Deep Research system scores twenty-six point six on that same benchmark — well clear of Web-Thinker. And on G-P-Q-A, Web-Thinker's seventy point seven never catches DeepSeek-R1 at six hundred seventy-one billion parameters, which scores seventy-four point two, or o1-preview at seventy-three point three.
[O] Right, but those are much larger, closed models. Within its own weight class — thirty-two billion, open — it's not close.
[G] That's fair, and it holds up there too. Against Search-o-one specifically, also a thirty-two billion open model, Web-Thinker's R-L version beats it by twenty-one point nine percent relative on G-A-I-A and thirty-six point two percent relative on H-L-E.
[S] And the report-writing side — where the "beats Gemini and Grok" claim actually comes from?
[G] On open-ended research report generation, scored one to ten on completeness, thoroughness, factuality, and coherence, averaged across two judge models, DeepSeek-R1 and GPT-4o, Web-Thinker's R-L version reaches an overall eight point one. That's ahead of Gemini two point zero Deep Research at seven point nine, and well ahead of Grok3 DeeperSearch at six point five.
[O] So the model isn't just answering questions faster, it's producing longer-form output that a couple of other language models rated higher than what two of the biggest labs in the world shipped.
[S] That's the framing I want to come back to in a minute. What about generalizing across model sizes?
[G] They also tested it on DeepSeek-R1 at seven billion, fourteen billion, and thirty-two billion parameters, after a small cold-start fine-tune plus the same R-L recipe. At every size it clears both direct generation and standard R-A-G, and the smallest model shows the largest relative gains — the seven billion version improves one hundred seventy-four percent on G-A-I-A and over four hundred percent on WebWalker Q-A, relative to direct generation with no retrieval at all.
[O] And the ablations — what's actually doing the work here, the architecture or the R-L?
[G] Architecture, clearly. Pulling the Deep Web Explorer out entirely is by far the biggest hit in the whole paper — the complex-reasoning average drops from forty-five point four down to thirty-eight point three, and report quality falls too. Even just turning off link-clicking, leaving search alone, drops the average to forty-two point six. Compare that to the R-L contribution: going from the untrained base model at forty-two point one, to offline D-P-O at forty-three point two, to the full iterative online version at forty-five point four. R-L is a real but secondary polish on top of an architecture that's already doing most of the work.
[O] Okay, I want to make the strongest case for what this means, because I think it's being undersold by how much attention the "beats proprietary systems" line is getting picked apart.
[S] Go ahead, make the case.
[O] The real finding isn't the report-writing score, it's the ablation table. Ripping out the Deep Web Explorer costs more than the entire R-L pipeline gains — that's a clean, well-isolated result. The reason this beats Search-o-one and standard R-A-G isn't a smarter reward function, it's letting the model actually click into pages instead of reading snippets. And that architecture generalizes — it holds at seven billion, fourteen billion, and thirty-two billion parameters, on a totally different backbone family. That's not a fragile result tuned to one specific model.
[S] I'll grant the ablation is clean, and the cross-scale generalization is real evidence. My problem is with how far the abstract stretches "outperforms strong proprietary systems." That claim is true exactly once, on report generation, and it's graded by an L-L-M judge with no reported human validation, on a compressed score band — five point nine to eight point one isn't much room to separate systems. And one of the two judges is GPT-4o, which is also one of the systems whose reports it's scoring. That's a textbook self-preference confound.
[G] That's a legitimate concern, and the paper doesn't run a check for it — no judge-agreement analysis, no swap test, nothing that would tell us whether GPT-4o is systematically friendlier to reports that resemble its own style.
[O] Fair, but even granting that, the verifiable-benchmark story on its own is still strong. Going from QwQ's own twenty-two point three to forty-eight point five on G-A-I-A doesn't need an L-L-M judge to be real — that's Pass at one against ground truth.
[S] Except it's Pass at one only — no pass at k, no variance reported anywhere, at a temperature of point seven, which isn't a deterministic setting. And accuracy itself is adjudicated by Qwen2.5, seventy-two billion, instruct, not exact string match, which adds grader leniency the paper never quantifies.
[G] Both real gaps. But the concern I'd actually put first, ahead of the judge and the grading, is contamination. Web-Thinker is querying the live internet at test time, on public benchmarks — G-P-Q-A, G-A-I-A, WebWalker Q-A, H-L-E — that have been online for a while. A live web agent can, in principle, retrieve the benchmark questions themselves, leaderboard discussions, or solution write-ups. A closed-book model structurally can't do that. The paper doesn't filter search results for this, doesn't audit for it, doesn't even mention it as a risk.
[S] That's the one that worries me most too. I don't think the whole result evaporates — the architecture story is too consistent across model sizes for that — but it means we genuinely don't know how much of the G-A-I-A jump is the model reasoning and searching better, versus the model finding a page that already had the answer.
[O] I'll take that. I still think the architecture finding survives — deep web exploration beats shallow retrieval, and that's shown four different ways. I just can't defend the specific "beats proprietary Deep Research" framing as cleanly as I wanted to going in.
[G] That's roughly where I'd land too. The clean, well-supported claim is: letting a reasoning model drive its own search and drafting, instead of following a fixed pipeline, beats every other open thirty-two billion system, and that's primarily an architecture story, not an R-L story. The claim that needs an asterisk is "rivals proprietary systems" — true on the one subjective, self-graded task, not true on any benchmark with a checkable answer, where OpenAI's Deep Research is still well ahead.
[O] So if the architecture story holds, what actually changes?
[G] It's a genuinely useful open recipe. Anyone building an agent on an open reasoning model now has a concrete, ablated answer for where to spend effort: deep navigation first, R-L second. The paper's own limitations section is honest about what's left — no multimodal input, so it can't read a chart or a screenshot inside its research loop, a fairly small fixed tool set, and no G-U-I-based browsing, so it can only click what's already exposed as a link or button, rather than operate a page the way a person actually would.
[S] For evaluation specifically, this is a preview of a problem that's going to keep showing up. Any benchmark a live-search agent can query becomes a moving target, and this paper doesn't build a contamination filter — honestly, right now almost nobody does. That's going to need the same kind of freshness controls a few benchmarks are starting to adopt.
[G] Agreed, and I'd add that once report quality is being judged by another language model, a self-preference check needs to become as standard as held-out human evaluation already is for other tasks. This paper is an early, useful example of exactly where that gap sits.
[G] If there's one line to take from this paper, it's that the win came from letting the model click deeper into the web and draft as it goes, not from the reinforcement learning wrapped around it — the ablations make that case cleanly.
[O] My takeaway: an open thirty-two billion model roughly doubling its own score on G-A-I-A and WebWalker Q-A, just by being allowed to navigate instead of skim, is a real architectural lesson, and I'd expect it to show up in the next generation of open agents.
[S] Mine's the caution: watch for the asterisk on "beats proprietary systems" — it's true on one self-graded task and not true anywhere there's a checkable answer, and until someone audits contamination on a live-search agent like this, treat the biggest jumps with a little suspicion.
