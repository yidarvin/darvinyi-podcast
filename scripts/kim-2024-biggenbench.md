[G] The complaint is about the evaluation criteria itself, not the models being evaluated. By the time this was written, LLM as judge pipelines, the Chatbot Arena and MT Bench lineage, AlpacaEval, ChatEval, had mostly converged on a small, fixed vocabulary: helpfulness, harmlessness, relevance, level of detail. Every response, on every task, gets scored against that same short list.
[S] What's actually wrong with that, mechanically? Helpfulness is at least a real property.
[G] It's real, but it's not diagnostic. The paper's own running example is a math word problem: given three positive integers x, y, and z where x squared plus y squared plus z squared equals five hundred sixty, find the product x times y times z. A coarse rubric can only ask whether the rationale is logically sound. It has no way to check whether the model actually performed the specific variable substitution steps that problem requires to reach the answer, nine hundred sixty.
[O] So "helpful" tells you the response exists and sounds reasonable, not whether it did the one thing that makes this particular answer correct.
[G] Exactly. There's a second gap the authors flag too, coverage bias. Most generation benchmarks disproportionately test instruction following, because it's easy to write generic criteria for. Capabilities like theory of mind or multi step tool use get comparatively little dedicated evaluation.
[S] Isn't FLASK already trying to fix the granularity problem? I remember it using something like a dozen criteria instead of one.
[G] FLASK is the closest prior work, and Kim and colleagues are explicit about the distinction. FLASK assigns each instance one of twelve fixed domain criteria, applied bottom up, after sampling instances from existing benchmarks. BiGGen Bench is built top down: capability, then task, then instance, then a criterion written specifically for that instance. The bet is that a criterion designed for the instance beats one recycled across many instances, even a more fine grained recycled set.
[O] That's a real design bet, not just "add more criteria."
[O] Walk us through the actual structure. What is a BiGGen Bench instance, concretely?
[G] Four things bundled together: a system message, an input, a reference answer, and a five point Likert rubric where all five score descriptions are written specifically for that instance, not reused. That sits on nine capabilities fixed in advance: instruction following, grounding, planning, reasoning, refinement, safety, theory of mind, tool usage, and multilingualism. Under those nine, they built seventy seven tasks, and under those tasks, seven hundred sixty five individual instances.
[S] Who actually wrote seven hundred sixty five rubrics? That's the part I want scrutinized.
[G] It's a four step, human in the loop pipeline, and it's worth walking through carefully because it matters for the critique later. Step one: eighteen coauthors, each owning one capability, hand crafted twenty five instances across five tasks apiece, while ten native speaking annotators across ten languages wrote multilingual instances separately. The first author then pruned twenty three confusing or unchallenging tasks, landing on seventy seven tasks and three hundred eighty five hand written instances.
[O] Step two must be where it scales up, because three hundred eighty five is well short of seven hundred sixty five.
[G] Right. Step two used a GPT four checkpoint to expand the seed set, but not zero shot. It was shown the human written instances per task as in context demonstrations, generated five candidate instances each time, and kept whichever candidate had the lowest similarity score to what already existed, to push for diversity. That brought the total to seven hundred seventy instances.
[S] So roughly half the final dataset is machine authored rubrics, human reviewed, not human written from scratch.
[G] That's accurate, and hold that thought, it's central later. Step three is cross validation: the same eighteen coauthors each reviewed two capabilities they hadn't written, roughly fifty instances apiece, checking task fit, difficulty, reference answer quality, and rubric quality. Anything one reviewer flagged got revised; anything both reviewers rejected got dropped. That trimmed five instances, landing on the final seven hundred sixty five. Step four collected real human Likert ratings on a subset of response models, through a recruit, qualify, annotate pipeline, which becomes the ground truth used later to validate the LLM judges themselves.
[O] And at evaluation time, how does a response actually get generated and scored?
[G] Each instance's system message and input go to what they call a Response LM. Post trained, instruction tuned or RLHF, models get zero shot prompting. Pre trained base models get something called the URIAL prompt instead, three shot demonstrations, specifically because base models otherwise tend toward what the paper calls accidental translation, answering in English regardless of what language was requested. That's also why multilingual tasks only score post trained models. The response then goes to an Evaluator LM, which reads that instance specific rubric and produces one Likert score from one to five, in a direct assessment format rather than a pairwise comparison.
[S] Why direct assessment instead of pairwise? Pairwise usually correlates better with human preference in other work.
[G] Scalability. Direct assessment means a new response model can be scored once, against the rubric, without re comparing it to every existing model in the pool. In total this runs across one hundred three response models, twenty eight pre trained base models from about one billion to one hundred forty one billion parameters, sixty one post trained open chat models, and fourteen proprietary models, judged by five evaluator models: two separate GPT four checkpoints, Claude three Opus, and two variants of the open source Prometheus two, an eight by seven mixture of experts, one of which was further trained specifically on one GPT four checkpoint's own judgments for this benchmark.
[O] Let's get to numbers. What actually falls out of running one hundred three models through this?
[G] The scaling story is the first big one. That's a genuinely smooth, predictable relationship. Post trained chat models are much messier, correlation point four seven, R squared point two two.
[S] Meaning parameter count alone stops explaining performance once you post train.
[G] That's the read, and it holds by capability too, base model scaling is tightest for tool usage and reasoning, weakest for theory of mind.
