[O] The S-one models. Trained hard on math, and M M L U Pro drops four to six percent. Getting smarter at competition problems made the model dumber at general knowledge.
[S] Which tells you reasoning training isn't reasoning training. It's math training wearing a reasoning costume.
[O] Unless the actual bottleneck was never the domain. It was that nobody could grade a chemistry or finance answer as cheaply as a math answer — and this paper builds the grader.
[S] A learned grader judging free-form answers. That's exactly the kind of thing that looks great in an ablation table and quietly falls apart under scrutiny.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the litsearch site. Today's paper is General-Reasoner, Advancing L L M Reasoning Across All Domains, by Xueguang Ma and colleagues at the University of Waterloo, the Vector Institute, TikTok, and M-A-P, posted in May of twenty twenty-five.
[S] It's on the docket because it names, directly, the thing that's been bugging methodologists about the whole reinforcement-learning-with-verifiable-rewards wave — every open replication of DeepSeek-R1-Zero lives in math and code, and this paper tries to break that ceiling.
[O] Joining us is Maya, who's studied this work closely. Maya, welcome to the show.
[G] Happy to be here — really two contributions stapled together, a dataset and a grader, and both matter more than either alone.
[S] Start with the setup. What is Zero R L, for anyone who hasn't been following this wave closely?
[G] DeepSeek-R1-Zero showed you could take a base language model, no supervised fine-tuning step at all, and train it with reinforcement learning directly, using nothing but a reward signal on the final answer, and reasoning behavior emerges on its own. Chain of thought, self-correction, all of it, without ever showing the model a worked example.
[O] Which triggered a wave of open replications — SimpleRL, DAPO, Open-Reasoner-Zero, DeepScaleR.
[G] Right, and every one of them lives almost entirely in math and code. That's structural, not coincidence — Zero R L needs two things, a lot of training questions, and a cheap, reliable way to check if an answer is right. Math and code are the only domains that hand you both for free, endless competition problems on the web, answers checkable by exact match or a unit test.
[S] So the moment you leave math, both of those advantages disappear.
[G] Exactly. A physics or finance answer might be a LaTeX expression, a vector, a percentage, a sentence with units, and a rule-based checker either rejects it outright or matches it for the wrong reason — that's the actual bottleneck, not that models can't reason outside math, but that nobody can grade it cheaply.
[O] And the S-one result from the cold open is the receipt.
[G] It's the paper's sharpest motivating data point. Train only on math, as S-one and S-one-point-one do, and general knowledge performance on M M L U Pro drops four to six percent. Optimizing hard for one narrow verifiable domain actively degrades the rest.
[S] So walk us through the fix. Two pieces, you said.
[G] A dataset, WebInstruct-verified, and a verifier model, General-Verifier. The data side starts from an existing corpus, WebInstruct, about five million naturally occurring question-answer instructions crawled from places like StackExchange and educational portals — most isn't usable as an R L task, no clean, checkable answer behind it.
[O] So how do you turn five million noisy instructions into two hundred thirty thousand usable ones?
[G] A four-stage filter. First, they trace each entry back to its original web page and re-crawl it, keeping only questions with a clearly human-written answer on the source site. Second, Gemini-one-point-five-Pro extracts the subset with genuinely verifiable short answers, down to roughly a million questions. Third, Gemini-two-Flash tags subject, difficulty, and answer type, used to rebalance away from math by filtering out anything below university level.
[S] And the fourth stage?
[G] The solvability filter, and it's the clever one. For every question, Gemini-two-Flash generates eight candidate chain-of-thought solutions. If all eight fail, the question is probably noise, so it's dropped. If all eight succeed, it's too easy to give a useful R L signal, dropped too. What survives is around two hundred thirty thousand questions, spread across math at about thirty four percent, physics at nearly twenty four percent, then chemistry, business, finance, economics, history, and biology, with answer types running from floats and expressions to lists and matrices.
[O] That's the data half. What does General-Verifier do differently from a rule-based checker?
[G] A rule-based checker only knows string or numeric equality, rejecting answers that are correct but differently phrased. The paper's own example — a parametric line written as "four plus eight t, one plus two t, seventeen minus t" versus the reference boxed as "x equals, y equals, z equals" those same expressions — a rule-based checker calls that wrong. General-Verifier is a compact, one point five billion parameter model, initialized from Qwen two point five Math, fine-tuned on those same Gemini-generated solutions and equivalence judgments. Given the question, the reference answer, and the student's answer, it produces a chain of thought and a binary verdict, equivalent or not.
[S] And that verdict is the reward signal going into R L.
[G] Directly. Plus one if the verifier passes the answer, minus zero point five if no answer can even be extracted, and a small penalty for length mismatch against the reference, to discourage rambling. That reward feeds a standard G R P O loop — sample a group of responses per question, normalize each reward against the group's own mean and standard deviation, update with a clipped policy gradient. No value network, no separate reward model beyond the verifier itself.
[O] And this is trained completely from scratch, true Zero R L?
[G] Yes — straight G R P O from base Qwen two point five, at seven and fourteen billion, and base Qwen three, at four and fourteen billion, no supervised fine-tuning at all. Up to seven hundred steps for Qwen two point five, four hundred for Qwen three, on four nodes of eight H one hundred G P Us each. Worth noting — average response length only grows from about seven hundred tokens to around a thousand, versus DeepScaleR, where traces run to thirty two thousand tokens.
[S] So it's not solving this by making the model think longer.
[O] Let's get to numbers, Maya. Where does this land?
[G] Across twelve reasoning benchmarks — M M L U Pro, G P Q A, Super G P Q A, TheoremQA, B B E H, plus the standard math suite. On Qwen two point five at seven billion, General-Reasoner scores fifty eight point nine percent on M M L U Pro, against forty seven point seven for base and fifty seven for instruct. At fourteen billion it reaches sixty six point six on M M L U Pro, against fifty three point three for base and sixty two point seven for instruct, plus forty three point four on G P Q A and thirty nine point five on Super G P Q A.
[S] Those are solid jumps over the base model. How does it compare to other R L recipes, not just to doing nothing?
[G] It beats SimpleRL, the math-only R L baseline, and Nemotron-CrossThink, the closest all-domain competitor, across M M L U Pro, G P Q A, and Super G P Q A. There's an efficiency story too — General-Reasoner at four billion, on the newer Qwen three backbone, hits sixty two point eight on M M L U Pro, beating the seven billion Qwen two point five version of itself, at fifty eight point nine.
[O] And the model that beats GPT-4o?
[G] The best model, General-Reasoner on Qwen three at fourteen billion, scores fifty six point one on G P Q A and fifty four point four on TheoremQA, ahead of GPT-4o's fifty and forty three point six on those same two benchmarks — Zero R L alone, no distillation from a bigger teacher.
[S] I want to come back to that comparison, because I don't think it tells the whole story. But finish the results first — what about math, since that's supposedly the domain being sacrificed?
[G] That's the cleanest result in the paper. Broadening the data doesn't cost math performance, it improves it — at fourteen billion on Qwen two point five, the diverse-data model averages fifty three point nine across the math suite versus SimpleRL's fifty point seven. A direct ablation confirms it — the same backbone trained on math-only data scores worse on math itself, forty eight point six versus fifty three point nine, and worse on every general benchmark too. Diversity wasn't a trade-off, it was a win.
[O] And the verifier ablation?
[G] Same backbone, Qwen three at four billion, trained a hundred twenty steps two ways, one with the learned verifier, one with the rule-based checker, everything else identical. The learned verifier wins on every metric, sixty point one versus fifty eight point one on M M L U Pro, thirty nine point four versus thirty seven point nine on G P Q A — and it's not a plateau effect, the rule-based run flattens around step sixty while the learned-verifier run keeps climbing through step one twenty. Separately, on fifty thousand answer pairs Gemini-two-Flash judged correct, the rule-based checker agreed only twenty two point two percent of the time, the learned verifier seventy eight point seven.
[S] So the rule-based checker was wrong more often than it was right, on answers a human, or at least Gemini, considered correct.
[O] Here's my case. This is the cleanest kind of empirical win — isolate one variable, data diversity, and it helps every benchmark, including the one it was supposedly trading against. Same for the verifier ablation, same backbone, same steps, only the reward signal changes, and it wins across the board. That's not marketing, that's a controlled experiment doing its job. And the efficiency story is real — a four billion parameter model beating a seven billion one is the kind of thing that actually changes what people deploy.
[S] I'll grant both ablations, they're clean, no complaint there. My complaint is how the headline numbers get framed. "Matches or beats GPT-4o" is true on exactly the two benchmarks picked to say it about, G P Q A and TheoremQA — on M M L U Pro, GPT-4o still leads, seventy four point six to seventy point three. And the big jumps are measured against the base model. Against the already instruction-tuned versions, gains shrink to two to nine points on Qwen two point five, and on the Qwen three fourteen billion backbone it's a wash — General-Reasoner actually scores lower than plain instruct on M M L U Pro, seventy point three against seventy point nine.
[O] That's one metric though, and G P Q A goes the other way on that same comparison.
[S] Sure, but that's my point — it's a mixed, entangled result on the strongest backbone, and the paper never runs the one ablation that would untangle it, holding the backbone fixed and comparing the recipe directly against a matched instruction-tuned baseline. Without that, you can't separate the recipe's contribution from the backbone's.
[G] Fair, and worth being precise about which comparison is doing the work in each headline number.
[S] Second complaint. The verifier's seventy eight point seven percent agreement number, agreement with what, exactly?
[G] With Gemini-two-Flash. The same model that generated the training annotations the verifier learned from.
[S] So the verifier is being graded on how well it imitates its own teacher, not on ground truth. If Gemini systematically misjudges some answer format, the verifier inherits that blind spot and then rewards it during R L, silently. There's no human-graded accuracy number anywhere in the paper.
[O] Is that fair, Maya, is there any independent check on the verifier at all?
[G] Only an indirect one — the math evaluations use a second judge, GPT-4o, for answer equivalence, so there's some cross-validation there, but the twelve non-math benchmarks have no human-graded verifier accuracy reported. Reward hacking, the paper doesn't investigate either — a learned reward model is the classic surface for a policy to game. Training here is short, under seven hundred steps, response length barely grows, which probably keeps hacking from surfacing, but that's an inference, not something measured directly.
[S] Third, and the big one for me — contamination. This dataset is crawled from StackExchange and educational portals, the same kind of sources M M L U Pro and TheoremQA themselves draw from, and nowhere in the paper do they report checking for overlap between training and eval questions.
[G] That's the single most consequential gap I'd flag too. Super G P Q A gets a partial pass — its own construction filtered for questions not findable on Google, so it's somewhat protected by design — but M M L U Pro and TheoremQA get no such protection, and the paper is silent on it.
[O] Even granting that gap, does it undercut the mechanism, or just the size of the headline numbers?
[G] My read, going a little beyond what the paper states, is the latter. The diversity and verifier ablations are internally controlled comparisons, so contamination would have to affect the diverse and math-only conditions differently to explain those results away, which seems unlikely — but it could absolutely be inflating the absolute benchmark numbers everyone will end up quoting.
[S] One more, quickly — the "all domains" framing in the title. Every benchmark here still has a short, checkable answer, multiple choice, a number, a short expression, the same structural shape as math, just relabeled across more subjects. Long-form, open-ended reasoning with no boxed answer is completely untested.
[G] Agreed, that's a scope claim worth pushing back on. The paper is honest this is Zero R L applied to short-answer verifiable questions across many disciplines — genuinely new and useful — but "all domains" in the title promises a little more generality than the evaluation delivers.
[O] Step back. If this holds up, what changes?
[G] It reframes what was blocking verifiable-reward R L outside math — it was never that other domains are unreasonable, it's that grading them was expensive. A compact, purpose-trained verifier is a genuinely portable idea — anyone building an R L V R pipeline for law, medicine, or finance now has a template: mine a diverse question set, train a small judge on the same annotations, and let it stand in for a much bigger judge at inference time.
[S] Which is why the evaluation-methodology gaps matter more here than in a typical paper. If your reward signal is a model instead of a rule, its own reliability is what the whole pipeline depends on — grading that against ground truth, not against the model that trained it, has to be step one for anyone reusing this recipe.
[O] Contamination too. Anyone adapting this to a new domain needs a decontamination pass baked in from day one, not bolted on after the fact.
[G] My one-sentence version — the bottleneck for general-domain reinforcement learning was never data or capability, it was that nobody could cheaply check a free-form answer, and a compact learned verifier turns out to be enough.
[O] Mine — a four billion parameter model beating a seven billion one, on better data and a better reward signal alone, is the most concretely useful result here, that's efficiency you can actually deploy.
