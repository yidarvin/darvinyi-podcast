[G] Right, but Acemoglu and Restrepo are blunt about it. A classifier can tell you a truck-driving job is technically automatable. It can't tell you whether truck drivers actually lose their jobs once markets and downstream industries adjust.
[S] So the fix is to stop asking a classifier and start watching an actual technology diffuse.
[G] Exactly. Industrial robots turn out to be a good candidate. The International Federation of Robotics has had a precise definition since the early nineties, an automatically controlled, reprogrammable, and multipurpose machine. [O] And robots weren't a hypothetical during that window. They were actually showing up.
[G] The US and Western European robot stock roughly quadrupled between nineteen ninety-three and two thousand seven, concentrated hard in a few industries. That gives the paper what a feasibility score never had, a real technology, with a measurable dose, a measurable date, and regions that got more or less of it.
[O] Walk us through the actual design, Julian. How do you turn some regions got more robots into a causal estimate?
[G] The paper builds a task-based model, extending earlier work by Zeira, by Acemoglu and Autor, and the authors' own earlier framework, where robots and workers compete over tasks inside an industry. Robots create what they call a displacement effect, directly replacing workers on tasks those workers used to do. That's offset by a productivity effect, cheaper output raises labor demand elsewhere in the economy.
[S] And the model collapses that into something you can actually regress.
[G] It does. Both effects reduce to a single sufficient statistic at the level of the local labor market, a commuting zone's exposure to robots. It's a Bartik-style, shift-share measure. For each commuting zone, you take its nineteen ninety employment share in every industry, and weight that by how much that industry's robot use grew nationally. A zone is exposed if it started out with a lot of workers in industries that later adopted a lot of robots.
[O] Which sounds clean, but I can already guess the objection.
[S] The objection is endogeneity. If an industry adopts robots because of some local shock, a union wage push, a regional recession, then US robot exposure isn't independent of the very labor demand shocks you're trying to isolate.
[G] Right, and that's the paper's central empirical move. They instrument US industry-level robot adoption with the adoption trend in five European countries, Denmark, Finland, France, Italy, and Sweden, the Euro Five. Those countries are further along the robotics frontier, largely for demographic reasons, aging societies with fewer middle-aged production workers adopt robots faster, a link the authors establish in a companion paper. US robot usage tracked the Euro Five average closely across nineteen ninety-three to twenty fourteen, running about twenty percent lower throughout.
[O] So the logic is, European adoption tells you how technologically ready an industry is to automate, purged of anything happening locally in the US.
[G] That's the idea, and it's a well-worn identification strategy in this literature.
[S] What's actually in the data, mechanically?
[G] A yearly survey of robot suppliers covering fifty countries, about ninety percent of the world robot market, across nineteen industries.
