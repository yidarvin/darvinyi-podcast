[O] Here's a sentence that should stop any ML researcher cold. Take a raw base model, one that has never seen a single instruction-tuned example, train it with nothing but a plus one for a right answer and a zero for a wrong one, and watch it start double-checking its own work.
[S] Here's the sentence I'd put right next to it. Nearly every open attempt to reproduce that trick ran on one specific model family that, as a base model, already double-checks its own work before any reinforcement learning ever touches it.
[O] Which is exactly the hole this paper drives a truck through. Ten base models, not one convenient family, and the self-checking aha moment behaves completely differently depending on which model you start with.
[S] And in at least one case, the response just gets longer while getting dumber, which is exactly the failure mode you'd miss if all you're tracking is word count.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a guest scholar take apart one paper from the litsearch atlas. I'm the optimist.
[S] And I'm the skeptic. Today's paper is SimpleRL-Zoo: Investigating and Taming Zero Reinforcement Learning for Open Base Models in the Wild, by Weihao Zeng, Yuzhen Huang, Qian Liu, and colleagues at H-K-U-S-T, TikTok, and Meituan, posted in March twenty twenty-five.
[O] Joining us is Maya, who's spent real time in this paper's tables and appendices. Welcome, Maya.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is one of those papers where the headline result is almost the least interesting part. It's the negative result buried in the framing that actually matters.
[S] Let's set up why this paper needed to exist. What claim is it responding to?
[G] DeepSeek-R1. The authors showed that starting from a pretrained base model, no supervised fine-tuning at all, and training it with a dead simple reward, correct answer plus one, wrong answer zero, long chain-of-thought reasoning and self-reflection emerge on their own. They called this zero R-L training, and the emergence of reflection got nicknamed the aha moment.
[O] Which is a genuinely wild claim if it's true. Reasoning behavior showing up from a bare reward signal, with no one ever showing the model what good reasoning looks like.
[G] Right, and that demonstration ran on DeepSeek-V3, six hundred seventy-one billion parameters. It was completely open whether that survives on the smaller, weaker open models everyone else actually trains.
[S] And the open-source reproduction wave didn't really test that, from what I understand.
[G] It didn't. Almost every reproduction converged on the Qwen two point five family, which, thanks to heavy synthetic-data pretraining, already follows instructions and shows backtracking and verification before any R-L touches it. So a Qwen-only study can't tell whether R-L creates reasoning behavior or just amplifies something already baked in.
[O] That's a real confound, not a nitpick.
[G] It compounds with a second problem. Most of these studies tracked exactly two numbers, accuracy and response length. A longer response can be genuine deliberation, or it can be repetitive nonsense. Length alone can't tell you which.
[O] So walk us through what this paper actually did differently, Maya.
[G] Ten base models, deliberately spanning families and sizes: Llama-3.1-8B, Mistral-v0.1-7B, Mistral-Small-24B, DeepSeek-Math-7B, four sizes of Qwen two point five from zero point five billion up to thirty-two billion, plus Qwen two point five Math seven B. Same recipe, same hyperparameters, across all ten.
[S] Same hyperparameters across a zero point five billion model and a thirty-two billion model seems aggressive.
[G] It is, deliberately. The point is what a bare-bones recipe does, not squeezing the best number out of each model. The only accommodation: the weakest instruction-followers get a simpler "reason step by step" prompt, because the authors found the complex prompt caused early instability.
[O] What's the actual training algorithm?
[G] G-R-P-O, Group Relative Policy Optimization. It's critic-free: it samples a group of responses to the same question and normalizes each one's reward against that group's own mean and standard deviation. Training data is only GSM8K and MATH, roughly eight thousand problems, and the reward is pure correctness: plus one if right, zero if wrong.
[S] No format reward at all?
[G] None. No penalty for failing to wrap the final answer in the right formatting. The authors argue that's a deliberate and important choice, and we'll get to why. One more detail worth naming: they drop G-R-P-O's original length-normalization term, which otherwise biases the objective toward longer outputs regardless of quality.
[S] I want to push on that. Isn't removing a length-related term from the objective just another lever that could inflate length, from the opposite direction?
[G] Fair pushback, but it's the opposite. The original term divides the reward by response length, favoring being short regardless of correctness. Dropping it just makes the reward correctness-only again — no bonus for length. Growth that shows up comes from G-R-P-O's group-relative advantage rewarding thorough correct answers, not this term.
[O] And the instrumentation is really the contribution here, more than the training recipe itself, right?
[G] Exactly, that's the real methodological move. Beyond accuracy and length, they add a Reasoning Behavior Ratio: G-P-T-four-o, following a published cognitive-behavior framework, classifies whether each response performs backtracking, verification, subgoal setting, or enumeration — labeled behavior, not word count. They also track a Clip Ratio, the share of responses truncated at the context limit, a tell for repetitive collapse, and an Average Stopped Length counting only normally-ended responses, so runaway truncated outputs can't quietly inflate the average.
[S] That's a meaningfully better instrument than counting tokens.
[G] It's the whole point of the paper. Without it, you cannot tell the difference between a model that learned to think longer and a model that learned to ramble longer.
[O] Give us the headline number first, Maya.
[G] With only about eight thousand training problems, zero R-L lifts every one of the ten base models, and the gains reach competition-level benchmarks, not just the easier training-distribution ones. Qwen two point five Math seven B goes from a thirty-seven point two average across six benchmarks up to fifty-nine point five. Mistral-Small twenty-four B goes from twenty-seven point six up to forty-nine point six.
[S] Those are big jumps for eight thousand problems.
[G] The weakest starting point moves the most in relative terms. DeepSeek-Math seven B goes from eleven point three up to twenty-nine point two, more than doubling, and after just eighty iterations its accuracy had already more than tripled, with responses growing from around three hundred tokens to over twelve hundred.
[O] Now the part I actually care about most. Does the aha moment show up everywhere, or not?
[G] Not everywhere, and that's the headline finding. For Qwen two point five seven B and thirty-two B, verification and backtracking are already high on step one and barely move — length grows, but no new reasoning behavior emerges, so length there is basically a red herring. The real aha moment, reflection appearing from nothing, shows up in models that didn't start with it: in Mistral-Small twenty-four B, verification and backtracking climb from nearly zero percent to roughly fifty percent.
[S] And that's the first time anyone's shown that outside the Qwen family?
[G] That's the paper's claim: observing it for the first time in small models outside Qwen, specifically Llama-3.1-8B and DeepSeek-Math seven B. Then there's the cautionary case that justifies the whole framework: Mistral seven B version zero point one also inflates response length substantially, but the outputs are incoherent, mixed-language gibberish, and its clip ratio is high. Length went up. Reasoning did not.
[S] "First time observed" is doing a lot of work for a preprint that's what, four or five months old at this point. That's a strong framing for something this early.
[G] It's the authors' framing, and I'd flag it the same way. What's solid is the behavior-ratio measurement itself, that verification climbs from near zero in these specific models. Whether it's truly unprecedented across the whole literature is a harder claim to fully verify.
[O] Which is exactly the case a length-only metric would have called a success.
[G] Precisely.
[S] What about the reranking objection, DeepSeekMath's old argument that R-L just reshuffles answers the base model could already produce, without expanding what it can actually do?
[G] The paper pushes back with pass at k. On Mistral-Small twenty-four B, the gap between base and R-L-trained model persists out to large k — thirteen to thirty absolute points as k rises to one hundred twenty-eight. By iteration one hundred, the model's pass at one, twenty-six point four, already beats the base model's pass at eight, nineteen point four, and that gap widens rather than closes.
[S] That's a real answer to the reranking critique, not just a restatement of accuracy going up.
[G] The authors read it as evidence that R-L is expanding fundamental reasoning ability, not just concentrating probability on outputs the base model could already sample. We'll come back to how settled that actually is.
[O] There's also a generalization result worth mentioning before we get to what breaks.
[G] Right. Trained on only eight thousand math problems, the models improve on completely out-of-domain benchmarks: I-F-Eval for instruction following, M-M-L-U for general knowledge, and G-P-Q-A Diamond for graduate-level chemistry, physics, and biology questions. Mistral-Small twenty-four B's average across those three goes from twenty-five point zero up to fifty-five point three.
[S] Let's get to what actually breaks this, Maya, because that's the part I'd actually use.
[G] Three failure modes. First, strict format rewards: penalize a response for not wrapping its answer correctly, and weaker models like Llama-3.1-8B chase the format, never learn to answer, and collapse. Even Qwen two point five seven B degrades later under it. Second, data difficulty has to match the model. Mistral seven B version zero point one collapses on hard data, earning almost no positive reward, so length balloons with no accuracy gain — Qwen two point five seven B is the mirror image, shrinking on easy data because it needs harder problems to reason deeper.
[O] And the third?
[G] A traditional S-F-T cold start hurts. The pre-R1 default was fine-tuning on short chain-of-thought data before R-L. Using a short-CoT dataset called NuminaMath as a cold start for Mistral twenty-four B, more S-F-T steps meant a lower final accuracy ceiling — at five hundred steps, verification and enumeration behaviors actively decline later in training. Imitating short reasoning traces first suppresses the free exploration R-L needs.
[S] So the pre-R1 conventional wisdom, warm up with S-F-T first, is actively counterproductive here.
[G] That's what this paper finds, yes, with the caveat that it's specifically short chain-of-thought S-F-T. The authors note that long chain-of-thought S-F-T might behave differently, and they don't fully settle that here.
[O] Okay, let's argue about what this means. My case first: this paper should put the "it's just a Qwen artifact" objection to bed. Ten families, ten sizes, and the core finding, reflection emerging from a bare correctness reward with zero supervision, replicates in models that start from nothing. That's the DeepSeek-R1 claim surviving contact with a representative sample, not one convenient model.
[S] I'll grant the sampling is real progress, a legitimate upgrade over the Qwen-only literature. But three things keep me from calling this settled. First, the AIME numbers are noisier than the headline table admits — only thirty problems, so one question is worth three point three percentage points. The paper reports both pass at one and an average across thirty-two runs: Qwen two point five Math seven B scores forty point zero on pass at one but only twenty-four point zero on the thirty-two-run average, and the headline "average" column is built from that noisier number.
[G] That's accurate, it's a real property of the table, not a misreading. To be fair to the authors, they do report the thirty-two-run average transparently right next to it, so a careful reader can see the gap. But yes, the single headline column that gets quoted most is the more volatile one.
[O] Fair, but that's a presentation quibble, not a finding that goes away. Mistral-Small twenty-four B's jump from verification and backtracking near zero to fifty percent isn't an AIME artifact. That's a completely different benchmark.
[S] Which brings me to my second concern, and I want to push you directly on this one, Maya. That fifty percent number comes from G-P-T-four-o classifying behaviors, not a human reading the traces. "Verification rose to fifty percent" is a classifier's output, not a ground truth.
[G] The authors anticipated that objection — there's a human-validation appendix checking G-P-T-four-o's labels against human annotation, which is the right move. But the headline emergence curves still inherit whatever biases that judge has, and an emergence story resting on an LLM judge is worth flagging every time, even a validated one.
[O] Okay, I'll take that point, it's a real caveat, not just skepticism for its own sake.
[S] Third, and this is the one I care about most: the "not just reranking" claim is measured only out to a k of one hundred twenty-eight. A later paper, Yue and colleagues, "Does Reinforcement Learning Really Incentivize Reasoning Capacity Beyond the Base Model," argues that at large enough k, the base model catches back up — meaning R-L sharpens which answers get sampled toward, without expanding what the model can reach.
[G] That's a real disagreement in the literature, and it uses this very paper's own models as its subject. I'd call reranking genuinely open rather than resolved by either side. SimpleRL-Zoo's evidence is real at the k they tested; it just isn't the last word.
[O] I still think the weight of evidence favors genuine capability growth over just reshuffling, especially combined with the generalization results. Improving on I-F-Eval and G-P-Q-A from eight thousand math problems isn't something a pure reranking story explains easily.
[S] That's actually a good point I hadn't fully weighed. Pure reranking of math answers shouldn't transfer to chemistry and physics questions. I'll concede that one moves me toward your side.
[G] If I'm scoring it: the "aha moment is model-dependent, not universal" finding is the most solid result in the paper, it survives everything we just discussed. The reranking claim is real evidence, not proof, and stays contested. And the headline accuracy table should be read alongside the thirty-two-run averages, not the flashier single-run column.
[O] Step back for a second. What does this mean beyond zero R-L specifically?
[G] The broadest lesson is about evaluation practice, not just R-L. Response length was the field's cheapest proxy for reasoning depth, and this paper shows it fails both ways: flat while reasoning grows underneath it, in Qwen's case, ballooning with none behind it, in Mistral seven B's case. Any claim that a method "taught a model to reason" leaning on length or accuracy alone deserves the same skepticism this paper applies to itself.
[S] Which is a good discipline for benchmark work generally. Track the mechanism, not just the outcome metric, and validate your automated judge against humans before you trust its curve.
[O] And practically, if you're actually trying to run zero R-L on a base model that isn't Qwen, this paper is close to a checklist: skip the strict format reward, match your data's difficulty to the model, and don't warm-start with short-CoT S-F-T.
[G] If I had to leave you with one line from the paper itself: the aha moment is real, but it's a property of specific base models catching up to a reasoning style they didn't already have, not a universal side effect of reinforcement learning, and response length alone will lie to you about which is happening.
[O] My takeaway: the fact that reflection emerges from scratch in Llama and DeepSeek-Math, not just Qwen, is the strongest evidence yet that this isn't a one-model party trick. It's a trainable capability with real conditions attached.
[S] Mine: trust the thirty-two-run averages over the flashier single-run column, remember the reranking question is still open, and always ask what your automated judge is actually measuring before you build a headline around it.
