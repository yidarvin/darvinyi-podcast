[S] Every expert this paper asked said somewhere between thirty-eight and thirty-nine percent faster. The developers themselves guessed twenty-four percent faster.
[O] And the actual result, measured with a stopwatch and a randomized trial?
[S] Nineteen percent slower. Not slower by a little. And here's the part that should really bother you: after living through the slowdown for weeks, the developers still walked away believing they'd been sped up by twenty percent.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio. I'm the optimist, and I want to say up front, this result does not spook me the way it seems to spook everyone else. There's a very specific setting doing a lot of work here.
[S] I'm the skeptic, and for once I'm the one who thinks the headline undersells how solid this is. This is one of the tightest field experiments on A-I and coding anyone has run.
[O] The paper is "Measuring the Impact of Early-2025 A-I on Experienced Open-Source Developer Productivity," from Joel Becker, Nate Rush, Beth Barnes, and David Rein at METR, posted in July twenty twenty-five.
[S] And joining us is Julian, who's spent a lot of time inside this study's design and its appendix. Julian, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. I'll say at the outset, this isn't an author's paper for me to defend or attack, it's one I've read closely because it's genuinely unusual in what it measured and how carefully it measured it.
[O] So set the stage, Julian. Why did METR feel like they needed to build a whole new randomized controlled trial instead of just reading the existing literature?
[G] Because the existing literature all pointed the same direction, and the authors thought that was an artifact of what was being measured. Sida Peng and colleagues, the GitHub Copilot study we covered on an earlier episode, found a fifty-six percent speedup, but the task was building a small H-T-T-P server from scratch, self-contained and plausibly similar to a model's training data. Other studies found gains too, but on outcome measures like lines of code written, commits made, or pull requests opened.
[S] And the problem with those outcome measures is they're gameable, even by accident.
[G] Exactly. If an A-I assistant makes you write more verbose code that does the same thing, your lines-of-code count goes up without your actual output changing. If it encourages you to split one unit of work into three smaller pull requests, your pull-request count goes up for free. None of those studies fixed the unit of work before treatment was assigned. So the authors built an experiment where the outcome is locked in before anyone knows whether they'll have A-I or not.
[O] That's the design move that makes this different from basically everything before it.
[G] It is, and I'd add a second thing the authors emphasize just as much: realism. Prior lab studies use short, self-contained, artificial tasks. METR instead recruited developers who are the actual maintainers of large, mature open-source repositories, and had them work on real backlog issues from those exact repositories.
[S] Not toy problems. Their own bug reports, their own feature requests.
[G] Right. On average these are repositories with twenty-three thousand stars, over a million lines of code, roughly ten years old, with hundreds of contributors and strict review standards. One contribution guideline the authors quote ends with, "the motivation for enforcing process is to ensure that all code contributions meet a certain quality threshold." That's the bar developers were clearing before A-I entered the picture.
[O] Walk us through the mechanics, Julian. How do you randomize something as messy as real day-to-day development work?
[G] Sixteen developers, each a genuine long-term maintainer, contribute a combined two hundred forty-six real issues from their own repositories. Before anything is assigned to a condition, each developer forecasts how long that specific issue would take both with and without A-I. Only after that forecast is recorded does a coin flip assign the issue to A-I-allowed or A-I-disallowed.
[S] So the forecast exists purely as a difficulty control, not as the headline number.
[G] Correct, and that matters for how the regression works. The authors estimate the percentage change in completion time using a log-linear regression of implementation time on treatment, controlling for the log of the pre-registered forecast. That control is what turns a raw thirty-four percent gap in average completion times into the reported nineteen percent, because A-I-allowed issues happened, by chance, to draw slightly harder after randomization.
[O] Who are these sixteen developers, concretely? I want to make sure we're not talking about junior engineers here.
[G] Typically over a decade of professional experience, and on average five years and about fifteen hundred commits on the specific repository they're working in, which is fifty-nine percent of that repository's entire lifetime. Not people encountering the codebase for the first time. They're paid one hundred fifty dollars an hour, ninety-three percent had used large language models before, but only forty-four percent had used Cursor, which is what most of them ended up on here — Cursor Pro with Claude three-point-five or three-point-seven Sonnet, whatever was frontier between February and June of twenty twenty-five.
[S] So most of them are learning a new tool live, mid-study.
[G] Most of them, yes, and the authors do check whether that hurts the result. No clear learning curve shows up across the first thirty to fifty hours of Cursor use, though that's a small sample, so I wouldn't lean on it too hard.
[O] And how do they get from "nineteen percent slower" to actually understanding why?
[G] This is the part I find most impressive. They collect screen recordings of developers working, then manually hand-label one hundred forty-three hours of that footage, twenty-nine percent of all developer-hours in the study, at roughly ten-second resolution. After filtering out broken footage, cheating, or a big mismatch between self-reported and recorded time, seventy-four issues and eighty-four hours remain for the fine-grained analysis.
[S] That's an enormous amount of manual coding effort for what could have just been a single regression coefficient.
[G] It's the difference between reporting a number and actually explaining a mechanism, and I think that effort is exactly why this paper is worth the attention it's getting.
[O] Let's get to the results, then. Julian, give us the headline number properly, in context.
[G] Across the study, developers completed one hundred thirty-six issues with A-I allowed and one hundred ten with it disallowed. The regression-based estimate is that A-I-allowed issues took nineteen percent longer. And this isn't a few catastrophic outlier tasks dragging up an average — the slowdown shows up across nearly every quantile of the completion-time distribution. It's a broad, consistent effect, not a tail event.
[S] Now the forecasting result, because for me that's actually the more damning half of the paper.
[G] Before the study, developers forecast that A-I would cut completion time by twenty-four percent. After finishing the study, having lived through the actual slowdown, they estimated post hoc that A-I had sped them up by twenty percent. Their beliefs barely moved, even though the ground truth pointed the opposite direction the entire time.
[O] But their forecasts weren't just noise, right? I remember reading they were still tracking something real.
[G] That's an important nuance. The correlation between a developer's forecast for an issue and how long it actually took is point six four for A-I-allowed issues and point five nine for A-I-disallowed issues. So they're genuinely well-calibrated on relative task difficulty, which issue will take longer than which. They just have the sign of A-I's effect flipped, and flipped confidently.
[S] And it's not just the developers. Outside experts did worse.
[G] Considerably worse. Thirty-four economics experts predicted a thirty-nine percent speedup, and fifty-four machine-learning experts predicted thirty-eight percent. Both groups were given detailed information about the study design and still overshot even further than the people actually doing the work.
[O] So what were developers actually doing differently, minute to minute, when A-I was allowed?
[G] Less time actively writing code, less time reading or searching documentation. In its place, three new categories appear: prompting the A-I, waiting on it to generate, and reviewing or cleaning up what it produced — roughly nine percent of total time on reviewing and cleaning, about four percent on waiting. Developers accepted fewer than forty-four percent of the A-I's suggestions outright, and most reported making substantial edits to the rest.
[S] There's a quote from one developer I keep coming back to.
[G] There are several good ones. One developer wrote that the A-I "made some weird changes in other parts of the code that cost me time to find and remove," and concluded the refactor needed for that particular pull request was, in their words, "too big," and that the A-I "introduced as many errors as it fixed." Another said flatly they'd "wasted at least an hour first trying to solve a specific issue with A-I" before doing it manually.
[O] Are there positive quotes too? I don't want to leave the impression this was uniformly bad.
[G] Plenty. One developer said Cursor "found a helper test function that I didn't even know existed" inside their own codebase. Several liked it for boilerplate, for unfamiliar APIs, for a first encounter with something like Git hooks. The paper isn't a story of A-I being useless, it's a story of the net effect, in this specific setting, being negative.
[S] Let's get to the factor analysis, because that's where the "why" actually lives.
[G] The authors go in with twenty-one candidate explanations for the slowdown, drawn from interviews, surveys, screen recordings, and subset analyses, and they sort them by the evidence. Five factors have real supporting evidence, ten come back mixed or unclear, and six are actively contradicted by the data.
[O] Give us the five that actually held up.
[G] Over-optimism about A-I's usefulness itself, which we've already covered. High developer familiarity with the repository, meaning slowdown is actually worse on issues developers know best, not better. The size and complexity of these mature repositories, which developers report genuinely limits how well A-I performs. Low reliability of the A-I's output, tied to that sub-forty-four-percent acceptance rate. And implicit repository context, the tacit knowledge a decade-long maintainer carries that an A-I model simply doesn't have access to.
[S] And the six they ruled out matter just as much to me, because those are exactly the ways this result could have been a mirage.
[G] Agreed. They checked whether the IDE switch to Cursor explained it — developers on comparable editors still show a slowdown, around twenty-four percent. They checked cheating or under-using A-I — it was used in all but sixteen point four percent of labeled allowed cases, with only three cheating instances across fifty-four recordings. They checked issue dropout, alternative outcome measures, alternative estimators, and non-frontier model usage. All six come back clean.

Sixteen elite maintainers on their own decade-old repositories is about as narrow a setting as you could design.

[O] So who's ahead after that exchange?
[G] I'd score it as: the optimist is right that this doesn't generalize to most software development, on the authors' own account, and the skeptic is right that the calibration failure is real evidence independent of scope, and that we shouldn't over-read the twenty-one-factor breakdown as more than exploratory. Both of those can be true at the same time, and the paper is honestly built to let them both be true.
[S] One more thing I want on the record before we move to implications. The currency question. These are February-through-June twenty twenty-five tools. Cursor Pro with Claude three-point-five and three-point-seven Sonnet. That's already dated by the time most people hear this.
[G] It is, and the authors flag that directly. Higher-reliability models, lower latency, more inference compute, or repository-specific fine-tuning could plausibly flip the sign here. They also mention preliminary, forthcoming evidence that fully autonomous agents on Claude three-point-seven Sonnet can often implement the core functionality of an issue on several of these repositories, even though they still miss documentation, fail linting, or skip tests. The ceiling is clearly moving.
[O] Which is exactly why I don't read this as a verdict on A-I coding tools. I read it as a very well-measured snapshot of one specific moment, in one specific setting.
[S] I'll take that snapshot framing, with one condition attached: it has to be beaten with the same rigor it was measured with. A pre-registered outcome, real screen recordings, a real randomization. Not a vendor benchmark, not a survey of developers who feel faster.
[G] That's the right bar. It connects to something bigger than this paper: coding benchmarks look impressive, and this is some of the best field evidence we have that benchmark performance and real-world uplift aren't the same claim. The gap between a model's score on a fixed evaluation and what it does inside a decade-old, one-million-line codebase is exactly what this paper measures.
