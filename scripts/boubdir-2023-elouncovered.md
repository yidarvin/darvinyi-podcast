[S] And I'm the skeptic. Today's paper is "Elo Uncovered: Robustness and Best Practices in Language Model Evaluation," by Meriem Boubdir, Edward Kim, Beyza Ermis, Sara Hooker, and Marzieh Fadaee at Cohere for AI and Cohere, posted to arXiv in November twenty twenty-three.
[O] Joining us is Ottavia, a visiting scholar who has studied this paper closely. Ottavia, welcome. Why does a rating system built for chess even need a memo like this one?
[G] Thanks for having me. Because by late twenty twenty-three, Elo had quietly become the default scoring layer for human-preference evaluation of language models — Chatbot Arena included, at the time — and nobody had rigorously checked whether the assumptions Elo makes about chess actually survive the trip to LLMs.
[S] Let's set the scene first. Chess Elo was designed in nineteen seventy-eight by Arpad Elo. What is it actually doing, mechanically?
[G] Two players each have a rating. Before a game, you compute an expected score for each from the gap between their ratings, using a logistic curve, so a four hundred point advantage implies ten-to-one odds. After the game, the winner's rating moves up and the loser's moves down, scaled by how surprising the result was.
[O] And that's exactly the shape of an "A versus B" LLM comparison — a human or a judge model picks a winner, and you feed that into the same update.
[G] Precisely, which is why Chatbot Arena adopted it, and it's why the paper cites a long list of alignment and instruction-tuning work that used paired human-preference comparisons in a similar spirit.
[S] So what's the catch? Comparing chess players and comparing frozen model weights don't sound like the same problem to me.
[G] They aren't. A chess player's skill drifts across a season — Elo was built to track that drift over a bounded, roughly sequential schedule. A language model's weights are frozen at evaluation time. There's no skill to track. Comparisons can happen in any order, even in parallel, and nothing about which model is actually better should depend on which vote a system happened to log first.
[O] So the paper isn't attacking Elo's chess use case. It's asking whether the same machine, pointed at a fundamentally different kind of competitor, still tells the truth.
[G] Exactly, and they frame that as two testable axioms. Reliability: a ranking shouldn't depend on match order, or swing wildly with hyperparameter choices. Transitivity: if A beats B and B beats C, A should rank above C. Neither had been rigorously checked for Elo applied to language models before this paper, despite it already running real leaderboards.
[S] Walk us through the actual mechanics before we get to what breaks.
[G] The expected score for model A is one over one plus ten to the power of the rating gap divided by four hundred. After the match, A's new rating is the old rating plus a K-factor, times the difference between the actual outcome — one or zero — and that expected score.
[O] The K-factor is the dial that controls how much a single match can move you.
[G] Right, and it's a real gap the authors point at: chess has established conventions, sixteen for experienced players, thirty-two for novices. There's no established K-factor for comparing language models. The authors treat it as an open hyperparameter and stress-test it directly.
[S] How do you even stress-test ordering without paying for fresh human votes for every scenario you want to try?
[G] They build a synthetic testbed first. Fix a true win probability for model A over model B — they sweep fifty-one percent, fifty-five percent, and sixty percent in the main results, with more skewed probabilities up to ninety percent covered in an appendix. Draw one thousand match outcomes as independent trials at that probability. Both models start at a rating of fourteen hundred.
[O] And the clever part is they don't generate new outcomes for each test.
[G] Right. They take that same one thousand outcomes and reshuffle them into different permutations — from a single ordering up to ten thousand — and replay the identical set of results in a different sequence each time. If Elo were truly order-invariant, the final ratings shouldn't care which permutation you played.
[S] And separately they vary the K-factor.
[G] Yes — one, eight, sixteen, thirty-two, sixty-four — across that same probability grid, holding everything else fixed, to isolate the hyperparameter effect from the ordering effect.
[O] What about transitivity? You can't stress-test that with just two models.
[G] They construct four three-model scenarios. One where A dominates B and B dominates C. One where A dominates B, but B and C are near-tied. One where A and B are near-tied, and B dominates C. And one where all three are close to each other. Each time, they check whether the Elo-implied ordering — A above B above C — actually falls out of the final ratings.
[S] All of that is still simulation. Did they validate it against anything real?
[G] They did, and this matters — it isn't just synthetic. They reuse human-annotated data from the authors' own prior work on prompt prioritization: four hundred prompts, one hundred each from SODA, CommonsenseQA, CommonGen, and AdversarialQA, all part of the Public Pool of Prompts corpus. The comparisons pit Flan-T5 models against Dolly models. One pairing is a decisive win — Flan-t5-xxl over Flan-t5-xl. The other, Dolly-v2-7b against Dolly-v2-12b, is a near coin flip. That gives them one easy case and one hard case, on real annotations, to check whether the synthetic story generalizes.
[O] And ties, in all of this?
[G] Excluded throughout, in both the synthetic and the real-world data. The paper states that plainly — it's a deliberate scope decision, not an oversight, but it's worth flagging now, because we'll come back to it.
[S] Give us the headline finding. What actually changes between one sequence and many?
[G] At a win probability of sixty percent or higher, a single sequence already gives a fairly stable, correctly-ordered rating. But right around fifty-one percent — genuinely close models, which is the common real-world case — a single sequence leaves the two models' Elo traces frequently intertwined. There's no reliable separation between them. You need on the order of one hundred permutations averaged together before the ranking stabilizes, and the instability plateaus once you cross that threshold.
[O] So more permutations is basically free insurance against the noise.
[G] It buys you a lot, but not everything — hold that thought for the K-factor result.
[S] Which is?
[G] For a single sequence at that fifty-one percent probability, the final Elo-score gap between the two models already leans slightly the wrong way, even at a K-factor of one — about three points in favor of the model constructed to be worse. Push the K-factor to sixty-four, same outcomes, same order, and that gap explodes past a hundred points in the wrong direction. The weaker model reads as decisively better, from the hyperparameter alone.
[O] That's from hyperparameter choice, holding the actual match results completely fixed.
[G] Completely fixed. Averaging over one hundred permutations flips the sign back to the correct direction, but it doesn't erase the sensitivity — the gap still ranges from about fourteen to twenty-four points across those same K-factors, a swing of more than seventy percent relative to the smallest value.
[S] What about transitivity? Did the constructed hierarchies actually hold?
[G] Two of the four scenarios showed violations, both flagged with an asterisk in the paper's own table, both at a K-factor of one with one hundred permutations averaged. In the scenario built as "A clearly beats B, and B and C are near-tied," C ends up rated higher than B, inverting the near-tie the construction intended. In the scenario built as "A and B are near-tied, and B clearly beats C," B pulls decisively ahead of A instead of staying close to it — again inverting an intended near-tie. Raising the K-factor to sixteen restored the expected ordering in both cases.
[O] So it's not random noise. It's a K-factor artifact you can actually name and fix.
[G] That's the constructive half of the finding, yes.
[S] And the real human data — did it back this up, or was that just a simulation quirk?
[G] It backs it up directly. Flan-t5-xxl over Flan-t5-xl — recorded win rates of sixty-four percent versus thirty-six percent — ranks the same way in every single K-factor and permutation-count combination they tested. Completely stable. But Dolly-v2-7b versus Dolly-v2-12b, at fifty-one versus forty-nine percent, actually flips which model wins depending on the hyperparameter cell. The larger Dolly model wins at a K-factor of thirty-two, the smaller one wins at K-factors of one and sixteen, and at K equals eight the winner even changes depending on how many permutations you average.
[O] So the exact instability the synthetic experiments predicted shows up on real annotated votes, on a real near-tied pairing.
[G] Exactly the pattern, exactly the kind of pairing — near-tied models — where the leaderboard use case actually lives.
[O] Okay, let me make the strong case here. This is early, November twenty twenty-three, and it correctly diagnoses a real fragility in a system that was, at that exact moment, ranking every major chatbot on the internet. Chatbot Arena itself later moved away from raw Elo to Bradley-Terry coefficients with confidence intervals. This paper was pointing at the right problem before the field fixed it.
[S] I'll grant the diagnosis is directionally right. But look at what's actually carrying the "current comparative evaluations of LLMs" claim in the abstract. The real-world validation is two model pairs, Flan-T5 and Dolly-v2, and both were already a generation or two behind what Chatbot Arena was ranking by the time this paper came out — GPT-4, Claude 2, a dozen open chat models. Nobody tested whether this reproduces on a close, frontier-model pairing under real crowd voting.
[G] That's a fair reading. The paper's own framing reaches further than its validation set does — two dated model pairs plus one cross-family comparison in the appendix is what's actually tested, not "current" leaderboards broadly.
[O] Sure, but the mechanism they identify, that order sensitivity gets worse exactly as models converge in quality, is a property of the update rule itself. The math doesn't care which models you plug in.
[S] The math argument I buy. My problem is the ties. Every experiment, synthetic and real, excludes tie outcomes entirely. Real arenas route a meaningful share of votes to "tie" or "both bad." That changes effective sample size and update variance. We genuinely don't know whether these instabilities get better or worse once ties are folded back in.
[G] The authors are upfront about that. It's explicitly named as future work, and multi-category outcomes are sketched out mathematically in an appendix as a possible extension — but never actually tested here.
[O] Still doesn't erase the core result on the binary comparisons they did run.
[S] Second problem, Ottavia: every simulation restarts both models from the same rating of fourteen hundred before replaying permutations. A real leaderboard never does that. A new model enters an already-populated, spread-out pool of established ratings. It's untested whether this ordering sensitivity is partly a cold-start artifact rather than a permanent property of Elo in a mature rating pool.
[G] That's a real gap, and it isn't addressed anywhere in the paper. Every experiment here is a fresh two-model or three-model tournament starting from a shared point.
[O] What about the actual fix, though — average over a hundred permutations, tune K down for close matchups? That's actionable today, cold-start question or not.
[S] It's actionable, but it's a band-aid, not a comparison. Section eight of the paper names Glicko and TrueSkill as more sophisticated statistical successors to Elo, and never runs its own ordering-and-K-factor battery against either one. So we don't actually know if those alternatives are more robust under the same stress tests, or just fragile in a different way.
[G] That's the sharpest limitation, honestly. The prescription in this paper is "tune Elo's hyperparameters and average over permutations." It's not "here is quantitative evidence you should switch systems." Whether Glicko or TrueSkill or Bradley-Terry actually fix this, rather than just relabel it, is a question this paper raises but doesn't answer.
[O] Even so, a methodology paper that gets practitioners to stop trusting a single Elo run from a single match order, on a system that was actively ranking real products, is doing real work regardless of how far its validation set reaches.
[S] Agreed on "it should raise your prior toward skepticism." I just wouldn't call it a comprehensive audit of Elo on the leaderboards it was already powering. It's a well-aimed early warning with a narrow evidence base.
[O] Where does this actually change practice, beyond "run more permutations"?
[G] The concrete guidance is: run at least one hundred permutations wherever feasible, use a smaller K-factor when win rates are close to even, save a larger K-factor for genuinely lopsided matchups where fast convergence matters, and never assume transitivity is automatic, especially near the top of a leaderboard where models cluster close together.
[S] Which is exactly the region that matters most to a reader deciding which frontier model to actually use — the close calls, not the blowouts.
[G] Right, and that's the paper's real contribution beyond the specific numbers. It reframes a leaderboard's single point-estimate Elo score as something with real, usually unreported, uncertainty attached to it. That has knock-on relevance anywhere a pairwise-judgment signal feeds a ranking, not just chatbot arenas, but any LLM-as-judge setup that aggregates comparisons into a score.
[O] And it's part of why some of the field's newer leaderboards report intervals and lean on batch estimation instead of a running, order-dependent update.
[G] My read, going a step beyond what's actually in this paper: Bradley-Terry addresses the order-dependence specifically, by estimating ratings from the whole batch of comparisons at once instead of updating sequentially. That part is well established outside this paper. Whether it also resolves the transitivity violations shown here is a separate question this paper simply doesn't test.
[G] If there's one line to take from the paper, it's this: Elo's assumptions were built for a game that language model evaluation doesn't resemble, and near-tied models are exactly where that mismatch bites hardest.
[O] My takeaway: this is the paper that should make you distrust any single leaderboard Elo number reported without a permutation count or a confidence interval sitting next to it.
