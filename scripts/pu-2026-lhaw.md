[S] Claude Opus four point five solves one hundred percent of a benchmark's tasks when the instructions are complete. Delete one sentence — not the whole task, one sentence — and it drops to fourteen percent.
[O] And when you let it ask a single clarifying question, it climbs back to seventy-eight. That's the number I want to sit with. An agent that gets most of the way back just by being allowed to ask.
[S] Most of the way. Never all the way back to one hundred, even with what's basically a perfect helper standing by. Today's paper says that leftover gap is the real finding.
[O] Then let's argue about which half of that story matters more, with someone who's actually read the paper end to end.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an AI optimist and an AI skeptic pull apart one paper with a guest who's studied it closely.
[S] Today's paper is LHAW, Controllable Underspecification for Long-Horizon Tasks, out of Scale AI, led by George Pu and Michael Lee, posted to arXiv earlier this year.
[O] Joining us is Nora, who's spent real time inside this pipeline and its numbers. Nora, welcome. Why does a paper about agents not knowing when to ask deserve a benchmark of its own?
[G] Because almost every existing benchmark assumes the task in front of the agent is already fully specified, and almost every clarification benchmark assumes asking a question is free. Long-horizon agents live in neither world. The paper's setup is an agent executing dozens to hundreds of sequential actions with only occasional human supervision, and it names three real costs to interrupting that agent: interruption itself, the human's context switch; latency, the workflow sitting idle waiting for a reply; and trust, an agent that asks about everything eventually gets ignored or shut off.
[O] So the failure isn't just the agent got it wrong. It's that silence and constant questioning are both failure modes, just in opposite directions.
[G] Exactly, and the authors point out that the benchmarks we already have only see one side of that. Long-horizon execution suites like TheAgentCompany, SWE-Bench Pro, and MCP-Atlas hand the agent a complete, well-specified task and just measure whether it finishes. They never test whether the agent notices something is missing, because nothing ever is. Clarification benchmarks like ClariQ, AmbigQA, QuestBench, and UserBench go the other way — they live in short, cheap conversational settings where asking a question costs nothing. Nobody had systematically injected ambiguity into an expensive, many-step workflow and measured what happens.
[S] Before we go further, I want the distinction pinned down, because ambiguity gets used loosely in this space. The paper draws a hard line between two things that sound similar.
[G] Right, and this matters for how to read every number later. Semantic ambiguity is when a request has multiple valid readings, and any of them gives you a complete, acceptable answer — that's the ClariQ, AmbigQA world. Underspecification is different. The request is missing something it actually needs, and it's unsolvable until that piece arrives. LHAW only targets underspecification, and specifically a flavor of it they call outcome-critical, where the missing detail doesn't just lower quality, it provably breaks the task.
[S] So this paper is deliberately not trying to solve the messier, more common case, where a user's intent is just genuinely ambiguous.
[G] Correct, and they say so directly. That's a real scope limitation, worth keeping in your back pocket for later.
[O] Nora, walk us through how you actually manufacture a missing piece of information on purpose. That sounds harder than it sounds.
[G] They built a taxonomy of four dimensions, and it wasn't invented from a whiteboard — it was reverse-engineered by looking at failure modes across thirty-three TheAgentCompany tasks and asking what kind of information, when absent, actually broke the agent. Goal is the deliverable itself, "write the summary" without saying for whom or how long. Constraint is the rule or threshold, "list the top customers" without saying top by revenue or by count. Input is where the data lives, "use the latest dataset" without saying which one or where it's stored. Context is the background knowledge, "send the report to the team" without saying which team, or by email or chat.
[S] Give me a concrete one, because those all sound similar in the abstract.
[G] There's a good one in the appendix. A payroll task tells the agent to use an attendance file and a salary-rates file to calculate total earnings and write it into a spreadsheet. Delete the sentence that says what calculation to perform, and the file paths are all still there, the agent can still open both spreadsheets — it just doesn't know if it's summing hours times rate, or something with overtime rules folded in. That's officially their Goal-dimension example. A different case just changes a hex color code and an alignment instruction in a formatting task — small, almost cosmetic-looking edits that are actually load-bearing for whether the output counts as correct.
[O] So the pipeline goes in and surgically removes exactly one of those, rather than just vaguely muddying the whole prompt.
[G] That's phase one, segment extraction and scoring. An LLM reads the fully-specified prompt, sometimes alongside grounding data like the correct trajectory or the evaluation checkpoints, and tags every atomic, removable piece of information by which of the four dimensions it belongs to. Then it scores each one on two axes, each just three levels, zero, half, or one. Criticality: would removing this actually cause failure. Guessability: could the agent infer it anyway from context. Multiply criticality by one minus guessability, and you get a priority score that ranks the segments that matter and can't be recovered by guessing.
[S] And then you just delete the highest-priority one?
[G] Phase two, candidate generation, gives you three ways to remove it. Delete just cuts the span out entirely. Vaguify swaps it for something hand-wavy, like turning a specific filename into "the file." Genericize swaps it for a generic placeholder, like "an appropriate format." You can also stack removals, take out two or three segments at once, and that's the knob for difficulty, because the agent now has to notice multiple missing pieces before it can even ask the right question.
[O] Here's what I'd worry about if I were building this. how do you know the thing you deleted actually created ambiguity, instead of just quietly turning it into a different, perfectly solvable task?
[G] That's phase three, and honestly it's the paper's real contribution. Most work in this space just asks an LLM whether a prompt looks ambiguous and takes its word for it. LHAW doesn't trust that judgment. It runs the underspecified variant through real agent rollouts, several trials each, and classifies the variant by what actually happened. If the agent fails every single trial, that's outcome-critical, a genuine blocker. If it sometimes succeeds but lands on different final states across trials, that's divergent, the agent is guessing, and sometimes guessing right. If it succeeds every time and always converges to the same outcome, that's benign, the agent reliably figured out the missing piece on its own, so it wasn't really ambiguous after all.
[S] And the fourth category is the one I actually care about, because it's the failure mode that would quietly poison a synthetic ambiguity benchmark.
[G] New task. If every trial fails, but they all converge to the exact same outcome, that usually means the deletion didn't create ambiguity, it created a different, coherent task, and the agent is reliably solving that new task instead. Their own example: delete the age range from "find users between eighteen and thirty-four," and you don't get ambiguity, you get a new, perfectly valid request, "find users." An LLM judge specifically screens for this pattern and throws those samples out before they ever reach the benchmark.
[O] So ambiguity here isn't a linguistic judgment call at all, it's an empirical label, defined entirely by the spread of outcomes you observe.
[G] That's the whole point of building it this way. Running that pipeline across three source benchmarks gives you two hundred eighty-five final variants. Eighty-five from TheAgentCompany's OwnCloud tasks, drawn from just thirteen underlying tasks and split forty outcome-critical, thirty divergent, fifteen benign. One hundred from SWE-Bench Pro, spanning seventy-five unique coding tasks, split thirty-one, thirty-four, thirty-five. And one hundred from MCP-Atlas, one variant per task, split fifty, thirty, twenty. Every source task was pre-filtered so the reference models could already solve it when fully specified, that way, when performance drops, you know it's the missing information doing the damage, not the task just being too hard to begin with.
[S] And how does an agent actually ask for help in this setup?
[G] They give it a tool called ask_user, hosted as its own server, which the agent can call mid-task. Behind that tool sits a simulated user, another LLM that can see the original, fully-specified prompt and the ground truth for exactly the segments that got removed. It's policy-constrained to answer only questions about those missing pieces and defer everything else. Worth flagging now, because it matters a lot later, that simulator effectively knows the answer key.
[O] Let's get to the numbers, Nora. Does the difficulty knob actually work the way you'd want it to?
[G] Cleanly. On MCP-Atlas, deleting a single segment takes models from close to one hundred percent baseline down to somewhere between forty-five and sixty percent. Delete two segments instead of one, and it drops further, to somewhere between thirty-five and forty-four percent. That's a real, controllable difficulty dial, not a cliff.
[S] But the outcome-critical label is where I want to see the paper actually earn its name. Does outcome-critical mean what it says?
[G] It does, and this is the sharpest result in the paper. Narrow down to just the outcome-critical subset of MCP-Atlas, fifty variants, and Claude Opus four point five goes from a perfect one hundred percent baseline down to fourteen percent. GPT-5.2, same story, one hundred down to fourteen. On SWE-Bench Pro's outcome-critical subset, GPT-5.2 goes from seventy-two percent all the way down to zero. Flat zero. Meanwhile the benign variants barely move at all, staying in the eighty-five to one hundred percent range across models, which is exactly the control you'd want. It tells you the collapse on outcome-critical tasks isn't some general fragility to weird prompts, it's specifically these agents failing to notice they're missing something they truly need.
[O] Now bring in ask_user. How much of that comes back, Nora?
[G] Meaningfully, but rarely all the way. On MCP-Atlas, Opus recovers from forty-seven percent up to seventy-eight, still short of its one hundred percent baseline. That pattern, big recovery, short of full baseline, holds across almost every model and every benchmark. There's exactly one exception: GPT-5.2 on SWE-Bench Pro gains twenty-two points overall to reach ninety-one percent. And on that benchmark's outcome-critical subset specifically, GPT-5.2's baseline is seventy-two percent, it collapses to zero without help, and with ask_user it climbs back to seventy-one, essentially matching its own baseline. That's the one case in the whole paper where clarification looks like a complete fix.
[S] What about the model that doesn't even bother asking?
[G] Sonnet four point five, interestingly. It posts the single highest underspecified score on SWE-Bench Pro, seventy-nine percent, and gains exactly zero from having ask_user available. It would rather guess than interrupt.
[O] That's not necessarily a failure though, if it's guessing well enough to hit seventy-nine percent without help, maybe it doesn't need to ask.
[S] Or it's the model most likely to silently ship a wrong answer with total confidence, which is precisely the failure this whole paper opens with.
[G] Both readings are defensible, and the paper actually gives you a metric built for exactly that argument. They call it Gain per Q, the performance gain divided by the number of questions asked, to separate models that ask a lot from models that ask well. GPT-5.2 is the clearest over-clarifier. it has the highest ask rate in every single domain, ninety-seven percent of MCP-Atlas trials, nearly two questions per trajectory on average, and that buys the largest raw score gains but the worst efficiency, just point zero seven on MCP-Atlas and point zero four on TheAgentCompany. Compare that to Gemini three Pro, which only invokes ask_user in eighteen percent of TheAgentCompany trials but posts the best Gain per Q anywhere in the paper, point two four. Sonnet four point five is the most efficient specifically on MCP-Atlas, at point one seven.
[O] So asking more isn't the same as asking better.
[G] Right, and there's a whole second experiment built around exactly that idea, the cost side. They attach a persona to the system prompt: a Supervisor who's sitting right there waiting and says don't guess, a Standard Assistant, or a Busy Executive who says only interrupt me if guessing wrong would cause a real failure. Facing the Supervisor, the agent asks two hundred sixty-six questions and lifts its checkpoint score to sixty-four point nine percent, but at a low efficiency, point zero nine percent gain per question. Facing the Busy Executive, it asks far fewer questions, one hundred twenty-four, ends up at a slightly lower fifty-six percent checkpoint score, but a higher point one three percent per question, fewer, more targeted questions. And there's a genuinely encouraging gradient underneath that: average questions per trial slides smoothly from point nine nine, for an outcome-critical task facing an attentive Supervisor, all the way down to point two two, for a benign task facing a Busy Executive.
[S] So the model does seem to have some working sense of when asking is actually worth it.
[G] It does, at least in aggregate. But the failure-mode analysis is a little less flattering. They run an LLM judge over every question the agent asks and tag it against seven failure categories. The single most common defect for every model, without exception, is question quality, mostly compound questions that bundle several things into one ask. It ranges from forty-five percent for Gemini three Flash up to ninety-seven percent for GPT-5.2. And here's the number that really lands: when you look only at trials where the task ultimately fails, the rate of under-clarification, not asking when it should have, jumps from twenty-six percent up to forty-eight percent. That's pointing the finger at the agent's own question quality as the reason it can't fully recover, not at the simulated user being unhelpful.
[O] One more thing I want on the record before we argue, the agentic scaffolding ablation. Does how you prompt the agent change any of this?
[G] A little, and in an interesting direction. Using GPT-5.2 on TheAgentCompany, the simplest strategy, CodeAct, wins on overall task success across the whole set. But on the hardest slice, outcome-critical tasks, the two heavier strategies, Reflexion and Plan and Execute, actually start from nearly zero success without any help at all, worse than CodeAct's own five percent. Once ask_user comes back into the picture, though, they edge ahead of both simpler strategies, landing at seventeen point nine and eighteen point four percent, with roughly a four-to-five-point edge in checkpoint score too. Plan and Execute also asks the most questions of any strategy, eighty percent of trials, explicit planning seems to surface more of the uncertainty that CodeAct just barrels through.
[O] Okay, my optimist case. This is the first time anyone's operationalized ambiguity by what an agent actually does instead of what an LLM guesses a sentence means, and that New Task filter is genuinely clever, it stops the benchmark from quietly rebranding "we made a different task" as "we made an ambiguous one." On top of that, the persona experiment shows these models aren't just randomly asking questions, they're scaling how much they ask to both the stakes of the task and the implied cost of the interruption. That's a real, measurable form of judgment, and GPT-5.2 on SWE-Bench Pro proves the ceiling isn't hypothetical, near-complete recovery, even on the hardest subset, is achievable today.
[S] I'll take the other side, and I want to start with the elephant in the room. That ask_user simulator isn't a real, fallible person, it's holding the answer key. It has the original specification and the exact ground truth for every removed segment. A real user doesn't know the canonical right answer half the time, and doesn't answer without noise. So every recovery number in this paper is measured against a best-case, oracle helper, and the real-world clarification gap is almost certainly wider than what's on the page.
[G] That's fair, and it's actually the more interesting reading once you sit with it. If agents still can't fully recover even with a helper who knows the exact answer, then the honest takeaway isn't "clarification mostly works." It's "these agents ask bad enough questions that even a perfect answer key can't save them."
[S] Exactly, and that leads into my second point. The entire ambiguity signal in this benchmark is generated and judged by one model, Gemini three Flash, it extracts the segments, generates the variants, and an LLM judge decides what counts as a new task. There's no human-labeled ambiguity anywhere in this pipeline to check any of it against.
[O] To be fair, they're not hiding that. They name it directly as future work, bridging to human-validated benchmarks.
[S] Naming a limitation doesn't remove it. A different extraction model could plausibly produce a meaningfully different benchmark, and every per-dimension number inherits whatever blind spots Gemini three Flash happens to have.
[G] I'd score that one mostly to the skeptic. It's a legitimate, unresolved confound, and the authors themselves seem to know it's the biggest open question. There's a third one worth putting on the table too, which is my own read as much as the paper's: the four dimensions aren't equally hard to recover from, because they're not equally explorable. An Input ambiguity, which file, is often something the agent could just resolve by listing a folder. A Goal ambiguity, what am I actually supposed to produce, usually can't be recovered that way no matter how much the agent explores. The paper folds that into the benign label through repeated sampling, but it means some of what looks like a per-dimension difficulty ranking is really a difference in how discoverable each dimension happens to be.
[O] I'll grant all three of those, Nora. But I still don't think they erase the headline. Even with a generous oracle helper and an all-LLM pipeline, the collapse-then-partial-recovery pattern shows up over and over, across three completely different benchmarks and five different model families. That's not the kind of thing you'd expect from a single model's idiosyncratic blind spot, that's a real, general property of how these agents handle missing information.
[S] Granted. I just don't want anyone walking away thinking these numbers are a hard ceiling on real-world clarification value. They're closer to a floor for how hard this problem still is, dressed up in the most favorable conditions the authors could construct.
[G] That's the fair split. The behavior is real and it replicates. The magnitude is optimistic, because every advantage in the experimental setup, the oracle answerer, the baseline-competence filtering that only keeps tasks these five models can already do, pass at three letting one lucky trajectory out of three count as recovery, all point the same direction, toward flattering the agents.
[O] Zooming out, where does this land for how people actually build evals?
[G] I think the sharpest connection is to judge reliability, which is a live worry across agent evaluation generally. Here, the same handful of models are doing extraction, generation, filtering, and grading, all in one pipeline, and that kind of self-referential setup is exactly the pattern that quietly inflates benchmark results elsewhere in the field. It's a different flavor of contamination risk than the usual "the model has seen this test set" concern, this is closer to the benchmark and the thing being measured sharing the same blind spots.
[S] And practically, if you're building or buying an agent for a real autonomous workflow, this paper's real warning isn't "ambiguity exists." It's that even a well-behaved agent with a perfect helper on call still asks worse questions than it needs to, which means the actual deployment risk sits in question quality, not just in whether the agent decides to ask at all.
[G] Agreed, and that decision-theoretic piece, actually pricing the cost of an interruption against the value of the answer, is explicitly left as future work by the authors themselves. Right now the paper shows you the gradient exists, not the optimal policy for acting on it.
[G] If I had to leave you with one sentence, it's this: LHAW measures ambiguity by what an agent does, not by what a model guesses a sentence means, and that's a genuinely transferable idea beyond this one benchmark.
