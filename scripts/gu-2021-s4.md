[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an AI optimist, an AI skeptic, and a visiting scholar sit down with one paper at a time.
[S] Today's paper is S-four: Efficiently Modeling Long Sequences with Structured State Spaces, by Albert Gu, Karan Goel, and Christopher Ré at Stanford, posted to arXiv in November twenty twenty-one and later published at I-C-L-R twenty twenty-two.
[O] Joining us is Sofia, who's spent a lot of time with the linear algebra underneath this one. Sofia, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. This is a paper where the empirical results are exciting, but the real story is in the method section — how the authors turned an idea everyone already knew was good into something you could actually train.
[S] Let's start with what was actually broken. Why do long sequences break every standard architecture?
[G] Long-range dependencies are the hard case for every model family. Recurrent networks lose the signal to vanishing or exploding gradients across thousands of steps. Convolutional networks have a bounded receptive field, so anything past that window is invisible. Transformers pay a cost that scales with the square of sequence length in self-attention, which becomes infeasible once you're past a few thousand tokens. The field built a benchmark specifically to expose this, the Long Range Arena, and its hardest task, Path-X, involves reasoning over sequences sixteen thousand three hundred eighty-four steps long. Before this paper, every model on that task sat at essentially random guessing.
[O] And there was already an attempt at fixing this with state space models, right, before S4?
[G] Right, the direct predecessor is called the Linear State Space Layer, or L-S-S-L, from the same lab about a year earlier. It uses a continuous state space model, the classical equation from control theory, x prime of t equals A x of t plus B u of t, and initializes the state matrix A using something called HiPPO — a theory of continuous-time memorization that gives the model a mathematically principled way to compress a long history into a fixed-size state. The L-S-S-L showed this genuinely works — on sequential M-N-I-S-T, just swapping in the HiPPO matrix took a random SSM from sixty percent accuracy to ninety-eight percent.
[S] So what stopped everyone from just using it?
[G] Cost, and stability. Computing the state naively takes order N squared times L operations and order N times L memory, for state size N and sequence length L — versus a theoretical lower bound of order L plus N for both. At a state size of two hundred fifty-six, that's orders of magnitude more memory than a comparably sized RNN or CNN. And the paper shows that the theoretically faster algorithms people had proposed for this were numerically unstable, because the HiPPO matrix is what's called highly non-normal — it resists exactly the kind of diagonalization trick you'd normally reach for.
[O] So S4's whole job is making that same idea actually computable.
[G] Exactly. Structurally, an SSM has two equivalent faces. Discretize the continuous system with a step size delta using the bilinear transform, and you get an RNN-style recurrence — the state updates step by step. But because the system is linear and time-invariant, you can also unroll that same recurrence into one big convolution, output equals a kernel K convolved with the input. Same operator, two representations — the convolution is parallel and ideal for training, the recurrence is stateful and ideal for fast, one-token-at-a-time generation.
[S] So where's the actual bottleneck?
[G] It's computing that convolution kernel K in the first place. The naive way requires taking successive powers of the state matrix out to the L-minus-first power — that's exactly the order N squared L cost from the L-S-S-L. The obvious fix is to diagonalize the matrix so powers become cheap multiplications. But the HiPPO matrix's diagonalizing change of basis has entries that grow like two to the four N over three power — astronomically large, so a direct diagonalization just blows up numerically on real hardware.
[O] So what's the actual trick that gets around that?
[G] The key structural fact, and this is the paper's real contribution: every HiPPO matrix can be written as Normal Plus Low-Rank — a part that's perfectly diagonalizable by a well-conditioned unitary matrix, minus a rank-one or rank-two correction. On its own that's not enough, because powering up a sum like that is still slow. So they combine three separate numerical-analysis ideas. First, instead of computing the kernel's coefficients directly, they evaluate its truncated generating function at the roots of unity in frequency space — this turns repeated matrix powers into a single matrix inverse, a resolvent, which can be inverse-Fourier-transformed back into the kernel. Second, the low-rank correction term gets removed using the Woodbury identity, reducing everything to the purely diagonal case. Third, that diagonal case is exactly a Cauchy matrix computation, which is a classical, well-studied problem in numerical analysis with fast, stable algorithms going back to the Fast Multipole Method.
[S] And that's the "four Cauchy multiplies" line from the abstract.
[G] Right — Theorem three in the paper: the whole convolution kernel reduces to four Cauchy matrix multiplies, giving quasi-linear compute in N plus L and linear memory in N plus L, which is essentially tight against that theoretical lower bound. And critically, it's stable, because the algorithm never actually forms that exploding diagonalizing matrix.
[O] What does an S4 layer look like once you put that all together?
[G] You initialize the SSM with A set to the HiPPO matrix, which by the Normal Plus Low-Rank decomposition is equivalent, under a change of basis, to a diagonal-plus-low-rank system with just five parameter vectors — Lambda, P, Q, B, and C — so five N trainable parameters total per layer. To handle real feature dimensions instead of a single scalar channel, they run H independent copies of this SSM and mix the H channels with a position-wise linear layer, with nonlinearities inserted between layers. Structurally that makes the whole thing look like a depthwise-separable convolutional network, except the convolution kernels are global across the entire sequence instead of a small local window, and they're generated by the SSM rather than learned directly.
[S] Let's get to the numbers. Start with the headline Long Range Arena result.
[G] Table four in the paper compares S4 against eleven efficient-Transformer variants across six LRA tasks. The best of those baselines, Luna two-fifty-six, averages fifty-nine point three seven percent. S4 averages eighty-six point oh nine percent, and on Path-X specifically it reaches ninety-six point three five percent — the first model ever to clear that task, where every prior model sat at roughly fifty percent, random guessing.
[O] Now, back to the discrepancy you flagged at the top.
[G] Right, it's worth being precise about. The paper's own abstract and introduction — the running prose — report an LRA average of eighty point four eight percent and a Path-X score of eighty-eight percent. But table four, in a later arXiv revision of the same PDF, reports eighty-six point oh nine and ninety-six point three five. Both numbers coexist in the current version. If you're going to cite quote "the S4 number," you need to say which one, and which revision.
[S] That's not a rounding error, that's an eight-point swing on the single most quoted statistic from this paper.
[G] It is a real gap, but one thing is worth noting: the direction is consistent across every task, the table numbers are uniformly higher than the prose numbers, which is more consistent with an improved or better-tuned later run than with an error. And under either version, S4 clears the best baseline by twenty to twenty-seven points. The ordering of the result doesn't change, just its exact size.
[O] And the efficiency story against the L-S-S-L specifically?
[G] At hidden dimension five hundred twelve, S4 trains twenty-nine point six times faster per step than the L-S-S-L, and uses three hundred ninety-two times less memory — thirty-three point five megabytes versus thirteen thousand one hundred forty megabytes. Against the efficient Transformers, at sequence length four thousand ninety-six, S4 runs about five point two times faster than a vanilla Transformer, right in the same range as Performer and Linear Transformer, which were purpose-built for speed.
[S] Does any of this transfer beyond the synthetic long-range benchmark, or is this a Path-X-only story?
[G] This is where I think the paper earns its "general sequence model" framing. On raw, sixteen-thousand-sample speech waveforms, no hand-crafted features at all, S4 hits ninety-eight point three two percent, beating a specialized convolutional baseline at seventy-one point six six percent and even a WaveGAN discriminator with ninety times more parameters at ninety-six point two five. On sequential CIFAR-ten, fed pixel by pixel with zero two-dimensional structure, it reaches ninety-one point one three percent, competitive with a full two-D ResNet-eighteen despite having no image-specific inductive bias at all. On WikiText-one-oh-three language modeling, simply swapping S4 in for self-attention in a strong Transformer baseline gets to twenty point nine five test perplexity — within point eight of the Transformer's twenty point five one — while generating sixty times faster, because at inference time it switches to its recurrent mode instead of reprocessing the whole context.
[O] And it adapts to a different sampling rate without retraining?
[G] Yes, and that's a genuinely distinctive capability. Because it's fundamentally a continuous-time model with an explicit step size delta, they test it at half the original sampling frequency on the speech task with zero retraining, just by changing that one scalar, and it still scores ninety-six point three percent. A standard discrete RNN or Transformer has no equivalent knob.
[O] Okay, let me make the strongest case for why this paper matters. One operator, two exact computational modes — a parallel convolution that trains fast, and a stateful recurrence that generates sixty times faster than a Transformer. It solved a benchmark task that had beaten every model that tried it, by a wide margin, not a narrow one. And the same architecture, essentially unchanged, transferred across raw audio, pixels, text, and time series without per-task redesign. That's a genuinely rare kind of generality.
[S] Here's my pushback. First, the LRA comparison isn't apples to apples — the paper itself literally tells you, quote, "please read appendix D.5 before citing this table," flagging that the efficient-Transformer baselines weren't re-tuned for these tasks the way S4 was. Some of that eighty-six versus fifty-nine gap is a tuning gap, not a pure architecture gap. Second, on WikiText-one-oh-three, S4 is still behind the Transformer, not ahead of it — "competitive attention-free" is a real result, but it's not the same claim as "better."
[G] Both of those land, and I want to be precise about how much. On the tuning caveat — the authors flag it themselves, in their own words, so it's not a hidden problem, it's disclosed uncertainty. That's honest, but it does mean the headline gap's exact size is partly an open question about protocol, not just architecture.
[S] And here's the sharper version of my skepticism — the paper's own ablation on sequential CIFAR-ten shows that a random Normal-Plus-Low-Rank matrix performs no better than a random dense one. The authors' own words: quote, "S4's effectiveness primarily comes from the HiPPO initialization, not the NPLR parameterization." So the clever linear algebra that makes this fast isn't where the accuracy comes from at all.
[G] I'd actually push back gently on calling that a weakness rather than the paper's honest framing of its own contribution. HiPPO's power was already established a year earlier, in the L-S-S-L paper by mostly the same authors. What this paper adds isn't a new source of accuracy — it's turning a matrix everyone already knew was good but nobody could actually run into something that trains in memory a single GPU can hold. That's an engineering contribution, and a legitimate one on its own terms.
[S] Fair — and honestly, the fact that they ran and reported that ablation themselves, instead of letting the benchmark sweep imply a new modeling primitive, counts in the paper's favor, not against it.
[O] There's also a numerical footnote worth stating plainly. The paper itself notes that this exact parameterization, Lambda minus P Q star, can become unstable when the discretized state matrix picks up eigenvalues on the right half of the complex plane. A follow-up paper a few months later fixed it by changing the parameterization to Lambda minus P P star instead.
[S] Which, again, is the kind of thing a skeptic wants surfaced — and to their credit, the authors surfaced it themselves in the discussion section rather than leaving it for someone else to discover.
[O] Zooming out — this became the direct architectural ancestor of a much longer line of work. Within about two years, diagonal simplifications like S-four-D and D-S-S appeared, and eventually the input-dependent selective state space model that became Mamba.
[G] Right, and a lot of that later work exists specifically to close the two gaps this paper leaves open — the numerical instability footnote, and the fact that S4's core parameters, aside from the discretization step, don't depend on the input at all, unlike attention.
[S] For evaluation practice specifically, the "read appendix D.5 before citing this table" line is worth remembering any time a headline benchmark table quietly depends on a preprocessing or tuning protocol that isn't visible in the table itself.
