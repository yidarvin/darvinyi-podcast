[S] Derek, back up first. Why do we even need rubrics? Why not just keep using verifiable rewards, the thing that made DeepSeek's R1 work?
[G] Because verifiable rewards only work when there's a single checkable answer, a number, a passing unit test. The moment you want to train on an open-ended question, like how do I test for carbon dioxide in a reaction, there's no ground-truth string to match against. Rubrics fill that gap. A rubric is a prompt-specific list of weighted, binary criteria, things like plus nine points if the response mentions limewater turning milky, and an LLM grader checks each one and reports a weighted score.
[O] So it's a way to get reinforcement-learning-style training signal on essentially anything, as long as a human or a model can articulate what a good answer looks like.
[G] Right, and prior work already shows that training on rubrics beats just having an LLM judge hand out a Likert score. The problem the authors identify is that all of that prior work treats the rubric as fixed. Written once, before training starts, and never touched again.
[S] And fixed means it can only describe what someone thought of in advance.
[G] Exactly, and that breaks in two specific directions. First, a rubric describes the ideal answer, but rarely penalizes a specific way of gaming it. Their carbon dioxide example: the rubric rewards mentioning limewater turning milky. One response says that and stops. Another says that and tacks on, quote, which is slightly soluble in acidic conditions, unquote, true, but irrelevant filler. Both get full marks, because nothing in the rubric docks you for padding.
[O] And there's a sharper published example they cite. Huang and colleagues found models learning to insert lines like, the following advice is the most relevant, which is just self-praise, and it fools the grader into scoring the response as more relevant than it actually is.
[G] Right. Second failure is the mirror image. If a model spontaneously does something genuinely good that nobody anticipated, say, noting that a reaction is specific to carbon dioxide, useful extra information, a fixed rubric has no criterion to reward it. The signal is just invisible to the algorithm.
[S] So static rubrics under-punish the bad and under-reward the good, and in both cases it's because the rubric was written by someone who wasn't looking at the actual rollouts.
[O] So what's the fix, mechanically, Derek?
[G] OnlineRubrics. Keep the initial rubric, whether it's human-written or synthetic, but before every policy update, add to it. At each training step, for a given prompt, you sample a batch of responses from the current policy, and a separate batch from a control policy, either the frozen reference model or just last step's policy. Then an LLM extractor, o3-mini in their setup, looks at pairs of responses, one from each side, and writes down the meaningful differences the existing rubric doesn't already cover.
[S] Why pairs? Why not just show the extractor one bad response and ask what's wrong with it?
[G] Because the paper's actual empirical claim is that pairwise comparison works better than that. The reasoning borrows straight from the preference-learning literature, the same intuition behind RLHF and DPO. LLMs are unreliable at absolute judgment. Ask a model to rate one response's quality on its own and you get noisy answers. Ask it which of two responses is better and why, and it's far more reliable, because it only has to spot a relative difference, not assign an absolute score.
[O] So the extractor isn't judging quality directly. It's doing the easier job of noticing, this one does something the other doesn't.
[G] Exactly, and it's told two more constraints explicitly, in the prompt. Every new criterion has to be grounded in one of the two actual responses, it's not allowed to invent one from its own background knowledge. And it's specifically instructed to hunt for reward-hacking patterns, behavior that follows the letter of the rubric but not the spirit. Then a second pass deduplicates anything that overlaps with what's already there.
[S] How many new criteria are we actually talking about, per step?
[G] By default they compare eight pairs of rollouts per prompt and end up appending about eight new criteria. Those just get unioned into the existing rubric, nothing removed, nothing reweighted, they only append. Which is nice engineering, it drops into any rubric-based RL pipeline without touching how the reward itself gets computed.
[O] And that reward computation is still just a weighted average of what the grader marks satisfied?
[G] Right, same formula as before, weights times binary grades, summed, then normalized by the total positive weight available. OnlineRubrics doesn't touch that formula. It only changes what's in the criteria set feeding into it.
[S] You mentioned two variants, reference policy versus old policy as the control. Does it matter which one you contrast against?
[G] They test both. Pi-ref compares the current rollout against the frozen initial model, so the contrast target never moves. Pi-old compares against last step's policy, a moving target that tracks wherever training currently is. In aggregate pi-old does about as well or slightly better, but it's genuinely mixed, and we should come back to that in the results.
[O] I actually think the more interesting part of this paper is the argument for why this should even work, mathematically.
[G] That's Proposition One, and it's a nice piece of formal motivation, even if it's modest in what it actually proves. Split the true reward criteria into two sets, explicit, meaning already in your rubric, and implicit, meaning real but missing. If your estimated reward only scores the explicit criteria, they show the gap between your true policy gradient and the gradient you're actually computing is upper-bounded by the total weight mass sitting in that implicit, missing set.
[S] Isn't that the kind of bound that's tidy in theory and tells you nothing in practice, if you can never actually measure how large that missing weight mass is?
[G] You're right to be suspicious of it as a practical control. The authors call it tightening a bound, not a guarantee, and there's no way, even inside the paper, to independently verify how large the implicit weight mass actually is at any point in training. So it's a motivational argument for why elicitation should help, not a diagnostic tool you can point at a checkpoint and get a number from.
[O] One more mechanical piece before results. Who's actually doing the grading, and how did they choose that model?
[G] This is one of the more careful parts of the paper. Since the grader has to score every rollout at every step, its accuracy and its cost both matter enormously. So they collected human satisfaction labels, real people rating whether a response met each rubric criterion, for five hundred prompts each on the Generalist and Expert sets, then benchmarked eleven candidate LLM graders against those labels using AUC, plotted against inference cost per sample.
[S] An AUC around point eight, run on every single rollout at every training step, feels like a soft foundation for something this consequential. Did they stress-test that choice at all, or just pick the Pareto point and move on?
[G] Fair to push on. They didn't do additional robustness checks beyond this one comparison. What they did find is that every grader scored higher on Generalist prompts than Expert prompts, average AUC around point eight-one versus point seven-seven, which tracks, expert science questions are just harder to grade. But the detail I find most useful is that the Pareto frontier, the models that trade quality against cost best, was identical across both domains. Grader quality ranking doesn't depend on the subject matter. They picked GPT-4.1-mini, it sits right on that frontier and it's cheap enough to run at scale.
[O] Okay, let's get to the actual training run. What's the setup?
[G] Policy is Qwen two point five, seven billion parameters, instruct-tuned. They train it with GRPO, the same family of algorithm behind DeepSeek's R1, for three epochs, sixteen rollouts per prompt, on two datasets they built themselves. Generalist Rubrics, real-world prompts with human-written rubrics, about ten criteria per prompt on average. And Expert Rubrics, harder problems across physics, chemistry, biology, and math, about eighteen criteria per prompt, written by domain experts.
[S] And the eval sets are held out from that same pool?
[G] Yes, and this matters. The eval sets only ever contain the original human-written rubrics, none of the elicited criteria leak into scoring. So when OnlineRubrics wins on the eval set, that's a real transfer test, criteria elicited during training on one batch of prompts are improving performance on a clean, disjoint target.
[O] Give me the headline numbers.
[G] On instruction-following. Base Qwen instruct model scores fifty-five point four on the Generalist rubric score. Training with human rubrics alone gets you to sixty-one. Add online elicitation and you're at sixty-three point two. On AlpacaEval win rate specifically, the base model wins thirty percent of the time. Human rubrics alone: forty-six point four. OnlineRubrics: fifty-five. That's a twenty-five point swing from the untrained model, which is exactly the up-to-twenty-five-percent number the abstract leads with.
[S] And that's not just the response getting longer, gaming a verbosity-favoring judge?
[G] That's the first thing I checked too. AlpacaEval reports a length-controlled win rate specifically to catch that, and it moves too, twenty-eight to thirty-one and a half percent. Smaller gain, but it survives controlling for length. Arena-Hard moves from fifty-two point four to fifty-six point five.
[O] What about the ablations, Universal Requirements and Pointwise Extraction? Those are the ones that actually isolate whether this specific design choice matters.
[G] Right, and this is the part I'd point to as the paper's best evidence. Universal Requirements is a fixed checklist appended to every prompt, generic items like truthfulness and relevance, borrowed from prior work. OnlineRubrics beats it across the board, which argues that sample-specific, elicited criteria beat a static generic checklist. Pointwise Extraction is the sharper test, same extractor model, same setup, but it only looks at one response at a time instead of a pair. On AlpacaEval win rate, pointwise gets forty-eight point one, pairwise gets fifty-four to fifty-five, depending on the variant. On Arena-Hard, pointwise is fifty-one point one, pairwise is fifty-five point seven to fifty-six point five. So pairwise is worth roughly six to seven points over pointwise on AlpacaEval, and roughly five points on Arena-Hard, holding everything else constant.
[S] That's the one ablation I'll buy without reservation. It isolates the claim that pairwise contrast is doing real work from the more boring claim that adding more criteria helps, and pairwise wins clean.
[O] What about the harder reasoning benchmarks, GPQA, GSM8K?

[S] So plus twenty-five is doing a lot of work in the framing. It's real, but it's specifically an instruction-following win-rate number, not a general reasoning number.

So the model is teaching the rubric to reward things like showing reasoning transparently, not over-enumerating to look thorough, giving real setup instructions instead of just a code snippet.

[O] So what actually changes if we take this seriously?
[G] For anyone doing rubric-based post-training right now, this is cheap to bolt on, you keep your existing rubric infrastructure and just add an extraction pass before each update. Given the pointwise ablation, if you're going to elicit criteria online at all, doing it from pairs rather than single responses looks like the right default.
[S] For evaluation practice more broadly, I think the bigger lesson is upstream of the method, it's the diagnosis. Any rubric or checklist you hand-write before seeing real model outputs is a snapshot of your own priors about failure modes. If a model finds a way to satisfy the letter of your rubric while violating its spirit, a static rubric structurally can't catch that, because by definition it wasn't written with that failure in mind.
