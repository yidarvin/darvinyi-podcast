[S] Here's the number that got me: on the task where that predecessor's headline trick should matter most, ripping the trick back out actually scores higher than leaving it in.
[G] Both of those are true, and they're sitting in the same paper, which is exactly why this one's worth taking apart carefully.
[S] "Fun" is one word for a result that quietly contradicts its own ablation table.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the litsearch atlas. Today's paper is "Addressing Function Approximation Error in Actor-Critic Methods," by Scott Fujimoto, Herke van Hoof, and David Meger, published at ICML in twenty eighteen — the paper that introduced T-D-three, Twin Delayed Deep Deterministic policy gradient, and it's since crossed seven thousand, one hundred and thirteen citations.
[G] Thanks for having me. And the usual disclaimer up front — I'm not one of the authors, so when I say "they show" or "the paper argues," that's exactly what I mean, not my own work.
[S] It's on the docket because for the better part of a decade it became the default thing every continuous-control paper had to report against. Worth understanding why that happened, and whether it fully deserves it.
[O] Theo, start with the disease before the cure. What's overestimation bias, for someone who knows Q-learning but hasn't thought hard about it in a while?
[G] In ordinary Q-learning with discrete actions, your update target comes from a max over your own noisy value estimates. Thrun and Schwartz showed back in nineteen ninety-three that even zero-mean noise turns into a positive bias once you push it through a max, because the max operator always tends to pick whichever action's estimate got lucky that update. That bias then propagates forward through the Bellman equation, update after update.
[S] And the field's standard answer to that in the discrete world is Double DQN — decouple which action gets picked from which network scores it.
[G] Right, and that's exactly where this paper gets interesting, because the authors ask a question nobody had really settled: does any of this happen in actor-critic methods for continuous control, where there's no explicit max, just a deterministic policy climbing the critic's gradient?
[O] And the answer is yes.
[G] The answer is yes, and they show it two ways. First analytically — under a deterministic policy gradient, the actor's own update is biased toward whatever actions the critic currently overvalues. Second empirically — they plot DDPG's learned value estimate against a Monte Carlo estimate of the true value, on Hopper and Walker two d, and DDPG's curve sits above the true curve for the entire million-step run.
[S] So the disease transfers cleanly. Does the fix transfer too?
[G] That's the twist — it doesn't, not cleanly. The natural actor-critic version of Double DQN uses the current policy network as one estimator and the slow-moving target network as the other. But the target network only ever gets nudged a little each update, a Polyak average, so it stays too close to the current network to act as an independent second opinion. The standard remedy, ported over directly, barely helps.
[O] So they needed something genuinely new, not just a port.
[G] Exactly — and that's the actual contribution here, three separate fixes, each aimed at a different piece of the problem.
[S] Let's take them one at a time, starting with the one that gives the paper its name.
[G] Clipped double Q-learning. Instead of one critic, T-D-three trains a pair of critics, Q theta one and Q theta two, off the same replay data. When it builds the Bellman target, it takes whichever of the two gives the smaller value — not an average, the minimum.
[O] Why the minimum instead of, say, an average?
[G] Because a minimum can only ever pull the target down toward or below the truth, never push it up. Worst case you get mild underestimation, and the authors argue that's a much safer failure mode — an underestimated action just gets quietly avoided by the policy, instead of being chased and re-amplified the way an overestimated one is.
[S] Does it use two full actors too, one per critic, the way a fully faithful Double Q-learning would?
[G] No, and that's a real simplification worth flagging. A fully faithful version would train two independent actors, one per critic. For efficiency, T-D-three shares a single actor, optimized only against the first critic, and folds the second critic in purely as the clipping partner.
[O] Fix number two.
[G] Delayed policy updates. The critics train every single step. The actor and both target networks update only once every two critic updates — a delay parameter, little d, set to two in the experiments. The logic: updating the actor against an under-converged, high-variance critic compounds whatever error is currently in that estimate, and that error feeds straight back into the next Bellman target, because the actor's action is exactly what the critic bootstraps off of at the next state.
[S] So you're deliberately starving the actor of updates to let the critic settle first.
[G] Right, at the direct cost of the actor being updated half as often over the same number of environment steps.
[O] And the third fix?
[G] Target policy smoothing. A fully deterministic target policy lets the critic latch onto narrow, spurious spikes in the value landscape — a single action with a suspiciously high estimate and no support from its neighbors is usually noise, not signal. So before feeding the target action into the target critics, T-D-three adds a small amount of clipped Gaussian noise to it — standard deviation zero point two, clipped to plus or minus zero point five. It's modeled explicitly on SARSA — fit the value of a neighborhood around the action, not just the point estimate.
[S] Put all three pieces into one target equation for me.
[G] The update target is the reward, plus the discount factor times the minimum of the two target critics, evaluated at the target actor's action plus that clipped noise. Twin critics limiting overestimation through the minimum, noise smoothing the target over nearby actions, and underneath all of it, the actor and target networks only actually moving once every two critic steps.
[O] That's a lot of moving parts for something billed as three simple modifications.
[G] Individually they're simple — each one is a handful of lines changed on top of DDPG. What's not obvious going in is that you need essentially all three together, which is exactly what the ablation gets into.
[S] Let's get to numbers, then. What's the actual test bed?
[G] Seven continuous-control tasks from the MuJoCo suite, run through OpenAI Gym, unmodified — HalfCheetah, Hopper, Walker two d, Ant, Reacher, Inverted Pendulum, and Inverted Double Pendulum. Each task runs for one million environment steps, evaluated every five thousand steps, across ten random seeds. Six baselines: original DDPG, a hyperparameter-matched "Our DDPG" control built to isolate the algorithmic change from architecture choices, PPO, TRPO, ACKTR, and SAC.
[O] Give me the headline wins.
[G] On the four harder locomotion tasks, the margins are large. HalfCheetah: T-D-three lands around nine thousand six hundred thirty-seven, next best is their own retuned DDPG at roughly eight thousand five hundred seventy-seven. Walker two d: T-D-three around four thousand six hundred eighty-three against PPO's roughly three thousand three hundred eighteen. Ant: T-D-three around four thousand three hundred seventy-two against ACKTR's roughly one thousand eight hundred twenty-two. Hopper: T-D-three around three thousand five hundred sixty-four, ahead of SAC's roughly three thousand.
[S] That's four convincing wins. What about the other three tasks?
[G] That's where it gets more honest. Inverted Pendulum is saturated — nearly every method hits the maximum possible score of exactly one thousand. On Reacher, T-D-three scores around negative three point six, their own retuned DDPG around negative four — those error bars overlap. On Inverted Double Pendulum, the original DDPG actually scores a touch higher than T-D-three, roughly nine thousand three hundred fifty-six against T-D-three's roughly nine thousand three hundred thirty-seven, again within error bars. The paper's own results table bolds both entries as joint-best on each of those two tasks.
[O] So the abstract's claim of beating everyone on every single environment tested—
[G] —is a clean win on five of seven, and a statistical tie on the other two. Worth knowing before repeating the abstract's line uncritically.
[S] Now the part that actually made me sit up — the ablation.
[G] Table two, and it only covers four of the seven tasks — HalfCheetah, Hopper, Walker two d, Ant. Pulling out delayed policy updates or target policy smoothing individually costs a little almost everywhere, never catastrophically. Pulling out clipped double Q-learning is different — Ant collapses from around four thousand one hundred eighty-five down to roughly eight hundred fifty, Walker two d drops from around four thousand five hundred sixty-five to roughly two thousand five hundred eighty. That's a component doing real, load-bearing work, on those two tasks.
[O] And on HalfCheetah?
[G] On HalfCheetah, removing clipped double Q-learning entirely scores around nine thousand seven hundred ninety-three — higher than the full method's own score in that same ablation run, roughly nine thousand five hundred thirty-three. The mechanism that gives the algorithm its name actively costs performance on the one task where the headline margin over baselines is largest.
[O] Alright, let me make the strongest case for why this still matters, that wrinkle included. This paper is a direct answer to the reproducibility reckoning that was hitting deep reinforcement learning right around then — Henderson and colleagues had just shown that headline algorithmic gains often evaporated once you controlled for seeds, unreported hyperparameters, and implementation details. This paper responds in kind: ten seeds per task, a hyperparameter-matched DDPG control instead of just quoting the original paper's numbers, and released code and learning curves. That discipline is a big part of why it's still the default baseline eight years later.
[S] I'll grant every bit of that discipline. My problem is with what clipped double Q-learning is actually doing. Taking the minimum of two critics isn't eliminating bias, it's trading one bias for another, overestimation for underestimation, on the argument that underestimation is the safer failure mode. Plausible, but nobody in this paper actually measures how far below the true value the clipped estimate sits, the way figure one measures DDPG's overestimation directly. And the HalfCheetah ablation shows that trade can cost you exactly where it matters most.
[G] Both points are fair, and the paper itself doesn't fully resolve that tension. I'd add that "Our DDPG," the control doing most of the heavy lifting in the head-to-head table, is the authors' own retuned version of a competitor — carefully matched, but still built by the people proposing the new method, and it wins or ties two of the seven tasks outright.
[O] To be fair, they're transparent about that, and the retuning benefits DDPG, not T-D-three.
[S] Sure, but there's a second thing buried in a footnote — an unresolved discrepancy between the SAC numbers they report and SAC's own paper, never reconciled in the main text. Part of it is that they trained SAC for one iteration per environment step instead of the four the original SAC authors used.
[G] That's accurate, and worth saying plainly — it's a fair-comparison choice, matching T-D-three's own training budget, but it does mean the SAC number here isn't SAC running at its best. There's a related irony the authors couldn't have known yet — later versions of SAC folded clipped double Q-learning into their own critic update, so the two algorithms partly converged after the fact.
[O] Which, if anything, is evidence the underlying idea was sound, even if this particular paper's ablation is messier than the abstract suggests.
[S] Or it's evidence the field adopted a heuristic that works well in aggregate without anyone fully pinning down when it helps versus when it hurts. Both readings fit what's actually in front of us.
[G] If I'm scoring it: the overestimation diagnosis and the delayed-update logic sit on solid ground, direct theoretical and empirical evidence. Clipped double Q-learning is the paper's most useful contribution in practice and its shakiest in isolation — real gains on some tasks, a wash or worse on others, and no direct measurement of the underestimation it actually introduces.
[O] Zoom out for me, Theo — what did this paper actually change in the field?
[G] Very concretely, T-D-three and SAC became the two default baselines that essentially every continuous-control paper reported against, for years. Almost nobody proposed a new off-policy actor-critic method without a T-D-three number sitting in the results table.
[S] There's an evaluation lesson underneath that too, and it's part of why this belongs on this show even outside reinforcement learning circles — a learned value function's confidence in itself isn't trustworthy just because the training curve is climbing. You have to check it against something external, the way figure one checks DDPG against Monte Carlo returns.
[G] My read, going a little past what's actually on the page — that's structurally the same failure mode people worry about with reward-model overoptimization today. A learned score, repeatedly bootstrapped against and maximized, will drift from ground truth unless something explicitly holds it down. The paper never draws that line itself, but the shape of the problem carries over.
[G] If you take one sentence from this: overestimation bias reappears in continuous control exactly like it does in tabular Q-learning, and T-D-three's fix — twin critics with a minimum, a delayed actor, and smoothed targets — works well as a package, even though its own ablation shows the trick in its name isn't uniformly helping.
[O] Mine is that the real legacy here is as much the evaluation discipline as the algorithm — ten seeds, a fair DDPG control, released learning curves — landing right when the field needed exactly that example.
[S] And mine is: read the ablation table before you cite the abstract. Five of seven convincing wins is still an excellent paper, it just isn't quite the "every single environment" claim it sometimes gets quoted as.
