Today's paper is Reasoning Gym, Reasoning Environments for Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards, out of Open-Thought, with co-authors and advisors also affiliated with Scale AI and University College London.
[S] It landed at NeurIPS twenty twenty-five in the Datasets and Benchmarks track, and it's on the docket because it sits right at the center of an argument the whole field is having, whether reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards can keep scaling once it runs out of curated math problems to check answers against.
[O] To help us get past the headline numbers, we've brought in Rhys, who's spent real time with this library and its verifiers.
[G] Glad to be here. I'd frame the whole project as an answer to a supply problem. Training with reinforcement learning on verifiable rewards, RLVR for short, needs an endless stream of problems with checkable answers, and the field's two existing sources, hand-curated datasets and scraped internet text, both run out or go stale. This paper's response is to stop shipping a dataset and start shipping the code that generates one.
[S] Rhys, walk us through why the existing sources actually break down. GSM8K and MATH aren't nothing.
[G] They're not, but they're finite, and old enough now to have soaked into a lot of pretraining data. RLVR works by scoring a rollout against a checkable final answer instead of a learned reward model, so the recipe is only as good as its supply of checkable problems. Hand-curated sets like GSM8K and MATH are expensive to build, fixed in size, and eventually get memorized rather than reasoned through. Internet-scraped alternatives are cheap but noisy and error-prone, and the authors note that's getting worse as more of the web itself becomes model-generated text.
[O] So it's a genuine ceiling, not just an inconvenience.
[G] That's their framing, yes, they call it the dataset ceiling. Once a fixed benchmark leaks into pretraining, a high score can reflect recall rather than reasoning, and a fixed dataset also gives you no way to make problems harder once a model has mastered the easy version.
[S] Is this the first attempt to fix that with generators instead of datasets?
[G] No, and the authors are upfront about that. Tülu 3 builds its reinforcement learning corpus from prompts with objectively checkable answers. DeepSeek-R1 bootstraps with a few thousand curated long chain-of-thought examples and then grows to roughly six hundred thousand verified trajectories through rejection sampling. TextArena packages over fifty text-based games for exactly this kind of training. Logic-RL procedurally generates logic puzzles and shows they generalize to math competitions. There's also Absolute Zero Reasoner, which self-evolves its own curriculum using a code executor as the verifier, and a dataset called SYNTHETIC-ONE with one point four million tasks and verifiers. The two closest relatives the authors name are KORGym, which focuses on games, and Reasoning Core and GEM, both general-purpose libraries of procedurally generated reinforcement-learning environments for language models, much like this one.
[O] So the pitch isn't "we invented procedural generation for reasoning." It's "we built the broadest, most systematically organized version of it, with a hundred domains and difficulty as a first-class parameter."
[G] That's a fair summary of where they're positioning themselves.
[S] Let's get concrete, then. What is a task in this library, mechanically?
[G] It's a generator, not a question-answer pair. There are over a hundred of them, spanning categories the authors group as algebra, arithmetic, algorithmic reasoning, code, cognition, formal logic, games, geometry, graph theory, pattern induction, and a set of ARC-style abstraction tasks. Each generator exposes three kinds of parameters. Difficulty parameters directly scale complexity, things like graph node counts, polynomial degree, or word length. Structural parameters change the problem's nature entirely, dimensionality, constraint types, proof depth. And stylistic parameters vary the presentation, variable names, number formats, framing, without touching difficulty at all.
[O] Give me an example so this isn't abstract.
[G] Take the Rubik's cube task. It renders a scrambled cube as a text grid and asks for a solution in Singmaster notation, the same shorthand cubers actually use. Or Rush Hour, a sliding-block puzzle rendered as an ASCII board, where the model has to move a target car out through walls and other vehicles. There's a FIGlet-font task, where large block letters made of ASCII characters spell a hidden word the model has to read. And there's a binary-matrix task, computing the Manhattan distance from every cell to the nearest zero. All four are procedurally generated, all four have one exact, machine-checkable answer.
[S] And no two instances are ever identical.
[G] Right, because they're sampled fresh from the generator's parameter space rather than stored. That's the paper's central claim about contamination, exact-instance memorization becomes impossible, and the full data-generating process is exposed rather than hidden behind a fixed answer key.
[O] What does that actually buy you, practically, beyond harder to cheat on?
[G] Two things. First, a real curriculum. Because difficulty is a tunable parameter rather than a fixed property of the dataset, you can raise the bar the moment a model is reliably solving the current level, instead of running out of harder examples. Second, an evaluation set that can keep pace with the frontier. If a model saturates the easy configuration, you don't need a new benchmark, you turn the same generator's difficulty knob.
[S] That sounds good in theory, but a knob you control is also a knob that can be gamed if the verifier is sloppy. What stops a model from just pattern-matching the surface form of a task type instead of actually solving it?
[G] That's exactly what the second of the paper's three design principles is meant to guard against. Algorithmic verifiability, every task grades automatically with no human judgment, which is what makes it safe to put directly into a reward signal. Large solution spaces, tasks are built so that guessing or template matching pays poorly, which is their explicit defense against reward hacking. And parametric difficulty control, the curriculum property we just discussed.
[S] Built so that guessing pays poorly is a design intention. Do they measure whether it actually holds?
[G] Not directly, no, it's asserted as a design principle rather than verified with, say, an adversarial probe of the verifiers. I'd flag that as a real gap, and we'll come back to it.
[O] What's actually happening during training, mechanically?
[G] They train Qwen 2.5 Instruct, a three-billion-parameter open model, using GRPO, the critic-free policy-gradient method introduced with DeepSeekMath. The training reward is a weighted sum, one point zero times an accuracy term from the verifier, plus zero point two times a smaller auxiliary term that rewards well-formatted output. So format is in the loop, but it's capped at a fifth of the weight of actually being correct. The whole experimental program, every training run across every experiment in the paper, cost roughly fifteen hundred A6000 GPU-hours, rented from RunPod.
[O] That's a modest budget for this much coverage.
[G] It is, and that cuts both ways. It's a genuine strength that this is reproducible on a small budget. It's also a reason every single number that follows comes from one small model trained relatively briefly, which is going to matter a lot once we get to the results.
