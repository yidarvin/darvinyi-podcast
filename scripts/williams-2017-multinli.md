[G] SNLI was right there, and it had done real work. Five hundred seventy thousand human-labeled sentence pairs, the first corpus big enough to train a neural sentence encoder from scratch. That's what triggered a wave of attention and memory models built specifically for entailment. But it had a structural flaw: every premise sentence in SNLI is an image caption, pulled from Flickr photos.
[S] Which sounds like a nitpick until you think about what image captions can't talk about.
[G] Right. Captions describe a static visual scene, so they're short and concrete. Whole categories of meaning barely show up: tense, belief verbs like "know" or "think," modals like "should," quantifiers. A model can top out on SNLI without ever being tested on them, because the test barely contains them.
[O] And the second problem was just a ceiling. The best models were already within a few points of human agreement, so you couldn't tell strong systems apart anymore.
[G] That's the paper's own framing, yes, limited headroom and limited coverage. There was also a related question the authors cared about. In vision, you can pretrain a feature extractor on one dataset and it transfers to a new one with little retraining. In language understanding at the time, that essentially never worked. Models trained on one style of text just didn't generalize to another, and nobody had a dataset built to actually measure that gap.
[S] So MultiNLI is trying to fix two different problems with one corpus. Make it harder, and make it a generalization test, at the same time.
[G] Exactly the pitch. Whether it delivers on both is worth separating out, and there's a lot to unpack.
[O] Walk us through how they actually built it. Same recipe as SNLI?
[G] Same collection mechanic, much wider net. A crowdworker sees one premise sentence and writes three hypotheses for it: one that's definitely true given the premise, that's the entailment label; one that's definitely false, contradiction; and one where it's genuinely unclear, neutral. What changes is where the premise sentences come from. Instead of one genre of image captions, MultiNLI draws on ten genres of American English.
[S] Name them for me, because "ten genres" undersells how different these are from each other.
[G] Five are written non-fiction: Slate magazine articles, Oxford University Press nonfiction, U.S. government reports and speeches, linguistics commentary from the Verbatim archives, and Berlitz travel guides. Two are spoken, transcribed: telephone conversations from the Switchboard corpus, and face-to-face conversation transcripts. Two are less formal written genres: contemporary fiction, and philanthropic fundraising letters. And the tenth is a single specialized source, the public report of the 9/11 Commission.
[O] That's a genuinely wide stylistic range. Government prose and telephone small talk are about as far apart as English gets.
[G] Which is the point. Altogether that's roughly four hundred thirty-three thousand pairs, about three hundred ninety-three thousand for training, twenty thousand held out for development, twenty thousand for test.
[S] Here's the part I actually want to slow down on, because it's the design choice the whole paper is built around.
[G] The matched-versus-mismatched split. Only five of the ten genres, fiction, government, Slate, telephone, and travel, appear in the training set at all. The other five, including the 9/11 Commission report and face-to-face conversation, appear only in development and test. So every model gets evaluated twice: once on matched test data, drawn from genres it trained on, and once on mismatched test data, from genres it's never seen a single example of. Same corpus, two different questions.
[O] One number for in-domain skill, one number for genuine transfer.
[G] That's the intent. On the collection side, they piloted five separate annotation prompts, one per genre family, each with its own FAQ, because a prompt written for concrete image captions doesn't obviously work for a government report. They ran this through a crowd platform called Hybrid, similar to Mechanical Turk, with an organized pool of three hundred eighty-seven annotators. Every development and test pair got relabeled by four more workers on top of the original writer, so you end up with five labels per example, and the gold label is whichever label wins the majority vote.
[S] And if nothing gets a majority?
[G] It's marked with a dash and excluded from standard evaluation. That happened to under two percent of pairs, about the same rate as SNLI.
[O] Nora, how well did that actually hold up, going from captions to government reports and 9/11 Commission testimony? I'd expect agreement to fall apart on harder text.
[G] That's the genuinely surprising result in this section, and the authors say so themselves, they call it surprising. Individual annotator agreement with the gold label was eighty-eight point seven percent on MultiNLI, versus eighty-nine percent on SNLI. Barely moved. And the gold label matched what the original writer intended ninety-two point six percent of the time, actually a bit higher than SNLI's ninety-one point two percent.
[S] Even though the text itself got harder.
[G] Measurably harder. Mean premise length is twenty-two point three words in MultiNLI versus fourteen point one in SNLI, with some premises running past four hundred words. And ninety-one percent of MultiNLI premises parse as a complete sentence, versus seventy-four percent for SNLI's captions. Hypotheses are also less copy-paste: only zero point nine percent of them are built by simply deleting a word from the premise, versus two point five percent in SNLI.
[O] Okay, let's get to the number that made this paper famous. How much harder is it, in practice?
[G] They trained three baselines to find out: a bag-of-words model, a bidirectional LSTM, and ESIM, which was roughly the strongest published entailment model at the time, all on top of three-hundred-dimension GloVe vectors. Trained and tested purely on SNLI, all three clear eighty percent: bag-of-words eighty point six, the LSTM eighty-one point five, ESIM eighty-six point seven.
[S] And on MultiNLI?
[G] Only ESIM breaks seventy: seventy-two point three percent on the matched test set, seventy-two point one on mismatched. The LSTM lands at sixty-six point nine on both. Bag-of-words is at sixty-four point eight matched, sixty-four point five mismatched. That's the fifteen-point gap you opened the show with. The same ESIM architecture drops from eighty-six point seven to seventy-two point three just by swapping which corpus it's trained and tested on.
[O] And the floor for comparison?
[G] A trivial most-frequent-class guess gets you about thirty-six point five percent on the matched set. So seventy-two percent is real signal, well above chance, just a lot further from the ceiling than SNLI models were.
[S] Here's the number I actually flag, though. They also trained on MultiNLI mixed with a slice of SNLI, to see whether more data just helps across the board.
[G] It doesn't, not for MultiNLI. Mixing SNLI in lifts ESIM's SNLI score dramatically, from sixty point seven up to seventy-nine point seven percent. But its MultiNLI score barely moves, seventy-two point three to seventy-two point four matched, and it dips slightly mismatched. The two corpora aren't interchangeable training signal, even though they're built the same way.

This corpus did exactly what it said on the label: it took a benchmark that had saturated and restored real headroom, and it did that while holding annotation quality steady on much harder text. [S] They do say that, and it's an honest hedge, but a hedge isn't a result. Second part: nowhere in this paper do they run a hypothesis-only baseline. A model that's shown the hypothesis sentence and nothing else, no premise at all, and still has to guess entailment, neutral, or contradiction.
[O] Why would that matter? The premise is half the input.
[S] Because crowdworkers were writing three hypotheses per premise to hit three specific labels, over and over, at scale. People develop tells under that kind of repetition, generic-sounding sentences for entailment, negation words for contradiction, long hedgy sentences for neutral. If those tells are strong enough, a model can score well above chance while literally never reading the premise.
[G] And that's exactly what happened, just not in this paper. Within about a year, two separate groups, Gururangan and colleagues, and Poliak and colleagues, ran that hypothesis-only baseline on crowdsourced NLI data. On SNLI it reached roughly sixty-seven percent. On MultiNLI, roughly fifty-three percent. Compare that to the thirty-six point five percent majority-class floor this paper reports as its difficulty bar.
[O] So a huge chunk of that seventy-two percent isn't reasoning about two sentences, it's pattern-matching one sentence's surface form.

They write, almost in passing, that their models "may be tracking it as a predictor of contradiction." That's the artifact showing up in their own data, one section before the paper where it gets named and measured by others.
[S] Which is my broader point about that phenomenon analysis, by the way, the thirteen tagged categories. Real, measurable diversity. But model accuracy on almost every one of those tags lands within about three points of the overall average. The one exception worth naming is comparatives and superlatives, where all three models lose three to four points.
[G] So the diagnostic proves MultiNLI contains harder phenomena. It doesn't show those phenomena are where the models actually fail. That's a real methodological achievement independent of whether the top-line accuracy number is fully clean.
[S] Sure, and I'd rather have this corpus exist, flaws included, than not. My objection isn't to the dataset. It's to trusting seventy-two percent as a clean read on sentence understanding without the hypothesis-only number sitting right next to it in the same table.
[G] If I'm scoring it: point to the optimist case on scope, diversity, and annotation quality, those are real and the paper demonstrates them directly. [O] So where does that leave the corpus for someone building an eval today?
[G] As a cautionary template, mostly. The lesson that outlived MultiNLI's own difficulty score is: if your data collection process rewards workers for hitting a target label, assume there's a shortcut baked into the label distribution, and go measure it before you publish a difficulty number. Report the hypothesis-only or premise-only score right next to your headline accuracy, not as a footnote papers later.
[S] It's the same discipline you'd want for contamination checks now, or judge reliability with LLM graders. Always ask what a model with degraded or missing input can still get right, because that tells you what your metric is actually rewarding.
[O] And worth saying, the headroom didn't even last. Within about a year, BERT alone pushed MultiNLI accuracy up to roughly eighty-six point seven percent, right back to where SNLI had topped out. Stronger models since have pushed it into the low nineties.

[G] If you take one thing from the paper itself, take this: MultiNLI proved you can scale entailment data collection across ten genres of English without losing annotation quality, and that's a real, demonstrated result.
