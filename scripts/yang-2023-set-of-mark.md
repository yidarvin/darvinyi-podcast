[O] Picture this. You show G-P-T-four-V a photo and ask it to output the coordinates of a dog in the image. It basically can't do it. Now you take the exact same photo, draw a few numbered marks on the regions in it, and ask the same frozen model which numbered region is the dog. Accuracy goes from twenty-five point seven percent to eighty-six point four percent. No training. No new weights. Just stickers.
[S] And I want to plant a flag right here at the top, because that number is doing a lot of persuading. It comes from roughly a hundred images, hand-picked, run one at a time through a chat window, with an off-the-shelf segmentation model doing a large share of the actual work before G-P-T-four-V ever sees the picture.
[O] Sure, but look at where that lands them. On referring expression segmentation, this zero-shot, frozen combination beats a specialist model that was fully fine-tuned for exactly that task. That's not nothing.
[S] It's something. Whether it's what the title claims, "extraordinary" grounding suddenly emerging inside G-P-T-four-V, or something a lot more mundane, like handing an already strong model a well-posed multiple-choice question instead of an open-ended one, is exactly what we should spend the next half hour figuring out.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist and a skeptic argue about a paper until someone who actually knows it settles the score.
[S] Today's paper is Set-of-Mark Prompting Unleashes Extraordinary Visual Grounding in G-P-T-four-V, by Jianwei Yang and colleagues at Microsoft Research, with collaborators at H-K-U-S-T and the University of Wisconsin-Madison, posted to arXiv in October twenty twenty-three.
[O] And joining us is Elena, who's spent real time with this paper. Welcome, Elena.
[G] Thanks for having me. And the standard disclosure up front: I'm not one of the authors, so when I say "they show" or "the paper argues," that's me describing their work, not claiming any of it as mine.
[S] Let's start with the gap, Elena. G-P-T-four-V landed and everyone was blown away by how much it could describe and reason about in an image. So what couldn't it do?
[G] Point precisely. The paper opens with concrete failure cases pulled from public reports at the time: G-P-T-four-V could hardly produce an accurate sequence of coordinates for a dog in a photo, and it failed on something as simple as locating a few traffic lights. High-level scene understanding was there. Fine-grained spatial grounding was not.
[O] Why not just train it to output coordinates directly? Plenty of detection models do that.
[G] Two reasons the authors give. First, forcing an autoregressive language model to emit numeric coordinate sequences fights against the fluency it's actually good at as a text generator. Second, and this is the more interesting point, it throws away spatial understanding that the vision encoder inside the model may already have, rather than exploiting it. They lean on Chen and colleagues' Pix2Seq reasoning here: coordinate regression is simply an unnatural output mode for a model built to produce language.
[S] Okay, but visual prompting wasn't a new idea in October twenty twenty-three. People were already drawing on images to help these models.
[G] Right, and the paper is explicit about that lineage. A red circle around an object, from the RedCircle work. A highlighted region, from another prompting paper. Circles and arrows, from the early qualitative "Dawn of Large Multimodal Models" report on G-P-T-four-V itself. All of those demonstrate the promise of pixel-level prompting, but every one of them is limited to pointing at one or a couple of objects at a time.
[O] And the deeper problem?
[G] Speakability. A red circle isn't something the model can cleanly say back to you in text. There's no natural way for G-P-T-four-V's output to unambiguously refer to "that circle" versus a different one if you've drawn several. So the actual gap this paper identifies is a prompting method that can mark many regions at once, with symbols the model can both recognize on the image and reproduce verbatim in its own words.
[S] Which is the whole idea in one sentence, basically. Turn pointing into naming.
[G] Exactly. And once you frame it that way, the method itself is almost embarrassingly simple to state, even though getting it to work took real engineering.
[O] Walk us through the mechanism, Elena, because "draw some numbers on the picture" undersells what's actually happening.
[G] There are three moving parts, and importantly, none of them touch G-P-T-four-V itself. This is entirely an input-image transformation. First, image partition. The authors assemble a toolbox of off-the-shelf segmentation models: SEEM and S-A-M for open-vocabulary, promptable segmentation, Semantic S-A-M for multi-granularity masks that can capture whole objects or just their parts, and Mask DINO where they need strong closed-set precision, like on COCO. Depending on the task, they pick whichever tool in that toolbox suits it, and it divides the image into a set of binary-mask regions, automatically or with a user click.
[S] Four different segmenters feels like a lot of moving parts for a paper about a simple prompting trick.
[G] It is, and that's worth sitting with, because every one of those tools has its own error profile, and none of that error gets separately measured later. But functionally, the point is granularity and coverage: S-A-M and Semantic S-A-M give you parts as well as wholes, SEEM gives you open vocabulary, Mask DINO gives you precision on a closed set like COCO's eighty categories.
[O] Second part?
[G] Marking. Once you've got regions, you have to decide what symbol goes where. The authors use numbers, letters, mask outlines, or boxes, chosen so the marks never collide semantically with the image's own content, no numeric marks on a photo of an arithmetic problem, no alphabetic marks on a screenshot full of text. Naively dropping each mark at its region's centroid causes constant overlaps in cluttered scenes, so they built what they call a mark-allocation algorithm. It sorts regions from smallest to largest area, excludes area already claimed by smaller regions already placed, then runs a distance transform on what's left to find the point inside each region that's farthest from any boundary. That pushes each mark toward open space instead of stacking on top of its neighbors.
[S] And the third part is the actual prompting?
[G] Right. Once every region has a unique mark, you can prompt plainly, no mention of marks at all, and the paper shows G-P-T-four-V will spontaneously ground its own answer in them anyway, saying something like "the lamp, which is numbered as twelve." Or you can interleave marks directly into the question, "which region is the laptop behind the beer bottle." Either way, because each mark maps one-to-one to a mask, anything G-P-T-four-V names can be traced straight back to a pixel-level region. That's the trick: a purely text-generating model ends up producing answers that are effectively pixel-grounded, without ever touching a pixel itself.
[O] So you've turned an open-ended pointing task into something closer to multiple choice, and multiple choice happens to be exactly the kind of thing a language model, with strong optical character recognition, is already good at.
[G] That's precisely the reframing, and it's the thing to hold onto going into the results, because it cuts both ways in how you interpret them.
[S] Let's get to numbers, then. What's the headline?
[G] Referring expression comprehension on RefCOCOg. Prompt G-P-T-four-V to output coordinates directly, and it scores twenty-five point seven percent accuracy. Add Set-of-Mark, same frozen model, zero additional training, and it jumps to eighty-six point four percent. That single comparison is presented as the paper's core verification of its own hypothesis.
[O] And that beats real specialists, not just other language models.
[G] It does. Eighty-six point one for Grounding DINO, eighty-five point eight for both PolyFormer and Ferret, eighty-four point four for MiniGPT-v2, eighty-two point six for Shikra. Those are strong, purpose-built systems, and this frozen, zero-shot combination edges past all of them on that number.
[S] "That number" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. What about the rest of the table?
[G] It's a broad table, six tasks. On open-vocabulary segmentation, G-P-T-four-V plus Set-of-Mark gets seventy-five point seven precision on COCO, close to fully fine-tuned Mask DINO's eighty point seven, and sixty-three point four on A-D-E-twenty-K, dramatically ahead of the zero-shot specialist OpenSeeD's twenty-three point four. On referring expression segmentation, measured in mean intersection over union, it hits seventy-five point six against specialist PolyFormer's sixty-seven point two. On video object segmentation on DAVIS twenty-seventeen, it gets seventy-eight point eight, best of any generalist model tested, ahead of SegGPT's seventy-five point six. Phrase grounding on Flickr thirty K is close to a tie, eighty-nine point two against Grounding DINO's ninety point five.
[O] That's a consistent pattern across genuinely different tasks, not one cherry-picked number.
[S] Consistent, sure, but look at how it was measured. Table one in the paper lists the actual sample sizes: a hundred images for COCO, a hundred for A-D-E-twenty-K, a hundred for Flickr thirty K, a hundred for RefCOCO, seventy-one videos for DAVIS. And every one of those specialist comparison numbers with an asterisk in their table is pulled from that specialist's own paper, evaluated on the full validation set. So most of this table is a hundred-image subsample going up against a full-dataset score.
[G] That's a fair and, frankly, an honest limitation the authors state plainly. They didn't have G-P-T-four-V API access in October twenty twenty-three, only a quota-limited chat interface, so every instance was run by hand, one new chat window per instance specifically to avoid context leaking between examples.
[S] Which also means no reported variance across repeated runs of the same prompt, and no independent check on how outputs were parsed and scored. If I wanted to reproduce these exact numbers today, I'd have real trouble.
[G] Agreed, and there's a second confound sitting right in the results, which the authors actually surface themselves through an ablation. Swap the segmentation toolbox's predicted masks for ground-truth masks on RefCOCOg, and mean intersection over union jumps from seventy-five point six to ninety point one. That's fourteen point five points, from mask quality alone, with nothing about G-P-T-four-V changing at all.
[O] So a meaningful chunk of the reported number is really measuring the segmenter, not the model.
[G] Right, and the paper is upfront about it, though it doesn't fully separate the two errors anywhere else in the results. There's a companion ablation on mark type, too: adding boxes on top of numbered masks lifts Flickr thirty K recall from eighty-four point four to eighty-nine point two, so the extra shape cue genuinely helps.
[S] Anything in that ground-truth ablation actually favor the optimist case?
[G] One thing worth flagging: the authors say most of the remaining failures under ground-truth masks weren't G-P-T-four-V getting confused, they were the human annotations themselves being ambiguous or wrong, like a referring phrase for "the legs of a person on the couch" where several people were annotated. So some of what looks like model error in these datasets may actually be dataset noise the model is correctly refusing to match.
[O] Let's do the actual debate, then, because I think this paper is a bigger deal than the sample-size caveats suggest. The idea spread almost immediately. According to the litsearch writeup, SeeAct was testing Set-of-Mark directly as a web-agent grounding strategy three months later, in January twenty twenty-four, and WebVoyager built its entire action space around a D-O-M-derived variant of the same idea. That's not a benchmark number, that's an idea an entire subfield adopted within a season. You don't need a huge validation set to prove a mechanism is real; you need it to be reproducible enough that other labs build on it immediately, and this one was.
[S] I'll make the opposite case, and it's not really about the sample sizes anymore, it's about what the paper is claiming to have discovered. The title says "extraordinary" grounding, and the discussion section calls it a "mystery." But look at what actually changed: they didn't teach the model anything new. They took a model that already has strong optical character recognition, and instead of asking it to regress raw coordinates, they gave it a closed set of numbered choices to select from. That's not an emergent capability appearing out of nowhere, that's a much more mundane story, a hard open-ended task turned into an easy closed one. And on top of that, there's no contamination discussion anywhere in the paper. COCO, RefCOCOg, Flickr thirty K, these are old, canonical, endlessly reproduced datasets almost certainly represented somewhere in G-P-T-four-V's training data.
[G] Both of you are pointing at something real, so let me split it. On contamination, Sam's right, it's simply not addressed. COCO and RefCOCOg and Flickr thirty K are about as web-scraped-to-death as image datasets get. DAVIS and A-D-E-twenty-K are somewhat less likely to be captioned all over the web in the same way, so those two results are probably a bit more informative about genuine generalization, but the paper doesn't make that distinction itself. On the "mystery" framing, the authors are actually more careful in the text than the title suggests. They tested other open models, LLaVA-1.5 and MiniGPT-v2, and found neither could interpret or ground on the marks at all, only G-P-T-four-V could. Their own hypothesis is scale, plus a data curation guess, that G-P-T-four-V may have seen a lot of clearly labeled figures and charts during training. And then, verbatim, they write that these suspicions are not grounded in fact, just empirical observation.
[S] So the paper's own explanation for its central finding is a shrug.
[G] Essentially, yes, and I think that's actually the most honest sentence in the discussion section. They have a real, replicated effect, twenty-five point seven to eighty-six point four, backed by two clean ablations that isolate specific variables. What they don't have is a mechanistic account of why only G-P-T-four-V does this. Those are different claims, and conflating them is where "extraordinary" starts to feel like it's carrying more weight than the evidence supports.
[O] But even without knowing the "why," the "that it happens" part is the part other people built on. SeeAct and WebVoyager didn't need the mystery solved, they needed the marks-to-grounding trick to actually work on a frozen model, and it did.
[S] Sure, and that's a real point in the optimist's favor. I'd just add that anyone building on this should also inherit the caveats, not just the headline number, because the segmenter-quality confound doesn't go away just because the idea proved useful elsewhere.
[G] That's the throughline here. There's a useful lesson for evaluation practice generally: a mechanism can be genuinely real and worth adopting immediately, while the specific benchmark numbers attached to its debut paper remain a rough proof of concept that a later, better-resourced study would need to firm up.
[O] Which happened for the diffusion of the idea, but arguably never happened for the numbers themselves. Nobody went back and ran this on the full validation sets once G-P-T-four-V got an API.
[S] And that's worth remembering every time this paper gets cited as "G-P-T-four-V beats specialist models," because the fine print is a hundred images and an ungraded segmenter standing behind that claim.
[G] If I had to leave you with one sentence, it would be the paper's own framing, not mine: making a visual prompt speakable, something the model can name back to you in its own text, is what unlocked grounding here, more than any change to the model itself.
