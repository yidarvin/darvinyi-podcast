[S] I'm the skeptic, and I think it's a landmark paper that, by its own admission, doesn't prove what its most-quoted number implies.
[O] The paper is "Large Language Models Encode Clinical Knowledge," from Karan Singhal and a large team at Google Research and DeepMind, published in Nature in 2023. You probably know it by its model's name, Med-PaLM.
[S] Joining us to walk through it is Julian, who's spent real time with this paper and the evaluation framework it introduced.
[G] Glad to be here. This is one of those papers where the headline number and the paper's actual contribution are almost different stories, and I think that tension is worth taking seriously rather than resolving too quickly.
[O] Julian, set the stage. Before this paper, how were people even evaluating whether a language model knew medicine?
[G] Almost entirely two ways, and the authors are pointed about both. First, multiple choice. You give a model a board-exam-style question, it picks an option, you score accuracy. That's the default because it's cheap and unambiguous to grade.
[S] But accuracy on a four-option question tells you almost nothing about whether a free-text answer is actually safe to act on.
[G] Exactly the authors' point. The second approach was long-form generation scored with something like BLEU, an automated text-overlap metric that compares a model's answer to a single reference string. The authors argue that's a poor fit for medicine, because a good answer can be phrased many different ways, and a text-overlap score can't tell you if an answer is factually wrong, incomplete, or dangerous.
[O] And medicine is exactly the domain where "dangerous" isn't an abstraction.
[G] Right. The authors frame answering a medical question as requiring three things at once: reading comprehension of the clinical context, recall of the correct medical fact, and reasoning over that knowledge. None of that shows up in a single accuracy number, and none of it was being measured for free-text answers at all. That's the gap this paper set out to close.
[S] So before we even get to a model, the actual contribution might be the evaluation, not the exam score.
[G] That's exactly the paper's own framing, and I'd hold onto it, because the headline the world remembers is closer to the opposite.
[O] Walk us through what they actually built, Julian. Start with the benchmark.
[G] It's called MultiMedQA, and it's a bundle of seven datasets, not one. Six already existed: MedQA, which is US Medical Licensing Exam questions, USMLE for short; MedMCQA, entrance-exam questions from Indian medical schools; PubMedQA, yes-or-no-or-maybe questions grounded in a PubMed abstract; the six clinical subtasks of MMLU, covering things like anatomy and professional medicine; and LiveQA and MedicationQA, both drawn from real consumer questions submitted to health information services.
[S] That's a lot of different formats under one name.
[G] Deliberately so. Multiple choice and long form, open domain and closed domain, exam questions and consumer questions. The point is that a model can't game one trick across all of that. And they add a seventh dataset themselves: HealthSearchQA, three thousand three hundred seventy-five consumer health questions pulled from real search engine queries. That's the new data contribution.
[O] So the benchmark is broad. What about the human evaluation you flagged as the real point?
[G] This is where the paper does its most careful work. They built a panel of nine clinicians, based in the UK, the US, and India, and had them rate long-form answers along twelve separate axes: agreement with scientific consensus, the extent and likelihood of harm, evidence of correct or incorrect comprehension, retrieval, and reasoning, inappropriate or missing content, and possible bias. Separately, five lay users, all based in India, rated whether an answer addressed what the person actually wanted to know, and how helpful it was. Every answer got exactly one rating, and raters didn't know which system produced it.
[S] One rating per answer. Hold that thought, we'll come back to it.
[G] That's coming, because the authors flag it themselves as a limitation, which I respect.
[O] Now the model side.
[G] They start from PaLM, a five-hundred-forty-billion-parameter language model, and its instruction-tuned sibling, Flan-PaLM, tested at eight billion, sixty-two billion, and five hundred forty billion parameters. To squeeze out multiple-choice performance, they stack three prompting strategies: few-shot examples written together with clinicians, chain-of-thought reasoning, and self-consistency, where you sample many reasoning chains for the same question and take whichever final answer shows up most often. They fixed that at eleven chains per question for the multiple-choice runs.
[S] And none of that touches the model's weights.
[G] Correct, pure prompting. But strong multiple-choice scores didn't fix the long-form answers. So the authors add their actual named contribution: instruction prompt tuning. Instead of fine-tuning all five hundred forty billion parameters, which is prohibitively expensive, or replacing the hand-written prompt entirely, they learn a small soft prompt: a hundred trainable tokens, about one point eight four million parameters total, prefixed in front of the full hard prompt of instructions and examples, with the rest of the model completely frozen.
[O] A hundred tokens, one point eight million trainable numbers, sitting in front of a five-hundred-forty-billion-parameter model.
[G] That's the whole mechanism. They trained it on just forty clinician-curated examples, for two hundred steps, using five clinicians from the US and UK to write and filter those exemplars. The resulting model is Med-PaLM: Flan-PaLM plus that instruction-prompt-tuned soft prefix.
[S] So to be precise about naming, since I think this matters later: Flan-PaLM is the base instruction-tuned model. Med-PaLM is Flan-PaLM plus this small trained prefix.
[G] That distinction is exactly right, and exactly where I'd tell listeners to pay close attention going forward.
[O] Let's get to numbers. Start with the exam scores everyone quotes.
[G] On MedQA, the four-option USMLE-style questions, Flan-PaLM five hundred forty billion hit sixty-seven point six percent accuracy. The prior best was PubMedGPT at fifty point three percent, so that's a jump of seventeen point three percentage points, and it beat a model called DRAGON, at forty-seven point five percent, by just over twenty. On the harder five-option version of the same questions, it scored sixty-two percent.
[S] Who exactly is it beating, though?
[G] I'll flag that now because it matters for the debate. PubMedGPT is two point seven billion parameters, DRAGON is three hundred sixty million, BioGPT is three hundred fifty-five million. Flan-PaLM is comparing a five-hundred-forty-billion-parameter general model against domain models that are two to three orders of magnitude smaller.
[O] Keep going, there's more than just MedQA.
[G] On MedMCQA, the Indian medical entrance exam questions, Flan-PaLM reached fifty-seven point six percent, past the prior best of fifty-two point nine from a model called Galactica. On PubMedQA it hit seventy-nine percent, edging past BioGPT's seventy-eight point two. But here's a wrinkle worth stating: the single-rater human ceiling on PubMedQA is itself only seventy-eight percent, so there's almost no headroom left on that dataset regardless of what any model does.
[S] So one of the four "state of the art" wins is basically already saturated.
[G] Fair characterization. On the clinical subtasks of MMLU, Flan-PaLM topped every other large model they compared against: OPT, BLOOM, Galactica, Gopher, Chinchilla. Eighty-eight point nine percent on college biology, eighty-three point eight on professional medicine, eighty point four on clinical knowledge.
[O] What about the ablations? What's actually doing the work?
[G] Two things matter most. Scale: going from eight billion to five hundred forty billion roughly doubled accuracy, and the eight-billion model was barely above random guessing on MedQA. And self-consistency: that sampling-and-voting trick is responsible for almost the entire MedQA jump, from sixty point three percent with plain few-shot up to sixty-seven point six. But the same trick actively hurt PubMedQA, dropping it from seventy-nine percent to seventy-five point two. And plain chain-of-thought, with no voting, never beat simple few-shot on any of the three datasets.
[S] So the "combination of prompting strategies" in the abstract is really "self-consistency helps a lot on one dataset and hurts another."
[G] That's a fair compression of it. One more result worth mentioning: a selective-prediction experiment. If you use the self-consistency vote count as a confidence signal and let the model skip the questions it's least sure about, accuracy on MedQA climbs to eighty-two point five percent once it's allowed to defer on forty-five percent of the questions.
[O] Now the part I actually think is the real headline: the human evaluation.
[G] This is where multiple-choice success stopped translating. On a hundred forty long-form consumer questions, clinicians judged only sixty-one point nine percent of Flan-PaLM's answers to be aligned with scientific consensus, versus ninety-two point nine percent for clinician-written answers. Instruction prompt tuning closed nearly the entire gap: Med-PaLM reached ninety-two point six percent.
[S] That's the number that actually moves me.
[G] Potential harm followed the same arc. Twenty-nine point seven percent of Flan-PaLM answers were flagged as potentially harmful, down to five point nine percent for Med-PaLM, against five point seven percent for clinicians. Correct retrieval of medical knowledge went from seventy-six point three up to ninety-five point four, versus ninety-seven point eight for clinicians. Missing content dropped from forty-seven point two percent down to fifteen point one, against eleven point one for clinicians. And on the lay side, perceived helpfulness rose from sixty point six percent to eighty point three, still short of clinicians at ninety-one point one.
[O] Nearly every axis, moving the right direction, from one cheap soft prompt.

[O] Alright, let's make the cases properly. I'll go first. This is a landmark paper because it did something evaluation papers rarely do: it built the yardstick and the model at the same time, and showed the yardstick actually surfaces problems the exam score hid. A hundred tokens, forty examples, two hundred training steps, and it moved scientific-consensus alignment from sixty-two percent to ninety-three percent and cut potential harm roughly five-fold. That's an efficient, generalizable alignment technique, and publishing "still inferior to clinicians" instead of overselling it is exactly the kind of rigor I want to see rewarded.

The paper's two famous claims, the exam score and the safety story, come from two different models on two different evaluations, and "Med-PaLM" gets public credit for both.

[S] Third: contamination. MedQA, MedMCQA, PubMedQA, and MMLU are all public. PaLM trained on seven hundred eighty billion tokens spanning the open web, books, and code. That's a dataset-level argument, not a check of whether these specific medical test questions sit in the training corpus.
[G] That's accurate, and it's the weakest piece of evidence in the paper for the exam claims specifically. No per-question decontamination check is run on MedQA, MedMCQA, PubMedQA, or MMLU. I'd call that an open question the paper doesn't resolve, rather than a refutation of the result, but the skeptic is right that the existing citation doesn't close it.
[O] Let me push back somewhere too, because I don't think the human eval gets a completely free pass either. Julian, is a hundred forty questions and nine clinicians actually enough to support a headline safety claim?
[G] Good pushback, and the authors are unusually candid here. They say directly, in their own limitations section, that the results rest on a single clinician or lay person rating each answer, and they call that a limitation to generalizability. They do run a non-parametric bootstrap for sampling uncertainty around each percentage. But bootstrap confidence intervals over one rating per item tell you about sampling noise, not about whether a second clinician would agree with the first one's rating.
[S] So the ninety-two point six percent for Med-PaLM has an error bar around which questions got sampled, but not around whether a different clinician would've rated that same answer the same way.
[G] Correct, and that's a real gap between what's measured and what a safety claim usually requires.

[G] Right, and that's the one axis where instruction prompt tuning regressed instead of improved. My own reading, going a bit beyond what the paper states directly, is that the tuned model produces longer, more complete answers, and more content is more surface area for something in it to be wrong. The "closes the gap to clinicians" framing genuinely skips past that trade-off.

[O] Julian, adjudicate for us. On balance, who's got the stronger case here?
[G] I'd score it this way. On "is the human-evaluation framework a genuine methodological advance," the optimist wins clearly, and I don't think that's close. On "does the exam headline prove a medical breakthrough," the skeptic wins: it's mostly scale, tested against undersized domain baselines, on datasets with no per-question contamination check. On "is Med-PaLM safe and ready for the clinic," neither side gets to claim victory, because the paper's own limitations section already concedes the sample size, the single-rater design, and the incorrect-content regression. The authors are careful people. Read past the abstract, and they said most of this about themselves.
[O] So what does this mean beyond medicine, for how we should be running evals generally?
[G] Three transferable lessons, I'd say. One: multiple-choice accuracy and any single automated metric like BLEU can diverge sharply from what actually matters, so wherever the downstream use is high-stakes, you need a rubric-based human evaluation layer, not just an accuracy leaderboard. Two: a small, cheap alignment technique like this soft prefix can move safety-relevant behavior a lot without touching the base model, which is a useful lever whenever full fine-tuning is too expensive or too data-poor. Three, and this is the cautionary one: publish your limitations as clearly as your results. Single-rater bias, no per-question contamination check, a metric that regressed. All stated in the paper itself, not buried in an appendix nobody reads.
[S] For me the biggest transferable lesson is the naming and framing gap. When two different systems produce two different headline numbers under one product name, readers merge them into one story. That's not unique to this paper, and it's worth watching for anywhere a benchmark score and a safety claim get bundled under a single brand.
