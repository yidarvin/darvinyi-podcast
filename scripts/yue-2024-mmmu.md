[S] And GPT-4, the same underlying model, blindfolded, given zero images, scores thirty-three point eight percent on that exact same test.
[O] Which sounds like a modest gap, until you notice that thirty-three point eight percent statistically ties the best open-source model that could actually see the pictures.
[S] So a chunk of a benchmark built to test "can you look at a chemical structure diagram and reason like an expert" turns out to be solvable with your eyes closed. That's the paper we're doing today.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio. Today's paper is MMMU, A Massive Multi-discipline Multimodal Understanding and Reasoning Benchmark for Expert AGI, by Xiang Yue, Yuansheng Ni, Kai Zhang, Tianyu Zheng, and colleagues, including Huan Sun, Yu Su, and Wenhu Chen, published at CVPR in twenty twenty-four.
[S] It's on the docket because it's basically the paper that reset what "solved" meant for multimodal models, right as the field was starting to believe vision-language models were done.
[G] Thanks for having me. I've spent a lot of time with this dataset and its appendix, and it's a genuinely useful benchmark with some real cracks in the headline number, which is exactly what makes it worth a close read.
[O] We'll get to those cracks, but first, walk us through why this needed to exist. Amelia, set the scene.
[G] By late twenty twenty-three, multimodal models looked almost solved on the benchmarks that existed. CogVLM, for instance, was hitting eighty-five percent on VQA-v2, ninety-two percent on ScienceQA-IMG, ninety-three percent on RefCOCO.
[O] So on paper, vision-language models had basically caught up to humans.
[G] On those particular tasks, yes, but those tasks test everyday perception. Is there a dog in this photo, what color is the sky, point to the object called "cup." That's not the kind of specialized seeing-and-reasoning a trained radiologist or a circuit engineer does.
[S] Meanwhile the depth side of the field lived entirely in text. MMLU, AGIEval, that whole family, tests whether a model has college-level knowledge, but shows it no images at all.
[G] Right, and there was one benchmark trying to bridge that, ScienceQA, which does span many subjects and does include images. But the majority of its questions sit at an elementary-to-middle-school level. It has breadth without depth.
[O] So nobody had built the multimodal version of a college final exam.
[G] Exactly, and the authors frame the target explicitly. There's a leveled taxonomy for AGI from Morris and colleagues, and Level 3 on that scale, "Expert AGI," is defined as roughly the ninetieth percentile of skilled adults across a broad range of tasks. College exams are built precisely to certify skilled adults in a discipline, so they're the natural instrument, and MMMU is essentially the multimodal sibling that MMLU and AGIEval left on the table.
[S] That's a fairly ambitious framing for a benchmark paper. "We're measuring progress toward Expert AGI" is a big claim to put in your title.
[G] It is, and to their credit the authors hedge it directly in the conclusion. They explicitly say MMMU shouldn't be read as a sufficient test for Expert AGI, only a necessary one. Strong performance doesn't prove you've reached that level, but weak performance is still informative.
[O] Okay, let's get into what's actually in the benchmark.
[G] Eleven thousand five hundred and fifty questions, spanning six disciplines: Art and Design, Business, Science, Health and Medicine, Humanities and Social Science, and Tech and Engineering. Underneath those sit thirty subjects and a hundred and eighty-three subfields.
[S] That's the breadth axis. What's the depth axis actually testing?
[G] Two things prior multimodal benchmarks skipped. First, image heterogeneity, thirty different image types. Not just photographs, but diagrams, tables, charts, chemical structures, paintings, medical scans, sheet music, geometric figures, pathology slides, microscopy images, comics. Second, interleaved text and images, meaning a single question can reference several images inline, tagged image one, image two, and so on, so the model has to fuse the two modalities rather than caption one picture and reason over the caption.
[O] Give me a concrete example.
[G] One sampled question shows a breast MRI, three separate sequences, subtraction, T2-weighted, and T1-weighted axial, and asks for the etiology of a finding in the left breast. You need radiology knowledge, and you need to actually read three medical images correctly. Another shows a transistor circuit and asks you to compute a voltage using standard circuit-analysis reasoning. A third is a music theory question, identifying which of four harmonic intervals is built incorrectly, straight from sheet music notation.
[S] How do you source eleven thousand five hundred questions like that without just scraping the open web wholesale?
[G] A team of over fifty college students, several of them coauthors, each specializing in one of the thirty subjects, collected questions from textbooks, past exams, quizzes, and lecture materials, and wrote new ones where they couldn't find good source material. That produced about thirteen thousand raw questions.
[O] And thirteen thousand down to eleven-five is what, cleanup?
[G] Three passes. First, deduplication, using lexical overlap and source-URL similarity to catch near-duplicates, reviewed by the authors. Second, a formatting and typo pass across everything collected. Third, difficulty labeling into four tiers, very easy, easy, medium, hard, and the very-easy tier, about ten percent of the pool, was dropped entirely as not meeting the design bar.
[S] And the contamination question. If you're pulling from textbooks and exam banks that are sitting in some web crawl somewhere, how do you guard against a model having just memorized the answer key?
[G] The mitigation is instructional, not structural. Annotators were told to favor questions where the answer isn't immediately adjacent, things where the answer lives in a separate answer-key document, or at the back of the textbook rather than right below the question. It's a sensible first line of defense. It is not a strong one, and we should come back to that in the debate.
[O] Noted. What about the splits and the actual scoring?
[G] Eleven-five-fifty splits into a hundred and fifty-question development set, five per subject, for few-shot prompting; a nine-hundred-question validation set, used for hyperparameter and prompt selection; and a ten-thousand-five-hundred-question test set. About ninety-four percent of items are multiple choice, the rest open-ended. Ninety-seven and a half percent include at least one image, and about seven percent carry more than one. On difficulty, it's roughly twenty-eight percent easy, forty-five percent medium, twenty-seven percent hard.
[S] And evaluation itself?
[G] Zero-shot, scored by micro-averaged accuracy. Because models often produce long chain-of-thought responses rather than a clean "the answer is B," the authors built rule-based regular expressions to extract the final answer. And here's a detail that matters a lot for reading the numbers later: if the extraction pipeline can't find a valid answer in a multiple-choice response, it doesn't count it wrong outright, it performs a random selection as a stand-in. Open-ended questions with no extractable answer are simply marked wrong.
[O] That sounds like it could quietly reward or punish a model based on how it formats an answer, not just on whether it knows the answer.
[G] It can, yes. Keep that in your pocket, it's going to matter.
[S] Let's get to the scoreboard, then.
[G] At submission, the best open-source models, models like BLIP-2 FLAN-T5-XXL and LLaVA-1.5, landed around thirty-four percent. GPT-4V, the strongest proprietary model tested, scored fifty-five point seven percent on the test set, fifty-six point eight on validation. Gemini Ultra was reported at roughly fifty-nine percent. Human experts, ninety college seniors representing all thirty subjects, three per subject, working open-book on the nine-hundred validation questions, scored eighty-eight point six percent at best.
[O] So an eighty-plus point gap between the best model and the best humans. That's a real headline.
[G] It is, and the paper frames it as evidence of the benchmark's rigor rather than a weakness in the models. But here's the row that actually matters most, and it isn't a vision model at all. GPT-4, with zero image input, scored thirty-three point eight percent on test, thirty-four point nine on validation. That's statistically tied with the best open-source models that were actually looking at the pictures.
[S] Which is the number from the cold open. Say more.
[G] It cuts two ways. One, it tells you a meaningful share of MMMU questions are answerable from the question text, the answer options, and the model's own parametric knowledge, without ever seeing the image. Two, it tells you that in late twenty twenty-three, open-source vision-language models weren't extracting much signal from their visual channel at all, otherwise they'd have cleared that text-only floor by a wide margin, and they mostly didn't. The authors also tried bolting OCR or LLaVA-generated captions onto the text-only LLMs, and that barely moved the needle. FLAN-T5-XXL alone scored thirty-one point two on test, and adding OCR or captions only nudged it up to about thirty-one point nine.
[O] Two more cuts sharpen this, right, difficulty and image type?
[G] Right. By difficulty, GPT-4V goes from seventy-six point one percent on Easy questions, down to fifty-five point six on Medium, down to thirty-one point two on Hard, and on that Hard slice it's sitting barely above open-source models, which cluster around twenty-six to twenty-nine percent, not far above the roughly twenty-four percent random floor. Its big lead on Easy questions has essentially evaporated by the time you get to Hard.
[S] And image type?
[G] Every model, GPT-4V included, is close to random guessing on geometric shapes, sheet music, and chemical structures, the image types least like what shows up in web-scale pretraining data. Performance is meaningfully better on photographs and paintings, the types that do resemble typical pretraining images. By discipline, GPT-4V's strongest area is Humanities and Social Science at seventy-six point three percent, and its weakest are Tech and Engineering at forty-one point seven and Science at forty-eight point four, exactly where the visuals get dense and the reasoning chains get long.
[O] What did the actual errors look like when you dig into a GPT-4V mistake?
[G] The authors hand-annotated a hundred and fifty GPT-4V error cases. Thirty-five percent were perceptual errors, where the model got the reasoning and the knowledge right but misread the image. Twenty-nine percent were a lack of domain knowledge. Twenty-six percent were flawed reasoning, where perception and knowledge were both fine but the logical steps broke down. The rest split across textual-understanding errors, outright refusals to answer, annotation mistakes in the data itself, and answer-extraction failures.
[O] Give me one of the perceptual ones, they're always the most interesting failures.
[G] There's a good one in the paper, a question about an oxygen-mask scenario on an airplane, with three sub-images the model has to map to options A, B, and C in reading order, left to right, top to bottom. GPT-4V recalls the correct concept, reconciling egoism with other-isms, and reasons about it accurately. It just maps the wrong image to the wrong letter, because the ordering was described in text rather than marked visually. Right knowledge, right reasoning, wrong pointer.
[S] That's not a knowledge failure at all, that's basic spatial indexing.
[G] Exactly, and there's a second pattern worth naming, text bias. In one case, a political cartoon literally depicts the United States as a "Savior," and GPT-4V overrides that visual content with its own prior association of the word "imperialism," and answers wrong. It's leaning on textual priors over what the image is actually showing.
[O] And the field moved fast after this, right? The paper's own numbers already look dated.
[G] Very fast. Over the following year, open models like LLaVA-1.6-34B and InternVL-Chat-V1.2 climbed to roughly forty-five to forty-six percent on test. Frontier models kept climbing on validation too, Claude 3 Opus at fifty-nine point four, Gemini 1.5 Pro at sixty-two point two, GPT-4o at sixty-nine point one. MMMU basically became the default multimodal leaderboard.
[O] Alright, let's have it out. My case: this paper did exactly what a field-defining benchmark should do. Multimodal models looked "solved" on the old benchmarks, eighty-five, ninety, ninety-three percent, and this reset that illusion in one stroke. Fifty-five point seven percent for the best model in the world, against eighty-eight point six for humans, is an honest, wide-open gap, measured across thirty genuinely different subjects and thirty genuinely different image types, not one narrow task. That combination hadn't existed before, and it immediately became the standard the whole field climbed against.
[S] Here's the deflationary case, and the paper hands us the evidence for it itself. GPT-4 with zero image input ties the best vision-equipped open models. That's not a footnote, that's a construct-validity problem. A meaningful fraction of a benchmark that calls itself "multimodal" is solvable without looking at the picture. And they report that number, credit to them, but they don't act on it. There's no vision-necessary subset, no per-item flag for "a blind LLM already gets this right," nothing that separates genuine multimodal reasoning from parametric-knowledge guessing.
[O] To be fair, reporting an inconvenient number at all is more than a lot of benchmark papers do.
[S] Agreed, and I'll give them credit for exactly that. But there's a second issue layered on top, the scoring mechanics. Unparseable multiple-choice answers get filled in at random rather than marked wrong, on a benchmark that's ninety-four percent multiple choice, with regex extraction pulling answers out of long chain-of-thought text. The reported accuracy is quietly blending genuine correctness with extraction luck and a roughly twenty-four percent random floor. Different model families format answers differently, so they're not cleanly comparable on this metric even before you get to what they actually know.
[G] I'd score this claim by claim, because you're both right about different things. On "did this reset the field's ceiling," that's clean and true. Before this paper, the community's honest read was "multimodal is basically solved," and after it, nobody believed that anymore. That's a real, valuable correction, and it happened because the benchmark is genuinely comprehensive, not because of marketing.
[S] I'll take that point.
[G] On construct validity, I think you're right, and it's the paper's sharpest actual weakness. A text-only GPT-4 row sitting within a point or two of the best vision-only open models is hard to explain away, and the honest fix, measuring blind-LLM solvability per item and shipping a vision-necessary subset, is straightforward enough that its absence reads as an oversight rather than a hard technical constraint. On the scoring mechanic, you're also right that it adds noise, though I'd weight it a bit less heavily than construct validity, since random-fill mostly bites models that fail to follow instructions, and the strongest models mostly do produce parseable answers.
[O] So how much of the headline number do you actually trust?
[G] I trust the ranking more than the absolute score. GPT-4V clearly outperforms twenty twenty-three-era open models, and clearly underperforms humans, that ordering is robust to all of this. What I don't fully trust is the precise gap, because the Hard slice and several image types sit near the random floor for everyone, which means the micro-average is disproportionately carried by the Easy tier, and that's exactly what a single headline percentage hides.
[S] There's a contamination angle too. Textbooks and exam banks that are two clicks from a search engine aren't a strong barrier against a model that's seen a paraphrase of the same question during pretraining, especially with no private, periodically refreshed split.

[O] So what does this actually change for how we build and read evals?
[G] A few concrete practices. First, report a blind baseline for any benchmark that claims to require a particular modality. If a text-only or vision-only ablation ties the full model, that's information the reader needs up front, not buried in an appendix. Second, treat "no valid answer" as its own category in scoring rather than randomly filling it in, an explicit abstention or parse-failure bucket keeps format-following separate from reasoning ability. Third, report per-slice numbers, not just one micro-average. This paper does report by difficulty and by image type, which is good practice, but the single headline percentage is still what gets quoted in every subsequent paper's related-work paragraph.
[S] And the contamination point generalizes past this one paper. Any benchmark built from public textbooks and exam banks needs a private, periodically refreshed holdout if you want its number to mean the same thing years later that it meant at release.
