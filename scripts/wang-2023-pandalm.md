[O] Here's a claim that should make you sit up: a seventy billion parameter open model nobody's heard of, built largely by copying GPT-3.5's homework, outscores GPT-4 at judging other language models.
[S] Copying GPT-3.5's homework, then grading it against a one thousand sample human test set it never saw. That's not nothing, but "outscores GPT-4" is doing a lot of work for a margin under half a percentage point.
[O] Half a point is still the right side of the line. And that's before you get to the part that actually matters: using that judge to pick better training recipes.
[S] Which is the part worth taking seriously. The "beats GPT-4" headline, less so.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio. Today's paper is PandaLM, An Automatic Evaluation Benchmark for LLM Instruction Tuning Optimization, from Yidong Wang, Zhuohao Yu, and colleagues at Peking University, Westlake University, and Microsoft Research Asia, published at ICLR twenty twenty-four.
[S] And joining us to actually referee this fight is Tamsin, a researcher who's spent real time in this paper's appendices.
[O] Tamsin, welcome. Set the scene for us — why was picking hyperparameters for instruction tuning such a mess back in twenty twenty-three?
[G] Because the only two ways to judge a candidate model were both broken for iterative use. Human evaluation is reliable, but it's slow, expensive, and inconsistent between annotators — you can't run it eighty times to sweep a hyperparameter grid. API judges like GPT-3.5 or GPT-4 solve the speed problem, but the authors call out three new ones: the model is closed-source so you can't inspect it, the API version drifts under you so an evaluation from last month may not reproduce today, and sending your candidate outputs to a third party risks leaking whatever's in your training data.
[O] And the metrics that are already free — task accuracy, or something like BERTScore comparing generated text to a reference — those don't work either?
[G] Right, and the paper is specific about why. Standard accuracy from a framework like lm-eval doesn't capture the subjective qualities that actually separate two competent instruction-tuned models — conciseness, clarity, whether the response follows the instruction, how comprehensive or formal it is. BERTScore and MAUVE measure similarity to a reference text, not which of two responses is better, and a similarity score isn't something a practitioner can act on when picking between checkpoints.
[S] So the gap is: nothing fast, free, reproducible, and actually judging quality the way a person would.
[G] That's exactly the gap PandaLM is built to fill.
[O] Walk us through what PandaLM actually is.
[G] It's a judge language model fine-tuned from LLaMA. Give it an instruction, an input, and two candidate responses, and it outputs three things: which response wins — response one, response two, or a tie — a natural-language reason for that verdict, and its own reference response, the answer it thinks would have been ideal.
[S] Why bother generating a reference answer? Isn't the winner label the actual product here?
[G] Because that third output is what makes the judgment diagnostic instead of just a scoreboard. Forcing the model to write a reason and construct its own ideal answer means it has to identify concrete problems — logical fallacies, unnecessary repetition, grammatical errors, missing context — rather than pattern-matching to a preference. The paper tests this directly: train a version of PandaLM on only the win, lose, tie label, no reason, no reference, and accuracy collapses from fifty-nine point two six percent to forty-seven point two five percent, F1 falls from fifty-four point five six percent to thirty-one point five two percent. The reasoning and reference outputs aren't decoration, they're roughly half the model's skill.
[O] That's a big ablation. So how do you get training data for something like this without paying humans to label a million comparisons?
[G] Distillation. Five similarly-sized foundation models — LLaMA seven B, Bloom seven B, Cerebras GPT six point seven B, OPT seven B, and Pythia six point nine B — are each instruction-tuned on the same Alpaca fifty-two thousand dataset, using Alpaca's own hyperparameters. Pairs of their responses to Alpaca's instructions get judged by GPT-3.5-turbo, following a self-instruct-style prompting setup, and that produces the training triples: a verdict, a reason, and a reference.
[S] And raw GPT-3.5 judgments are noisy.
[G] Very. Two filters clean it up. Hand-crafted rules strip out invalid evaluations first. Then, more importantly, every comparison gets run twice with the two responses swapped in order — if GPT-3.5 flips its verdict just because the same pair got reordered, that's a position bias, and the sample gets dropped rather than kept. What survives is three hundred thousand training samples.
[O] And the actual training run?
[G] Standard sequence-to-sequence fine-tuning, cross-entropy on next-token prediction, no separate classification head. Eight A100 eighty-gigabyte GPUs, DeepSpeed with ZeRO stage two, b-float16 precision, AdamW at a learning rate of two times ten to the negative five with a cosine schedule, two epochs, batch size two per GPU with eight-step gradient accumulation, inputs truncated to one thousand twenty-four tokens.
[S] Two epochs on three hundred thousand samples is not a huge run by today's standards.
[G] It isn't, and that's part of the point — a seven billion parameter model, cheaply tuned on distilled data, ends up competing with GPT-4 on this task. There's a nice appendix result that shows why fine-tuning matters here. An untuned LLaMA seven B, just scoring which continuation has higher likelihood with zero fine-tuning, gets twelve percent accuracy. An untuned thirty billion parameter LLaMA does better, thirty-one point four three percent, climbing to forty-two point two four percent with five-shot prompting. PandaLM-7B, fine-tuned and generative, hits fifty-nine point two six percent. Purpose-built beats big, at least for this task.
[O] How do we know PandaLM's opinions are actually good, and not just a copy of GPT-3.5's opinions?
[G] That's what the test set is for, and it's built completely separately from the training data. The instructions and inputs come from self-instruct's human-evaluation set — genuinely human-written contexts pulled from Grammarly, Wikipedia, National Geographic, and roughly a hundred other apps and sites, not anything model-generated. Three independent human annotators, hired from an annotation company with no relationship to the authors, each label every instance. Samples where the three disagreed too much got dropped, to keep every annotator's agreement with the others above a Cohen's Kappa of point eight five.
[S] What's the final size, after filtering?
[G] Two thousand five hundred raw instances, filtered down to one thousand. The label split is four hundred twenty-two wins for response one, four hundred seventy-two for response two, and one hundred five ties. Final pairwise kappas were point eight five, point eight six, and point eight eight across the three annotators.
[S] Let's get to the actual scoreboard. How does PandaLM do against GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 on that one thousand sample human test set?
[G] Table two in the paper. GPT-3.5 scores sixty-two point nine six percent accuracy, with an F1 of fifty-eight point two percent. GPT-4 scores sixty-six point four seven percent accuracy, F1 of sixty-one point eight percent. PandaLM-7B comes in at fifty-nine point two six percent accuracy, F1 fifty-four point five six percent — below both. But PandaLM-70B hits sixty-six point eight seven percent accuracy, and an F1 of sixty-nine point two three percent.
[O] So PandaLM-70B genuinely edges past GPT-4.
[G] On accuracy, yes, sixty-six point eight seven against sixty-six point four seven, a gap of about four tenths of a percentage point. On F1 it's a clearer separation, about seven and a half points, mostly because PandaLM-70B has much higher precision, seventy-four percent against GPT-4's sixty-six point two percent.
[S] Four tenths of a point is not what I'd call "surpasses." That's noise-adjacent.
[O] Hold that thought, we're doing the debate properly in a minute. Tamsin, what about generalizing beyond Alpaca-style instructions?
[G] They test that with domain shift — legal LSAT questions, and biomedical PubMedQA and BioASQ, none of which resemble the Alpaca training distribution. It's a three-way win, lose, tie classification, so random guessing sits around thirty-three percent. PandaLM-7B clears that everywhere: forty-seven point one seven percent on LSAT, sixty-one point five four on PubMedQA, fifty-one point five two on BioASQ. PandaLM-70B does considerably better across the board: sixty-six point zero four percent on LSAT, seventy-six point nine two on PubMedQA, seventy-seven point two seven on BioASQ.
[S] Those domain-shift numbers are graded against GPT-4, not humans, right?
[G] Correct, and the paper says so directly — "due to constraints in time," GPT-4 stands in for the gold label there instead of human annotators. That's disclosed, but it means those specific numbers measure agreement with GPT-4, not with ground truth.
[O] Now the part I actually care about — does using PandaLM to pick hyperparameters produce a better model, or is this all just judge-versus-judge trivia?
[G] This is the paper's real payoff. PandaLM-7B searches eighty hyperparameter configurations per base model — five epoch checkpoints, four learning rates, two optimizers, two schedulers — split into blocks of twenty with sequential pairwise comparisons, eight hundred comparisons total across the five models. The winning configuration per model then gets compared against that same model tuned with Alpaca's stock hyperparameters, on identical training data, judged independently by GPT-3.5, GPT-4, and human annotators, across a hundred seventy held-out instructions.
[S] And the result favors PandaLM's picks.
[G] Every judge, every base model. Averaged across the five models, under GPT-4 the PandaLM-tuned versions win forty-seven wins against twenty-six point two losses. Under human judges it's even more lopsided, seventy-nine point eight wins against twenty-five point two losses.
[O] That's the strongest number in the whole paper to me. It's not "PandaLM agrees with GPT-4," it's "using PandaLM makes a materially better model, and humans confirm it."
[S] I'll grant that one's clean — same training data, same base models, only the hyperparameter selection changes, and humans independently prefer the PandaLM-selected version by more than three to one.
[G] One wrinkle worth flagging, though. When they also check the PandaLM-tuned models against pure downstream-task benchmarks — ARC, HellaSwag, MMLU, TruthfulQA — most models improve, but Cerebras actually gets slightly worse on that lm-eval average, dropping from about thirty-eight point four percent to thirty-seven point five percent, even though humans, GPT-4, and GPT-3.5 all independently preferred the PandaLM-tuned Cerebras in the pairwise comparisons. Subjective preference and objective task accuracy aren't the same thing, and they can point in different directions for the same model.
[O] Okay, let's actually argue about what this means. My case: this is a genuinely useful primitive for the field. You get a judge that's within half a point of GPT-4 on accuracy and beats it on F1, it costs nothing per call once trained, it never changes version under you, and none of your data leaves your machine. It's not just agreeing with GPT-4 in the abstract — pointed at a real hyperparameter search, it produces models that human comparisons prefer over the Alpaca defaults by more than three to one. That's not a toy benchmark win, that's a better model at the end of the pipeline.
[S] My case: PandaLM is not an independent judge, it's a compressed copy of GPT-3.5's opinions with exactly one bias filtered out, response-order flipping. Every one of those three hundred thousand training labels came from GPT-3.5, checked only for internal consistency, never for correctness. So when PandaLM-70B "beats" GPT-4 by four tenths of a point on accuracy, that isn't evidence PandaLM understands quality better, it's evidence a model trained on GPT-3.5's opinions happens to correlate slightly more with these particular human labels than GPT-4 does. And the model most people would actually deploy, PandaLM-7B, trails GPT-3.5 on every single metric in Table two, despite the abstract calling it "comparable" to GPT-3.5 and GPT-4.
[G] Both points are grounded, and I'd add the one the paper spends the least time on: the ceiling problem. The three human annotators only agree with each other at a Cohen's Kappa between point eight five and point eight eight. That's not literally the same statistic as PandaLM's accuracy number, but it's the right intuition check — even humans checking each other's work land in that band, and PandaLM-70B's accuracy against the human majority label is sixty-six point eight seven percent, well below where two humans agree with each other. The paper says as much itself, proposing point nine zero agreement as, quote, a challenging yet achievable goal above the point eight five human baseline it already has. "Surpasses GPT-4" is real on the metric it's measured on, but it's surpassing another imperfect judge, not closing the gap to human-level agreement.
[O] So it's less "solved evaluation," more "found a slightly better proxy."
[G] That's a fair read. Though I'd push back gently on one part of the skeptic case, the claim that PandaLM was never tested on models stronger than its distillation pool. There's an appendix result, a model-shift analysis, where they run PandaLM against sixty-five billion and seventy billion parameter LLaMA-1-versus-LLaMA-2 comparisons, well outside the six to seven billion parameter range it was trained and validated on. PandaLM prefers LLaMA-2 there, matching what humans prefer, with an F1 around point six eight. It's narrow, three model-pair comparisons is not a benchmark, but it's more generalization evidence than "distilled judge, never tested outside its box" gives credit for.
[S] Fair, I'll take that correction. Thin evidence, but not nothing.
[O] And the reproducibility and privacy argument, is that actually tested, or just asserted?
[G] Mostly asserted. The paper is explicit that by "privacy" they mean the principle behind federated learning, keeping data local, not something they measured. There's no leakage audit, no comparison of what an API call would have exposed versus what stays on-device. It's a real structural property of running an open model locally, but it isn't an empirical result the way the accuracy numbers are.
[O] Zooming out, where does this sit in the bigger evaluation story?
[G] PandaLM is an early, deliberately modest entry in what became a much bigger line of work, LLM-as-judge. Within about a year, benchmarks like MT-Bench and AlpacaEval were running similar pairwise-judge setups at far larger scale, and a whole sub-literature grew up specifically auditing judge biases, length bias, position bias, self-preference, that this paper only partially addresses here.
[S] Which is the honest frame for it — a proof of concept that an open, offline judge is viable at all, not a finished solution to judge reliability.
[O] And a genuinely useful one for anyone who can't, or won't, send training-adjacent data to an API.
[G] If there's one line to remember: PandaLM shows a small, open judge, distilled from GPT-3.5 and filtered for one specific bias, can get close enough to GPT-4 to be useful for the job that actually matters here, ranking hyperparameter configurations, where humans confirm its picks are genuinely better.
[O] My takeaway: this is what a private, reproducible evaluation pipeline looks like, and it's going to matter more as people stop trusting API judges by default.
[S] Mine: read every "beats GPT-4" headline as "beats GPT-4 on this specific thousand-sample test," and check what the seven billion parameter version, the one you'd actually run, really does before you believe the seventy billion parameter footnote.
