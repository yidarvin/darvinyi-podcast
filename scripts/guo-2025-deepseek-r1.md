[G] Glad to be here. And to be clear up front, I didn't write this work, I've just read it closely, so when I say "the authors show," I mean exactly that and nothing more.
[S] Let's start with the setup, then, before we get to the fireworks. What was broken about the way reasoning models were built before this?
[G] The standard recipe going into late twenty twenty-four was: take a base language model, supervise it on human-written chains of thought, then polish with reinforcement learning from human feedback. Chain-of-thought prompting had pushed math and code accuracy up substantially. But every bit of that gain rode on annotated reasoning traces written by people.
[O] Which is expensive and doesn't scale.
[G] Right, that's the first problem the authors name directly. The second is subtler and more interesting: if you train a model to imitate human reasoning, its ceiling is human reasoning. It can't discover a better, non-human-like way to solve a problem, because you've only ever shown it human ways.
[S] And OpenAI's o1 had just shown that scaling reasoning length at inference time works spectacularly.
[G] Exactly, but the method behind o1 was never published. So the open question this paper set out to answer was sharp: can a model develop reasoning entirely on its own, from nothing but a reward for getting the final answer right, skipping supervised fine-tuning altogether? If reasoning is something you can incentivize rather than teach, the annotation bottleneck disappears, and so does the human ceiling.
[O] And they answer yes, in public, with weights released.
[G] They do. And this became, as far as I can tell, the first reasoning model paper to be peer-reviewed and published in Nature.
[S] Okay, so walk us through the method. What actually happens in the room where this training occurs?
[G] Start with DeepSeek-V3-Base, a six hundred seventy-one billion parameter mixture-of-experts model, thirty-seven billion active per token, pretrained on fourteen point eight trillion tokens. They apply reinforcement learning directly to it, no supervised fine-tuning first. The reward isn't a learned network, it's a rule: an accuracy reward checks the final answer against ground truth, a boxed number verified by a parser for math, a compiler running test cases for code. A format reward just checks the model wraps its thinking in "think" tags. Nothing constrains how it reasons inside those tags.
[S] So no supervision at all on the actual reasoning process.
[G] None. The optimizer matters too. They use Group Relative Policy Optimization, GRPO, inherited from earlier DeepSeek math work. The standard approach, PPO, needs a separate value model roughly as large as the policy, just to estimate how good each token is, doubling memory. GRPO deletes it.
[O] How does it estimate value without one?
[G] For each question it samples a group of outputs, say sixteen, scores every one, and uses the group's own mean and standard deviation as the baseline. An output is good exactly to the degree it beats its siblings on the same question. No separate network, roughly half the memory, cheaper rollout at scale.
[S] Elegant trick. Does it actually work?
[G] Run that loop, sixteen samples per question, batch size five hundred twelve, around ten thousand four hundred update steps, and you get DeepSeek-R1-Zero. Zero human reasoning data, zero supervised fine-tuning. But it has real problems: chains of thought that are hard to read, English and Chinese mixed mid-sentence, and because rule-based reward only covers verifiable domains, it's weak at writing and open-domain question answering.
[O] So the model you'd actually want to talk to isn't R1-Zero.
[G] Right, which is why the released DeepSeek-R1 is a four-stage pipeline, not a single RL run. Stage one, cold start: fine-tune the base model on a few thousand curated long chain-of-thought examples, clean and human-readable, written in first person. Stage two, reasoning RL: the same rule-based RL as R1-Zero, plus a language-consistency reward measuring what fraction of the reasoning is in the target language, which slightly hurts accuracy but kills the mixing. Stage three, rejection-sampling SFT: generate responses, filter for correctness, train on about six hundred thousand reasoning samples plus two hundred thousand non-reasoning ones, restoring general ability. Stage four, preference RL: add a sixty-six thousand pair helpfulness reward model and a hundred six thousand prompt safety reward model, but only in the last four hundred steps.
[S] Why only the last four hundred steps?
[G] Because longer exposure to a model-graded reward gets hacked. The policy learns to satisfy the reward model's proxy rather than the underlying intent, and the authors show this directly: in an ablation, the measured reward keeps climbing while held-out accuracy on complex reasoning peaks and then falls. Textbook reward hacking, and the four-hundred-step cap is a direct mitigation for something they watched happen in their own runs.
[O] I love that they show you the failure mode instead of hiding it.
[S] I'll give them credit for that, it's genuinely unusual candor for an industry paper.
[O] Let's get to numbers, because this is where the paper earns its reputation. What happens to R1-Zero during that ten-thousand-step RL run?
[G] On AIME twenty twenty-four, thirty competition math problems, pass at one climbs from fifteen point six percent at the start to seventy-seven point nine percent by the end. With self-consistency across sixteen samples it reaches eighty-six point seven, above the average human competitor. And with no length incentive anywhere in the reward, the model's average response length grows on its own from a few hundred tokens to over ten thousand. Nobody told it to think longer, it learned that thinking longer pays.
[S] What's actually happening inside those longer responses?
[G] The authors track the frequency of reflection words, and specifically the word "wait," and both spike sharply partway through training. That's the signature behind the "aha moment." There's a table showing an intermediate checkpoint literally writing, quote, "Wait, wait. That's an aha moment I can flag here," then re-deriving a step it had gotten wrong. Self-verification, backtracking, exploring alternatives, none of that was in the reward. It's emergent.
[O] And the polished DeepSeek-R1, after the four stages, how does it actually compare to OpenAI's o1?
[G] Close to parity on math, ahead on some code metrics. AIME twenty twenty-four, seventy-nine point eight for R1 versus seventy-nine point two for o1 dash twelve seventeen. MATH-500, ninety-seven point three versus ninety-six point four. LiveCodeBench, R1 leads, sixty-five point nine versus sixty-three point four, and it sits at the ninety-six point three percentile on Codeforces. o1 keeps an edge on GPQA Diamond, seventy-five point seven versus seventy-one point five, and on SimpleQA, forty-seven versus thirty point one for R1.
[S] Worth saying, the human Ph.D. baseline on GPQA in the paper is eighty-one point two, so even the frontier models trail expert humans there.
[G] Right, that's included, not omitted. And the multi-stage pipeline is what makes R1 usable day to day, not just a benchmark specialist. Instruction-following jumps from R1-Zero's forty-six point six to eighty-three point three, AlpacaEval from twenty-four point seven to eighty-seven point six. R1-Zero can solve hard math and be nearly useless as an assistant. R1 does both.
[O] Now the distillation result, because this is the one that got the whole field's attention beyond just DeepSeek's own benchmarks.
[G] They take the eight hundred thousand samples from the rejection-sampling stage and fine-tune open Qwen and Llama models on them, no reinforcement learning on the student, just SFT. DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B scores twenty-eight point nine on AIME. GPT-4o scores nine point three, Claude-3.5-Sonnet scores sixteen. A one point five billion parameter model outreasons both on math.
[S] Worth being precise about what that's not: not beating o1-mini, not beating a reasoning model, beating non-reasoning generalists on a narrow benchmark.
[G] Fair, and the paper's own framing agrees, they say "non-reasoning baselines." The bigger structural finding: run large-scale RL on Qwen2.5-32B-Base from scratch, the same recipe as R1-Zero, over ten thousand steps, and you get roughly forty-seven percent on AIME. DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-32B, the same base just fine-tuned on R1's outputs, reaches seventy-two point six. Distilling beats growing a reasoner from scratch at that size, by a wide margin.
[O] Obvious economic reading: don't RL every model size, do it once on a big one and distill down.
[G] That's their own conclusion too, nearly verbatim.
[S] Let's talk about what didn't work, because I think that section gets skipped in most summaries of this paper.
[G] It shouldn't be skipped. They tried a process reward model, scoring intermediate steps rather than just the final answer, and found three problems: it's hard to define a discrete reasoning "step" in open-ended text, hard to automatically judge whether a step is correct, and any model-based reward eventually gets hacked. It helped a little for reranking but wasn't worth scaling up.
[O] And Monte Carlo Tree Search, inspired by AlphaGo?
[G] Explicitly citing AlphaGo and AlphaZero, yes. Break answers into steps, search the space like a game tree. Two hard walls: token generation is a vastly larger search space than a board game, so they capped how far each node could extend, which just pushed the model into local optima. And AlphaGo's success depended on a value model that kept getting better at judging positions, while a comparably reliable value model over partial reasoning chains proved much harder to train.
[S] So two ideas straight from the game-playing RL literature just didn't transfer.
[G] Correct, and that negative result is informative, not just a footnote.

[S] Before we wrap, does any of this actually matter for how people evaluate models day to day, or is it purely a training-recipe story?
[G] Quite a bit, actually. The whole method only works where a reliable verifier exists, a rule that checks correctness cheaply and can't easily be gamed. That constraint is exactly why the field's attention shifted hard toward rubric-based reward design right after this paper, trying to extend verifiable-style rewards into domains like open-ended writing or agentic tasks where no clean rule exists. And the reward-hacking curve in the appendix, reward climbing while true accuracy falls, is the illustration evaluation researchers now point to when explaining why you can't just hand a policy a learned reward model and walk away.
[O] So this is as much an evaluation-methodology paper as a training paper.
[G] I'd go that far. Contamination discipline, held-out fresh test sets, honest ablations on your own failure modes, that's the part I'd want every reasoning-model release to imitate, regardless of whether GRPO itself becomes the standard algorithm.
