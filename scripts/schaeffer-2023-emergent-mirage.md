[S] Here's a claim that should unsettle anyone tracking scaling: a model can go from zero to competent on a task the instant it crosses some hidden size threshold, with no warning at all.
[O] Except this paper says that jump might not live in the model. It might live in the ruler you're holding up to it.
[S] Same weights, same outputs, and depending on how you score them, you either witness a miracle or you see a straight line.
[O] So which one is real. That's the whole episode.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio. I'm the optimist, and I think this paper is one of the cleanest pieces of measurement science anyone's aimed at a large language model claim.
[S] I'm the skeptic, and I think "mostly a mirage" is doing a lot of work in that title, and we're going to press on exactly how much.
[O] The paper is "Are Emergent Abilities of Large Language Models a Mirage?" by Rylan Schaeffer, Brando Miranda, and Sanmi Koyejo at Stanford, a NeurIPS paper from 2023 that's since pulled in more than seven hundred citations.
[S] And to keep us both honest, we've brought in Desmond, a visiting scholar who's spent real time with this paper's math and the debate it kicked off.
[G] Glad to be here. This is one of those papers that reframes an entire conversation without training a single new model.
[S] Let's set the trap this paper is springing. Back in 2022, the field got excited about "emergent abilities" — the idea that a capability is simply absent in smaller models and present in larger ones, in a way you could not have predicted by extrapolating the small-model trend.
[G] That definition traces to Jason Wei and colleagues, and it names two properties that make emergence exciting: sharpness, the jump looks instantaneous, and unpredictability, nobody could have named the scale in advance.
[S] And this wasn't a fringe idea — it showed up in the original GPT-3 report, and separately in Ganguli and colleagues' work on predictability and surprise in large generative models.
[O] And the framing borrowed real prestige — the physicist P. W. Anderson's "More Is Different," the idea that a complex system can grow entirely new properties you could not have seen coming from its microscopic rules.
[S] Which is exactly why this got taken seriously in safety circles. If capabilities can switch on without warning, a bigger model might one day acquire something dangerous with zero notice.
[G] The authors noticed something almost too clean to be coincidence. The tasks where emergence gets reported cluster around two scoring rules: multiple choice grade, full credit only if the correct option carries the single highest probability, and exact string match, the output has to match the target character for character or it scores zero.
[O] Both of those hand out zero partial credit.
[G] Right. And that's the question nobody had asked first: is the cliff in the model's mind, or in the way we're grading its homework?
[S] Walk us through the alternative explanation, Desmond.
[G] It starts from something almost boringly well established: neural scaling laws. Within a model family, the per-token cross-entropy loss tends to fall smoothly, as a power law in the parameter count. That's been observed across a dozen prior papers, in language, vision, even board games.
[O] So the model is quietly, continuously getting better at predicting the next token the entire time.
[G] Exactly, and that means the probability of getting any single token exactly right rises smoothly toward one as you scale up. Here's the move: suppose the task you're grading requires getting a whole sequence of L tokens all correct — every digit of an addition problem, say. If each token's correctness is roughly independent, the probability of scoring a full one is the per-token probability raised to the L-th power.
[S] And raising a smooth quantity to a power is exactly the kind of operation that turns a gentle curve into a cliff.
[G] That's the entire mechanism. A quantity rising smoothly toward one, once exponentiated, spends a long time near zero and then shoots up late. Plotted against parameter count on a log scale, it looks like nothing was happening and then everything happened at once. That's accuracy, or exact match. Multiple choice grade produces the same shape through a different route — it's a step function, so a tiny shift in probability mass flips the score from zero to one outright.
[O] But keep the exact same model outputs and change only how you score them —
[G] — and the picture inverts. Token edit distance just counts how many tokens are wrong, so it scales close to linearly with the per-token error rate. No exponent, no step. Same underlying numbers, but now it's a ramp. The same swap works for multiple choice grade: replace it with the Brier score, which is continuous, and the step dissolves into a slope.
[S] So the authors are naming three ingredients that manufacture the illusion, not one.
[G] Right. First, a metric that nonlinearly or discontinuously deforms the per-token error rate. Second, too little test data to actually resolve how a small model is performing, so merely-bad reads as flatly incapable. Third, too few large models sampled to see the trend before the jump. None of those three is a fact about what's happening inside the model. All three are facts about the experiment.
[O] That's the theory. Where's the evidence?
[G] Three complementary tests, each harder to wave away. First, they went to the one family they could query directly: InstructGPT and GPT-3, four sizes — three hundred fifty million, one point three billion, six point seven billion, and one hundred seventy five billion parameters — on two tasks already cited elsewhere as emergent, few-shot multiplication of two two-digit integers and few-shot addition of two four-digit integers.
[S] And under plain accuracy?
[G] Textbook cliff, right where the target string hits four or five digits. Then, holding those exact same model outputs fixed, they re-score with token edit distance, and the family improves smoothly and predictably across the whole range. Same outputs. Only the ruler changed.
[O] That's a genuinely clean control — nobody can argue the model changed, because it didn't.
[G] There's a second piece. If accuracy is exponentiating a smooth curve, the theory predicts small models aren't actually at zero, they're just below what a small test set can resolve. So they generated more test examples to raise the resolution, and every model in the family scores above chance, with accuracy falling off roughly geometrically as target length grows, exactly the shape the math predicts.
[S] Okay, that's one model family, on arithmetic. What about everyone else's claims — LaMDA, PaLM, the whole zoo of papers that started this?
[G] That's test two: a meta-analysis of BIG-Bench, the large multi-task benchmark suite most of those claims draw on. Of the thirty nine preferred metrics BIG-Bench uses, at most five show any hint of emergence by their scoring criterion. On a hand-annotated list of one hundred thirty seven claimed emergent abilities that Jason Wei himself compiled, it narrows to four metrics. And more than ninety two percent of every claimed emergent ability sits under just two of them: multiple choice grade, at seventy six point one percent, and exact string match, at sixteen point four percent.
[O] If emergence were really about the model and the task, you'd expect it to show up regardless of which reasonable metric you used.
[G] Instead it clusters almost entirely on the two harshest ones. As a direct confirmation, they took LaMDA's emergent abilities as reported under multiple choice grade and re-scored the identical tasks with the continuous Brier score. The emergence vanishes.
[S] That's still working from published numbers, not model outputs they control themselves.
[O] Fair, but stay with me — the next test is where they stop analyzing anyone else's claims and start making their own.
[G] Exactly right. They manufacture the effect on demand, in a domain where nobody had ever reported emergence before: vision.
[S] Vision models "emerging"?
[G] Shallow autoencoders trained on CIFAR-100 — reconstruction error falls smoothly as you add capacity, completely unremarkable. Then they define a new metric: the fraction of images reconstructed below a fixed error threshold. Pick the threshold right, and a sharp, "emergent" jump in reconstruction ability appears out of nothing. They do the same with autoregressive transformers classifying Omniglot handwritten characters — accuracy on getting one character right climbs smoothly, but redefine success as getting every character in a five-character sequence right, and a brand-new "emergent" classification ability springs into existence. Same architecture, same training run, different bar.
[O] Two architectures isn't a fluke, that's a pattern.
[G] They push it to a third, too — a plain convolutional network, a LeNet, trained on MNIST digits. Redefine accuracy as getting several digits in a row all correct, and the same ordinary, gradually rising accuracy curve turns into a manufactured cliff as that requirement climbs. Three architectures, three tasks, one mechanism, and none of them had ever been described as emergent before the authors went looking for the effect on purpose.
[S] Same trick that condemned the accuracy metric in the first place.
[G] Which is really the whole argument in one sentence: on a single fixed set of model outputs, whether you see emergence is decided entirely by the metric you chose to grade them with.
[O] Alright, let's make the case both ways. My honest read: this is one of the cleanest pieces of measurement science I've seen applied to an LLM claim. It derives a mechanism, predicts three separate consequences, and confirms all three. That's how you attack a scientific claim properly, and I think it mostly wins.
[S] My case: mostly winning isn't fully winning. A smooth per-token loss does not make the thing you actually ship predictable. If I deploy an arithmetic tool and I care whether the final answer is exactly right, I'm using the harsh metric by definition, because that is what "right" means to my user. Relabeling the discontinuity as living in the metric doesn't make my planning problem, forecasting when the tool becomes trustworthy, go away.
[G] That's a fair hit, and one the authors don't fully resolve. Their own language is careful — they describe the claimed abilities as something that "might likely be" a mirage, not a proven impossibility, and they're explicit that nothing in the paper should be read as claiming large models can never display a genuinely emergent ability. What they've shown is that the cleanest reported cases dissolve under a metric swap. What they haven't shown is that every capability people actually care about decomposes the way arithmetic does.
[O] Meaning?
[G] The whole mechanism leans on a task factoring into roughly independent tokens, which digit-by-digit arithmetic does beautifully, and the authors themselves flag that the independence assumption is false and the match is only qualitative. It's far less obvious that multi-step chain-of-thought reasoning, or learning a genuinely novel task in-context, or following a complicated instruction, reduces cleanly to getting L tokens each independently right.
[S] So the strongest, most direct evidence is really one model family on one narrow task type.
[G] Correct — GPT-3, four sizes, arithmetic. LaMDA and the rest of BIG-Bench arrive through meta-analysis of published numbers, not fresh re-runs the authors controlled themselves. And they concede, in their own results, that up to five of the thirty nine BIG-Bench metrics still show some emergence signal even after their analysis, a residue they raise but don't fully chase down.
[O] There's also a worry that cuts the other way, in the authors' favor: multiple comparisons. BIG-Bench alone is something like two hundred twenty tasks, forty metrics apiece, across roughly ten model families — on the order of a million task, metric, and model triplets. At that scale, some of them look "emergent" by pure chance, no metric trickery required.
[S] Which argues for being even more skeptical of the original claims, not less.
[G] Right, that one cuts toward the authors. It's also worth naming that this isn't the only account on the table — other work has modeled emergence as a real, piecewise change in the governing scaling law itself, or as something that can be real under stronger assumptions about the training data. This paper doesn't engage those head to head. Its claim is narrower: that the specific evidence offered for emergence so far is largely explained by metric choice.
[O] So where does that leave us — is emergence dead?
[G] I'd put it this way: the paper decisively shows a large share of reported emergence is a measurement artifact. It does not show that a model can never acquire a capability abruptly in a way that matters to a downstream user. Those are different claims, and only the first one is fully nailed down here.
[S] So what does a benchmark builder actually do differently on Monday morning?
[G] Three concrete things the paper argues for. Treat the task and the metric as two separate design choices, not one. When you do pick a discontinuous or nonlinear metric, make sure you have enough test data to resolve small-model performance, so a merely-bad model doesn't get misread as categorically incapable. And correct for multiple comparisons when you're scanning hundreds of tasks and dozens of metrics for a headline result.
[O] There's an obvious tie back to evaluation practice generally — this is the same failure mode as a leaderboard that rewards exact match over graded partial credit. You get a cliff that's really a grading artifact, not a discovery.
[S] Which matters a lot for safety arguments specifically, since "capabilities can appear without warning" was doing real work in some alignment reasoning. If most of that warning-free jump is actually metric-shaped, that argument needs to be made more carefully, against the metric you actually deploy.
[O] It's also a pretty direct argument for why so much of the benchmarking world has been drifting toward graded, rubric-style scoring instead of blunt pass or fail. This paper is close to a formal proof of what pass or fail throws away.
[G] Agreed, and that might be the most important sentence in the paper, even though it reads like a footnote: a task and its metric are separate, meaningful choices, and conflating them is where this whole confusion started.
[G] If I had to leave the listener with one line, it's this: before you believe a capability jumped, ask what would have happened to the exact same model outputs under a kinder metric.
[O] Mine is that this is measurement hygiene winning a real argument — a paper that changed how carefully people talk about scaling curves, without training a single new model.
[S] And mine is that "mostly a mirage" still leaves a residue worth watching. The paper itself admits some emergence survives its own test, and the metric you actually ship with is usually the harsh one. Full writeup, with all the figures and exact numbers, is on the litsearch site.
