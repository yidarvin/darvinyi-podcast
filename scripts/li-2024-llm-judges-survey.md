[G] Section seven, and it's the strongest part of the paper by a wide margin. Fourteen named biases, sorted into four families. Presentation-related: position bias, where a judge favors an answer based on where it sits in the list, and verbosity bias, favoring longer answers regardless of content. Social-related: authority bias, bandwagon-effect bias, compassion-fade bias, and diversity bias, judgments shifting based on cited authority, apparent consensus, whether a model's name is anonymized, or identity markers in the text. Content-related: sentiment bias, token bias, and contextual bias. [S] Fourteen is a lot of ways to fail. What actually holds that list together, besides "things that go wrong"?
[G] Each entry gets a definition, a worked example, and a rundown of proposed fixes. For self-enhancement bias, a method called PRD has multiple judge models rank and discuss each other's calls, and a separate framework called CALM found the simplest fix works just as well, don't let the same model generate the answer and grade it.
