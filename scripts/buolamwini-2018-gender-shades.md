[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a guest scholar take apart one paper at a time. I'm the optimist.
[S] I'm the skeptic. Today's paper is Gender Shades: Intersectional Accuracy Disparities in Commercial Gender Classification, by Joy Buolamwini and Timnit Gebru, presented at the Conference on Fairness, Accountability, and Transparency in 2018.
[O] It's one of the most cited papers to ever come out of that venue, and for good reason — it's the paper that made "test it on the subgroup you're worst at" standard practice. Joining us to walk through it is Naomi, who's studied this audit closely.
[G] Happy to be here. This is one of those papers where the method matters more than the specific numbers, though the numbers are still doing a lot of work eight years later.
[S] Naomi, set the scene. What was broken before this paper?
[G] Two things, and they compound. First, the commercial systems — by 2017, Microsoft, IBM, and a company called Face++ were all selling gender classification as an API, and none of them reported accuracy broken down by any demographic group. Second, the benchmarks researchers could have used to check that themselves were badly skewed: the authors label two standard face benchmarks by skin type and find IJB-A seventy-nine point six percent lighter-skinned, and Adience eighty-six point two percent lighter-skinned. Neither had skin-type labels at all before this paper added them.
[O] So you could hit ninety percent overall on a benchmark that's eighty-six percent one kind of face, and never learn anything about the other fourteen percent.
[G] Exactly, and that's not hypothetical. The paper cites NIST's own 2015 gender-classification report, which had already found female faces misclassified one point eight to twelve point five percentage points worse than male faces, across nine algorithms. So the gender gap was known — what nobody had done was cross it with skin type and look at the combination.
[S] And this isn't an academic exercise. Face analysis was already load-bearing: the paper cites reporting that at least one hundred seventeen million Americans were in some law-enforcement face-recognition network, and earlier work found lower face-recognition accuracy for people labeled female, Black, or between eighteen and thirty.
[G] Right — so the stakes were already real before this paper. What was missing was a benchmark that could even ask the intersectional question, and a norm of reporting subgroup error instead of one number.
[O] Naomi, walk us through how they actually built a benchmark that isn't skewed.
[G] The core move is the Pilot Parliaments Benchmark, or PPB — one thousand two hundred seventy unique subjects, drawn from official parliamentary portraits: three African countries, Rwanda, Senegal, South Africa, and three Nordic countries, Iceland, Finland, Sweden.
[S] Why parliaments, of all things?
[G] Two reasons, and both are clever. Practically, parliamentary websites publish consistent, high-resolution, frontal headshots under an open license, which solves the sourcing problem cleanly. And they picked the specific countries using the Inter-Parliamentary Union's own gender-parity rankings — Rwanda has the highest share of women in parliament of any country in the world, and the Nordic countries cluster near the top too. Country selection is what buys you gender balance; pairing African with Nordic nations buys you the skin-tone range.
[O] And the composition numbers back that up?
[G] It's close to balanced on both axes — forty-four point six percent female, fifty-five point four percent male, forty-six point four percent darker-skinned, fifty-three point six percent lighter-skinned. Cross them and you get four subgroups: darker female at twenty-one point three percent of the benchmark, darker male at twenty-five percent, lighter female at twenty-three point three percent, lighter male at thirty point three percent. Compare that to IJB-A, where darker females are four point four percent of subjects, or Adience, where they're seven point four percent.
[S] So the smallest slice in the old benchmarks becomes a real, measurable slice here.
[G] That's the entire trick. You can't report an error rate on a group your test set barely contains.
[O] What about the labels themselves — who decided what counts as "darker"?
[G] They use the Fitzpatrick scale, a six-point dermatological classification originally developed to assess sun sensitivity and skin-cancer risk, not a face-recognition tool. Three annotators, including the authors, labeled every PPB image, and a board-certified surgical dermatologist provided the definitive labels. For analysis, the six types collapse into two bins — lighter is Fitzpatrick one through three, darker is four through six.
[S] Deliberately choosing race-adjacent countries but labeling skin type instead of race — why not just label race directly?
[G] The paper is explicit about this. Race and ethnicity categories are socially constructed and inconsistent across geography and time — someone labeled Black in the U.S. can have any skin tone. Skin type, estimated visually, is a narrower, more consistent signal, even though the authors admit it's an imperfect proxy, and that camera sensors themselves are historically calibrated to expose lighter skin better.
[O] And then the actual audit — three commercial APIs, treated as black boxes.
[G] Microsoft's Cognitive Services Face API, IBM's Watson Visual Recognition, and Face++, a computer-vision company based in China. All three were evaluated in April and May of 2017 — send an image in, read a male or female label back out, no access to model internals. Google didn't have a public gender classifier at the time, so it isn't in the study.
[S] Why Face++ specifically, out of all the companies that existed?
[G] That's deliberate too. There's prior work suggesting face-recognition systems built in Western countries and those built in Asian countries each perform better on their own regions' faces — the so-called other-race effect. Face++ being China-based let them test whether that pattern held here. It's more complicated than a clean East-versus-West story, and we'll come back to that in the debate.
[O] And the metric — they're not just reporting one accuracy number, right?
[G] They follow the NIST precedent and then go further. For every subgroup — all subjects, male, female, darker, lighter, and the four intersectional cells — they report true positive rate, error rate, which is just one minus TPR, positive predictive value, and false positive rate. The decisive move is computing all of that inside each subgroup separately, never pooled into one number.
[S] Give me the top-line table.
[G] Overall accuracy: Microsoft ninety-three point seven percent, Face++ ninety percent, IBM eighty-seven point nine percent. On paper, all three look production-ready.
[O] And then you split it four ways.
[G] Then it falls apart. Darker-female error rates: twenty point eight percent for Microsoft, thirty-four point five for Face++, thirty-four point seven for IBM. Lighter-male error rates: zero for Microsoft, point eight for Face++, point three for IBM. Same systems, same weeks of testing — the worst subgroup runs roughly forty times the error rate of the best one for Microsoft, and over a hundred times for IBM.
[S] Walk through the two marginal gaps too, because they're each real before you even get to the intersection.
[G] Every classifier does worse on female faces than male faces, by eight point one to twenty point six percentage points, and worse on darker faces than lighter faces, by eleven point eight to nineteen point two points. Both trends replicate what NIST had already found for gender alone. What's new is crossing them: the maximum spread between any classifier's best and worst subgroup is thirty-four point four percentage points, and that's IBM.
[O] Here's a stat I think gets undersold whenever this paper is quoted: darker females are twenty-one point three percent of the benchmark, but they account for sixty-one to seventy-two point four percent of all the misclassifications, across all three systems. Lighter males are thirty point three percent of the benchmark and contribute zero to two point four percent of the errors.
[S] So if you're a company averaging your error rate across the whole test set, one subgroup is silently absorbing almost all of it.
[G] Exactly, and that's the mechanism, not just the headline — the majority subgroups are accurate enough that a handful of failures on a minority subgroup barely moves the average.
[S] Did they check whether this was really about skin tone, or just about image quality — different countries, different cameras?
[G] They did, and it's one of the paper's stronger design moves. South Africa alone has a large lighter-skinned and a large darker-skinned population, photographed under comparable conditions in the same parliament. On that subset alone, four hundred thirty-seven subjects, the same pattern holds: darker-female error rates of twenty-three point eight percent for Microsoft, thirty-six percent for Face++, thirty-three point one percent for IBM.
[O] So it's not a cross-country confound — the disparity survives within one country, one set of cameras, one photographic setup.
[G] Right. There's one more result worth flagging: IBM was the only vendor returning a confidence score alongside its label. Its confidence on lighter subjects sits tight near one point zero. On darker females, it ranges from roughly point seven five down toward the floor — the system wasn't just wrong more often on that subgroup, it was less sure of itself too, which at least suggests the errors aren't pure noise.
[O] Let me make the strongest case for why this paper matters as much as its citation count suggests. This is a landmark audit, full stop — not just three numbers, but a repeatable method: build a balanced benchmark, slice by intersection, never trust the average. And it produced real change. A 2019 follow-up, Actionable Auditing, found vendors actually closed these gaps within months of being named publicly. One modest academic dataset moved three billion-dollar companies.
[S] I'll grant the impact. My deflationary case is about what the paper can and can't claim on its own evidence. It's a spring-2017 snapshot of three specific products — the moment those companies patched, the specific numbers went stale, which the authors themselves would tell you. And it's testing the easy case: one thousand two hundred seventy well-lit, front-facing, cooperative-subject portraits. Binary gender classification, not detection, not recognition, not verification — the tasks that actually put people into law-enforcement databases.
[O] The South Africa control answers the image-quality objection, though.
[S] It answers image quality. It doesn't answer sample size. Split one thousand two hundred seventy people four ways and some cells get thin — Iceland's darker-skinned cells are literally zero subjects. And the "other-race effect" test is murkier than advertised. Face++, the one non-Western vendor, tied IBM for worst on darker females, at thirty-four point five percent. If the hypothesis was that an Asia-based company would do better on non-White faces, that's not what happened.
[G] Both points are real, and the authors are unusually candid about most of this themselves. On sample size, yes — PPB is explicitly called a pilot, and the conclusion calls for a larger benchmark as future work. On Face++, the paper doesn't oversell the other-race hypothesis; it reports that Face++ was actually best on darker males, at point seven percent error, and tied worst on darker females — a more specific and more surprising finding than a clean East-versus-West story would have been.
[O] Naomi, what about the construct itself — gender reduced to a binary label?
[G] That's the limitation I'd put first, and the authors state it plainly: labeling gender as male or female is reductive, and it can't capture gender identity or non-binary people. The labels here come from parliamentary records, titles, and photo appearance — a proxy for how someone is perceived, not a claim about who they are. Skin type standing in for race carries the same kind of caveat: a scientifically grounded label, but explicitly not equivalent to race as socially experienced.
[S] So scoring this claim by claim: the intersectional method, proven. "This generalizes beyond a curated, well-lit portrait set," unproven, and the authors know it. The specific 2017 error rates, already historical.
[G] I'd score it about the same, and add one thing in the paper's favor that's easy to miss — it never claims more than it shows. The conclusion explicitly calls for extending the audit to detection, recognition, and verification, and to unconstrained images, rather than implying the portrait-benchmark result already covers those. For a paper this influential, it's notably careful about its own scope.
[O] Zoom out — what actually changed in how the field evaluates things, because of this paper specifically?
[G] Disaggregated evaluation went from a nice-to-have to close to a requirement, at least in fairness-adjacent work. This paper is one of the direct empirical arguments behind datasheets for datasets and model cards — documenting who's in your training and test data, not just your headline accuracy.
[S] It's a lesson that generalizes past computer vision, too. Any benchmark can hide a subgroup failure behind an average, whether the subgroup is demographic, or defined by task difficulty, or by anything else unevenly represented in your test set.
[G] Right — the question this paper forces isn't "what's your accuracy," it's "what's your accuracy on your worst subgroup, and how would you even know if you didn't ask." That question outlived the three vendors and the 2017 numbers by a long way.
[G] If there's one line to take from this paper, it's that an aggregate number can certify a system as ninety percent accurate while it fails one in three people in the group it serves worst — and the only way to catch that is to build a test set that actually contains that group.
[O] Mine: this is the audit that proved outside researchers could force real product changes at real companies, with a benchmark anyone could build themselves. That's a template worth reusing well past gender classification.
[S] And mine: read the numbers as a method demonstration, not a current scorecard — the vendors have shipped plenty since 2018. For the full tables and our references, the writeup is on the Litsearch site. Naomi, thank you.
[G] Thank you both.
