[G] A real one. Object detection, attribute classification, and crowdsourcing had all matured enough that generating a full sentence to describe an image looked tractable. But nobody had a trustworthy way to score whether one generated sentence was better than another.
[O] What were people doing instead?
[G] Mostly paying humans to rate captions, one to five, on grammar, saliency, correctness, sometimes judged pairwise. That works, but it's slow, expensive, hard to reproduce across labs, and there's no principled way to fold several separate quality axes into one number you can put on a leaderboard.
[S] So the field borrowed metrics from other fields instead.
[G] Right. BLEU from machine translation, ROUGE from summarization, later METEOR, which did somewhat better because it adds stemming and a loose synonym match. But all three were built and tuned for their own tasks, and the paper notes their correlation with human judgment at the level of a single sentence had been, in the authors' words, repeatedly questioned.
[O] So you've got expensive-and-slow on one side, and cheap-but-untrustworthy on the other.
[G] That's the gap. And the paper's real contribution is a reframe of the target, not just another formula. The authors make this observation directly: what humans like often does not correspond to what is human-like. The caption people rate as "best" is frequently not the one most typical of how people actually describe that image.
[S] Meaning quality and typicality are different targets, and everyone had been chasing the slippery one.
[G] Exactly. So instead of chasing "quality," they define the target as consensus, how closely a candidate matches the way the majority of people would describe that specific image. That's concrete. You can build a metric for it, once you actually have enough human descriptions per image to know what the majority says.
[O] Which is the third piece, the datasets. Walk us through all three parts, Grant, because there's a human protocol, a metric, and new data, and they lean on each other.
[G] Start with the human protocol, since it's the ground truth everything else gets validated against. Instead of an absolute one-to-five similarity rating, which is noisy and hard to calibrate across workers, they use a forced-choice triplet. A worker sees three sentences, A, B, and C. A is a real human reference description. B and C are two candidate captions. The only question is which of B or C is more similar to A.
[S] No definition of similarity given?
[G] None, deliberately. Leaving "similar" undefined and just asking for a relative comparison keeps the task objective in a way an absolute one-to-five score never is. They pair each candidate pair against every reference sentence for an image, collect three votes per triplet, and the majority winner across the whole reference set is the human-preferred caption. A metric's accuracy is then just the fraction of candidate pairs where the metric's higher-scored caption matches that human pick.
[O] Okay, and CIDEr itself.
[G] Every word first gets reduced to its stem, so "fishes," "fishing," and "fished" all collapse to "fish." Each sentence is then represented as the set of n-grams it contains, from single words up through four-word phrases. Here's the mechanism doing the real work: each n-gram gets a TF-IDF weight, term frequency inverse document frequency. The term-frequency part rewards n-grams that recur across this image's own references. The inverse-document-frequency part is the log of the total number of images divided by how many images have that n-gram anywhere in their references, and it downweights n-grams that show up everywhere.
[S] So generic phrases like "there is a" or "in the" get discounted automatically.
[G] Right, and that's not a side effect, it's doing double duty as a saliency signal. The metric leans on whatever words actually distinguish this image from every other image in the dataset. For each n-gram length, CIDEr is then the average cosine similarity between the candidate's TF-IDF vector and each reference's vector, which captures precision and recall at once. The final score sums those four n-gram lengths with equal weight, one quarter each. The authors tested other weightings and found uniform worked best.
[O] And the two new datasets exist because you can't measure "majority opinion" from five samples.
[G] Correct. Existing caption datasets topped out at five descriptions per image, nowhere near enough to estimate what most people would say. So they collected PASCAL-50S, fifty sentences each for the one thousand images in the existing UIUC Pascal Sentence dataset, and ABSTRACT-50S, fifty sentences each for five hundred clipart scenes from an existing Abstract Scenes dataset. All gathered on Mechanical Turk, restricted to US workers with a ninety-five percent approval rate and at least five hundred prior approved tasks, one sentence per worker per image. Average sentence length came out to eight point eight words for PASCAL, ten point five nine for ABSTRACT.
[S] That's six hundred eighty-three and four hundred sixty-five separate workers, if I've got the paper's subject counts right, just to build the reference pool.
[G] That's right, and it's the unglamorous part of the contribution that makes the flashier part possible.
[O] Last piece, CIDEr-D.
[G] The authors are upfront that the moment a metric becomes a target people optimize against, it needs hardening. CIDEr-D makes three changes. It drops stemming, so word forms have to be exactly correct. It adds a Gaussian penalty, with a sigma of six, on the gap between candidate and reference sentence length, so you can't inflate a score by rambling. And it clips each n-gram's candidate count to its count in the references, so repeating a high-confidence phrase over and over stops paying off. A factor of ten keeps the scale comparable to other metrics. CIDEr-D correlates with the original CIDEr at a Spearman value of point nine four, so it's nearly the same ranking, just harder to game, and that's the version that shipped inside the MS COCO caption evaluation server.

[O] What does this mean for people building eval today, beyond captioning specifically?
[G] The transferable lesson is less about TF-IDF and more about method. Validate a metric against a real human judgment protocol before trusting it, be explicit about how many reference points that judgment needed, and state plainly what your metric optimizes for versus what a person would actually call quality. That checklist applies just as much to a modern LLM-judge rubric as it does to an n-gram cosine.
[S] Reference-count sensitivity specifically is a lesson a lot of current benchmark builders keep re-learning the hard way, whether that's the number of raters per item or the number of reference solutions per problem.
