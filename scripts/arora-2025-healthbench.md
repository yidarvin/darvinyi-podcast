[O] Here is a claim that should stop you mid sip of coffee. Handed the exact same open-ended health conversations, unassisted physicians scored worse than every single frontier model tested, GPT-3.5 Turbo included.
[S] Handed the exact same conversations, sure. But asked to do something they never actually do in a clinic, type a long chat reply cold, no chart, no patient in the room, no time pressure removed. That is not "AI beats doctors." That is "AI beats doctors doing an unfamiliar task, graded on the AI's own terms."
[O] Fair, but even with that asterisk, the trend line underneath it is one of the fastest I have seen on any eval this year. Scores nearly quadrupled in under two years, and the newest jump is bigger than everything that came before it, combined.
[S] And the benchmark producing that trend line was built by OpenAI, is currently topped by OpenAI's own models, and is graded by an OpenAI model. Before we get excited about the number, let us get precise about what it actually measures, and who is holding the ruler.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper at a time. I am the optimist.
[S] I am the skeptic, and today's paper is going to earn every ounce of that job title.
[O] The paper is HealthBench: Evaluating Large Language Models Towards Improved Human Health, from Rahul K. Arora, Jason Wei, and a long list of colleagues at OpenAI, with Karan Singhal as the senior author. Joining us is Rafael, who has spent real time inside this benchmark. Rafael, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. HealthBench is probably the most ambitious attempt yet to grade a language model the way an actual physician would grade a colleague's note.
[S] Rafael, start with what it is replacing. What was wrong with how we used to grade models on health?
[G] The dominant evaluations were exam-style multiple choice, MedQA, PubMedQA, the USMLE-derived question sets. A model picks A, B, C, or D, and that becomes a proxy for medical competence.
[O] Which is a strange proxy, because no patient has ever described their symptoms as a four-option multiple choice question.
[G] Exactly, and the authors frame the failure in three parts. Meaningful, does the score reflect real-world impact, when the task is open-ended and underspecified. Trustworthy, was the eval ever checked against real physician judgment. And unsaturated, is there still room to tell models apart, because once GPT-4-class models hit the high eighties and nineties on the old exams, the benchmark stopped telling anyone which model is actually safer to deploy.
[S] Saturation is the quiet killer of a lot of benchmarks. Once everyone is near the ceiling, you are measuring noise, not capability.
[G] Right. And the conversations HealthBench is trying to capture are messier than any exam question. A user who buries the actual emergency in the third message. A nurse asking for a discharge summary. A parent in a low-resource clinic with a feverish baby and half the usual diagnostic tools unavailable.
[O] So the problem is not just "make a harder multiple choice test." It is that the format itself is the wrong shape for the task.
[G] That is the bet, yes. Once you let a model write a paragraph instead of picking a letter, you lose exact-match scoring, and you are left with weak language metrics or slow human review that does not scale. HealthBench's answer is a gold rubric instead of a gold answer, a physician-written checklist graded by a model whose grading has itself been checked against physicians.
[O] Rafael, walk us through the actual object here. What is one HealthBench example?
[G] Five thousand conversations between a user and a model, each ending on a message the model has to answer, averaging two point six turns, some running to nineteen. Most are synthetic in an interesting way, physicians enumerated the situations that matter, someone revealing an emergency gradually, a clinician requesting a note summary, a vague query needing a follow-up, and a language model pipeline turned those situations into realistic conversations, filtered for realism using OpenAI's o1-preview. The rest comes from physician red-teaming and rewritten Google HealthSearchQA queries.
[S] So the conversations themselves are mostly machine-generated, just steered by physician-written scenarios.
[G] Correct, worth flagging now, we will come back to it. What physicians wrote by hand is the rubric, self-contained criteria about what a good response should or should not do. Something like "states that this presentation warrants immediate emergency care," worth positive points, or "fails to ask about the duration of chest pain," worth negative points. The median example has eleven criteria, some have as few as two, one has forty-eight. Across the dataset, that is forty-eight thousand, five hundred sixty-two unique criteria.
[O] And the scoring math?
[G] A model-based grader goes through the rubric one criterion at a time, met or not met, full points or nothing. Sum the points earned, positive and negative both, divide by the maximum positive points available, that is the score for that example, and because negative criteria exist, a response can go below zero. The headline number is the mean across all five thousand examples, clipped between zero and one hundred percent.
[S] Negative scores per example is a nice design touch. Saying something actively dangerous costs you more than just saying nothing.
[G] Right, and two more layers make the single number diagnosable. Seven themes, the real-world task categories, things like global health, responding under uncertainty, emergency referrals, context seeking. And five axes, what each criterion is testing: completeness, accuracy, context awareness, communication quality, instruction following. Completeness alone is about thirty-nine percent of every criterion in the dataset, accuracy is thirty-three.
[O] Which matters a lot for reading the results, because if forty percent of your grade is "did you say everything," a model that simply says more has a structural advantage.
[G] Exactly the tension we will get to. One last piece: HealthBench Consensus pulls out thirty-four pre-written criteria, attached to a conversation only when two or more physicians independently agree it applies, appearing over eight thousand times, low-noise and physician-validated, anchoring the trustworthiness analysis later. HealthBench Hard is a separate, one-thousand-example subset chosen because frontier models struggle on it.
[S] And the physician side, because "two hundred sixty-two physicians" is doing a lot of work in that abstract.
[G] A genuinely large effort, eleven months, physicians with practice experience across sixty countries and twenty-six specialties, fluent enough in forty-nine languages to contribute in them. They started with just over a thousand interested applicants and kept about one in four, based on the quality of paid introductory tasks that included actual rubric-writing samples. Every physician was compensated.
[O] Okay, give me the scoreboard.
[G] Overall scores span from sixteen percent for GPT-3.5 Turbo up to sixty percent for o3. GPT-4o from August twenty twenty-four sits in between at thirty-two percent. The authors highlight that the jump from GPT-4.1 to o3, roughly forty-eight percent to sixty percent, is a bigger single step than the entire GPT-3.5-to-GPT-4o transition that preceded it.
[S] Sixty percent as a "solved it" number would worry me in a lot of domains. In health it should worry everyone.
[G] It should, and the authors seem to agree, because on HealthBench Hard, the thousand toughest examples, o3, the best model overall, only manages thirty-two percent. There is still enormous headroom on exactly the cases that matter most.
[O] What about across labs, not just OpenAI's own lineup?
[G] o3 leads at sixty percent, then Grok 3 at fifty-four, Gemini 2.5 Pro at fifty-two, GPT-4.1 at forty-eight, down to Llama 4 Maverick at twenty-five. The theme breakdown shows where the real gaps live: every model does best on emergency referrals and tailoring communication to expertise, worst on context-seeking, global health, and health data tasks. Models are good at answering. They are much worse at recognizing they should ask a question first.
[S] Which is precisely the instinct you would want most from a cautious clinician.
[O] There is a cost story here I think is underrated. GPT-4.1 nano, a small, cheap model, outperforms August twenty twenty-four's GPT-4o while costing twenty-five times less to run, part of what the authors call a new performance-cost frontier, which matters enormously for a low-resource clinic.
[S] Cost efficiency is real progress. Reliability is the number I actually trust, though. The paper measures worst-at-k, the worst of k independent responses to the same question. o3 averages sixty percent, but its worst-at-sixteen score drops by about a third relative to that mean. One bad answer can outweigh a dozen good ones in this domain.
[G] A real caveat, and the authors are upfront about it. o3 does more than double GPT-4o's worst-case reliability, genuine progress, just not proportional progress.
[O] And the length question, since "the model just writes more" is the obvious objection to any rubric benchmark that rewards completeness.
[G] They checked it two ways. Within a model, the correlation between response length and score is small, negative point oh five three for GPT-4o, positive point one two three for o3. And head to head, o3 beats GPT-4.1 seventy-two point nine percent of the time overall, and still wins sixty-three point seven percent of the time when only comparing responses of similar length. Length explains some of the gain, not most of it.
[S] Smaller than I expected. I will grant that one too.
[O] Now the part that made me sit up: physicians versus models. Unassisted, no reference, physicians scored thirteen percent, below every frontier model. Hand them four references from September twenty twenty-four models, and physicians meaningfully improved on them, thirty-one percent against the references' twenty-eight. Hand them references from the April twenty twenty-five models instead, and that edge vanishes, physician edits land at forty-eight percent, tied with the models' own forty-nine, just as likely to worsen the draft as improve it.
[G] Which the authors read as a real signal, not just a curiosity. The newest models had become genuinely hard for a physician to improve on, at least on this specific task, under this specific rubric.
[S] "This specific task" is doing a lot of work there.
[O] Last piece, and it is the load-bearing one: is the grader any good? None of these numbers mean anything if the model doing the grading does not agree with actual physicians.
[G] They meta-evaluate on the thirty-four consensus criteria, over sixty thousand physician-graded instances. Scored the same way a physician is scored against peers, GPT-4.1 as grader gets a macro F1 of point seven oh nine, ahead of o4-mini and o3, clear of the random baseline at point five oh. Against the physician cohort itself, GPT-4.1 beats the average physician in five of seven themes.
[S] So the grader is about as good as a randomly chosen physician. Genuinely strong for a model grader, but a much lower bar than "as good as consensus."
[O] Let me make the strongest case I can, Rafael. This is fast, real progress on a benchmark designed to resist the failure modes of everything before it. Meaningful, rubric-graded open-ended conversation instead of multiple choice. Trustworthy, they built and reported a meta-evaluation instead of just asserting the grader works. Unsaturated, the best model in the world still cannot crack a third of the hardest examples. If you are deciding whether frontier models are getting safer to put in front of a patient, this is the best public evidence I have seen.
[S] I will grant every one of those and still tell you the headline number deserves suspicion. The other eighty-six percent, the majority of every score we just quoted, runs on criteria written by a single physician with no cross-validation. Grader fidelity is proven exactly where grading is easiest, and unmeasured where most of the score is actually made.
[G] A fair reading of the paper's own numbers, and it is not hidden, the authors state that split themselves. I would add that physician-physician agreement on the consensus set only runs fifty-five to seventy-five percent. Physicians disagree with each other almost as much as the model disagrees with them.
[S] Sure, but most rubric evals are not asking me to trust the number as a proxy for patient safety. Second point, completeness weighting. Forty percent of every criterion is whether you said everything you should, which is why they had to spend a whole section proving length is not driving the gains, and their own numbers show it is not nothing, o3 wins seventy-two percent of head-to-heads uncontrolled versus sixty-four percent length-controlled. A rubric mean structurally rewards the long, hedge-everything answer over the short, correct one.
[G] I would push back there. A clipped mean over eleven criteria is not purely rewarding length, it is rewarding coverage of things physicians said mattered. The length-controlled win rate staying above sixty percent is real evidence the models are not just padding.
[O] And that is exactly why I would read "models beat physicians" carefully rather than throw it out. Unassisted physicians scored thirteen percent on a task they do not normally perform, writing short by habit, under a metric that rewards thoroughness. That is not really "AI beats doctors." It is "AI beats doctors doing something doctors do not train for, under AI-friendly scoring rules."
[S] Which the paper's own discussion section admits, to its credit. That caveat rarely survives contact with a headline, though.
[G] It does not, and that is worth saying plainly on air. This is a benchmark for models responding to health conversations, not a study of clinical outcomes, and not a study of physicians at their actual job.
[S] Last thing, contamination. They did the responsible things: a canary string, a private held-out set to catch overfitting, a request not to post examples publicly. Credit for that. But synthetic generation cuts both ways. A language model pipeline wrote most of these conversations, which means they may encode what a language model thinks a patient sounds like, subtly favoring models from a similar lineage, and probably under-sampling the messy, low-literacy queries the global-health framing says it cares about most.
[G] The paper does not have an answer for that one. It is future work, not a refuted concern.
[O] Score the debate for me, Rafael.
[G] On craft and intent, meaningful, trustworthy, unsaturated, genuinely well executed, the optimist wins clearly. On whether the sixty percent headline means what a casual reader will assume, the skeptic wins: the meta-evaluation covers the easy fourteen percent, and the completeness weighting is a real structural bias worth remembering every time this number gets quoted.
