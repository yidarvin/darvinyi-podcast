[S] Ninety-nine point nine nine percent confidence. That's a state-of-the-art ImageNet classifier looking at a field of colored static and declaring, with near-total certainty, that it's a king penguin.
[O] And that headline number is the easy part to remember. What this paper is really asking is more radical: was confidence ever tied to recognition in the first place?
[S] This wasn't a photo. It wasn't a natural image nudged by a tiny amount. It was built from nothing at all.
[O] Which is exactly why it should worry anyone who treats a softmax score as proof the model saw something real.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from litsearch dot darvinyi dot com. Today's paper is "Deep Neural Networks are Easily Fooled: High Confidence Predictions for Unrecognizable Images," by Anh Nguyen, Jason Yosinski, and Jeff Clune, published at CVPR in 2015.
[S] Joining us is Julian, who's spent a lot of time in this literature. Julian, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is one of those papers everyone cites but half-remembers as just "noise gets called a lion." There's a lot more going on underneath that.
[O] Which is exactly why we wanted to slow down and actually read it properly.
[S] Set the scene for us, Julian. Late 2014, before this paper landed — where does it sit relative to the adversarial examples work people already knew about?
[G] A few months earlier, Szegedy and colleagues had shown something that shook people. Take a photo a network correctly classifies as a lion, apply a change so small a human can't perceive it, and the network will call it a library instead. That's a perturbation attack: it starts from a real, correctly labeled image and nudges it.
[O] Right, and that's already unsettling, because it means the decision boundary sits uncomfortably close to real images.
[G] This paper asks a more basic question. Forget nudging a real photo — what if you build an image completely from scratch? No natural starting point, no constraint that it resemble anything. Just optimize one number: the network's own predicted probability for a class you choose.
[S] And if that number can be pushed arbitrarily high on an image that looks like nothing, then confidence was never doing the job people assumed it was doing.
[G] That's the seed of the whole question. The authors frame the stakes directly: near-human accuracy on ImageNet had people asking how close DNN vision really is to human vision. This is one of the first hard answers, and it isn't flattering.
[O] Walk us through what they actually built, Julian.
[G] Two trained networks, both from the Caffe software package, both left completely unmodified — they're fixed scoring functions to optimize against, nothing more. One is a reproduction of Krizhevsky and colleagues' AlexNet, trained on the 1.3 million image ImageNet dataset, scoring 42.6 percent top-one error, close to the original paper's 40.7. The other is a ReLU variant of LeNet trained on MNIST digits, at 0.94 percent error against the original LeNet-5's 0.8 percent.
[S] So respectable, standard-issue networks for 2014. Not a toy setup rigged to fail.
[G] Exactly, and that matters for the conclusion. Then three separate ways of generating images, all climbing toward the same objective: the softmax score for one target class, and nothing else.
[O] Three methods. Start with the first.
[G] Direct encoding, an evolutionary algorithm. Literally one number per pixel — grayscale for MNIST, three color values for ImageNet — initialized as uniform random noise and mutated pixel by pixel. Rather than run one search per class, they use something called MAP-Elites: a single run keeps a champion image for every class at once, a thousand champions evolving side by side, each one replaced whenever a mutation scores higher for that class.
[S] So raw noise, pixel-level mutation, no structure imposed anywhere.
[G] Right. The second method inverts that. Indirect encoding, through what's called a compositional pattern-producing network, or CPPN. Instead of storing pixel values directly, you evolve a small network that maps a pixel's coordinates to a color, and because that compact genome touches many pixels through shared structure, the resulting images come out regular — symmetric, repeating, patterned. The same encoding that, on a crowd-sourced site called PicBreeder, has separately produced images humans recognize and even like.
[O] So one method produces static, the other produces something closer to abstract pattern.
[G] And the third method drops evolution entirely. Gradient ascent in pixel space, replicating a technique from Simonyan and colleagues: start from the ImageNet mean image plus a touch of noise, then backpropagate the gradient of the target class's softmax score straight into the pixels and follow it up.
[S] Three completely different search processes, but all climbing the same hill — one number, no penalty whatsoever for looking natural.
[G] That's deliberate. If all three converge on the same finding, it isn't an artifact of one particular search method.
[O] So does it work?
[G] On MNIST, almost immediately. Both encodings produce images the network calls a digit with 99.99 percent confidence in under 50 generations, for every digit class, and by 200 generations the median across all ten digits is 99.99 percent. And these do not look like handwritten digits — the direct-encoding ones look like colored static.
[S] What about the CPPN ones? You said those come out patterned.
[G] They do, and this part is genuinely interesting. Images the network calls a "1" tend to have vertical bars. Images it calls a "2" tend to have a horizontal bar low in the frame. That pattern recurs across fifty independent runs in the supplementary material. It isn't random — the search is finding real discriminative features the digit classifier actually learned.
[O] ImageNet is the harder test, though — a thousand classes, far richer images.
[G] Direct encoding really struggles there. Even after twenty thousand generations, median confidence across all thousand classes is only 21.59 percent. But forty-five of those thousand classes still get pushed past 99 percent confidence, so it's rare, but not absent.
[S] And the CPPN version?
[G] Dramatically more effective, with a quarter of the compute. Five thousand generations, median confidence 88.11 percent — a gap over direct encoding's 21.59 that the authors report as statistically significant, with a p-value under one in ten thousand by a Mann-Whitney U test. High-confidence images turn up across most of the thousand classes.
[O] Here's the number that stopped me. They also ran the real ImageNet validation set through the same network. Correctly classified real photos get a median confidence of 86.7 percent. The synthetic CPPN images' median of 88.11 sits right in that range — statistically, the fooling images don't even register as anomalies to the network.
[S] That's the number that should worry people more than the ninety-nine-ninety-nine headline. It's not that confidence occasionally spikes on garbage — it's that garbage can sit at the same confidence level as the network's genuinely correct predictions.
[G] And the images aren't purely arbitrary either. Some CPPN images carry real features of the target class — a starfish image with patches of blue water and orange, a baseball with red stitching on white. The authors also ablated repeated pattern elements in some images, removing extra copies of a shape, and confidence measurably dropped when they did. So the network is reading something real, just not what a human would call the object.
[O] Why isn't there just one generic "fool anything" pattern that the search converges on every time?
[G] Good question, and the paper addresses it directly. There's a surprising amount of diversity across runs. Closely related categories — say three species of lizard, or three breeds of small fluffy dog — tend to get fooled by similar-looking images within a single evolutionary run, almost clustering phylogenetically. But different independent runs produce different image types for those same related categories. So there isn't one master key. There are many distinct discriminative shortcuts per class, and evolution stumbles onto a different one nearly every time.
[S] Which argues against this being one narrow, patchable seam, and for it being a broad property of how these decision boundaries get carved up.
[S] Does any of this generalize beyond one specific trained network, or is each fooling image basically fitted to one model's private idiosyncrasies?
[G] They tested that directly, taking CPPN images evolved against one network and feeding them to a second. Two versions: same architecture with only a different random initialization, and genuinely different architectures, AlexNet against GoogLeNet. For the same-architecture case, on average about 64.4 percent of ImageNet images get the same top-one label from both networks, and 46.0 percent for MNIST. Cross-architecture transfer is weaker but still real — roughly 20.7 percent of images evolved for GoogLeNet also fool AlexNet, and 17.3 percent the other way.
[O] So it isn't that each image is a private key that only unlocks the one model it was evolved against.
[G] Right, though I'd temper that. Only a small share of those matching-label images clear the 99 percent confidence bar on both networks at once — about 6.1 percent for ImageNet, 27.3 percent for MNIST. So the very strongest fooling images are still somewhat network-specific, but the underlying shortcuts they exploit are shared enough to transfer at real rates across independently trained, and even differently architected, models.
[S] That's the detail that keeps this from reading as one buggy checkpoint's quirk. If it were purely one model's idiosyncrasy, a different random seed would fix it outright, and it clearly doesn't.
[O] Alright, I want to make the strongest case for what this means, because I think it's more hopeful than the headline suggests. This is a diagnosis of the boundary of one 2014 discriminative classifier, not a permanent property of neural networks. The authors' own explanation is that a discriminative model's decision regions in high-dimensional space are simply much larger than the training manifold — that's a property of drawing boundaries with softmax, not proof that these systems can never be trusted. The field has spent a decade since building better calibration and contamination-aware evaluation because of exactly this paper.
[G] Worth noting, that mechanism has held up well. The authors point out that a discriminative model's decision regions are far bigger than the training manifold, and a related, contemporaneous study connects that directly to these models behaving in a largely linear way in high-dimensional space — the same locally-linear behavior that the perturbation-attack line of work uses to explain its own results. So the two failure modes likely share a root cause, not just a family resemblance.
[S] I'll push back there. Look at what they actually tried as a fix, because it isn't encouraging. Retrain the network with fooling images as an explicit negative class, repeat for fifteen rounds on MNIST — and new 99.99 percent fooling images keep appearing every single round, even once the eleventh retrained network's negative class held twenty-five percent of its own training set. That's not a bug you patch. That's a moving target.
[G] The ImageNet retraining result is more nuanced, and worth being precise about. Median confidence for newly evolved images against the retrained network did drop, from 88.1 percent down to 11.7, a real and statistically significant improvement. But when they then fed genuinely recognizable CPPN images from PicBreeder to that same retrained network, it misclassified sixty-four percent of them as "CPPN-like" on sight. It didn't learn what a starfish or a coffee pot actually looks like. It learned a shortcut — a generic texture signature for CPPN-style images.
[O] Which is still progress, though. A shallow fix that raises the cost of the attack isn't nothing.
[S] It's not nothing, but it's exactly the failure mode I worry about across this whole literature: a fix that closes the precise hole that was measured and leaves the surrounding wall exposed. There's a stronger version of this test buried in the supplementary material, too, and I think it settles the point. They tried an ensemble of three different architectures — CaffeNet, AlexNet, GoogLeNet — which is the plausible real-world defense people reach for. After only four thousand generations, evolution still found images fooling all three networks at once, for two hundred thirty-one of the thousand classes, at ninety percent confidence or better.
[G] That's the single number I'd hold onto if I only remembered one thing from the supplement. Ensembling is the textbook mitigation, and this paper shows it slows the attacker down without closing the gap.
[O] Fine, I'll concede the naive fixes don't hold up. But the framing still matters to me: this is diagnostic, not doom. It's the paper that motivated years of work on calibration and out-of-distribution detection precisely because the field took the diagnosis seriously instead of shrugging it off.
[S] Diagnostic, I'll grant you that much. My residual worry is scope. These are two single-run, 2014-vintage architectures, with no modern regularization and no contemporaneous calibration baseline tested side by side. This paper alone can't tell us whether the gap shrinks with scale or a better training recipe.
[G] That's a fair limitation, and the paper doesn't close it — it wasn't trying to. What it does establish cleanly is that softmax confidence, by itself, is not evidence of recognition. Whether later architectures narrow that gap is a question for the papers that came after this one.
[O] So where does this land for people actually building evaluation pipelines today?
[G] The most direct line runs to out-of-distribution detection and calibration. From the maximum softmax probability baseline through later methods like ODIN, Mahalanobis distance, and energy-based scores — all of them are, in some sense, answering the question this paper first raised: if softmax confidence isn't trustworthy on its own, what score would be?
[S] It's also a warning about what a leaderboard number can hide. An accuracy figure says nothing about how a model behaves on inputs from outside the distribution it was scored on. This paper is the demonstration that "high confidence" and "correct" can come apart completely, and by a lot.
[G] It's worth keeping this distinct from the other adversarial-example thread that broke the same month, Goodfellow and colleagues' fast gradient sign paper. That line perturbs a real, already correctly classified image by an imperceptible amount. This paper starts from nothing and builds an image outward with no natural starting point at all. Different mechanisms, same underlying lesson: softmax confidence is fragile, just approached from opposite directions.
[S] Two papers, the same month, converging on the same uncomfortable conclusion from opposite ends. That's not a coincidence worth reading past quickly.
[S] The authors also spell out where this bites outside the lab. A security system built on face or voice recognition could in principle be defeated by an input that reads as noise to any human standing nearby, and the same logic extends to safety-critical deployments like driverless cars.
[O] Which is a good reminder that "fooling image" doesn't just mean a funny picture of static — it means the confidence number itself can't be trusted as a safety gate on its own, without something else backing it up.
[O] And there's a genuinely fun coda here. Some of these CPPN images were submitted to a juried student art exhibition at the University of Wyoming, and accepted alongside human-made work.
[G] They were. A companion paper from the same group leans into that directly, treating the network-plus-evolution pipeline as a creative search tool rather than only an attack. Worth a look if that side interests you.
[G] If there's one line to take from the paper itself, it's this: a discriminative model's confidence tells you where you stand relative to a decision boundary, not whether the input resembles anything the model was actually trained on.
[O] My takeaway is that this is the paper that forced the field to stop treating softmax scores as a free proxy for understanding, and that discomfort produced a decade of better tools.
