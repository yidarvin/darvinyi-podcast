[O] What about benchmarks that already use experts and rubrics for this? Surely someone's tried.
[G] A few have, and the authors survey them directly. BigLaw Bench and something called CorpFin exist for law and finance respectively, but they're private, so nobody outside those companies can use them. ProfBench and APEX are public and rubric-based, but they're tiny, eighty tasks and two hundred tasks. You can't build a reproducible, fine-grained measurement program on that scale.
[S] So the pitch is public, large, and expert-authored. Pick any two isn't good enough, they want all three.
[G] That's exactly the design brief. Nineteen thousand-plus rubric criteria, eleven hundred tasks, fully open-sourced.
[O] Walk us through what's actually in it.
[G] Eleven hundred tasks total, six hundred in finance, five hundred in law. Every one was written by one of a hundred eighty-two credentialed professionals. On the legal side that means a JD or an international equivalent. On finance, a Master's degree, a CFA charter, or six-plus years of professional experience. And they weren't writing exam questions, they were asked to bring the actual questions they or their clients face.
[S] How do you know that claim holds? Anyone can say "these are real professional questions."
[G] The diversity is the tell. The tasks span a hundred fourteen countries and forty-seven separate US jurisdictions, across twenty-five topics split between finance and law. That's not the distribution you get from a handful of annotators inventing hypotheticals at their desks.
[O] And these aren't all single questions, right? There's multi-turn.
[G] About thirty percent of the conversations are multi-turn, up to ten turns. The human expert writes every user turn. The intermediate assistant turns, the back-and-forth that builds context, are sampled from open models, GPT OSS twenty B, Mistral Medium, DeepSeek R1. Only the model's final response gets graded.
[S] Now the part I actually care about, because this is where a benchmark like this lives or dies. The rubrics.
[G] Each task ships ten to thirty criteria, and every criterion carries an integer weight from negative ten to positive ten, never zero. Positive criteria reward something a good response should do, negative criteria punish something it shouldn't. Annotators first pick one of six severity bands, from Critically Important down to Critically Detrimental, and only then assign the number inside that band. That forces a hierarchy instead of an annotator just guessing whether something is a seven or an eight.
[O] And the rubrics themselves go through review, not just the authoring?
[G] Two experts, always. Expert one writes the prompt and the initial rubric. Expert two independently rewrites and fixes both, and leaves feedback. Programmatic checks then enforce that every criterion is atomic, objective, and self-contained, meaning it can't be judged without outside context. At the end there's a formal quality-control pass: human-to-LLM inter-rater agreement lands at a Cohen's kappa of point eight oh two, and in a separate validation study, independent experts agreed the criteria were justified ninety-three point nine percent of the time.
[S] That's a genuinely serious pipeline. How does scoring actually work once you have all these weighted criteria?
[G] An LLM judge grades every criterion independently as satisfied or not. A response's score is its earned weighted credit divided by the total positive weight available, and the overall model score is the mean across tasks, floored at zero so it can't go negative.
[O] And the Hard subset, where does that come from?
[G] Following HealthBench's method, they rank every task by how well models did on average and carve out the hardest three hundred finance tasks and two hundred fifty legal tasks. That's the subset the headline numbers usually refer to.
[S] Last piece before results. Who's the judge, and how did they pick it?
[G] They tested three candidates, o4-mini, GPT-4.1, and Claude Haiku 4.5, against two human experts grading GPT-5 and Claude Sonnet 4.5 responses over a hundred and one tasks. o4-mini landed a kappa of point six oh three, GPT-4.1 point six oh five, both close to the expert-versus-expert baseline of point five eight nine. Claude Haiku 4.5 lagged at point five three five. They picked o4-mini, mainly because it's cheaper to run at scale.
