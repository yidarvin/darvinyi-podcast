[S] Here's an uncomfortable fact buried in this year's WMT metrics report. On Chinese to English, the official human reference translation scored an MQM error of four point eight three. The best machine translation system in the whole competition scored two point one zero. The reference — the thing every metric was told to treat as ground truth — was worse than the machine.
[O] And the fix the organizers ran with is almost elegant. Build a synthetic reference by stitching together whichever candidate, human or machine, scored best on each individual segment. Do that, and the reference's error rate drops to point six six, well under one minor mistake per segment on average.
[S] Elegant, but they call it cherry-picking themselves, in print. You're selecting the reference using the same human ratings you then use to validate metrics against that reference. That is about as circular as meta-evaluation gets.
[O] Which is exactly the kind of tension this paper is full of, and it is honest about nearly all of it. Let's get into it.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take one paper apart.
[S] Today's paper is "Results of WMT23 Metrics Shared Task: Metrics Might Be Guilty but References Are Not Innocent," from Markus Freitag and a large team of co-organizers spanning Google Research, Unbabel, Microsoft, and several university labs, published at WMT twenty twenty-three.
[O] It's the annual report card for every automatic machine translation metric you've ever cited — BLEU, chrF, COMET, MetricX — and this year it comes with a genuinely uncomfortable finding about the references those metrics lean on. Ottoline, welcome to the show.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is one of those meta-evaluation papers worth reading twice, because on the surface it's a leaderboard, but underneath it's an audit of the entire evaluation apparatus the field depends on.
[S] Ottoline, ground us first. What actually happens in a metrics shared task, and why has WMT run this every year since two thousand eight?
[G] Every year, WMT collects dozens of automatic evaluation metrics. Some are the simple overlap-based kind, like BLEU or chrF, which count matching words or characters between a translation and a reference. Others are learned neural metrics, like COMET, MetricX, or BLEURT, trained to imitate human judgments. The organizers score the same pool of machine translation outputs with every submitted metric, then check which metric agrees best with careful human quality judgments. Whichever metric agrees most becomes the field's most trusted yardstick, and that trust cascades into every later paper that reports one translation system as better than another using that metric.
[O] So this is the paper that decides which yardstick everyone else gets to use.
[G] Essentially, yes. This year the human judgments come from professional translators doing Multidimensional Quality Metrics annotation, MQM for short, where instead of one holistic score, an annotator marks specific errors in the text, categorized and weighted by severity.
[S] The paper says last year's version of this evaluation had real problems. What was actually wrong with it?
[G] Two things. First, WMT twenty-two scored metrics across two hundred and one separate tasks and then averaged the ranks, which the organizers themselves call unwieldy. Every time someone submits a new metric, you have to rerun pairwise significance tests against every existing metric, and that new entrant can shuffle the whole leaderboard's order just by showing up. Second, and more structural, reference-based metrics all assume the human reference translation they're compared against is trustworthy. Nobody was routinely checking that assumption.
[O] And there's a third problem this paper tackles almost as an aside — the folk wisdom around what a metric score difference even means.
[G] Right. Practitioners routinely treat something like "two BLEU points better" as a real, human-noticeable improvement, without ever having validated that intuition against what human annotators actually judge as significant.
[S] Let's get concrete, then. What's actually being measured, and on what data?
[G] Three language pairs, each chosen to stress a different condition. English into German is paragraph-level rather than sentence-level, which is unusual and harder. Hebrew into English is the low-resource case, sentence-level. Chinese into English is the high-resource case, also sentence-level. Every metric, reference-based or reference-free, scores the same pool of competing systems for all three, and gets checked against fresh MQM ratings collected specifically for this task.
[O] And the headline methodological change is how those comparisons roll up into one final score.
[G] Instead of two hundred and one tasks and rank-averaging, this year uses exactly ten tasks across three types of statistic, and takes a straight weighted average. System-level pairwise ranking accuracy, pooled across all three language pairs, carries weight three. Segment-level pairwise accuracy with tie calibration, which credits a metric for correctly predicting when annotators called two translations a tie, carries weight one, once per language pair. Pearson correlation, at both the system and segment level, for each language pair, adds six more tasks at weight one each.
[S] Why upweight system-level accuracy specifically, three times over everything else?
[G] The paper doesn't really defend that choice beyond precedent from last year's scheme. It's also worth noting the ten tasks aren't statistically independent — several are computed from the same underlying MQM ratings, just sliced differently. The organizers disclose that, but they don't correct for it.
[S] That's the first crack in the foundation, and we're only a few minutes in.
[O] Let's talk about the reference-fixing idea, because that's the paper's most interesting methodological contribution. Ottoline, walk us through how you build a synthetic reference.
[G] The insight comes from noticing the Chinese-to-English human reference scored badly on MQM, worse than nearly every machine system it was meant to grade. So for every source segment, the organizers look at every available candidate translation, any competing MT system's output or the original human reference, and pick whichever one got the lowest MQM error score for that specific segment. Stitch those winners together, segment by segment, and you get a synthetic best-of-everything reference. The original human reference is allowed to win its own segments, like any other candidate.
[S] And the human reference did still win some.
[G] It did, two hundred and nine segments, out of the full Chinese-to-English test set, despite scoring far worse overall than the top machine systems. That tells you something interesting — the reference wasn't uniformly bad, it was unevenly bad. The organizers confirmed with the translation vendor that the same translator worked the entire set, so there's no clean explanation like a different, weaker translator handling those segments.
[O] One more method piece, and this is honestly the most immediately useful thing in the paper for anyone writing an evaluation section.
[G] The calibration study. For every pair of competing systems, they run a one-sided paired t-test on segment-level MQM scores, checking whether humans would call the difference significant. Then they fit an isotonic regression, a model guaranteed to be non-decreasing, mapping a metric's score difference between two systems to the probability that the human-judged difference clears that bar. Do that across many system pairs, and you get a lookup table: how big does a BLEU gap, or a COMET gap, actually need to be before it reliably reflects something a human would notice?
[S] Let's get to the leaderboard, then.
[G] XCOMET-Ensemble wins outright, at point eight two five average correlation across the ten tasks. Right behind it, tied at point eight zero eight, are the reference-free XCOMET-QE-Ensemble and the reference-based MetricX-23. GEMBA-MQM, which prompts a large language model directly rather than training a dedicated regressor, comes in at point eight zero two. Then the older, still-respected baselines: COMET at point seven seven nine, BLEURT-20 at point seven seven six, BERTscore further back at point seven four two. And the classic overlap metrics land near the bottom of the pack that still beats random guessing — BLEU at point six nine six, chrF at point six nine four.
[O] So neural, learned metrics are just categorically ahead of counting overlapping words, confirming what last year already showed.
[G] Confirmed again, and confirmed a second way. The organizers cross-checked against a completely separate human evaluation campaign, Direct Assessment Scalar Quality Metrics, run across eight language directions instead of three. GEMBA-MQM stayed the top-ranked metric under both systems of human judgment.
[S] But that cross-check is also where I'd push back on the leaderboard, because it's not all good news for the winner.
[G] It isn't. Under that same Direct Assessment check, XCOMET-Ensemble, this year's declared winner, drops from second place to tenth. MetricX-23 drops from third to eleventh. The organizers say plainly they see no clear pattern behind the drop.
[S] The actual winner of the shared task falls out of the top ten under a different, equally legitimate human evaluation protocol. That's worth sitting with, because it undercuts the entire premise of crowning one winner.
[O] Let's get to the granularity finding next, because it's a warning for anyone reading a single correlation number off a leaderboard.
[G] Figure five in the paper groups correlation by granularity. Nearly every metric climbs steeply from an overall score up to system level, several clear point nine there, and then falls sharply back down at the segment level, often under point five. A metric that separates whole translation systems almost perfectly can still be mediocre at judging one sentence pair against another.
[O] Now the reference-quality result, the one from our cold open.
[G] Chinese-to-English: the human reference scored an MQM error of four point eight three, worse than the best machine system's two point one zero. Swap in the synthetic best-of-everything reference, and every reference-based metric's correlation with human judgment jumps, at both the segment and system level. For English-to-German, where the human reference was already solid, two point nine six against the best system's three point seven two, the synthetic swap barely moved segment-level correlation and had only a mixed effect at system level. Same method, opposite outcome, because the underlying reference quality was different going in.
[O] Which is exactly the control you want to see — it isolates reference quality as the actual variable, not some artifact of the synthetic-construction trick itself.
[S] And the calibration numbers, the ones that kill the "two BLEU points" folklore.
[G] The eighty-percent-confidence threshold for a human-judged significant difference ranges enormously by language pair: two point two BLEU points for Chinese-to-English, three point five for Hebrew-to-English, and all the way up to eleven BLEU points for English-to-German. The matching COMET thresholds, in absolute terms, are far smaller: point zero one three, point zero one four, and point zero three across those same three directions.
[O] Eleven BLEU points. Most people would call a two-point gap a landslide.
[G] Which is precisely the organizers' point. The folk number is off by up to roughly five and a half times, depending which language pair you're in.
[S] One more results thread worth naming: the challenge sets.
[G] Three independent research teams submitted contrastive test suites this year, targeting specific error types rather than general translation quality. The largest, ACES, covers thirty-six thousand examples across sixty-eight error phenomena, and finds no clear winner among this year's metrics, and that neural metrics still lean on lexical overlap more than genuine semantic content than you'd hope. A second challenge set finds reference-free metrics built on language-agnostic multilingual embeddings specifically struggle to catch untranslated text, or text translated in the wrong direction entirely. A third, testing low- to medium-quality machine output rather than the strong systems everyone usually benchmarks against, finds some metrics collapse to a small handful of universal scores and even rank bad systems in reverse order.
[O] Let's do the debate, because there's real tension to work through. I'll make the optimist case first. This is a field policing itself, hard. Neural, learned metrics are unambiguously better than counting overlapping words, confirmed two different ways this year: the ten-task MQM ranking and the independent Direct Assessment check both say the same broad thing. And reference-free metrics are maturing fast enough that they're now genuinely competitive, which future-proofs the whole enterprise against exactly the reference-quality trap this paper just exposed.
[S] I'll grant the neural-over-overlap result, it's about as settled as anything gets in this field. What I won't grant is that we should trust the crowned winner. XCOMET-Ensemble tops the headline leaderboard and then falls to tenth under a different, equally valid human evaluation protocol collected on a broader set of languages. That's not a footnote, that's the ranking failing its own robustness check.
[G] Both points are grounded in what the paper actually shows. What I'd add is that the organizers themselves don't resolve which ranking is right — MQM and DA plus SQM differ in language-pair coverage and annotator protocol, so the disagreement could reflect either ranking's own noise rather than a flaw specific to XCOMET.
[S] There's a second thing I want on the record: the conflict of interest. Several task organizers, including people whose names are on this very paper, also submit their own metrics into the competition they're grading.
[G] That's disclosed directly in the paper's ethics section. Organizer-affiliated metrics used frozen, pre-existing versions dated before this year's task, and newer COMET versions were built without touching the test data. But there's no separate leaderboard excluding organizer-affiliated entries to actually demonstrate that the disclosure held in practice — a reader has to take it on trust rather than see it proven in the numbers.
[O] To be fair, that's a structural problem with running any shared task in a small field. The same handful of labs both build the best metrics and organize the competition that ranks them. There's no obvious way around that without losing the expertise needed to run the task at all.
[S] Fair, but disclosure isn't demonstration. My last point is the synthetic reference. The organizers themselves call it cherry-picking, in print. They select each segment's reference using the very human ratings later used to validate metrics against that same reference. That's circular by construction, and the paper never quantifies how much of the correlation jump is genuine reference-quality signal versus that circularity.
[G] That's the sharpest unresolved thread in the whole paper, and the organizers are candid about not resolving it. The clean experiment would be a held-out human reference, never used to build the synthetic one, to separate real signal from cherry-picking. It's raised as an open question, not answered.
[O] I'll concede the ranking-instability point is more serious than I first gave it credit for. But I still think the direction of travel, better metrics tested more rigorously and reported more honestly, is the real story here, even with those open threads.
[S] Agreed on direction of travel. I just don't think anyone should cite "XCOMET-Ensemble is the state of the art" without the Direct Assessment asterisk attached.
[O] Zoom out for a second, Ottoline. Why should someone who doesn't work on machine translation care about any of this?
[G] Because the pattern generalizes past MT immediately. Any field that validates an automatic evaluation metric by correlating it against human judgment, summarization, dialogue, increasingly large-language-model judges scoring open-ended answers, inherits the same two soft spots this paper surfaces. The human judgments themselves carry their own protocol-dependent noise, and if the metric is reference-based, the reference's quality is a hidden variable nobody routinely audits.
[S] And the calibration table is the single most exportable artifact here. Any time someone reports that model B beats model A by two points on some automatic metric, the honest question is whether two points clears the threshold that would make a human actually notice. This paper is one of the only places that's been worked out explicitly, for at least these three language pairs.
[O] It won't transfer numerically to other domains, but the method, calibrate your metric deltas against real significance testing rather than assuming, absolutely should.
[G] If there's one line to take from this paper, it's the title itself. Metrics are getting better and better at approximating human judgment, but the field can't keep treating the human reference as beyond suspicion. Sometimes the reference is the thing that's actually broken.
[O] My takeaway: this is meta-evaluation done with real self-awareness. The organizers flag their own weak points instead of burying them, and that's rarer than it should be.
[S] Mine: don't cite a metrics leaderboard winner without checking whether it holds up under a second, independently-collected human evaluation. This year's did not, quietly, in a table most people will skip. For the full tables and figures, check the writeup on the litsearch site.
