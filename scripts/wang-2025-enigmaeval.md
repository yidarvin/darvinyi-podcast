[S] Joining us to walk through it is Marcus, who has spent real time in the puzzle-hunt archives this benchmark draws from. Marcus, welcome to the show.
[G] Thanks for having me. What drew me to this paper is how unglamorous the method is. No adversarial prompt engineering, no red-teaming against known model weaknesses. The authors just took puzzles that human communities already built to be hard, handed them to frontier models mostly as-is, and the models still couldn't do it.
[O] Let's set the stage, Marcus. Why did we need a new reasoning benchmark at all? We already have MMLU, GPQA, MATH, OlympiadBench.
[G] Because all of those, however hard, live inside a well-defined problem space. There's a clear question, a clear domain, and a clear notion of what a correct answer looks like. Frontier models are saturating them fast. The multimodal benchmarks that exist, MMMU, MathVista, VISTA, test vision-language understanding, but again on structured tasks with an obvious question being asked. Nobody was testing the thing that actually happens in real creative problem solving: a long, unstructured, multimodal challenge that arrives with no instructions at all, where step one is figuring out what kind of problem you're even looking at.
[S] And that's where puzzle hunts come in.
[G] Right. A single puzzle-hunt problem might weave wordplay, an acrostic hidden in a poem, a grid you have to read diagonally, a pop-culture reference, and a final indexing step, with nothing telling you any of that is happening. Teams of skilled human solvers take hours to days on the hard ones. It's a genuinely untapped source of difficulty that wasn't designed with language models in mind at all.
[S] But puzzle benchmarks already exist. What was wrong with the ones we had?
[G] They're narrow by construction. PUZZLES, PuzzlePlex, PuzzleBench, GridPuzzle, RiddleSense, each one fixes a single puzzle type and a consistent format: sudoku, crosswords, grid logic, mostly text only. That makes them tractable to build, but it defeats the entire point. The thing puzzle hunts test is format diversity itself, the lateral move of recognizing what kind of reasoning a given puzzle even calls for. EnigmaEval's bet is that this unstructured, cross-domain inference is the actual cognitive frontier, and that puzzle hunts, because they have unambiguous final answers, let you measure it cheaply and verifiably.
[O] Okay, Marcus, take us into the method. What exactly is in this dataset?
[G] Eleven hundred eighty-four puzzles, scraped from eight archived puzzle-hunt events, split by difficulty into a Normal set of nine hundred forty-nine puzzles and a Hard set of two hundred thirty-five. The Normal split is dominated by PuzzledPint, eight hundred thirty-eight of the puzzles, a monthly beginner-friendly pub event whose own house guideline is that a team of inexperienced solvers should finish in under two hours. Then smaller sets: CS50x Puzzle Day, Puzzle Potluck, Mark Halpin's cryptic crosswords, and a small 2020 hunt called CRUMS. The Hard split is Halpin's Labor Day Extravaganza, a hundred fifty-three multi-day puzzles for experienced solvers, seventy-two from MIT Mystery Hunt, and ten from Grandmaster Puzzles. Hard puzzles typically need five or more non-trivial steps, with intermediate answers only thematically hinted by flavor text, no explicit confirmation along the way.
[S] Eighty-eight percent of your easy split coming from one beginner pub event feels worth flagging now, we'll come back to it.
[G] Fair, and I will come back to it too. The clever part of the design is that every puzzle exists in two formats, which lets the benchmark separate two things that usually get confounded. The raw format is a PNG of the original source PDF, or for web puzzles an automated full-page screenshot, an end-to-end test where the model has to parse the document itself. The transcribed format is a human-curated, standardized text-and-image representation that preserves layout and visual structure but strips source-identifying headers and footers. That isolates reasoning from OCR and document parsing.
[O] And building that transcribed version sounds like it was not cheap.
[G] It wasn't. Automating the transcription with language models proved impractical given how much the formats vary, so human annotators hand-transcribed every puzzle, preserved grid structure, stripped anything that would leak the source, and a separate set of reviewers verified both the puzzle and the solution. That curation work was done by Scale AI. The team also filtered the raw material hard: advanced meta-puzzles, ones that combine answers from earlier puzzles, were dropped unless they were solvable from prior answers and titles alone. Seventy-seven meta-puzzles survive that filter, all in the Normal split. Audio, video, and interactive puzzles were excluded outright, and only puzzles under Creative Commons licenses or with explicit author consent made it in.
[S] And grading? Because for a benchmark this unstructured, grading is usually where things get mushy.
[G] It's the opposite here, deliberately cheap and unambiguous. Every solution is a single word or short phrase, annotated into one of three answer patterns, a single answer, a pair, or a comma-separated list. Model outputs are compared to ground truth by plain string matching, no LLM judge, no rubric. Models get a format-specific system prompt requiring a step-by-step solution plus a final answer in a fixed format. And it's a genuinely elaborate prompt: it hands the model a tutorial of puzzle-hunt heuristics, look for acrostics, try indexing, convert numbers to letters, check for pop-culture references. Accuracy is reported at default temperature, averaged over three runs, with standard deviations under zero point six percent across the board.
[O] So walk us through the headline result.
[G] Every frontier vision-language model achieves notably low accuracy. The best, OpenAI's o1, reaches seven percent on Normal and zero percent on Hard. Every other model tested lands around one percent on Normal, and all nine models score zero percent on Hard. o1's reasoning training buys it roughly a five-times lead over the next tier, but in absolute terms the leader is still failing ninety-three percent of the easy puzzles. The authors are explicit that this difficulty is organic, not adversarial. Nothing was curated to exploit known model weaknesses. The puzzles are simply hard, and the failures come from genuine demands for lateral, multi-step, cross-domain reasoning.

[S] There are two more results in here I want on the record before we argue about what any of it means.
[G] The meta-puzzle result is the sharper one. On the seventy-seven meta-puzzles, the model is handed the correct component answers and only has to combine them. o1 still solves just eight point seven percent, and everyone else is at or under two percent. That's not a reasoning failure in the usual sense, it's the model failing to attend to information sitting right there in its own prompt, closer to a long-context failure. And on contamination: puzzle solutions are public online, so some models may have seen them during training, yet the uniformly near-zero scores show no detectable inflation. [O] Zooming out, what does this actually mean for how we should think about evaluation going forward?
[G] The framing the authors land on is that EnigmaEval joins Humanity's Last Exam as a new class of deliberately unsaturated benchmark. Where Humanity's Last Exam probes structured academic knowledge, EnigmaEval probes the integration of reasoning in instruction-free, creative problem-solving, and it produces even lower scores. There's a real methodological lesson underneath that framing for anyone building evals: separating end-to-end performance from raw capability, the way the dual format does here, is a pattern worth stealing regardless of the domain. And treating privacy as a contamination defense, keeping the benchmark unreleased and continuously refreshed rather than published once, is a deliberate design choice other benchmark builders should be considering as public test sets keep leaking into training data.
