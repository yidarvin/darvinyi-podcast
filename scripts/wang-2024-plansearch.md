[O] Here's the paradox this paper opens with. Scaling training compute made language models dramatically better. Scaling inference compute — just sampling the model more times and picking the best answer — barely moved the needle at all.
[S] And their diagnosis is almost insulting to the models. Ask an LLM for two hundred solutions to a hard problem, and you don't get two hundred different attempts. You get one idea, wearing two hundred different outfits.
[O] Which is exactly why I like this paper. If the problem is disguised as a search problem but is actually a diversity problem, the fix isn't a smarter search algorithm. It's asking for different ideas.
[S] "Just ask for different ideas" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. Let's see if it survives the data.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio. I'm the optimist half of this show.
[S] And I'm the skeptic half. Today's paper is Planning In Natural Language Improves LLM Search For Code Generation — PLANSEARCH for short — a ten-author team out of Scale AI, corresponding authors Evan Wang and Hugh Zhang, published at ICLR twenty twenty-five.
[O] Joining us is Maya, who's spent real time inside this paper's plan trees. Maya, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is one of my favorite recent papers, because it turns a vague intuition — the model needs to be more creative — into something you can actually measure and predict.
[S] Start with the bitter lesson framing, since the paper leans on it. What's the actual gap they're diagnosing?
[G] The bitter lesson says two things scale better than anything else: learning and search. Learning has clearly paid off for LLMs. Search — the thing behind superhuman Go and poker — hasn't, despite everyone trying repeated sampling: draw many completions at high temperature, keep whichever one passes. Pass at k rises with k, but only logarithmically, so you burn enormous compute for small gains.
[O] Why is that happening, mechanically?
[G] Their claim is post-training is the culprit. Instruction tuning and RLHF-style preference optimization reward producing one best answer, which collapses the output distribution around a single mode. So resampling doesn't explore alternatives — it resamples noise around the same idea.
[S] Is that asserted, or do they actually show it?
[G] They show a striking case in an appendix figure. DeepSeek-Coder-V2-Lite-Base starts well below its own instruct-tuned version at pass at one, as expected. But as k rises, the base model's curve crosses above the instruct model's — less optimized single-shot accuracy, but more underlying idea diversity, and at high k, diversity wins.
[O] So post-training is trading away exactly the resource that search needs.
[G] That's their reading. It's not universal across every model and benchmark, they say so themselves, but it's a clean illustration of the mechanism they're chasing.
[O] Why code, though, rather than some other domain?
[G] Because code sidesteps half the problem. Most search settings need two things: generate many candidates, then select the winning one. For open-ended tasks, like generating poetry, selection is itself hard, there's no cheap way to know which output is best. Code is different. Any candidate can just be executed against test cases. So if search underperforms on code, the bottleneck has to be generation diversity, not selection, which makes code the cleanest place to isolate the diversity hypothesis.
[S] That's a fair experimental-design choice. It also means the headline results don't automatically transfer to domains where selection itself is hard.
[G] Right, and the paper is upfront that its claims are scoped to code specifically.
[S] Maya, before the algorithm — there are two motivating experiments first, and I think they're actually the more convincing part of the paper. Walk through those.
[G] Agreed, and they're clever because they isolate variables. First: backtranslation. Take a program already known to solve a problem, compress it back into a natural-language sketch, and hand only that sketch, no code, to a fresh model call. Even a roughly ten-token sketch sharply raises the solve rate, and accuracy keeps climbing as the sketch gets longer.
[O] So possessing the right idea is most of the battle.
[S] That's still a somewhat artificial setup, though — the sketch is backtranslated from a solution already known to be correct. What's the second experiment?
[G] More natural. Generate five ideas per problem with no gold answer involved, sample twenty-five implementations per idea, and look at the distribution of solve rates. If ideas didn't matter, you'd expect a smooth spread. Instead it polarizes hard toward zero percent or one hundred percent — once you've committed to an idea, whether it succeeds is basically decided already.
[S] Okay, that's a genuinely clean result. So how does PLANSEARCH turn that into an algorithm?
[G] It's the top rung of a three-step ladder. Rung one is repeated sampling, the baseline — few-shot prompt, draw n solutions directly. Rung two is IdeaSearch: ask for a natural-language solution in one model call, then hand that written idea to a second call and ask it to implement it. Splitting idea and code into separate responses beats ordinary chain-of-thought, where reasoning and code sit in one response.
[O] Interesting — just writing the idea down as its own artifact and handing it off helps.
[G] It does, empirically. PLANSEARCH is the full version: instead of one idea, it searches a whole tree of them. From the problem statement, it prompts for three to six first-order "observations," hints and partial insights, some deliberately wrong, since their only job is pushing toward different ideas, not being correct. It forms every subset of at most two observations, and for each subset generates a further round of merged, second-order observations, capping the tree at depth two for cost.
[S] Wait — deliberately wrong observations are fine?
[G] Correct, that's a deliberate design choice. From that tree, each leaf becomes a natural-language solution, and here's a nice trick: for every idea, they also generate an adversarial twin, by telling the model to assume its own idea is wrong and produce a critique, which doubles the pool. Each surviving idea is then translated step by step, idea to pseudocode to Python, deliberately granular so the model doesn't collapse back to its default solution partway through. One full run produces something like three hundred to four hundred candidate programs.
[O] That's a lot of generation for a single problem.
[G] It is, and that cost matters for how we should read the results later. There's also a genuinely novel measurement contribution: a way to actually score idea diversity instead of gesturing at it. Take a pool of programs, form every pair, backtranslate each to natural language, and ask an LLM judge whether the pair implements the same idea or different ideas. The diversity score is one minus the fraction of pairs judged similar — zero means every program is the same idea in disguise, one means every pair is genuinely distinct.
[S] So it's literally: what fraction of your two hundred samples are different bets, versus cosmetic rewrites of the same bet.
[G] Exactly that.
[S] And that's expensive to compute for every problem, presumably?
[G] It would be, so they subsample: for large pools, forty codes are drawn and every pair among those forty is compared, with GPT-four-o-mini doing the judging. It's an approximation, but it's applied consistently across every model and method, so the comparisons stay fair even if the absolute score isn't perfectly precise.
[O] Maya, give me the headline number.
[G] On LiveCodeBench, the contamination-controlled competitive-programming benchmark, PLANSEARCH on top of Claude 3.5 Sonnet reaches seventy-seven percent at pass at two hundred. The best any model manages without search at all, at pass at one, is forty-one point four percent, from DeepSeek-Coder-V2. So PLANSEARCH is nearly double the best no-search number, and it beats the best non-search pass at two hundred too — GPT-four-o's repeated sampling tops out at sixty point six percent.
[S] Does that hold across every model, or is Sonnet a lucky pick?
[G] It holds everywhere. Across GPT-four-o-mini, GPT-four-o, DeepSeek-Coder-V2, and Sonnet, PLANSEARCH at pass at two hundred beats both repeated sampling and IdeaSearch at pass at two hundred, for every model. On LiveCodeBench specifically: GPT-four-o-mini goes from a pass at one of thirty-nine, to sixty-four point nine with PLANSEARCH. GPT-four-o: forty-one point three, up to seventy-three. DeepSeek-Coder-V2: forty-one point four, up to seventy point three. And Sonnet: forty point three, up to seventy-seven.
[O] And on the other two benchmarks?
[G] HumanEval-plus and MBPP-plus are already close to saturated, but PLANSEARCH still moves them. GPT-four-o goes from eighty-six point four up to ninety-nine point five pass at two hundred on HumanEval-plus. Sonnet goes from seventy-seven point one up to ninety-three point seven on MBPP-plus. Less dramatic in absolute terms, since there's less room left, but the ordering holds everywhere.
[S] Any efficiency angle here, or is this purely about the accuracy ceiling?
[G] There's a genuinely surprising line in the abstract on that. Running PLANSEARCH on top of a small model, GPT-four-o-mini, outperforms larger models that aren't using search at all, after merely four attempts. So this isn't only a story about pushing frontier models higher, it changes the compute tradeoff for small models too.
[O] So a small model with a good search strategy can beat a big model with none, almost immediately.
[S] What about a model that's already doing search-like reasoning internally?
[G] That's their most honest inclusion, and I think it's the single most interesting data point in the paper. They test o1-mini, which already spends inference-time compute on its own. Its repeated-sampling pass at two hundred is ninety point eight. PLANSEARCH only nudges that to ninety-one point three — the gain nearly vanishes.
[O] Which actually supports their story, in a strange way — o1's training may already be buying the diversity PLANSEARCH is manufacturing at inference time.
[S] Or it just means PLANSEARCH runs out of room once a model is already strong. Either reading is consistent with one data point.
[G] Fair, and the paper doesn't over-claim there. Two more results worth holding onto. Public-test filtering, since every benchmark ships a few public tests you can filter candidates against before submitting, shifts the whole curve left by roughly ten times: PLANSEARCH reaches seventy-seven point one percent at just pass at twenty, matching its unfiltered pass at two hundred number, and it beats filtered repeated sampling by a flat thirty to forty points even at only ten completions.
[O] So in a realistic deployment where you get to run public tests first, you don't actually need two hundred samples.
[S] Hold that thought, because I want to come back to it. What's this diversity "law" you mentioned earlier?
[G] Take every combination of model, method, and benchmark, plot its measured diversity score against its relative improvement from pass at one to pass at two hundred, and you get a strikingly tight, monotone, positive relationship. Diversity predicts how much a method gains from scaling samples. PLANSEARCH sits in the high-diversity, high-gain corner, around a diversity score of point five nine for Sonnet, with a relative gain near one point seven eight, and even o1-mini falls on the same line.
[O] That's the part that elevates this from "here's a good trick" to "here's a design principle." If diversity is measurable and forecastable, you can optimize for it directly instead of stumbling into it.
[O] Let me make the strongest optimist case. This isn't one cherry-picked win, it's consistent across four models and three benchmarks, and contamination is handled properly: LiveCodeBench is restricted to problems from May through September twenty twenty-four, after Sonnet's April twenty twenty-four knowledge cutoff, with the same window for every model, not just their best one. They even included o1-mini, a comparison that undercuts their own headline. And the diversity law turns a vague intuition into a measurable, transferable design target.
[S] I'll grant all of that. Here's my deflationary case, in three parts. First: pass at two hundred is an oracle metric. It means a correct solution exists somewhere in the pool of two hundred, and recovering it requires a verifier, the hidden test suite, to actually pick the winner. Without that selection step, seventy-seven percent is an upper bound on what's recoverable, not a number you get to keep, and we just heard that PLANSEARCH often lowers pass at one, the number closest to real deployment.
[G] Accurate, and to their credit the paper doesn't hide it, the filtering result, pass at twenty reaching seventy-seven point one, is their answer to exactly that objection. But it leans on every problem shipping usable public tests, which won't always be true outside a benchmark.
[S] Second: the main table is normalized per attempt, not per unit of compute. PLANSEARCH builds a whole observation tree, dozens of model calls, before writing a single line of code. Comparing methods attempt-for-attempt flatters whichever one thinks longest per attempt. There is a compute-matched comparison in an appendix, and even the authors concede PLANSEARCH only wins there, quote, "if sufficient compute is expended."
[G] That's a real caveat, and it's worth knowing it exists, most of the headline numbers in this paper are the attempt-matched ones, not the compute-matched ones.
[O] Okay, I'll take that hit. Third point?
[S] The diversity metric itself. It's LLM-judged, GPT-four-o-mini deciding whether two backtranslated ideas count as the same, and it's measured on the same problems whose search gains it's used to predict. So "diversity predicts search gains" is partly a statement about one model's notion of sameness, not a fully independent quantity. There's also a technical wrinkle: the pass at k estimator assumes independent successes, and the paper's own appendix admits PLANSEARCH's generations aren't independent, since they share observation-tree ancestry.
[O] Maya, adjudicate. Who's winning these exchanges?
[G] Let me score it by severity. The compute-matched concession is the biggest one, it should change how anyone reads the headline number. The diversity metric being self-referential is a real limitation, but softer: it's still predictive, just not a clean, provider-independent measurement. The independence violation is the softest of the three, it's a known wrinkle in how pass at k gets computed for any correlated sampling method, and they flag it themselves rather than burying it.
[O] So my score: the core claim survives. Search over natural-language ideas genuinely beats search over tokens or code, across models and benchmarks, and the diversity law is a real, useful forecasting tool.
[S] And mine: believe the relative ordering, be skeptical of the absolute seventy-seven percent as anything deployable today, and always ask whether you're looking at the attempt-matched table or the compute-matched one.
[G] I'd adjudicate it about the same way. The method and the mechanism are well-supported. The size of the headline number needs exactly the asterisks you two just put on it.
[S] What actually changes if this holds up?
[G] The paper's own answer, in its limitations section, is that the durable fix isn't inference-time search at all, it's re-architecting post-training around search, so a model ships with diversity built in rather than trained away. They point at reasoning-style training as the likely direction, and note a natural next step is training directly on PLANSEARCH's successful plans, distilling pass at k back into pass at one without inference-time tricks.
[O] There's a broader evaluation lesson buried in here too, past the code angle. An oracle metric like pass at two hundred can make a method look transformative while quietly assuming away the hardest part of deployment, which is selection. A benchmark that reports a correct answer exists somewhere in the pool, without also reporting how you'd find it, is telling you half a story.
[S] Which is exactly why I'd want compute-matched, verifier-realistic numbers as the default in this literature, not an appendix footnote.
[G] There's one more wrinkle in their limitations section worth having on record. With public-test filtering applied, most base models actually outperform their own instruct-tuned versions at pass at one, across nearly every dataset they tested. That's the diversity trade laid bare: base models are worse single-shot, but once cheap filtering can promote a good idea out of a diverse pool, the base model's extra diversity starts winning even at low k.
[O] Which is a genuinely strange picture of where the value from post-training might be misallocated. It also connects to something bigger than this one paper — most public leaderboards report only a single pass at one number, and that number hides exactly the dimension, diversity, that decides whether a model is worth pairing with more inference-time compute at all.
[G] If I had to leave you with one sentence: the bottleneck to search was never the algorithm, it was that post-trained models had quietly stopped having different ideas, and PLANSEARCH shows that searching natural-language idea space, not code space, restores that diversity and predictably improves with it.
