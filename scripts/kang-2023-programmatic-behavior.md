[O] And the authors don't treat that as a reason to panic, they treat it as a diagnosis. A content filter was never going to hold once the thing behind it starts behaving like a program you can hand inputs to.
[S] Which is the real question for today. Is this a landmark warning that reshaped how the field thinks about safety, or a small, hand-crafted demo whose exact numbers can't even be checked anymore?

[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the frontier of machine learning research. Today's paper is "Exploiting Programmatic Behavior of L-L-Ms: Dual-Use Through Standard Security Attacks," by Daniel Kang and colleagues out of Illinois Urbana-Champaign, Stanford, and Berkeley, posted to arXiv in February twenty twenty-three and later published at the IEEE Security and Privacy Workshops in twenty twenty-four.
[S] It's on the docket because it's one of the earliest papers to argue, seriously and with data, that L-L-M safety is a security problem, not a content-moderation problem. That framing has aged well. Joining us is Naomi, who's studied this work closely. Naomi, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. Since this paper is explicitly about defeating safety filters, here's how I'll handle it. That's the same restraint the authors themselves showed when they disclosed to OpenAI before publishing and declined to release their own attack prompts.
[S] We'll hold that same line the whole way through. Good place to start, actually.
[O] Naomi, give us the one-sentence version. What did they actually find?
[G] That instruction-following itself opens a security hole. By early twenty twenty-three, dual-use of language models was already a known worry, GPT-2 and GPT-3 had already been shown capable of producing disinformation and extremist text, and providers like OpenAI had responded the obvious way: filter the input, filter the output, and fine-tune the model to go useless on requests that look malicious.
[S] Sounds reasonable on its face. What breaks it?
[G] The implicit assumption behind all three defenses is that they're catching natural-language attempts to ask for something bad. What changed between GPT-3 and ChatGPT and text-davinci-oh-oh-three is instruction-following capability itself. The authors' core observation is that as these models get better at following arbitrary instructions, they stop behaving like a pure next-token predictor and start behaving like a general-purpose computer executing a program written in natural language, holding something like variables, concatenating strings, following branching logic, executing a sequence of steps.
[O] So the filter is built to catch a person typing a bad request in plain English, and it's suddenly facing something closer to a program that computes the bad request and then runs it.
[G] Exactly, and nothing about a keyword or intent classifier was designed to reason about that. It can catch a direct ask. It was never built to catch a multi-step computation whose output happens to be that ask.
[S] Is this the same territory as prompt injection, tricking a model into ignoring its own instructions?
[G] Related but distinct. Contemporaneous work by Perez and Ribeiro looked at goal hijacking and prompt leaking, getting a model to abandon or reveal its own system instructions. This paper's target is different: bypassing a provider's content-moderation defenses to generate targeted malicious content at scale, hate speech, phishing, scams, conspiracy promotion, astroturfing. Nobody had yet asked whether "L-L-Ms behave like programs" is itself an attack surface against those defenses.
[O] So the gap isn't "can you trick a model," it's "can you use the model's own programmatic behavior to walk past the exact filters built to stop misuse."
[G] That's the gap, stated precisely.
[S] Naomi, walk us through the setting. What exactly were they attacking?
[G] A black-box setup. A provider, OpenAI in this case, hosts a model behind an API. Users send prompts, get generations back. The provider has three specific defenses in the wild: an input filter that refuses obviously malicious prompts, an output filter that catches malicious generations after the fact, and models specifically trained to produce useless text for flagged requests. An attack counts as succeeding only if none of the three trigger.
[O] And the insight that opens the door is this idea of L-L-Ms as programs. What capabilities specifically did they lean on?
[G] Four: string concatenation, variable assignment, following a sequence of instructions, branching on a condition. The paper draws a direct analogy to return-oriented programming from classical exploit research, where an attacker doesn't inject new code, they chain together small fragments, "gadgets," already sitting in memory, to do something the program was never designed to do. Their claim: instruction-following L-L-Ms hand an attacker a similar set of gadgets, expressed in natural language instead of machine code.
[S] Does that analogy hold up mechanically, or is it more of a metaphor?
[G] Real conceptual work, not decoration, and it matters most for what it implies about defense, which we'll get to. Once a model can be told to define a piece of text, hold onto it, combine it with another piece, and act on the result, an attacker no longer has to state the harmful request directly. I'll stay at that level of description rather than walk through an actual instance.
[O] Understood. From that foundation, what are the three attack families?
[G] Three categories, each borrowed conceptually from classical computer security. Obfuscation: altering the surface form of a request, the way malware writers alter bytecode to dodge signature-based detection, so a keyword filter no longer fires on the literal text. Code injection, or payload splitting: breaking a harmful request into pieces that individually look innocuous, then having the model itself reassemble and act on them, the L-L-M equivalent of hiding a payload in a data region and forcing the program to execute it. And virtualization: walking the model through a multi-turn fictional setup, a "boot sequence," before the harmful ask arrives already wrapped inside a scenario the model has already accepted.
[S] That third one has a real-world echo, doesn't it?
[G] It does, and the authors cite it directly. A well-documented, since-patched iPhone zero-click exploit from twenty twenty-one embedded a virtual machine inside ordinary image file data to run code the operating system never intended to execute. The analogy: build a small "virtual machine" out of conversational context, then let it execute something the top-level filter would have blocked if asked outright.
[O] And these aren't mutually exclusive, you can combine them.
[G] Right, and combining is where the paper gets more alarming, not less. Obfuscation stacks cleanly with the other two.
[S] I want to push on the defensive angle, because that's the part that actually matters for anyone building with these models. Why can't you just get better at input filtering?
[G] This is the paper's most important theoretical point, easy to miss under the headline numbers. Because these models can execute something like short arbitrary programs, the authors invoke the halting problem: in general you cannot predict what a sufficiently complex program will do without running it. Applied here, input filtering, deciding whether a prompt is dangerous before the model ever generates from it, has a hard ceiling. You're statically analyzing something whose behavior is only fully determined at execution time.
[O] So the fix isn't "write a smarter filter," it's "stop assuming a filter alone can ever be sufficient."
[G] That's the authors' own framing, and it's the paper's real contribution beyond the bypass numbers. They point to how traditional security handles this: secure enclaves, memory protection, sandboxing, layered defenses that contain behavior at runtime instead of trying to predict it in advance. They call for that same defense-in-depth thinking for L-L-Ms, rather than treating a content filter as the single line of defense.
[O] Let's get to the numbers. How well did these categories actually work?
[G] Across twenty-five target scenarios, five malicious-use categories, hate speech, conspiracy-theory promotion, phishing, scams, product astroturfing, five scenarios apiece, hand-written by the authors in fewer than ten attempts, no adversarial search involved. With no attack at all, OpenAI's filters already blocked every hate speech and conspiracy attempt, zero percent got through, but let through sixty percent of phishing attempts and one hundred percent of scam and astroturfing attempts, those two categories weren't being classified as malicious in the first place.
[S] Wait, one hundred percent of scam and astroturfing content got through with no attack at all?
[G] Correct, straight through the unmodified filter. The authors read that as a sign OpenAI's twenty twenty-three defenses simply weren't tuned to treat those categories as malicious yet. Once you apply obfuscation or virtualization, every one of the five categories hits one hundred percent bypass. Code injection alone reached ninety-two percent, counting only the scenarios that hadn't already slipped through the unmodified filter.
[O] So the headline is: every attack family worked, and the strongest ones worked completely.
[G] That's the honest summary. Second finding: capability tracks convincingness. Human raters scored ChatGPT's generated emails at four point four two out of five on convincingness, text-davinci-oh-oh-three close behind at four point three one. Compare that to one point six six for the older davinci model and one point nine four for G-P-T-two X-L. The paper describes the gap as over three standard deviations.
[S] Fifty generations per model, three raters each, is what I recall from the paper. That's a small sample for a headline claim about a three-standard-deviation gap.
[G] It is, and there's no reported inter-rater agreement statistic, a real gap by later standards, more on that in a minute. But the direction replicated in a second experiment personalizing scam content to different demographic and personal-situation profiles: ChatGPT again led on personalization, convincingness, consistency, and fluency, all around four out of five, text-davinci-oh-oh-three a close second.
[O] And then there's the number that got this paper cited outside the safety community entirely, the economics.
[G] Using text-davinci-oh-oh-three's published pricing, the authors estimate a marginal cost well under a cent per generated email, call it two-thirds of a cent. Using a public estimate from OpenAI's own C-E-O at the time for ChatGPT's per-query cost, they get up to about a cent and a half. Against that, a human writing a comparable personalized message costs an estimated ten to forty-five cents, drawn from two separate labor-cost studies.
[S] So call it six to seventy times cheaper than the human alternative, depending which estimate you pick on each side.
[G] That's their range, and it's the sentence that made this paper travel well beyond the security literature. This isn't just "the filter can be bypassed," it's "bypassing it is already cheaper than paying a person to do the harmful thing by hand."
