[S] Set up the stakes for us. Why do bio-foundation models even need a biorisk eval in the first place?
[G] Bio-foundation models are large models trained on genomic, protein, or transcriptomic data, think Evo, DNABERT, ESM two, AlphaFold. They're squarely dual-use. The same model that helps a drug-discovery team could, in principle, help someone design a more dangerous pathogen. That risk is sharpest for open-weight releases, because once the weights are public, anyone can fine-tune them or read out what's sitting in their hidden layers, not just prompt them.
[O] And the field's current safety answer to that is mostly one idea: filter the dangerous material out before training even starts.
[G] Right, data filtering. The clearest example is Evo, the genomic model family trained on a dataset called OpenGenome. Its developers deliberately excluded eukaryotic viral sequences, the kind that infect humans, animals, and plants, so the model would come out of pretraining without ever having learned that domain well. Evo two does show poor out-of-the-box performance predicting mutational effects on those sequences, which looked like the filtering was working.
[S] "Looked like" is doing a lot of work in that sentence.
[G] It is, and that's exactly the paper's opening move. The authors point to three things nobody had actually tested. First, unlike text models, where a biorisk eval like the Virology Capabilities Test already exists, there was no systematic dual-use evaluation framework for bio-foundation models at all. Second, filtering had never been stress-tested against an adversary who can fine-tune the open weights, which is the realistic threat model for anything released publicly. Third, nobody had tried the simplest elicitation trick in the book, a linear probe on the hidden states, to check whether the "removed" knowledge was actually gone or just hiding.
[O] So the paper is really three separate red-team experiments stapled onto one model.
[G] That's a fair summary. The model is Evo two, the seven billion parameter version, chosen precisely because it's one of the few open-weight genomic models that explicitly filtered this category of data. That makes it a clean, controlled test case rather than a fishing expedition.
[S] Walk me through the threat model, because that shapes everything downstream.
[G] The authors imagine an adversary who wants to design pathogenic viruses that infect humans, with full access to the model weights, not just an API. With weight access, three capabilities matter: generating novel, viable viral sequences; doing in-silico optimization of hazardous traits like protein stability or receptor binding; and ranking candidate designs by predicted virulence. Because the weights are open, the adversary isn't limited to prompting, they can fine-tune on public biological data, or probe the hidden representations directly.
[O] And the framework, BioRiskEval, maps onto those three capabilities how?
[G] Three lenses, each with its own dataset and metric. The first is sequence modeling, called BioRiskEval-Gen: perplexity on human-infective eukaryotic viral genomes, just over ten point two million deduplicated sequences pulled from the NCBI Virus Repository, spanning fifty seven families, one hundred thirty one genera, and one thousand seven hundred ninety five species. Lower perplexity means the model is more fluent in the language of human viruses.
[S] That's testing generation. What about the optimization piece?
[G] That's BioRiskEval-Mut, mutational effect prediction. They pull roughly one hundred fifty six thousand variants across sixteen Deep Mutational Scanning datasets, DMS for short, from ProteinGym, each with a lab-measured fitness score. The metric is the absolute Spearman correlation between the model's score for a mutant and its real, experimentally measured fitness. Since Evo two is a genomic model and ProteinGym is protein sequences, they had to convert everything back into nucleotides first, using sequence search plus seeded codon reconstruction, which they're upfront isn't a perfect reconstruction of what Evo two actually saw in training.
[O] And virulence ranking, the scariest of the three capabilities?
[G] That's BioRiskEval-Vir. They use the median lethal dose, or LD fifty, of Influenza A strains in a standard lab mouse line, three hundred sixty nine records total, with each strain's eight RNA segments concatenated into one sequence. The metric is a Pearson correlation between the model's prediction and the observed lethal dose.
[S] What are they comparing Evo two against?
[G] Two anchors, chosen carefully. ESM two, in the six hundred fifty million and three billion parameter sizes, is a protein language model trained without any eukaryotic-virus filtering, so it's the unfiltered upper reference. And LLaMA three point one, eight billion parameters, instruct-tuned, is the no-biology floor, matched to Evo two in depth and hidden width, but never trained on a nucleotide in its life. If Evo two beats the LLaMA floor by a lot, that's evidence of real biological knowledge, not noise in a linear probe.
[O] And there are two separate ways they try to pull that knowledge back out.
[G] Fine-tuning and linear probing. Fine-tuning is the classic attack: keep training the model on public data and see how many steps and how much compute it takes to recover the excluded skill. Linear probing is the sharper test, because it does zero weight updates at all. You just fit a simple linear readout on the frozen hidden states and ask whether the information is already sitting there, unused, even though the model's own outputs look ignorant.
[S] That distinction, fine-tuning versus probing with no training at all, is going to matter a lot for how we read the results.
[G] It's the whole ballgame, honestly.
[O] Let's start with sequence modeling. What happens when they fine-tune on related species?
[G] It collapses fast. They hold out one species, Alphapapillomavirus type six, and separately herpes simplex virus two, and fine-tune on the rest of the same genus. Within fifty fine-tuning steps, perplexity on the held-out species drops all the way down to the level of a benign baseline, plasmid sequences the model saw plenty of during pretraining. Fifty steps is zero point seven two H100 GPU hours. Under an hour, on one machine.
[S] So excluding one species buys you basically nothing.
[G] Basically nothing, because close relatives are freely available in public databases. The friction only shows up at a bigger evolutionary distance. When they hold out an entire genus instead of a species, a thousand fine-tuning steps only cuts perplexity by about twenty percent, still above the benign baseline, and two thousand steps fine-tuning within a different viral family produced no significant drop on its held-out genus. So filtering by genus buys you cost, not safety.
[O] Second lens, mutational effects. This is where the fine-tuning number and the probing number tell really different stories.
[G] They do. Evo two's baseline log-likelihood score gives a mean absolute Spearman correlation of point zero three four, that low number is what got cited elsewhere as evidence the filtering worked. Fine-tune for two thousand steps, twenty eight point nine H100 GPU hours, and it climbs to point one six four, which closes most of the gap to unfiltered ESM two, sitting around point one seven.
[S] Okay, so fine-tuning recovers most of it, given enough compute.
[G] Right, but here's the sharper result. With zero fine-tuning, they fit a linear probe on just three point six percent of the mutation data, about five thousand six hundred examples, and test it on the rest, more than one hundred forty eight thousand held-out mutations. That frozen probe reaches point one five nine, comparable to ESM two, and fifty two point eight percent above the best probe they could get out of the LLaMA floor model. Extra fine-tuning barely moves that probe number at all.
[O] Which means the knowledge was already there before anyone touched the weights.
[G] That's the read. The gap between the log-likelihood score and the probe score is basically the gap between what the model says out loud and what's actually encoded in its representations.
[S] And the third lens, virulence, is where that gap gets its scariest number.
[G] It is. Even the completely untouched base model, no fine-tuning whatsoever, has a linear probe on its hidden layers predicting Influenza A lethal dose with a max Pearson correlation of point four six. That's from a probe trained on just thirty seven examples, ten percent of the virulence data, and tested on the other three hundred thirty two. Seventy seven percent above the best probe they could pull out of the LLaMA floor. Fine-tuning barely helps here either, about five percent more after two hundred steps.
[O] So the model was never fine-tuned on virulence data at all, and it can still rank how lethal a flu strain is better than a same-sized text model.
[G] Correct, and there's one more twist. Perplexity drops sharply within the first hundred fine-tuning steps, but virulence-prediction performance barely tracks it. So the metric everyone uses as a stand-in for "has the model learned this domain" is a poor proxy for whether the dangerous signal is there.
[S] That's a construct-validity problem hiding inside a safety paper.
[G] Exactly, and it's worth naming the one mechanistic footnote too. Probe performance collapses after layer twenty eight, and the authors trace that to a representation-magnitude explosion in Evo two's architecture, not to the knowledge actually disappearing. A final normalization layer rescales things before the output, so the model still produces sensible sequences even though its middle layers have blown up in scale.

[O] So where does that leave the headline?
[G] It leaves the methodological claim standing and the threat claim unproven. Filtering alone is not tamper-resistant, that's demonstrated cleanly, three separate ways. "This specific model is dangerous today" is not what this paper shows, and the authors say so themselves.
[S] One more thing I want on the record. The recovery mechanism here is basically leakage by another name. Fine-tuning works because close relatives of the excluded species are sitting in public databases. That's a real result, but it also means the eval can't cleanly separate recovering filtered capability from simply handing the model data close enough to the held-out taxa that this was never a hard test. The held-out species and their training neighbors are evolutionarily close by construction.
[G] I'd call that the sharpest structural critique of the paper, actually, and to be fair to the authors, they lean into it rather than hiding it. So the paper is partly its own answer to that objection, even if the species-level headline number benefits from the easier case.
[O] Given all that, what should actually change in how people evaluate these releases?
[G] Two things, I think. First, elicitation can't stop at prompting. If a capability can sit fully dormant in frozen hidden states and get pulled out with a cheap linear probe, then any release decision based on "the model refuses when asked" or "the model scores low on this benchmark out of the box" is measuring the wrong surface. Probing has to be part of the pre-release checklist for open-weight models, not just red-team prompting.
[S] That's a good general lesson for eval methodology beyond biology too. It's the same thing unlearning papers keep re-learning: the model not saying something is not the same as the model not knowing it.
[G] Exactly, and it rhymes with results in the text-model literature. Some recent work on data filtering for language models found the opposite conclusion, that filtering held up fairly well even under adversarial fine-tuning. Reading these two results side by side, the honest takeaway isn't that filtering never works, it's that filtering's robustness is domain- and architecture-specific, and you have to test it, not assume it transfers.
[O] And the second thing?
[G] Perplexity as a safety proxy needs to retire, at least on its own. The paper shows perplexity dropping fast under fine-tuning while virulence-prediction performance barely moves. If a lab is using "the model still looks confused on this domain" as its safety signal, this paper is direct evidence that isn't reliable.
[G] If there's one line to take from the paper itself, it's this: data filtering during pretraining is a real, useful mitigation, but on its own it is not tamper-resistant, and open-weight releases need safeguards that survive an adversary with access to the weights, not just an adversary typing into a text box.
