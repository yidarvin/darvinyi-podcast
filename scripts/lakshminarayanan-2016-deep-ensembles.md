[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio. Today we're on "Simple and Scalable Predictive Uncertainty Estimation using Deep Ensembles," by Balaji Lakshminarayanan, Alexander Pritzel, and Charles Blundell at DeepMind.
[S] Posted in December 2016, published the following year at NeurIPS 2017. And joining us to walk through it is Naomi, who's studied this paper closely.
[G] Glad to be here. This is one of those papers where the idea is almost embarrassingly simple to state, and yet it's held up for nearly a decade as the strongest non-Bayesian uncertainty baseline in the field.
[O] Naomi, before we get into the recipe, set the scene. Why was this even a hard problem in 2016?
[G] Because the standard answer to "does my network know what it doesn't know" was Bayesian. Put a prior over the weights, compute or approximate the posterior given the data, and use that distribution to quantify uncertainty in predictions.
[S] And exact Bayesian inference over the weights of a deep net is intractable, so the whole field became a menu of approximations.
[G] Right. Laplace approximation, Markov chain Monte Carlo, variational Bayes, stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics, expectation propagation. Each one requires real surgery on the training procedure, extra hyperparameters, and often extra compute at test time.
[O] And the one that actually caught on in practice was Monte Carlo dropout, from Gal and Ghahramani in 2016.
[G] Right, because it's cheap: you just leave dropout on at test time and treat repeated stochastic forward passes as samples from an approximate posterior. It's popular because it's easy, not because anyone was fully convinced it was the right approximation.
[S] And the paper makes a point I actually like here: the quality of any of these Bayesian methods depends on two things you can't easily verify. How good the approximation is, and whether the prior you picked is even reasonable for the problem. They call bad priors "priors of convenience," and say they can produce unreasonable uncertainty no matter how careful the inference is.
[G] That's the authors' framing, and it's the real motivation. They ask: is there a non-Bayesian way to get equally good, or better, predictive uncertainty, using nothing more exotic than the standard training pipeline?
[O] And they define what "good" even means along two axes, which I think is underrated as a contribution on its own.
[G] Calibration and robustness to distribution shift. Calibration means a model's confidence scores match its actual long-run correctness rate, measured with proper scoring rules like negative log-likelihood and the Brier score. It's explicitly orthogonal to accuracy: a network can be accurate and miscalibrated, or the reverse.
[S] And the second axis is whether the model gets appropriately less confident on inputs unlike anything it trained on, instead of staying confidently wrong.
[G] Exactly, what the field calls out-of-distribution robustness. Get both of those right with something a practitioner can drop into an existing pipeline, no new inference machinery, minimal tuning, and that was the open problem.
[O] So walk us through the recipe. Naomi, you called it embarrassingly simple to state.
[G] Three ingredients, and I want to be precise about which one is doing what, because that matters later. First, train each network with a proper scoring rule as the loss, not the more common alternative.
[S] Define proper scoring rule for the listener who hasn't touched this since a stats class.
[G] It's a scoring function whose expected value is uniquely maximized by the true data-generating distribution. Ordinary softmax cross-entropy for classification already is one, so nothing changes there. The real departure is on the regression side.
[O] Where normally a network just outputs one number, a point prediction, trained to minimize squared error.
[G] Right, and squared error doesn't capture uncertainty at all. Instead, the network outputs two values: a predicted mean and a predicted variance, and it's trained to maximize Gaussian log-likelihood instead of minimizing mean squared error. A network that predicts an overconfident, too-small variance pays a steep penalty when it turns out to be wrong.
[S] So ingredient one alone, before any ensembling, already lets a network say "I don't know" by widening its own variance instead of silently absorbing noise into a point estimate.
[G] That's exactly the authors' framing, and it's worth sitting with, because it's the part people skip past to get to the ensembling.
[O] What's ingredient two?
[G] An optional smoothing step. For each training point, generate an adversarial example with the fast gradient sign method, nudging the input a small amount, one percent of that dimension's range, in the direction that most increases the loss. Add that as an extra training example.
[S] That sounds like it's about robustness to attacks, which is a different literature entirely.
[G] The authors frame it differently, and I think it's the more interesting reading. They argue it's a computationally cheap way to smooth the predictive distribution in the one direction the network is most likely to be wrong, standing in for the intractable alternative of smoothing along every direction in input space.
[O] And ingredient three is the one in the title.
[G] Train M copies of the network completely independently. Random initialization, random data order, no bootstrap resampling, no parameter sharing, zero interaction during training. The recommended default is M equals five. At prediction time you treat the ensemble as a uniformly weighted mixture, average the softmax outputs for classification, and for regression, collapse the mixture of Gaussians into one Gaussian by matching its mean and variance.
[S] Why no bootstrap? Bagging is the classic way to inject diversity into an ensemble.
[G] They tried it and it hurt performance. Because a bootstrap sample only contains about sixty-three percent of the unique training points, on average, and deep nets typically do better with more data, not less. Random initialization and data shuffling turned out to be enough diversity on their own.
[O] There's a toy example in the paper that makes this concrete, a one-dimensional regression problem, y equals x cubed plus noise, twenty training points.
[G] It's a great illustration precisely because it isolates each ingredient. A plain ensemble of squared-error networks only captures uncertainty implicitly, through disagreement between point predictions, and the predicted band barely widens away from the data. Switch a single network to the log-likelihood loss, and the band widens appropriately outside the data region, even with no ensembling at all. Add adversarial training, and it smooths further. Only the full recipe, five log-likelihood networks together, gives a band that's tight near the data and widens cleanly as you move away from it.
[S] Naomi, I want to push on something before we get to the big numbers. The framing going around this paper is that ensembling is the ingredient doing the heavy lifting, with the other two as cheap add-ons. Does the paper's own data actually support that?
[G] This is a good place to flag a real discrepancy, because the appendix has an ablation that tells a more specific story than that framing suggests. Table two in the appendix compares an ensemble of squared-error networks using empirical disagreement as its uncertainty proxy, against a single network trained with the log-likelihood loss, against the full five-network deep ensemble.
[O] And?
[G] On Boston housing, the NLL for a five-network squared-error ensemble is about seventeen point three. A ten-network version is about ten point six. Switching to a single network trained with log-likelihood, no ensembling at all, drops that to about two point five five. The full five-network deep ensemble is two point four one.
[S] So the jump from squared-error ensembling to log-likelihood training is enormous, and the further jump from one log-likelihood network to five of them is comparatively small.
[G] Exactly, and the paper's own appendix caption says this "clearly demonstrates the effect of learning variance." For the regression NLL numbers specifically, the proper scoring rule is what's doing most of the heavy lifting, and ensembling adds a real but noticeably smaller increment on top. Where the paper wins over any looser retelling is here in the actual table, so that's the version worth trusting.

[O] What about MNIST and SVHN classification?
[G] They sweep the ensemble size, one up to fifteen networks on MNIST with a simple MLP, one up to ten on SVHN with a VGG-style convnet, and compare four variants: plain ensembling, ensembling with random-direction augmentation, ensembling with adversarial training, and MC-dropout at the matching sample count. Every ensemble variant beats MC-dropout at every ensemble size, on classification error, NLL, and Brier score simultaneously.
[S] And adversarial training?
[G] Consistent boost on MNIST at every ensemble size. On SVHN it only helps at M equals one, and the benefit fades as the ensemble grows. [O] Meaning MC-dropout keeps handing out high-confidence labels on exactly the examples it should be least sure about.
[G] While the ensemble's stated confidence actually tracks its real reliability. That's the practical payoff of the whole calibration story.
