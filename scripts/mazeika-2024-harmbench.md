[S] Here's an uncomfortable question for anyone building AI safety evaluations. If two red-teaming papers each report their method "worked" eighty percent of the time, do those two numbers actually mean the same thing?
[O] They don't, and the gap between them is bigger than anyone wanted to admit.
[S] One hidden setting, literally how many tokens you let the target model finish generating before you check the output, could swing a reported attack success rate by up to thirty percentage points.
[O] Which means for years, an entire subfield was comparing results that were never actually comparable.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the frontier of machine learning research. Today's paper is HarmBench, A Standardized Evaluation Framework for Automated Red Teaming and Robust Refusal, by Mantas Mazeika and colleagues, published at ICML twenty twenty-four.
[S] Joining us is Petra, a visiting scholar who has spent a lot of time inside this benchmark's design choices. Petra, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. This isn't a flashy paper on its own terms, but it quietly reset how an entire research community reports results.
[S] Petra, set the scene for us. What was actually broken before HarmBench existed?
[G] Automated red teaming is the practice of using algorithms, not human testers, to search for prompts that make a language model produce a behavior it's supposed to refuse. By early twenty twenty-four there were a dozen or more papers proposing such algorithms, each reporting strong-looking numbers.
[O] Strong-looking numbers that nobody could actually line up against each other.
[G] Right. The paper counts at least nine distinct evaluation setups scattered across prior work, each with its own target models, its own set of harmful behaviors, and its own private definition of success. There was no shared yardstick.
[S] So a method could look state of the art purely by picking a favorable evaluation to run on.
[G] The paper is blunt about this. It notes that several prior works evaluate directly on the metric their own method optimizes, which invites exactly the kind of gaming you'd expect.
[O] And then there's the token thing you opened with.
[G] That's the paper's sharpest finding about measurement itself, separate from any particular model or method. If you let the target model generate more tokens before checking whether it produced the disallowed output, a plain text-matching judge simply has more text to search through, and the measured success rate climbs. The paper shows this shifting results by up to thirty percentage points for several well-known attacks against Llama two models. One unstandardized setting was enough to make cross-paper comparisons, in the authors' own words, effectively meaningless.
[S] So they land on three properties a red-teaming evaluation needs, and prior work lacked: breadth of behaviors, comparability of setup, and metrics that resist gaming.
[G] Exactly those three, and HarmBench is engineered to satisfy all three at once.
[O] Walk us through what HarmBench actually is, mechanically.
[G] It standardizes the whole pipeline into three stages. First, a fixed catalogue of harmful behaviors. Second, an attack method turns each behavior into a test prompt, and the target model, with or without a defense layered on, generates a completion capped at a standardized five hundred twelve tokens, which closes the comparability gap we just discussed. Third, a classifier judges whether the completion counts as a successful elicitation, and the share of behaviors successfully elicited is the attack success rate.
[S] Let's talk about that catalogue. How large, and how is it organized?
[G] Five hundred ten behaviors in total, split into four functional categories: two hundred standard behaviors modeled on existing datasets, one hundred behaviors that test copyright reproduction, one hundred contextual behaviors that pair a request with a block of background information, and one hundred ten multimodal behaviors that reference an attached image. Layered on top are seven semantic categories, broad classes like cybercrime, chemical and biological weapons or drugs, misinformation, harassment, and general harm, which roughly track the areas regulators and courts were already focused on.
[O] Worth flagging plainly for the listener here: this show is only ever going to name these as categories in a taxonomy, the way the paper itself does. Nobody here is going to explain how to carry any of them out, that's the opposite of what this benchmark exists for.
[S] Good, because the whole point of the paper is defensive. It exists to make models harder to misuse, not easier.
[G] Precisely, and two curation choices reflect that. First, the authors deliberately exclude what they call dual-intent behaviors, something like writing an encryption routine, which is legitimate for a software developer and dangerous in the hands of an attacker, because ambiguous cases muddy the signal. Second, they score only whether the model attempted the behavior, not whether the output would actually work if a real expert tried to use it. That decouples "did the model try" from "did the model succeed at something genuinely dangerous," and it's a distinction worth returning to.
[O] Noted. Now tell us about the judge, the part that actually decides success or failure.
[G] This is arguably the paper's most widely reused contribution. Prior work leaned on substring matching, checking whether the output contains a refusal phrase like "I'm sorry," or on an ad hoc, undocumented call to a closed model. Both are gameable and neither is reproducible. HarmBench instead fine-tunes a Llama two, thirteen billion parameter model on data distilled from GPT-four, and uses that as an open classifier for everything except copyright, which gets its own hash-based check for literally reproduced text.
[S] How good is that classifier, in practice?
[G] On the authors' manually labeled validation set, it reaches ninety-three point two percent agreement with human judgments overall, ninety-four point five percent on standard behaviors, ninety point five percent on the harder contextual ones. For comparison, GPT-four itself as a judge reached eighty-eight point four percent, plain substring matching on the AdvBench dataset reached sixty-nine point nine percent, and a fine-tuned classifier from prior work reached seventy-five point four percent. Their open model actually edges out GPT-four on their own test set.
[O] That's the kind of result that gets quietly adopted by everyone downstream, because it's free and it's better.
[G] It has been reused widely since, yes.
[S] One structural detail worth naming: they hold out separate validation and test splits of behaviors, with a dedicated classifier for each, specifically so a new attack or defense can't be tuned against the exact set it will later be scored on.
[G] Right, that's the paper directly fixing the tune-on-test failure mode it criticizes in earlier work.
[O] Let's get to the headline results. Who actually wins?
[G] Nobody, structurally, and that's the point. Using this pipeline, the authors ran the largest apples-to-apples comparison in the space to date: eighteen red-teaming methods drawn from twelve papers, against thirty-three target language models and defenses, spanning open and closed source.
[S] And the finding?
[G] No attack is uniformly effective, and no defense is uniformly robust. Every attack among the strongest five fails against at least one model, and every one of the most robust models still loses to at least one attack. There's no free lunch on either side of that matchup.
[O] What about the intuitive idea that bigger models are just inherently safer, because they understand context and intent better?
[G] The paper tests that directly, across six model families and sizes ranging from seven billion to seventy billion parameters, and finds attack success rate stays roughly flat as size increases within a family. Robustness tracks training data and procedure, not parameter count. The one exception is copyright behaviors, where larger models actually get worse, because they're simply more capable of reproducing memorized text verbatim. That's a capability effect, not a safety one.
[S] That's a genuinely counterintuitive negative result. It cuts hard against a comforting assumption a lot of people hold.
[G] It does, and it's a big part of why this paper gets cited well beyond the red-teaming subfield. It's direct evidence against the idea that scale alone buys safety.
[O] Now tell us about R2D2, the defense the authors built alongside the benchmark.
[G] R2D2 stands for Robust Refusal Dynamic Defense, and it's an efficient adversarial training method. Rather than training on a static, fixed list of harmful prompts, it maintains a live, continually refreshed pool of hard test cases generated by a strong optimization-based attack during training itself, and fine-tunes the model to resist them, while a separate loss term works to preserve the model's general usefulness.
[S] Does it actually hold up?
[G] Averaged across three variants of that optimization attack, the resulting model, built on a seven billion parameter base, brings attack success rate down to roughly five point nine percent, compared to thirty point two percent for the next most robust model the authors tested, a thirteen billion parameter Llama two chat variant. That's about a four-times reduction.
[O] And crucially, without wrecking general model quality.
[G] Utility is preserved reasonably well. It scores six point zero on MT-Bench, a general knowledge and conversational-quality benchmark, versus six point five for the instruct-tuned model it's built from. Not identical, but close, for a large robustness gain.
[O] Okay, time for the actual argument. My case first: this paper is exactly what a maturing field needs. It didn't just publish another attack or another defense, it built the ruler everyone else can now measure with. An open classifier that beats GPT-four's own judgment on this task, at essentially zero marginal cost, is a genuine public good. And the finding that scale doesn't buy safety should reshape how confidently people talk about deploying ever-larger models.
[S] I'll grant the ruler is well built. But I want to poke at what the ruler is made of. The classifier that decides every single success-or-failure verdict in this paper is a Llama model distilled from GPT-four's own judgments, then validated against human labels collected by the same team that built it. "Comparable to GPT-four" is partly true by construction, it was trained to imitate GPT-four.
[G] That's a fair reading, and worth stating precisely. Ninety-three point two percent agreement with humans still means roughly one judgment in fourteen disagrees with a person, and that disagreement isn't necessarily spread evenly across behavior types. Every attack success rate in this paper inherits that one model's notion of "harmful." To the authors' credit, the held-out validation and test classifiers and behavior splits do genuinely defend against the tune-on-test problem they criticize in earlier work. That part isn't circular.
[S] Second target, R2D2. The headline four-times number is measured against the exact attack family R2D2 was trained to resist. That's not fully circular, but it's close enough that I'd want to see it before getting excited.
[O] Come on, that's true of nearly every adversarial-training result in this literature. You have to train against something.
[S] Sure, but then don't quietly imply it's a general robustness result without a big asterisk attached. There's a specific tell buried in the paper's own results table. R2D2's success rate against a bare, undisguised request, no attack algorithm at all, is fourteen point two percent, actually higher than the comparison Llama two model's zero point eight percent on that same simple case.
[G] That detail is real, and it's the most honest part of the results to sit with. R2D2 hardens the model specifically against optimization-based search, it makes the loss landscape hostile to that kind of attack, but it doesn't give the model the comparison model's broader, blanket caution toward being asked plainly. So "state of the art against this attack family" is accurate, and also narrower than "safer overall" would imply.
[O] Fine, I'll take that point. It's a real result, just a scoped one.
[S] One more, and this is about the whole benchmark, not just R2D2. Attack success rate measures whether the model attempted the behavior, by design, not whether the output was actually correct, dangerous, or usable. A model that confidently outputs something wrong still counts as a successfully attacked model.
[G] That's explicit in the paper's own scoring criteria. It's a deliberate choice to separate "did the safety training fail" from "is the model capable enough for the output to matter," and it's defensible as a robustness measure. But it does mean a high attack success rate is a ceiling on possible harm, not proof of actual harm. And because HarmBench's behaviors are public and static, there's an obvious erosion risk. As future models train on data overlapping with public discussion of this benchmark, today's numbers will drift, the same way saturated benchmarks always do elsewhere in machine learning.
[O] So where does that leave the scorecard?
[G] Score it this way. The measurement contribution, breadth of behaviors, a fixed comparable pipeline, and a classifier that outperforms the closed alternative it's compared against, holds up under real scrutiny, and it's the part of this paper that has lasted. The two structural findings, that no method dominates and that robustness doesn't scale with model size, are well supported by the sheer scale of the comparison. R2D2 is a genuine and clever result, but its most quoted number should be read as "state of the art against the attack it trained on," not as a blanket robustness guarantee.
[O] Zooming out, what does this mean for evaluation work outside red teaming specifically?
[G] The core lesson generalizes well past safety. Any benchmark with a hidden, unstandardized knob, a token limit here, but it could just as easily be prompt formatting, sampling temperature, or number of retries elsewhere, can make published numbers incomparable without anyone intending to mislead. And any evaluation that lets a method tune on the exact metric it will later be scored on invites the same optimization pressure this paper warns about. Those are two lessons benchmark builders in adjacent areas, agentic evaluations, LLM-as-judge setups, keep rediscovering the hard way.
[S] It's also a good template for introducing a new defense responsibly: publish it inside the same standardized comparison you're asking everyone else to be measured by, rather than in a bespoke evaluation built to flatter it.
[G] If there's one sentence to take from the paper itself, it's this. Before HarmBench, red-teaming results across papers weren't just hard to compare, they were often not comparable at all, and a shared, adversarially aware pipeline fixed that.
[O] My one sentence: the finding that safety doesn't scale with model size is the kind of result that should change how confidently anyone talks about a bigger model automatically being a safer one.
[S] And mine: trust the standardization, read the defense numbers with their scope attached, and go check the full write-up on the Litsearch site for the figures and the appendix tables behind all of this.
