[S] Take nine hundred twelve real questions people actually posted to Excel help forums, keep the spreadsheets exactly as messy as those users left them, and grade every model the way a coding competition grades a submission, against several hidden test cases instead of one.
[O] And under that grading, the strongest model of twenty twenty-four clears about one in five real tasks.
[S] While a human who actually knows Excel clears seven in ten.
[O] That gap is the whole episode. Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper at a time. I am the optimist, and I think this benchmark is exactly the kind of hard, honest measurement the spreadsheet-agent space needed.
[S] I am the skeptic, and I want to know how fair that eighteen percent versus seventy-one percent comparison actually is before anyone treats it as gospel.
[O] The paper is SpreadsheetBench: Towards Challenging Real World Spreadsheet Manipulation, by Zeyao Ma, Bohan Zhang, Jing Zhang, Jifan Yu, Xiaokang Zhang, Xiaohan Zhang, Sijia Luo, Xi Wang, and Jie Tang, out of Renmin University of China, Tsinghua University, and Zhipu AI, published at NeurIPS twenty twenty-four's Datasets and Benchmarks track. Joining us is Cosima, who has spent real time with this paper. Cosima, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. This is one of those benchmark papers where the construction pipeline is doing as much of the intellectual work as the leaderboard.
[O] So start us on the gap. Why did the authors think existing spreadsheet benchmarks were flattering the models?
[G] Three concrete failures. First, the instructions in prior benchmarks are synthetic. SheetCopilotBench and SheetRM generate queries with self-instruct, an LLM writing plausible-sounding tasks. InstructExcel has crowdworkers write short instructions for a given sheet. None of that captures how a real user actually asks for help.
[S] How different is different, concretely?
[G] Word count is the clean signal. Average instruction length is twenty-seven point nine words in SheetCopilotBench, nine point eight in InstructExcel. In this benchmark it is eighty-five point seven. A real forum post carries context a synthetic prompt never does: the functions the user already tried, like VALUE, SUM, and SUMPRODUCT, the wrong answer they got, and often a worked example of the output they want.
[O] That is a real texture difference. A synthetic prompt says "calculate the sum." A real user says "I tried these three formulas, here is what broke, here is roughly what I want it to look like."
[G] Failure two: the spreadsheets themselves. Prior benchmarks use a single, regular relational table, basically the same shape as inputs to TableQA or Text2SQL. Real spreadsheets have no fixed boundaries, multiple tables can share one sheet, headers can be nested, incomplete, or missing entirely, cells can hold free-form text, and formatting like color or bold can carry meaning the task depends on.
[S] And the third failure?
[G] Grading. Prior work tests one instruction against one spreadsheet, one time. A solution can be right for that specific sheet and still be a false positive, brittle the moment a value changes. The authors call this a leaky ruler, and it is the failure their evaluation metric is built to close.
[O] So walk us through how nine hundred twelve real forum questions actually become a benchmark. That is not a trivial pipeline.
[G] Four stages. Sourcing pulls from four high-traffic Excel forums and blogs, ExcelForum, Chandoo, MrExcel, and ExcelGuru, targeting categories like formulas, VBA and macros, and pivot tables. Filtering keeps only posts that are marked solved, purely about spreadsheet manipulation rather than software UI questions like input boxes or buttons, feasible to test, and representative, so annotators keep a spread of easy and hard questions rather than cherry-picking simple ones.
[S] Who decides "solved" when the forum itself does not tag it?
[G] GPT-4 infers it from cues like a closing thank-you note, and annotators check borderline calls. Stage three is formatting: the post's solution, often scattered across several replies, gets condensed by GPT-4 into one self-contained instruction, which annotators verify and tag with the exact answer position, a specific cell range for a cell-level edit, or the full range for a sheet-level rewrite.
[O] And stage four is where the online-judge idea comes in?
[G] Test-case construction, and this is the genuinely clever part. Annotators take the original spreadsheet, apply the human solution for one input-output pair, then hand-edit the spreadsheet twice more, hunting for real-world corner cases, their own example is a cell that should hold a zero but was left blank, and apply the same solution to each. That yields three test cases per instruction on average, two thousand seven hundred twenty-nine spreadsheet files in total.
[S] Three genuinely different spreadsheets, not three copies with the same values relabeled.
[G] Correct, and structurally similar but numerically different, which is exactly what lets a single generated solution get tested for robustness rather than memorization.
[O] Tell us about the contamination defenses, because a dataset scraped from public forums is a leakage risk almost by construction.
[G] Three deliberate perturbations. The instruction is GPT-4-rewritten so a model cannot pattern-match the original post. The spreadsheet is modified twice over, so a memorized file from pretraining will not match what the model sees. And the answer position is moved, so a copied forum solution writes to the wrong cells. Together those close off the easiest ways a model could cheat by recognition rather than reasoning.
[S] That is a well-designed defense. I will flag one soft spot in it later, but it is the right instinct.
[O] What about how models are actually asked to produce answers? Is this fill-in-the-cell, or something closer to programming?
[G] Deliberately code. The setup is instruction, spreadsheet, and ground-truth answer triples; the model emits a code solution, runs it against the spreadsheet, and the result is compared to the ground truth. That choice matters for the results, we will get to why. Everything stays in plain XLSX, and models interact with files through ordinary Python rather than the Excel API, so the benchmark stays software-independent by design.
[O] And the grading formula itself, the online-judge part.
[G] Borrowed straight from competitive programming. Soft restriction follows the IOI convention, partial credit for the fraction of test cases passed. Hard restriction follows ICPC rules, a solution scores only if it passes every test case, nothing otherwise. The efficient part: one generated solution per instruction is reused across all three test cases, rather than re-running inference for every perturbed variant, which is how a lot of prior robustness benchmarks work.
[S] What did the finished dataset actually look like, statistically?
[G] Nine hundred twelve instructions, two thousand seven hundred twenty-nine test cases, spanning ten manipulation verbs: find, extract, sum, highlight, remove, modify, count, delete, calculate, display. Eighty percent of sheets sit within forty-nine rows and thirteen columns, but the long tail runs past one hundred columns and twenty thousand rows. Thirty-five point seven percent hold multiple tables, and forty-two point seven percent use non-standard headers, nested, incomplete, or missing. Twenty Excel specialists built it, with two validators and two authors doing a second round of quality control.
[O] Now the number everyone is going to remember. What did the actual leaderboard look like?
[G] Wide and low. Scores ranged from zero point zero five percent up to twenty-three point six five percent across every model tested. GPT-4o was the strongest LLM, at eighteen point three five percent overall soft, split fifteen point oh three for cell-level and twenty-three point six five for sheet-level, with fifteen point oh two percent on the stricter hard restriction. TaPEx and TaPas, the two TableQA models, scored zero and were dropped from the table entirely.
[S] Zero. Not "poor," zero.
[G] Zero, because those models produce a textual answer rather than executable code, and this task fundamentally rewards code. That is the paper's first big analytical point: coding ability predicts performance better than general capability does. DeepseekCoder at thirty-three billion parameters scores two point six oh percent, beating the much larger seventy-billion-parameter Llama-3 at one point three two percent.
[O] What about the product-side numbers, Copilot in Excel and SheetCopilot?
[G] Copilot in Excel reached roughly twenty percent, and SheetCopilot running on GPT-4 reached fourteen percent, both ahead of GPT-4o's raw eighteen point three five. And then Excel experts, hired to solve the same tasks by hand, hit seventy-one point three three percent soft and sixty-two percent hard.
[S] That is the number from the cold open. Five and a half times the best language model.
[O] Which is exactly the kind of gap that tells you a benchmark still has real headroom left, rather than being saturated on day one.
[G] Second finding: multi-round prompting. Give models a ReAct-style loop, letting them request more of the spreadsheet or see code-execution errors and retry, and almost everyone improves substantially, GPT-3.5 goes from two point three four percent to seven point oh nine, some models improve close to tenfold. GPT-4o is the exception, it drops slightly, from eighteen point three five to sixteen point nine six.
[S] Why would more information make the strongest model worse?
[G] The authors' explanation is subtle. GPT-4o dutifully follows the instruction to retrieve spreadsheet contents, and ends up re-fetching rows it was already given in the initial prompt, duplicating context rather than adding new signal. Weaker models, ironically, ignore that retrieval step and just work off what they were handed initially, so they do not pay the same tax.
[O] That is a genuinely interesting instruction-following side effect, not a capability failure.
[G] Third finding: soft and hard scores stay close together, GPT-4o's gap is only about three points, eighteen point three five down to fifteen point oh two, meaning models overwhelmingly pass all three test cases or none, not some. Difficulty also scales cleanly with complexity: on GPT-4o, accuracy falls from twenty point six three percent on sheets of fifty rows or fewer to eleven point five percent above that, and from twenty-one point one two percent on single-table sheets to thirteen point seven one percent on multi-table sheets.
[S] And simply feeding a model more of the spreadsheet does not rescue it?
[G] No. Going from five input rows to ten barely moves GPT-4o's score at all, which the authors attribute to a context-length effect working against them, more tokens, not necessarily more useful signal.
[O] Let's have the actual debate, because I think this paper earns real optimism. A benchmark built entirely from authentic user demand, graded like a coding competition against held-out test cases, and it still finds a five-times gap to human experts. That is not a benchmark that is about to get saturated by a prompting trick. It is measuring something real, and it gives the field years of runway to close an honest gap.
[S] I will grant the construction is careful. My objection is about what the leaderboard is actually telling us in twenty twenty-six. This is a mid-twenty-twenty-four snapshot. The model roster is GPT-4o, GPT-3.5, Llama-3, Mixtral, DeepseekCoder, CodeQwen. No reasoning models, no Claude, no Gemini, nothing remotely close to the current frontier. Calling eighteen percent "the ceiling" is really calling it "the twenty twenty-four ceiling," and that is a very different claim.
[G] That is fair, and it is worth saying plainly: the paper itself never claims eighteen percent is a permanent ceiling, only a mid-twenty-twenty-four measurement. The benchmark's value is that later work can rerun exactly this evaluation against newer models, and people have been doing precisely that.
[O] Which is my point, though. A hard, well-built benchmark being immediately outdated by model progress is a sign of a healthy field, not a flaw in the measurement.
[S] Sure, but my second objection is about fairness within this leaderboard, not time. GPT-4o's eighteen point three five percent is measured on the full nine hundred twelve instructions through a reproducible Python harness. Copilot in Excel's twenty percent and SheetCopilot's fourteen percent come from a five percent sample, and the human baseline's seventy-one percent comes from fifty instructions, graded by hand inside each product's own interface, not that same pipeline.
[G] Also fair, the authors are upfront that cost drove that choice, evaluating every product and human by hand across all nine hundred twelve was not practical. But it does mean the marquee comparison, twenty percent product versus seventy-one percent human versus eighteen percent GPT-4o, is directional, not strict apples-to-apples.
[O] Directional is still informative, though. Nobody's confidence interval on "humans are dramatically better than any current model at this" is wide enough to flip that conclusion.
[S] Agreed, my worry is someone quoting twenty percent versus seventy-one percent as precise later, without the asterisk. On contamination, though, real credit is due. GPT-4-rewritten instructions, twice-modified spreadsheets, moved answer positions, that is a genuinely thoughtful three-layer defense against a model just recognizing a memorized forum post.
[G] It is one of the strongest parts of the design, and I would only add one small caveat: because GPT-4 does the rewriting, real users' phrasing gets smoothed toward whatever GPT-4 considers coherent English, which may drift slightly away from how people actually type when they are frustrated and asking for help.
[O] A minor cost for a real leakage defense, in my view.
[S] I will take the trade. My last real objection is about the grading's precision, not its concept. Answers are graded by exact match at an annotator-placed position, so a model that produces a correct value written slightly off-target reads as simply wrong. And the fact that soft and hard scores track so closely, models passing all three test cases or none, actually cuts both ways: it could mean solutions genuinely are robust once they work, or it could mean three test cases is too coarse to separate a robust solution from a lucky one.
[G] The authors concede exactly that, in their own words they, quote, did not meticulously devise corner cases for every question, end quote, an honest limitation, not a hidden one. Two more from reading closely: the source forums are all English-language, so how this transfers to non-English-speaking users is genuinely unclear, and every task is graded as one isolated edit, even when the instruction requires several chained operations. Whether solving nine hundred twelve isolated questions correlates with sustained, multi-step spreadsheet work across a real session is still open, and the paper does not settle it.
[O] Those both sound like natural extensions for someone building on this, not flaws in what is already here.
[S] Agreed, they are the right next benchmark, not a rebuttal of this one.
[O] So where does this land for someone building spreadsheet agents right now?
[G] Three concrete actions would raise confidence. Rerun the leaderboard against current frontier and reasoning models, since eighteen percent is already stale. Score every product and the human baseline through the same automated harness on the full nine hundred twelve, so the marquee comparison stops needing an asterisk. And either add more test cases per instruction toward genuine online-judge density, or report inter-annotator agreement on answer-position placement, so we know how much of the remaining gap is model weakness versus grading noise.
