[S] One line in this report should stop you cold. A team took the same starting checkpoint, ran it through reinforcement learning, and separately through distillation from a bigger teacher, and distillation won while burning about a tenth of the compute.
[O] And that's the boring way to say it. The exciting way is: they found real evidence that imitating a smarter model can outrun rewarding a smaller one to get smarter on its own.
[S] Sure, but it's one model size, one narrow slice of math and code. I want to know it generalizes before anyone rewrites their training playbook.
[O] Fair, but pair that with a single checkpoint that switches between fast chatting and slow, careful reasoning using a budget dial the model was never trained to obey, and you've got a report with two genuinely new mechanisms in it. That's rare.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar sit down with one paper from the litsearch site and figure out what actually holds up.
[S] Today's paper is the Qwen3 Technical Report, from the Qwen Team at Alibaba, posted May twenty twenty-five. It documents the open-weight model family spanning from a six-hundred-million-parameter model up to a two-hundred-thirty-five-billion-parameter flagship.
[O] Joining us is Elena, who has spent real time with this report and the models it describes. Welcome, Elena.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is one of the more interesting open-weight releases of the year, not because any single benchmark number is a record, but because of what the training recipe reveals about how these labs now actually build models.
[S] So set the scene. What problem was Alibaba solving here, beyond just training a bigger model?
[G] The paper is candid about this. Going into twenty twenty-five, the open-weight ecosystem had split into two species of model. Fast, general chat models like GPT-4o or the Qwen team's own Qwen2.5, and separate reasoning-heavy models like OpenAI's o3, DeepSeek-R1, and the Qwen team's own QwQ-32B, which spend far more compute reasoning before answering. The report's own phrase is that using both meant "switching from Qwen2.5 to QwQ," depending on whether your next query is a quick chat turn or a hard math problem.
[O] So every reasoning-versus-speed tradeoff was a model swap, not a setting you could turn.
[G] Exactly. Two other gaps too: Qwen2.5's multilingual coverage stopped at twenty-nine languages and dialects, and every smaller model needed its own full post-training run — no cheap way to push capability down from the flagship.
[S] Those sound like three separate engineering problems though. Why one architectural change instead of three fixes?
[G] Because the fix for the first — fusing thinking and non-thinking into one checkpoint — also enables the third. Once you have a model that can operate at different reasoning depths, distilling that behavior down to smaller sizes becomes one pipeline instead of two.
[O] That's the elegant part. One decision, two birds.
[S] Let's get into the architecture, because I don't want to take "unified" on faith. What's actually in these models?
[G] The backbone is a conservative extension of Qwen2.5. Grouped query attention, SwiGLU activations, rotary position embeddings, and RMSNorm all carry over. Two changes: they drop the query-key-value bias term Qwen2 used, and add Q-K normalization to the attention mechanism, which the authors say keeps training stable.
[O] And the lineup itself is wide. Walk us through the sizes.
[G] Six dense models — six hundred million, one point seven billion, four billion, eight billion, fourteen billion, and thirty-two billion parameters — plus two mixture-of-experts models. The smaller one has thirty billion total parameters with three billion active per token. The flagship has two hundred thirty-five billion total, with only twenty-two billion firing per token. All eight share a vocabulary of roughly one hundred fifty-two thousand tokens.
[S] So the MoE models trade lower per-token compute for more total memory. What changed there versus Qwen2.5?
[G] They dropped the shared-expert design Qwen2.5's MoE used, and added a global-batch load-balancing loss, spreading routing more evenly across the one hundred twenty-eight experts — eight activate per token — instead of letting a handful absorb most of the traffic.
[O] Now the headline pre-training number: thirty-six trillion tokens.
[G] Double Qwen2.5's token count, triple the language coverage — one hundred nineteen languages instead of twenty-nine. A meaningful chunk isn't scraped text either. They fine-tuned their vision-language model to extract text from a large volume of PDF documents, and used their own Qwen2.5, Qwen2.5-Math, and Qwen2.5-Coder models to synthesize trillions more tokens as textbooks, question-answer pairs, and code.
[S] Models generating training data for their successor. I want to flag that loop now, because it matters later when we talk about contamination.
[O] Noted. What are the actual pre-training stages?
[G] Three. A general stage — thirty trillion tokens at a short sequence length, building broad world knowledge across all one hundred nineteen languages. A reasoning stage — about five trillion more tokens, up-weighting STEM, code, and synthetic reasoning data, with a faster learning-rate decay. And a long-context stage — hundreds of billions of tokens at a thirty-two-thousand-token sequence length, which also pushes the rotary position embedding base frequency from ten thousand to one million, and layers in a technique called YARN plus dual chunk attention for four-times more usable context at inference.
[O] So the bigger models end up with a hundred-twenty-eight-thousand-token context window, even though only trained natively at thirty-two thousand.
[G] Right — the two smallest dense models stay capped at thirty-two thousand, everything four billion and up gets extended to one hundred twenty-eight thousand.
[S] Now the post-training pipeline. This is where the real story is.
[G] Four stages, for the flagship and the thirty-two-billion dense model. Stage one is long chain-of-thought cold start — supervised fine-tuning on math, code, logic, and STEM problems with verified answers. They use Qwen2.5's seventy-two-billion instruct model to filter out queries answerable without reasoning, so the model can't shortcut, and QwQ-32B to generate candidates, filtered for repetition, guesswork, and mismatches between the thinking and the final summary.
[O] So stage one teaches the shape of long reasoning, not correctness yet.
[G] Right, deliberately light-touch. Stage two is reasoning RL, using an algorithm called GRPO — it scores each sampled response against the others in its own batch instead of training a separate value network — over three thousand nine hundred ninety-five query-verifier pairs, chosen to be unseen, learnable, maximally hard, and broad across domains. This alone takes the flagship's AIME twenty twenty-four score from seventy point one to eighty-five point one, over one hundred seventy RL steps, with no manual hyperparameter intervention.
[S] Fifteen points from one RL run with zero babysitting is a strong claim. Did they say why it was so stable?
[G] They point to controlling the model's entropy, keeping it steadily rising or flat rather than collapsing, plus a large batch size and many rollouts per query. It's a training-recipe claim more than a mechanism claim — no entropy curves given to check it yourself.
[O] Stage three is the fusion step, where thinking and non-thinking merge into one model?
[G] Yes, thinking mode fusion. They continue fine-tuning the stage-two model on a blended dataset: thinking data from rejection-sampling that same model's own outputs, and non-thinking data covering coding, math, instruction following, multilingual tasks, creative writing, and role-play. The slash-think and slash-no-think chat flags get introduced here, so a developer can toggle the mode per turn.
[S] And this is where the thinking-budget mechanism comes from, which is getting most of the attention.
[G] It's genuinely the most interesting design choice in the report. Once a user sets a token limit and the model hits it mid-reasoning, the system inserts a fixed stop-thinking instruction, and the model commits to an answer using whatever partial reasoning it had built up. The authors are explicit this was never trained as its own objective — no budget-conditioned loss, no budget curriculum. It emerges as a side effect of learning to handle both complete and incomplete reasoning traces.
[O] That's more convincing precisely because nobody optimized for it.
[G] Stage four is general RL, across more than twenty tasks — instruction following, format adherence, tool-calling for agent behavior with real multi-turn environment feedback, retrieval-augmented generation, and open-ended preference alignment. Three reward types combine: rule-based rewards for anything checkable by a program, a model-based reward where Qwen2.5's seventy-two-billion instruct model grades against a reference answer, and a trained preference model for the subjective, no-reference cases.
[S] And the small models skip all four stages entirely?
[G] They skip the reinforcement-learning parts specifically. Five dense sizes below thirty-two billion, plus the small MoE model, go through strong-to-weak distillation instead — an off-policy phase where the student learns from teacher outputs in both modes, then an on-policy phase where the student generates its own sequences and is trained to match the teacher's output logits by minimizing K-L divergence, rather than just imitating text.
[O] And the claim is that's roughly a tenth of the compute of the full four-stage pipeline.
[G] That's their number, and unusually for a claim like that, they actually ran the head-to-head to back it up.
[O] Let's get to the headline scores, because the flagship numbers are strong.
[G] The flagship, in thinking mode, posts eighty-five point seven on AIME twenty twenty-four, eighty-one point five on AIME twenty twenty-five, seventy point seven on LiveCodeBench version five, a CodeForces rating of two thousand fifty-six, and seventy point eight on B-F-C-L version three, the Berkeley function-calling benchmark for tool use.
[S] Where does that land relative to the models everyone actually compares against?
[G] Between DeepSeek-R1 and Gemini 2.5 Pro on AIME twenty twenty-four — R1 scores seventy-nine point eight, Gemini 2.5 Pro ninety-two. Not the top scorer, but it's doing that while activating only twenty-two billion of its two hundred thirty-five billion parameters per token.
[O] That parameter-efficiency angle carries through to the base-model comparisons too.
[G] It does. The flagship base model beats DeepSeek-V3's base model on fourteen of fifteen pre-training benchmarks, using about a third of the total parameters and two-thirds of the activated ones. And the thirty-two-billion dense base model, with under half the parameters, beats Qwen2.5's seventy-two-billion base model on ten of fifteen.
[S] Fourteen of fifteen sounds decisive, but give me the sharpest, most controlled result in the paper, not the flashiest headline number.
[G] The on-policy-distillation-versus-RL ablation on the eight-billion model, the best-designed experiment in the report. Both runs start from the same off-policy-distilled checkpoint. On-policy distillation reaches seventy-four point four percent pass-at-one on AIME twenty twenty-four, ninety-three point three pass-at-sixty-four, for eighteen hundred GPU hours. Direct reinforcement learning instead reaches sixty-seven point six pass-at-one, ninety pass-at-sixty-four, for seventeen thousand nine hundred twenty GPU hours.
[O] So roughly ten times the compute for a worse result.
[G] And not just worse on pass-at-one. RL leaves pass-at-sixty-four completely unmoved, ninety before and after, while distillation pushes it to ninety-three point three. The authors read that as evidence a strong teacher expands the student's exploration space in a way RL alone doesn't — RL reshapes probability mass the model already has, distillation imports genuinely new behavior from the teacher.
[S] That's a real mechanistic difference, not just a bigger number. I'll give them that one.
[O] There's also a nice curve on the thinking budget itself, how performance scales as you let the model think longer.
[G] Across four benchmarks spanning math, code, and science, pass-at-one rises smoothly as the thinking budget grows from one thousand to thirty-two thousand tokens. AIME twenty twenty-four alone climbs from roughly forty-two to eighty-six across that range, and it hadn't saturated by the thirty-two-thousand-token cap they tested.
[O] Time for the argument, and I'll go first, because I think this report understates how big a deal it is. Two genuinely new, controlled findings — the emergent thinking budget, and distillation beating RL by an order of magnitude on the same checkpoint — plus a parameter-efficient flagship competitive with the frontier. That's a credible claim that the field's next scaling axis is teaching smaller models to imitate a bigger reasoner, not training everything with RL from scratch.
[S] I'll take the other side, starting with what's missing. This is a thirty-five-page report, with a thirty-six-trillion-token corpus assembled partly by OCR-ing PDFs and partly by their own older models generating synthetic data, and it never once mentions decontamination, contamination, or leakage, except in two bibliography citations for other people's contamination-resistant benchmarks. Qwen2.5's own report disclosed an explicit longest-common-subsequence decontamination rule. Qwen3 just drops that disclosure entirely.
[G] That's accurate, and it's the clearest regression relative to its own predecessor. I looked for it specifically, because it's exactly the kind of thing worth checking, and it isn't there. Given the corpus includes model-synthesized data and OCR'd documents that could plausibly overlap with public benchmark text, the omission is more than a formatting choice.
[O] I'll concede that one completely, that's a real gap. But does it undermine the two headline mechanisms we just walked through?
[G] Not directly. Both results are about relative performance under matched conditions, same checkpoint, same benchmark, so contamination wouldn't explain why distillation beats RL from an identical checkpoint. Those two results survive the contamination critique.
[S] Fine, but there's a second issue. Every competitor number here is self-run. Their own words: "all models are evaluated using the same evaluation pipeline." That controls for harness variance, sure, but it also means every DeepSeek-V3, Llama-4, and Gemma-3 score is Qwen's own reproduction, not each lab's official number. And the comparison set isn't even fixed — Grok-3-Beta and Gemini 2.5 Pro only show up in the thinking-mode table, GPT-4o and Llama-4 only in the non-thinking one.
[O] That's a fair complaint about presentation, but a self-run harness isn't automatically worse. Sometimes it's more honest than cherry-picked official numbers.
[G] Both can be true. A consistent harness reduces one kind of noise and introduces another, since nobody outside Alibaba can audit the baselines. There's a third thing too — a chunk of the internal justification for stages three and four rests on four in-house benchmarks, described in one sentence each and never released.
[S] Which is my point. You can't independently check whether those stages earn their keep.
[G] To be fair, the report is honest about a real cost there. For the thirty-two-billion model, stages three and four together lower AIME twenty twenty-four from eighty-three point eight to eighty-one point four, and LiveCodeBench dips too. They didn't hide that regression. Rarer than you'd think in a flagship release.
[O] So even the skeptic has to admit the paper is unusually candid about its own downsides, at least once.
[S] Agreed, I'll score that as a point in their favor. My overall read: trust the two controlled experiments, discount everything resting on the in-house benchmarks or the self-run tables until someone outside Alibaba reproduces them.
[G] That's roughly where I land too. The mechanisms are real. The surrounding evaluation apparatus asks for more trust than Qwen2.5's report did, not less.
[O] So if this holds up under outside scrutiny, what changes?
[G] The practical implication is for anyone deploying models at multiple sizes. Instead of a separate post-training pipeline per size, you post-train the biggest model well and distill the rest — on-policy, not just training on teacher text — and get better quality per GPU hour than direct RL at the smaller sizes.
[S] From an evaluation standpoint, the thing I'd want the field to copy is that disclosure — a stage that costs accuracy on two benchmarks even though it's the stage they ship anyway. That "here's what we traded away" table should be standard, not a pleasant surprise.
[O] And the contamination gap is worth flagging precisely because it's checkable. Anyone comparing Qwen3 against contamination-resistant benchmarks should treat a suspiciously clean score with more caution than they'd give Qwen2.5's numbers.
[G] If there's one sentence, it's this: distillation from a strong teacher beat reinforcement learning by roughly ten times on compute, from the same starting checkpoint, and that's the finding here most likely to hold up.
[O] Mine is that fusing thinking and non-thinking into one dial, with a budget that emerged without being trained for, is the most genuinely new piece of model design here, and I think other labs will copy it.
[S] Mine is: read the base-model comparison tables and the distillation ablation closely, trust those, and wait for someone outside Alibaba to check the rest. For the full benchmark tables, the figures, and the complete eval-rigor critique, check out the writeup on the Litsearch site.
