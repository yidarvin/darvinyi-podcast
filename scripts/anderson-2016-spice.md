An automatic metric, they argue, should score exactly those propositions, not the surface phrasing.
[O] Which is a genuinely different target than every metric before it was chasing.
[G] It is. Bleu and ROUGE came out of machine translation and summarization and got repurposed for captions. METEOR added stemming and synonym matching but is still fundamentally an n-gram alignment. CIDEr, the strongest of that lineage, weights n-grams by how distinctive they are and sums cosine similarity, so it discounts generic phrases, but it never leaves the world of shared words and phrases.
[S] Okay, so how do you actually score meaning instead of words? Walk us through the machinery, Dean.
[G] Two stages. First, a probabilistic context-free grammar dependency parser, pre-trained, run over the raw caption using Universal Dependency relations, to get its syntactic structure. Second, that dependency tree gets mapped onto a scene graph through a rule-based system, a modified version of the Stanford Scene Graph Parser, using nine linguistic rules.
[O] What comes out the other side?
[G] Three kinds of pieces. Objects, so "girl" and "court." Attributes attached to objects, so the adjectival modifier "young" attaches to "girl." And relations between two objects, so "girl" connects to "court" through the relation "on top of." Every one of those gets flattened into a tuple, one, two, or three elements long, and the union of a caption's tuples is what actually gets scored.
[S] And scoring is just an F-score over those tuples?
[G] Precision is the fraction of the candidate's tuples that also show up in the reference set's tuples, recall is the reverse, and SPICE itself is the harmonic mean of the two, an F-one score. A binary matching operator does the work, and two tuples count as matched if their lemmatized word forms are identical, or, reusing METEOR's own machinery, if they land in the same WordNet synonym set.
[O] So "bicycle" and "cycle" can match through WordNet even with almost no shared letters.
[G] Exactly that kind of case. One design choice is worth flagging here, because it's a deliberate departure from related work. There's no partial credit. If a tuple gets the two objects right but the relation wrong, that's zero, not half credit.
[S] Isn't that unusually harsh, though? A relation that's almost right getting scored identically to one that's completely wrong seems like it could make the metric noisy right at the margin.
[G] That's a fair objection, and the authors address it directly rather than dodging it. Their argument is that relations like "in" or "on" are so common that applying one to the wrong pair of objects arguably deserves nothing, not half. Whether that's the right design philosophy is a judgment call, but it does mean two scene graphs that are almost identical can score further apart than a casual read of the two sentences would suggest.
[O] What about the other thing you mentioned, no cross-dataset statistics?
[G] Right, unlike CIDEr, SPICE uses no corpus-level word frequencies at all, so it needs no reference corpus to calibrate against and behaves identically whether you're scoring five images or fifty thousand. And they made two changes to the parser they borrowed. The original duplicates object nodes for plural or numeric modifiers, so "three dogs" becomes three separate dog nodes. SPICE drops that and encodes the count as an attribute instead, so a wrong count costs one error, not three. They also added a rule guaranteeing every noun becomes an object node even when the parser can't attach any relation to it, so nothing silently falls out of scoring.
