[S] Here's a fact that should bother anyone who trusts a benchmark: in this paper's own Atari scoreboard, the method wins most of the games and still loses on final performance.
[O] And yet it's the algorithm sitting under nearly every RLHF pipeline that made ChatGPT-style models behave the way they do.
[S] Those two things can both be true, and figuring out how is basically today's whole episode.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take one paper apart. Today: "Proximal Policy Optimization Algorithms," by John Schulman, Filip Wolski, Prafulla Dhariwal, Alec Radford, and Oleg Klimov, out of OpenAI in twenty seventeen.
[S] Joining us is Maren, who has spent a lot of time in this exact corner of reinforcement learning. Maren, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. This is a paper worth slowing down for, because almost nobody reads it carefully anymore — they just import it.
[O] Set the scene for us, Maren. What was actually broken in twenty seventeen that this paper set out to fix?
[G] Three families of methods were competing for deep reinforcement learning at the time. Deep Q-learning worked beautifully on Atari, but the authors point out it fails on many simple problems and had never been shown to work on continuous control. Vanilla policy gradient methods are simple, but the paper says plainly that they have poor data efficiency and robustness.
[S] Because you can only take one gradient step per batch of data before you have to throw it away and go collect more.
[G] Exactly. Try to squeeze a second update out of the same trajectory and, in the authors' words, it often leads to destructively large policy updates. The policy can jump so far in one step that it forgets how to act at all.
[O] So the fix that already existed was trust region policy optimization — TRPO.
[G] Right, from Schulman's own earlier work. TRPO maximizes that same kind of surrogate objective, but subject to a hard constraint: the KL divergence between the old policy and the new one can't exceed some threshold. It's reliable, and it works.
[S] But.
[G] But it's genuinely complicated. Solving that constrained problem needs the conjugate gradient algorithm, plus a linear approximation to the objective and a quadratic approximation to the constraint. And the paper notes it isn't compatible with architectures that include noise, like dropout, or that share parameters between the policy and the value function.
[O] Which rules out a lot of the network designs people actually wanted to use.
[G] It does. So the goal here is stated explicitly: get TRPO's data efficiency and reliable performance, but with a method that's scalable, robust across tasks without per-task tuning, and — the key constraint — first order only. No second derivatives, no conjugate gradient solver.
[S] Walk us through the actual mechanism, then. How do you get TRPO's stability without TRPO's machinery?
[G] Start with the probability ratio: r of theta equals the new policy's probability of taking the action, divided by the old policy's probability of that same action. At the very start of any update, before anything has changed, that ratio is exactly one.
[O] And TRPO's surrogate objective is just the expected value of that ratio times the advantage.
[G] Right — they call that L-C-P-I, for conservative policy iteration, which is where the idea originally came from. Maximize it with no constraint at all, and the optimizer will happily push the ratio arbitrarily far from one to chase a good advantage. That's the destructive update again.
[S] So PPO's answer is to clip the ratio instead of constraining the KL divergence directly.
[G] That's the whole idea. The clipped objective takes the minimum of two quantities: the ordinary ratio times the advantage, and that same ratio clipped into the window from one minus epsilon to one plus epsilon, times the advantage. Epsilon is typically point two.
[O] Walk me through why taking the minimum matters, not just the clipping on its own.
[G] Because clipping alone would still let the optimizer chase reward right up against the boundary and stop there with no penalty. Taking the minimum makes the whole thing a pessimistic bound — you only ignore the probability-ratio change when it would make the objective look better than it should, and you keep it when it makes the objective look worse. So there's never an incentive to move outside the trusted band. Push toward a good action and your gradient rewards you only up to the edge of that window; past it, the objective flattens and pushing further buys you nothing.
[S] That's a heuristic dressed up as an objective function, though. It isn't actually enforcing anything like TRPO's constraint.
[G] Correct, and it's worth being precise about that. The clipped objective equals the unconstrained one to first order right at the old policy, so for a tiny step it behaves exactly like ordinary policy gradient — the clip only bites once you've moved far enough to hit the boundary. The paper's own Figure two makes this concrete: they interpolate along an actual update on a Hopper control task, and the clipped objective peaks almost exactly where the realized KL divergence hits about point zero two. So empirically the clip enforces something that looks like a small trust region. It's just never guaranteed the way TRPO's constraint is.
[O] And this is what lets you reuse the same data multiple times, which is the efficiency story.
[G] Right — because the objective no longer blows up when you take a second or third gradient step on the same batch, the algorithm can afford multiple epochs of minibatch stochastic gradient descent, or in practice Adam, on the exact same rollout. Concretely: N parallel actors each collect T timesteps, advantages are estimated with a truncated version of generalized advantage estimation, and then you optimize the clipped surrogate for K epochs over all of those samples before collecting anything new. Vanilla policy gradient gets one update per sample. This gets K.
[S] What's K, typically?
[G] Ten epochs of reuse on the MuJoCo benchmark, three on Atari — different amounts of reuse for different problem types. And when the policy and value function share parameters, the full objective adds a value-function squared-error term and an entropy bonus on top of the clipped surrogate, so it stays a single scalar loss you can just call backward on.
[O] They also tried a KL-penalty version instead of clipping, right — adapting a penalty coefficient up or down toward a target divergence?
[G] They did, and reported the result plainly: the adaptive KL penalty performed worse than the clipped surrogate. They kept it in the paper only as a baseline, which is an honest choice — it would have been easy to bury that.
[S] Let's get to the numbers, then. What actually won?
[G] Two separate experiments. First, an ablation across surrogate objectives on seven MuJoCo continuous-control tasks, one million timesteps each, three random seeds, scores normalized so a random policy scores zero and the best result on each environment scores one, then averaged over all twenty-one runs. No clipping and no penalty at all scores negative point three nine — it actually destroys the policy on HalfCheetah, worse than doing nothing. Clipping with epsilon at point one scores point seven six. Epsilon at point two, the value they adopt, scores point eight two — the best of anything they tried, including every KL-penalty variant.
[O] So point two isn't arbitrary. It's the empirical winner on their own sweep.
[S] On their own seven tasks, with three seeds each and no error bars reported. Hold that thought, I want to come back to it.
[G] Second experiment: with epsilon fixed at point two, this method goes head to head against TRPO itself, the cross-entropy method, an adaptive-stepsize vanilla policy gradient, and A2C both with and without a trust-region variant, on those same seven MuJoCo tasks. The paper's own words: it outperforms the previous methods on almost all the continuous control environments.
[O] And there's a showcase result too — the humanoid.
[G] A three-dimensional Roboschool humanoid that has to run, steer toward a moving target, and get back up after being pelted with cubes, trained out to tens of millions of timesteps. It isn't a head-to-head comparison — it's a demonstration that the method scales past small toy control problems into something with real degrees of freedom.
[S] Now Atari, which is the part I actually find most interesting, because it's the one place the story isn't a clean win.
[G] Across all forty-nine games in the Arcade Learning Environment, they score two different ways. By average reward across the entire training run, which rewards fast learning, this method wins thirty of forty-nine games, ACER wins eighteen, A2C wins one. But by average reward over just the last hundred episodes, which is closer to final performance, the order flips: ACER wins twenty-eight, this method wins nineteen, A2C wins one, and there's one tie.
[O] So it gets there faster, but ACER often ends up further along.
[S] Which the abstract quietly acknowledges. It says the method does significantly better than A2C and only similarly to ACER, then leans on "much simpler" to carry the rest of the argument.
[G] That's an accurate read of their own framing. The honest summary of the Atari result is: this is the sample-efficiency winner, not the final-score winner, and the paper is candid about that distinction even while emphasizing simplicity as the real selling point.
[O] Okay, let me make the strongest case I've got. This is one of the highest-leverage simplifications in the field's history. You take an algorithm that needed a conjugate-gradient solver and a quadratic constraint, and you replace it with a change to a loss function that a first-year engineer can implement in an afternoon. That tradeoff, a small amount of theoretical purity for a massive gain in usability, is exactly why it ended up as the default optimizer for reinforcement learning from human feedback, the RLHF stage behind ChatGPT-style models, and why it's a direct ancestor of newer methods like GRPO in the reasoning-model lineage. Simplicity that survives contact with a hundred different research groups is not a small thing.
[S] My strongest case, then: the empirical foundation under that reputation is thinner than the reputation suggests. Three random seeds, no confidence intervals, on a normalization scheme that measures relative ordering within their own seven-task suite rather than absolute capability. The headline epsilon of point two is selected on the exact same tasks it's then reported as winning on, and it doesn't even transfer — Atari uses a completely different, annealed clip that decays from roughly point one down toward zero over training. So "robust with little tuning" is true for the clip as an idea, and overstated for that specific number. And every baseline, TRPO, A2C, ACER, the cross-entropy method, is tuned by the same lab. "Well-tuned" is asserted, not something a reader can audit.
[G] Both of those are fair, and I'd score them accordingly. On the theory, credit is due for the framing as a pessimistic lower bound — genuinely elegant — but the clip does not actually bound the KL divergence the way the paper's language implies. It only zeroes the local gradient once you're past the boundary; across ten epochs of reuse, the realized policy can still drift further than epsilon nominally allows. That's exactly what the point-zero-two KL number in Figure two is quietly confirming after the fact, not guaranteeing in advance. On the seed count, the skeptic's case is simply correct — three seeds is thin by the standards this field adopted within a couple of years, and there's no getting around that gap retroactively.
[S] And on the code-level point, this is the one I'd push hardest on. The paper doesn't mention advantage normalization, value-function clipping, learning-rate annealing, or orthogonal weight initialization. Later reproduction studies found that real-world performance depends heavily on exactly those implementation choices, none of which appear in the algorithm as written on the page.
[G] That's documented, and it's a legitimate mark against the paper as an evaluation, though I'd separate it from the paper as an idea. The clipped objective is genuinely what it claims to be; the fact that it took years of follow-up work to figure out which auxiliary tricks made it reliable in practice says something about how thin a conference-length paper could be in twenty seventeen, not about a flaw in the core mechanism.
[O] I'll take that. Where I won't concede is the ACER comparison. Losing the final-score column on Atari doesn't undercut the pitch, because nobody adopted this method for peak Atari score. They adopted it because it doesn't fall over when you point it at a new domain with default settings, and the ACER result actually understates how painful ACER is to get working at all.
[S] Also fair. My discomfort was never that it underperforms, it clearly doesn't. My discomfort is that the paper's own evidence for why it's reliable is exactly the kind of small-sample, self-tuned comparison the field later learned to distrust.
[O] So zoom out. What changes if we just accept all of that at face value?
[G] Practically, not much needed to change, because the field already ran with it. This method became the default policy-optimization algorithm across robotics, game-playing, and eventually language-model post-training. When OpenAI needed an optimizer for InstructGPT's reinforcement-learning stage, it was already sitting there as the trusted, simple choice, and that lineage runs straight through to the RLHF behind ChatGPT-style models and onward into GRPO and other more recent reasoning-focused variants.
[S] Which is where I'd draw the evaluation-practice connection directly. When you drop this method into RLHF, the reward signal is a learned or human proxy, not a ground-truth simulator reward, and its tendency to push right up to whatever boundary it's given becomes reward hacking against that proxy instead of against MuJoCo. That's not a critique of this specific paper — the authors never claim to have solved reward misspecification — but it is the paper's central mechanism showing up as a liability in exactly the setting that made it famous.
[G] The paper doesn't speak to that at all, to be clear. It's a twenty seventeen robotics-and-Atari paper, and the RLHF application is a downstream fact about how the field later used the method, not a claim the paper itself makes.
[S] My one-sentence takeaway: read the ablation table before you read the abstract. The real lesson here isn't that clipping is magic, it's what happens when you actually test five alternatives instead of asserting one.
[O] Mine is that simplicity compounds. An algorithm three people can actually implement correctly is worth more to a field than one that scores two points higher on a benchmark nobody outside the original lab can reproduce.
[G] And the paper's own one-sentence version: you don't need TRPO's full machinery to get most of TRPO's reliability — a clipped ratio is enough, and that trade defined a decade of reinforcement learning. Find the full write-up, figures included, on the Litsearch site.
