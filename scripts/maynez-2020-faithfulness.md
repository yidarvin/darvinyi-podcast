[O] Let's get into the actual definitions, because "hallucination" is doing very specific work here.
[G] This is the paper's central conceptual contribution. A span of the summary counts as a hallucination if it is not supported by the document; legitimate paraphrases or content you can genuinely infer do not count. They split hallucinations into two kinds. Intrinsic hallucinations use the document's own entities and concepts but misrepresent the relationship between them. Extrinsic hallucinations introduce material the document never mentions at all, which might happen to be true, background knowledge the model got lucky on, or might simply be invented.

[G] Directly on the metric-validity question. This is one of the earliest large papers to show, with a controlled human study rather than a handful of anecdotes, that a widely deployed metric can be completely blind to the property you actually care about. This entailment-based check is a direct ancestor of the factuality metrics, and eventually the hallucination checks, that follow once the field starts treating large language model hallucination as its own subfield.
