[S] Bloomberg spent over a million GPU hours and fifty-three days training a fifty-billion-parameter language model from scratch, on a corpus it spent four decades collecting — then locked the whole thing inside the building.
[O] And on the numbers that matter to Bloomberg, it crushed everything near its size. On one internal sentiment task it scored nearly eighty against competitors stuck in the teens and twenties.
[S] Except the paper itself admits the model probably saw the unlabeled version of that exact test data during training. That's not a small footnote.
[O] It's not, but it's also not the whole story. Let's actually work through what Bloomberg built and what it proves.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the litsearch site. Today's paper is BloombergGPT: A Large Language Model for Finance, by Shijie Wu and colleagues at Bloomberg, posted in March of twenty twenty-three.
[S] It's on the docket because it's one of the clearest test cases anyone's run on a question a lot of companies are quietly asking: if you sit on a genuinely unique proprietary corpus, is training your own LLM from scratch worth it, or should you just fine-tune something open?
[O] Joining us to walk through it is Elena, who has studied this paper closely. Elena, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. It's a paper that's aged in an interesting way — read today, it's as much a lesson in how to evaluate a domain model honestly as it is a lesson in how to build one.
[S] Start with the actual gap, Elena. Why did Bloomberg think finance needed its own model instead of just using GPT-3 or one of the other big general models already out by early twenty twenty-three?
[G] Their argument is about failure modes specific to the domain. The paper's own example: a headline like "Company to cut ten thousand jobs" reads negative in ordinary sentiment, but in financial sentiment, cutting costs can be positive for the stock, because it boosts investor confidence. General-purpose models, trained mostly on broad web text, don't reliably pick up conventions like that, or the vocabulary in filings, tickers, and earnings-call transcripts.
[O] And at that point nobody had actually published a large language model tuned specifically for finance.
[G] Right, the authors say so plainly — despite plenty of financial NLP work on sentiment, named entity recognition, and question answering, no LLM specialized for the domain had been reported in the literature. There were smaller masked models tuned for finance, like FinBERT, but nothing at LLM scale trained ground-up on financial data.
[S] So the obvious counter is: fine-tune an open model on financial text instead of paying to train one from nothing.
[G] And the paper is upfront that this is the real question — not "can a model learn finance," but "given Bloomberg's forty years of curated financial documents nobody else can access, does a model built on that unique asset beat the alternative, and does it cost anything on general ability?" That second half matters — earlier domain-specific efforts, models built purely on biomedical or scientific text, gained a lot in-domain but usually gave something up everywhere else.
[O] So the wager is: take the proprietary-data advantage, keep it Chinchilla-sized and sensible, and see if you can have it both ways.
[G] That's exactly the bet. Fifty-one percent of training on Bloomberg's own data, the rest on general text, and see whether the model ends up best-in-class on finance while staying competitive everywhere else.
[O] Walk us through FinPile, Elena — that's the actual moat here, right?
[G] It is. FinPile is three hundred sixty-three billion tokens pulled from Bloomberg's own archives, in five pieces: a targeted web crawl of financially relevant sites at two hundred ninety-eight billion tokens, news at thirty-eight billion, filings at fourteen billion, press releases at nine billion, and Bloomberg's own authored journalism at five billion. Every document is time-stamped, from March of two thousand seven through July of twenty twenty-two.
[S] Filings — that's the category the paper flags as the biggest advantage, isn't it?
[G] Right, and there's a good reason. Filings like ten-Ks and ten-Qs are technically public, but they're long PDF documents dense with tables that most public training sets never bother extracting properly. Bloomberg already does that processing as part of its core business, so it's sitting on clean, structured text almost nobody else has at this scale.
[O] And they didn't train purely on that. They added general data too.
[G] Three hundred forty-five billion more tokens — The Pile, C4, and a fresh Wikipedia dump — bringing the total corpus to roughly seven hundred billion tokens, held almost exactly to a fifty-fifty split between finance and general text. That ratio is deliberate: they refuse to let FinPile drop below half the mix, even though diluting it with more general web data would have been easy.
[S] Which raises the sizing question. How do you pick a model size when one side of your data is capped?
[G] This is where it gets interesting. They size the model using the Chinchilla compute-optimal recipe — the idea that for a fixed compute budget there's an optimal balance of parameters to training tokens, rather than just going as big as possible. Starting from a budget of one point three million GPU hours on A100s, their fits to the Chinchilla equations put the optimum around fifty-three billion parameters and one point one trillion tokens.
[O] But they didn't have one point one trillion tokens.
[G] Exactly — they had about seven hundred billion, and weren't willing to shrink FinPile's share to pad that out. So they're data-limited, not compute-limited, the opposite problem from most big labs at the time. They pick the largest model they can fully train on the tokens available, keep about thirty percent of compute in reserve for restarts, and land on fifty point six billion parameters — seventy layers, forty attention heads, hidden dimension seven thousand six hundred eighty. Architecturally it's a BLOOM-style decoder with ALiBi positional encoding, so it extrapolates past its training context length at inference.
[S] You mentioned the tokenizer earlier as a big deal. Why does it matter this much?
[G] Because the authors rank it as the second-biggest driver of quality, right behind the curated dataset. Instead of an off-the-shelf byte-pair-encoding tokenizer like most models used then, they train their own Unigram tokenizer, which keeps multiple candidate segmentations as probabilities rather than committing to one greedy merge. They tested vocabulary sizes from twenty-five thousand up to five hundred fifty thousand, picked whichever encoded C4 most efficiently, and landed on one hundred thirty-one thousand and seventy-two tokens — more than double the roughly fifty thousand that was standard at the time.
[O] And the actual training run — that's the part I find genuinely refreshing. They published what they call Training Chronicles.
[G] They did, and it's unusually candid. They trained one hundred thirty-nine thousand two hundred steps, about fifty-three days, on five hundred twelve A100 GPUs, getting through only about eighty percent of one epoch — five hundred sixty-nine billion of the seven hundred nine billion available tokens. Late in the run, after step one hundred fifteen thousand five hundred, validation loss went flat, and they intervened three times: shrinking the learning rate to two-thirds, then halving it again and adding dropout, then halving it once more.
[S] That's the kind of detail that almost never survives into a published paper.
[O] Let's get to the actual numbers. Who did they compare against?
[G] Three open models run in-house, under one identical few-shot harness with no fine-tuning and no chain-of-thought prompting — GPT-NeoX at twenty billion parameters, OPT at sixty-six billion, and BLOOM at one hundred seventy-six billion. They also quote GPT-3 numbers from other papers, since they didn't have API access to run it themselves.
[S] Running the open baselines yourself, under one identical harness — that part is genuinely well done.
[G] It is, and it's worth crediting. No per-model prompt tuning, same few-shot setup throughout. The first check is a clean one: bits per byte on a held-out slice of FinPile, deduplicated and strictly later in time than training. BloombergGPT has the lowest loss in every category, and the gap is largest on filings — the PDF-locked documents the other models essentially never saw.
[O] And on the actual financial benchmarks?
[G] On five external financial tasks — sentiment analysis, headline classification, named entity recognition, and a numerical-reasoning task called ConvFinQA — BloombergGPT wins four of five and posts a win rate of zero point nine three. The standout is ConvFinQA, answering questions over earnings-report tables across a conversation: forty-three point four one, against thirty point oh six, twenty-seven point eight eight, and thirty-six point three one for the others.
[S] Those numbers still sound modest in absolute terms, though. Forty-three out of one hundred isn't exactly dominant.
[G] That's fair, and worth sitting with — this is all zero-fine-tuning few-shot prompting, so the absolute bar is low across every model. The claim is comparative: best among LLMs of its size class, not state-of-the-art on the task itself.
[O] Where it gets dramatic is the internal Bloomberg benchmarks.
[G] Right, and this is where the numbers get enormous. On internal aspect-sentiment tasks, BloombergGPT averages sixty-two point four seven against competitors in the high twenties and thirties, winning every task. On one slice, equity-news sentiment, it scores seventy-nine point six three against fourteen point one seven, twenty point nine eight, and nineteen point nine six — a fifty-plus-point gap.
[S] And that's exactly the number I want to flag, because the paper itself tells you why it's inflated.
[G] It does, and to their credit they say it plainly: it's likely that the unlabeled text underlying those internal sentiment and NER sets appears somewhere inside FinPile, so the model may have seen this exact text during pretraining, even though the labels themselves were held out.
[O] What about the other side of the bet — did specializing on finance cost them anything on general ability?
[G] Not much, by their own measurement. Across BIG-bench Hard, MMLU, knowledge tests, reading comprehension, and linguistic tasks, BloombergGPT is consistently the best of its size class. It edges out BLOOM one hundred seventy-six billion on MMLU — thirty-nine point one eight versus thirty-nine point one three — though BLOOM is over three times its size. It trails GPT-3 across the board, and loses to BLOOM outright on BIG-bench Hard, forty-one point nine seven versus forty-four point nine one.
[S] So "no degradation" is true, but it's a thin, careful version of true.
[G] That's the right way to read it. The wins over same-size peers are real and fairly consistent. The wins over much larger models are occasional and narrow.
[O] Okay, let's make the actual cases. I'll go first: this paper proves something real. If you have a genuinely unique, large, clean proprietary corpus, training your own domain model is a legitimate strategy, not vanity. They hit their stated goal — best-in-class on finance without visibly damaging general ability — with real discipline: identical harness for every open baseline, a temporally clean held-out test, and published Training Chronicles most labs would never let out the door.
[S] I'll take the other side. Start with the number doing the most work in this paper: seventy-nine point six three versus fourteen point one seven on equity-news sentiment. That's the kind of gap that gets a paper cited constantly and gets quoted in every "domain LLMs win" slide deck since. The authors themselves tell you it's compromised — the model likely saw that text during pretraining. Strip that number out, or discount it heavily, and the story gets a lot less dramatic.
[G] Both are defensible, and the paper gives you the tools to separate them. The contamination disclosure applies specifically to the internal sentiment and NER sets — the biggest, most-quoted margins. It doesn't apply to the held-out bits-per-byte test, deliberately built clean: deduplicated and strictly future relative to training. That test still shows a real, if smaller, advantage. My read: the domain-pretraining effect is genuine, but overstated in this paper by exactly the tasks that got the most attention.
[O] That's a fair haircut, but it doesn't erase the general-benchmark result, which isn't contaminated the same way.
[S] It doesn't, but it raises a separate problem — the comparison set. This is a March twenty twenty-three paper. ChatGPT had been out for months, and GPT-4 launched two weeks earlier. Neither appears anywhere. The strongest comparison is GPT-3 numbers borrowed from an older paper. And they explicitly skip fine-tuned small models, something like a task-specific FinBERT, citing differences in evaluation setup — exactly the comparison that would tell you whether fifty billion parameters and fifty-three days of training bought anything a much cheaper approach couldn't.
[O] That's a real gap, I'll grant that one.
[G] It is, and it's the strongest skeptical point here. The paper is honest about what it measured, but that's narrower than the version that circulated afterward. It shows domain-mixed pretraining beats open general models of the same size, under a fair, identical-harness comparison. It doesn't show this was the cheapest or best way to get those gains, because it deliberately didn't run that comparison.
[S] And the flashiest use cases — generating Bloomberg Query Language from plain English, writing headlines, answering "who's the CEO of this company" — those are never scored at all. Just hand-picked examples.
[G] Correct, no quantitative evaluation on any of that, no error analysis, and given this is a domain where a wrong number is a real liability, that absence is more notable than in most papers.
[O] Zooming out, does this still matter given that BloombergGPT was never released?
[G] I think it matters for the road not taken. Within about a year, open financial fine-tunes — projects like FinBen and PIXIU — showed you could capture a lot of this benefit by fine-tuning an existing open model instead of pretraining from zero, while general models kept getting stronger regardless. This paper reads today less like a template everyone copied and more like an expensive, well-documented experiment that helped settle the question the other way.
[S] Which is itself a useful contribution to evaluation practice, even if the headline conclusion didn't hold up as an industry playbook.
[G] Agreed, and there's a lesson here for anyone building evals: the win-rate metric they use treats a zero-point-one-point win the same as a fifty-point win. That's a real methodological weakness worth flagging — it's easy to summarize "best in class" without the number telling you how close the race actually was.
[O] So the lasting value might be more in how carefully they reported it than in what it proved about domain pretraining.
[G] That's my read, going a bit beyond just what's stated in the paper. The self-disclosed contamination and the published training instabilities are the part of this paper that aged best.
[G] If I had to leave listeners with one line, it's this: BloombergGPT is a real, fairly-tested improvement on finance tasks for models its size, but its most dramatic numbers are compromised by contamination the authors admit to themselves, and the paper conspicuously avoids the comparisons that would tell you whether training from scratch was worth it.
[O] My takeaway is the opposite emphasis: this is what honest domain-model research looks like, contamination disclosure and all, and more papers should show their training scars this openly.
