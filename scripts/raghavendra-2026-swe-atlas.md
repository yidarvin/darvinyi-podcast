[S] Here's a number that should make every coding-agent benchmark nervous. Grade a refactor purely by whether the test suite still passes, and the top models score somewhere around eighty percent.
[O] Grade the exact same refactors against what a senior engineer would actually expect on code review, and that score falls off a cliff.
[S] We are talking a fifteen to forty point collapse, depending on the model. That is not noise. That is a benchmark telling us the thing everyone has been measuring was the easy part.
[O] Which is exactly why I am excited about this paper, and exactly why I suspect you are about to spend the next half hour telling me why I should not be.

[O] Welcome back to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar sit down with one paper at a time.
[S] Today's paper is SWE Atlas, Benchmarking Coding Agents Beyond Issue Resolution, out of Scale AI, led by Mohit Raghavendra and colleagues.
[O] Joining us to walk through it is Freya, a researcher who has spent a lot of time with this benchmark's rubrics and its failure logs. Freya, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is a fun one, because it is less about a new leaderboard and more about what the old leaderboards were quietly leaving out.
[S] Let's set that up, then. Why does the field need another coding benchmark when SWE-bench and its descendants already exist?

[G] Because SWE-bench, SWE-Bench Pro, Terminal-Bench, they all frame software engineering as one move. Here is an issue, produce a patch, run a hidden test suite, pass or fail. The authors call that functional resolution, and it is clean and programmatically verifiable, which is exactly why the field converged on it.
[O] And exactly why it is saturating. If everyone optimizes the same narrow slice, of course models start looking superhuman on it.
[G] Right, and the paper's argument is that functional resolution is a narrow slice of what engineers actually do all day. Onboarding into an unfamiliar repo and explaining why something misbehaves at runtime. Writing tests that anticipate edge cases instead of just turning a suite green. Refactoring a mess into something clean without changing a single observable behavior. None of that is fix the failing test.
[S] So the worry is you get agents that are excellent patchers and lousy engineers, good at shipping the fix, careless about whether the codebase is healthier or worse afterward.
[G] That is close to the paper's own phrase, patchers versus engineers. And the reason nobody measured this well before is that it resists programmatic verification. There is no single hidden test that tells you whether a Q and A answer is comprehensive, or whether a refactor is clean. The prior attempts are thin too. A benchmark like CoReQA is answerable by just reading the code statically, and existing test-writing benchmarks mostly check whether one bug-reproduction test fires.
[O] So the gap is not that nobody thought of this, it is that nobody figured out how to grade it.

[S] So how do you grade something that does not reduce to pass or fail on a test suite, Freya?
[G] That is the core contribution. SWE Atlas is two hundred eighty-four expert-authored tasks pulled from eighteen actively maintained open-source repositories, split into three workflows. Codebase Q and A at one hundred twenty-four tasks, Test Writing at ninety, Refactoring at seventy. Each workflow splits further into sub-categories, Q and A alone covers architecture and system design, root-cause analysis, onboarding, security, and API integration.
[O] What actually makes a task here different from a SWE-bench task, mechanically?
[G] Two design choices. First, under-specification. SWE-Bench Pro and Terminal-Bench write fully specified, unambiguous prompts. SWE Atlas deliberately writes the way a real developer would ask, sometimes vague, goal-oriented, often requiring the agent to actually run the application and observe live behavior rather than just read the source. The problem statements are net-new too, rewritten by experts rather than copied off the original GitHub issue.
[S] And the second choice?
[G] A hybrid verifier. Wherever a programmatic check is possible, they use it. Wherever it is not, they layer on rubric-based grading, a checklist of self-contained, binary yes-or-no checks, each graded independently by an LLM judge. A task only counts as solved if every programmatic gate clears and every must-have rubric item comes back yes. Miss one edge case in an otherwise-working refactor and the whole task fails.
[O] That is a strict bar. What do those rubrics actually check, category by category?
[G] Codebase Q and A is rubric-only, answer comprehensiveness plus negative rubrics that penalize things you should not say. Test Writing layers three checks, a manifest check where a judge confirms your listed tests match what you actually added, a mutation check where your tests have to pass on working code and then fail once the target function is swapped for a no-op stub, and rubrics for comprehensiveness and placement. Refactoring combines a regression check, the project's suite plus hidden tests must not flip from passing to failing and you are not allowed to touch the test files, with rubrics for maintainability, cleanup, and anti-patterns.
[S] That is a lot of rubric surface area per task.
[G] On average, ten and a half rubrics for Q and A, seventeen for Test Writing, seventeen and a half for Refactoring, and refactoring tasks carry about eighteen tests each on top of that. The quality control is heavy too, an expert writes a reference solution that passes everything, an independent quality-control team reviews it, then three more trusted experts audit every task, and any rubric two of the three flag as invalid or ambiguous gets pulled.
[O] And they run models on their real production scaffolds, not some stripped-down harness?
[G] Both, deliberately. Frontier models run on their native vendor scaffold, Codex CLI, Claude Code, Gemini CLI, to reproduce the real end-user experience. But a subset also runs on mini-SWE-agent, which gives the model nothing but a bash command, specifically so you can compare raw model capability with the scaffold's own tooling stripped away. That distinction matters a lot later in this conversation.

[S] Let's get to the leaderboard, then immediately complicate it.
[G] On headline Pass at one, averaged over three trials, GPT 5.4 on Codex leads at forty-three point five percent, Opus 4.7 on Claude Code follows at forty-one point nine, effectively tied under their own best scaffolds. The best open-weight model, GLM 5, manages twenty-four percent. The weakest entrants sit in the high teens.
[O] So best case, the best coding agent in the world is solving fewer than half of these tasks.
[G] And it is worse than the headline suggests, because Pass at one is an average across trials, not a guarantee. The consistency score, that is the fraction of tasks where all three trials succeed, drops thirty to fifty percent relative to that headline average. Even the leaders only hit about twenty-nine percent there.
[S] Now give me the number you opened the show with.
[G] Right, the signature finding. Grade Refactoring purely on whether the tests still pass, and every model lands somewhere around sixty to eighty percent, which looks exactly like the saturation everyone already complains about in SWE-bench. Switch to the rubric and scores collapse and spread out, a gap of roughly fifteen to forty points for Refactoring, ten to fifteen for Test Writing. Every model passes the functional check more often than the rubric, on both workflows.
[O] Where does that gap actually live? Spread evenly, or concentrated somewhere specific?
[G] It concentrates exactly where an engineer would care most. For refactors it is Code Maintainability and Artifact Cleanup, models leave dead code, stale imports, and missed call sites behind even when the tests are green. For test writing it is Test Comprehensiveness, the top models are still missing edge cases.
[S] And the failure modes differ by vendor, which I find more interesting than the leaderboard itself.
[G] They really do. On Codebase Q and A, the strongest models are visibly execution-oriented, resolve rate tracks with how many times the agent actually runs code, and the best runs spin up the real application and fire live requests. But GPT models fail predominantly on missing or incomplete coverage, they run experiments but do not address every sub-question in the prompt. Claude models fail predominantly on no runtime evidence, forty-six percent of the time, falling back to explaining the code statically on a task that explicitly demanded live proof.
[O] Give me a concrete case, not just a percentage.
[G] There is a nice one. A task asks an agent to explain why inbound email replies sometimes route to the wrong user in an open-source mail app. GPT-5.4 spins up the actual database, plants two contacts sharing an address, fires four controlled reply events, and reports the resolved identifiers at each step. It nails two of the three required observations, but never constructs the no-match scenario the rubric demanded, so it still fails on a single missing edge case. Weaker models in the same task never leave static code reading at all.
[S] So even the best trajectory in the paper fails on a completeness technicality.
[G] Exactly, and that pattern repeats in Test Writing, in a different shape. On a task where the mutation replaces an entire function body with a no-op, GPT-5.4 wrote assertions that were trivially true under that no-op, checking a message count that stayed at zero either way. The reference solution instead asserts observable side effects, messages actually getting marked read, mailboxes actually shrinking, so it catches the regression the agent's version missed. Writing more tests is not writing good tests.
[O] What about Refactoring at scale? Real refactors are not small diffs.
[G] That is where it gets bleak. Gold patches in this benchmark range from thirty-five lines to over two thousand. Performance collapses steadily as the required change grows, and the bottleneck is multi-file coordination, the strongest models touch more of the required files than weaker ones but still miss call sites on a meaningful share of tasks. There is a small uptick at the very largest bucket, but that turns out to be mostly mechanical mass renames, not real logical refactors. And it is not just a lines-of-code story, C and C++ tasks specifically run roughly half the pass rate of Go, Python, or TypeScript across the whole benchmark.
[S] Any silver lining on cost, at least?
[G] A little. Plotting cost per task against pass rate on Q and A and Test Writing traces a real frontier, from Gemini 3 Flash at about thirty-five cents a task and fifteen percent passing, up through GPT 5.4 at roughly a dollar ninety a task and forty percent passing. Opus sits above that frontier on cost without beating GPT 5.4 on capability, so it is dominated on this particular tradeoff.
[O] One more result I want on the record before we argue, the sub-agent behavior.
[G] Claude Opus 4.6 spawns a sub-agent in ninety-six and a half percent of Q and A trials, averaging two point six delegated agents each time. Sonnet 4.6 delegates even more often, in ninety-nine percent of trials. Gemini 3.1 Pro, given the exact same delegation tool, uses it in roughly one percent of trials and just runs as a single loop. Same capability, wildly different strategy.

[O] Okay, my optimist case, on the record before Sam pokes holes in it. This is the best-instrumented coding benchmark I have seen, it does not just measure whether the code runs, it measures whether an engineer would approve the pull request. And the field is already responding to exactly the right incentive, cleaner test writing, not spammier test writing.

Forty-three percent Pass at one is not coding agents are good at this, it is coding agents fail most of the time, and the consistency numbers make it worse, under-thirty-percent reliability means you cannot trust a single run. Opus 4.7 scores thirty-eight point nine percent there, GPT 5.4 scores thirty-eight percent.

[G] They do, narrowly. Which means a real chunk of that famous GPT-5.4-versus-Opus-4.7 gap on the front page is not model versus model, it is Codex versus Claude Code. The paper runs both comparisons transparently, to its credit, but the headline table is the one that gets quoted.
[O] That is a legitimate point for Sam. But I will take the other side of the judge-reliability question, because that part is unusually rigorous. Across over thirteen hundred rubric-level judgments, three separate judge models agree with each other at a Cohen's kappa around point seven four to point eight four, Macro F1 point eight seven to point nine two. Re-run the same thirty responses five times and you get unanimous verdicts ninety-nine point two percent of the time.
[S] Freya, agreeing with itself is not the same as being right. Three frontier judges can share the exact same blind spot and call it consensus.
[G] That is the correct objection, and here is the part I actually want to correct on air, because the site's own writeup gets this slightly wrong, it frames a human-versus-judge study as the missing piece that would close the loop. The paper has one. Two hundred rubric gradings, human raters against the three judge models, on real Opus and GPT-5 responses. Kappa ranges point seven eight to point eight eight, Macro F1 point eight nine to point nine four.
[O] So there is a ground-truth check, and it is respectable.
[G] It is respectable and it is smaller, two hundred gradings against the thirteen-hundred-plus judge-versus-judge comparisons, and it runs a touch lower than the judge-versus-judge agreement, which is exactly what you would expect if the judges share some correlated bias relative to a human rater. Sam's instinct is directionally right, it is just not the study does not exist, it is the study is thinner than the headline consistency numbers suggest.
[S] I will take that adjustment. It is still meaningfully more validation than most rubric-based benchmarks bother to run.
[O] Can we talk about contamination, because I think that is the strongest part of the paper. Every refactoring task is anchored to a real merged pull request, so the gold code is sitting in public git history. They ran their own memorization screen, mean Jaccard similarity on passing solutions is point five one for Opus 4.6 and point three five for GPT-5.4, nowhere near the near-one clustering you would expect from copy-paste memorization.
[S] I will grant that is more transparent than the industry norm, OpenAI has stopped reporting SWE-Bench Verified over exactly this concern. But no clear evidence of memorization is a hedge, not a clean bill of health, and the paper says so itself. They also found four out of two hundred ten trials had a model searching raw GitHub content for the solution, a small number, but it happened, and every one of those attempts still failed.
[G] The authors are careful with that language throughout, and I would rather see a paper hedge honestly than round up to contamination-free.

[O] Stepping back, what does this mean for how the field should build coding benchmarks going forward?
[G] I think the real contribution is not the leaderboard, it is the measurement instrument. Grade by tests, then grade the same artifact by professional rubric, is a pattern that generalizes past coding. Anywhere a functional metric can be satisfied while the underlying quality is still bad, this two-gate structure exposes it.
[S] It is also a caution for anyone building agentic evals generally. Mixing native scaffolds with the model under test quietly conflates two variables, and this paper is a live example of how much that can move a leaderboard.
[O] And for anyone actually shipping these agents, do not read forty-three percent on a hard, professionally-graded benchmark as not ready. Read it as still needs a human in the loop for exactly the categories this benchmark isolates, which the authors say outright in their own limitations section.

[G] If there is one sentence to take from this paper, it is that functional correctness and engineering quality are different axes, and the industry has mostly been measuring one of them.
[O] Mine is that the improvement trajectory is real, the Opus family climbs steadily across every workflow over an eight-month window, so this gap is closing, just not as fast as the pass-rate headlines make it look.
[S] Mine is that the sharpest number in this whole paper is not on the leaderboard at all, it is that the ranking of the two best models flips depending on which scaffold you are measuring, and that should make everyone reading these tables slow down.
