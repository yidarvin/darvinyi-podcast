[S] Every major lab will tell you their flagship model can read a context window of a hundred thousand tokens or more, and on the classic needle-in-a-haystack test, most of them score close to a perfect ceiling doing it.
[O] Which sounds like long-context retrieval is basically a solved problem, until you change one detail in the test and watch eleven of thirteen of those same models fall to half their own short-context score.
[S] The one detail: whether the question actually shares any words with the fact it's trying to find.
[G] That's the entire wager of this paper. A lot of what looked like long-context reasoning turns out to be keyword matching wearing a disguise.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the frontier of AI research. Today's paper is NoLiMa: Long-Context Evaluation Beyond Literal Matching, by Ali Modarressi and colleagues at LMU Munich and Adobe Research, published at ICML twenty twenty-five.
[S] And walking us through it is Konstantin, who's studied this work closely. Konstantin, welcome to the show.
[G] Glad to be here.
[O] Konstantin, set the stage for us. What exactly is broken about the way we've been testing long-context models?
[G] The standard tool is needle-in-a-haystack: bury one fact, the needle, inside a huge pile of irrelevant text, the haystack, then ask a question that should retrieve it. It's been the default long-context test for a couple of years, and people keep making it harder, more needles, distractor text, chains of facts that require multi-step reasoning.
[S] And scores on those harder versions kept climbing anyway, which is usually the sign a benchmark needs replacing, not patching.
[G] Right, but the authors' argument isn't just "it's saturated." It's that the needle and the question almost always share literal vocabulary. If the needle states a fact using certain words, the question usually reuses those same words, so a model can locate the needle by matching tokens instead of actually understanding what's being asked.
[O] Which matters because attention mechanisms are extremely good at exactly that, finding and copying repeated tokens. It's called induction-head behavior, and it has nothing to do with genuine reasoning over meaning.
[G] Exactly, and the authors don't just assert this, they measure it. They compute ROUGE precision, essentially how many of the question's words literally appear in the relevant context, across a set of existing benchmarks. Vanilla needle-in-a-haystack scores zero point nine oh five on that measure. RULER's synthetic retrieval task scores zero point five seven one. Even a downstream long-document QA benchmark, Infinity Bench, hits zero point nine six six.
[S] So on every one of those, the model barely has to search. The words practically point at the answer.
[G] And that's the gap NoLiMa is built to close. Its own score on that same measure is zero point zero six nine, more than an order of magnitude lower than any of the others in that comparison.
[O] Which means if a model does well on NoLiMa, it can't be leaning on that shortcut, because the shortcut was engineered out.
[S] So how do you actually build a needle that shares no words with its own question?
[G] You link them through world knowledge instead of vocabulary. The paper's own example: the needle reads, "Actually, Yuki lives next to the Semper Opera House," paired with a question asking which character has visited Dresden. No token in the question appears in the needle. The model has to know the Semper Opera House is in Dresden to connect the two.
[O] So the connection isn't hard for a person. It just can't be found by string search.
[G] Right, and some of the links use commonsense rather than geography. One needle describes a character who's avoided meat and fish for years, paired with a question about who wouldn't eat a fish-based meal. There's also a harder two-hop version: instead of asking about Dresden directly, the question asks about the surrounding state, which requires the model to know Dresden sits in Saxony, two inferential steps instead of one.
[S] And there's an inverted-order variant too, isn't there?
[G] Yes. Normally the character's name comes before the keyword in the needle sentence; the inverted version puts the keyword first and the name after. That tests whether a model's causal attention can trace backward through a sentence as easily as it traces forward.
[O] What about the haystacks themselves, the filler text the needle gets buried in?
[G] Ten open-license books, each at least fifty thousand tokens, but they're not used as continuous chapters. The authors chop them into snippets under two hundred fifty tokens and concatenate randomly sampled snippets from across the books until the haystack clears sixty thousand tokens. That's specifically to blunt memorization, since these are public books a model may have seen in training.
[S] That's a real design strength. Continuous chapters would let a model exploit narrative structure or plain memorized text instead of the needle.
[G] There's also a two-stage filtering pipeline before any needle goes in. First, an embedding model, Contriever, flags haystack words that are suspiciously close, semantically or as substrings, to the question's keywords, and those get manually removed. Second, an instruction-tuned Llama three point three seventy billion model scans the haystack in roughly two hundred fifty token chunks against every question, flagging any chunk that could plausibly answer it, with a human confirming every flag. That repeats until nothing more gets flagged.
[O] And they sanity-checked the filtering model itself.
[G] Yes. As a positive control, they planted needles in a hundred randomly chosen chunks and had the same Llama model try to detect them. It found the planted answer ninety-nine point eight percent of the time, which is good evidence its "not found" flags during redaction mean the answer really isn't there, not that the model just missed it.
[S] What's the final scale of the test set?
[G] Fifty-eight question-needle pairs across five keyword-type groups, each with a default and inverted version, some with one-hop and two-hop variants. Every pair gets tested at context lengths from two hundred fifty tokens up through thirty-two thousand, with the needle placed at twenty-six evenly spaced positions across five different randomly built haystacks, seven thousand five hundred forty individual tests per context length.
[O] And how do they score a model fairly, given some models are just better at the underlying task regardless of context length?
[G] They define a base score: for each pair, take its best result across the three shortest lengths, two hundred fifty, five hundred, and one thousand tokens, then average that across all pairs. Then they define effective length, adapted from a similar idea in the RULER paper: the longest tested context length at which a model still scores at least eighty-five percent of its own base score. It's a per-model threshold, not a fixed cutoff, isolating degradation from context length itself.
[O] Let's get to the numbers, because this is where it gets brutal.
[G] Thirteen models were tested, every one publicly claiming support for at least a hundred twenty-eight thousand tokens of context. Base scores were mostly strong, twelve of the thirteen effectively clearing eighty-five percent, with only Llama three point one eight billion falling well short, at seventy-six point seven percent.
[S] So on short context, the task itself is fair. It's not secretly too hard.
[G] Correct, that's the point of the base score. But look at effective length. GPT-4o is the standout: a ninety-nine point three percent base score, and it holds up to an effective length of eight thousand tokens before dropping, still at sixty-nine point seven percent by thirty-two thousand. Every other model tested caps out at an effective length of two thousand tokens or less.
[O] Two thousand. Out of a claimed context window of a hundred twenty-eight thousand, or for some of these, a million or two million tokens.
[G] Llama three point three seventy billion is a clean example, ninety-seven point three percent base score, effective length of two thousand tokens, and by thirty-two thousand tokens it's down to forty-two point seven percent. Command R plus falls hardest in relative terms: a ninety point nine percent base score collapsing to seven point four percent at thirty-two thousand tokens.
[S] So the headline number, eleven of thirteen models land at half their base score or worse by thirty-two thousand tokens, that's not one bad model. That's almost the entire field.
[G] Right, and it's a much steeper decline than the same models show on other long-context benchmarks with a similar setup but without the literal-overlap control. Llama three point one seventy billion, for instance, has an effective length of sixteen thousand tokens on BABILong and thirty-two thousand on RULER, but only two thousand on NoLiMa.
[O] Which is exactly the evidence that those other benchmarks were partly measuring keyword search, not context understanding.
[G] There's a mechanistic layer to this too. Two-hop questions, the ones requiring an extra inferential step, are consistently harder than one-hop questions, and that gap widens as context grows. Inverted-order needles are also harder than the default order, which the authors read as evidence that a model's causal attention traces backward through a sentence less easily than it traces forward.
[S] What did the position-in-context analysis show? Because "lost in the middle" is already a known effect.
[G] It's there for the simpler one-hop tasks, performance dips when the needle sits in the middle of a thirty-two-thousand-token context. But for two-hop tasks it's different: the entire performance curve sinks toward zero as context grows, no matter where the needle sits. To figure out whether that's about position encoding or about attention itself, the authors ran a version where the needle's distance from the question is held fixed, always somewhere in the final two thousand tokens, while only the total context length changes.
[O] And performance still dropped.
[G] It still dropped, which rules out position encoding as the explanation, because the relative distance never changed. It points at the attention mechanism itself getting overwhelmed as total context grows, once there's no literal cue to lean on.
[S] Does reasoning help at all? Chain-of-thought, or actual reasoning models like o1?
[G] It helps, but doesn't rescue the collapse. For Llama three point three seventy billion on two-hop questions at thirty-two thousand tokens, chain-of-thought prompting raises accuracy from twenty-five point nine percent to thirty-four point three percent, a thirty-two point four percent relative gain, but that's still well below the one-hop, no-chain-of-thought baseline of fifty-six point two percent. And on a specially selected "hard" subset of the ten toughest pairs, even OpenAI's o1 falls from a near-perfect base score of ninety-nine point nine percent down to thirty-one point one percent at thirty-two thousand tokens.
[O] So reasoning models degrade too, just from a higher starting point.
[G] Right. And the cleanest confirmation of the whole thesis is the ablation: put the literal cue back, make it multiple choice, with the correct character's name listed among four options, and one-hop accuracy at thirty-two thousand tokens jumps from fifty-six point two percent back up to ninety-three point one percent. Restore the shortcut, and most of the "long-context failure" disappears.
[O] Okay, here's my optimist case. This paper is genuinely useful because it isolates the actual variable that matters. It's not saying models can't do long context, GPT-4o clearly generalizes further than the rest, holding above fifty percent of its base score all the way out to a hundred twenty-eight thousand tokens in the paper's own extended results. That tells you the capability is real, and this benchmark gives labs a clean, contamination-resistant signal to train against.
[S] I'll take the other side. What you're calling a clean signal is a narrow one. Fifty-eight question-needle pairs isn't a lot of coverage, and every one is built from the same recipe, a Western literary or geographic commonsense link, opera houses, states, countries. If a model's world knowledge has gaps in exactly that kind of fact, the benchmark can't tell you whether that's a context-length failure or just an unfamiliar fact.
[G] That's a fair critique, and it's one the paper doesn't fully close. There's no reported human or open-book ceiling on the hardest conditions, no baseline showing that a person, or a model with no context-length constraint at all, reliably gets these right. Without that, you can't cleanly separate "too hard because it's long" from "too hard because it's obscure."
[O] But the control test cuts against that somewhat, Llama three point three at ninety-nine point eight percent on the positive-control check shows the facts themselves are answerable when a model can actually see them nearby.
[S] That test is about the filtering model recognizing a nearby answer, not about whether every needle-question link is intuitive to every evaluated model at zero distraction. Those aren't the same claim.
[G] Also worth flagging: the whole roster is a frozen snapshot, thirteen models current for a submission in February twenty twenty-five, plus the o1 and o3-mini reasoning models and a DeepSeek R1 distilled model as an add-on. The paper's own footnote says newer models, GPT-4.1 among them, have improved long-context performance, but those results are pushed to an appendix rather than the main table.
[O] Which means the headline sixty-nine point seven percent number for GPT-4o is already dated against whatever's shipping now.
[S] And that's my real worry with any benchmark this pointed. It's built to expose one specific failure mode, which is great science, but it invites people to quote the raw percentages as a general long-context leaderboard, when really they're a relative measure of how much a model degrades once you take the crutch away.
[G] I'd score that point to Skeptic. The authors themselves frame it as revealing a limitation, not as ranking overall long-context ability, but the numbers are so stark they do get cited as if they were a leaderboard.
[O] Fine, I'll concede the leaderboard framing is a misuse, but I'd still push back on calling the underlying design flawed. Zero literal overlap is an artificial constraint, sure, but it's an artificial constraint chosen specifically to isolate a real capability, the same way you'd control a variable in any experiment.
[G] That's the strongest defense of it, and it's basically what the authors argue too, that this measures something real even if it's not a naturalistic task. Where I'd push back on both of you is contamination. This is actually one of the paper's stronger points: haystacks are randomly concatenated book snippets rather than continuous chapters, character names are randomized per instance, and that ninety-nine point eight percent control result is decent evidence the redaction process isn't overzealous. It's cleaner hygiene than most static long-context benchmarks manage.
[S] I'll grant that. My skepticism is about scope and shelf life, not about whether the experiment itself was run carefully.
[O] So where does this leave anyone building a real system, say a retrieval pipeline or a search product?
[G] The authors make that connection explicitly in their conclusion. In a retrieval system, the document that actually answers a query might have very little lexical overlap with the query itself, while some other, wrong document has plenty of overlap. If a model is quietly relying on literal cues the way NoLiMa shows, it can get distracted by the wrong document precisely because that document looks more similar on the surface.
[S] Which is a bigger deal than a synthetic benchmark result. It means the failure mode generalizes to exactly the setting most people actually deploy long-context models in.
[O] And for evaluation practice broadly, the real contribution might be that ROUGE comparison across benchmarks. Anyone releasing a new long-context benchmark now has a cheap way to check whether they've accidentally built another literal-matching test.
[G] If there's one sentence to take from this paper, it's that a benchmark's saturation doesn't mean a capability is solved. It might mean the benchmark has a shortcut, and NoLiMa is a careful demonstration of exactly that shortcut for long context.
[O] My takeaway is that the gap between claimed context windows and actual usable context is much larger than the marketing suggests, and now there's a rigorous way to measure it.
