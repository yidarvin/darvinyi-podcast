[O] Picture a question you can't answer by matching keywords to one sentence. You have to find a person in one Wikipedia article, then use their name to unlock the fact you actually need in a second article. That's the promise behind today's paper, and when it landed, it looked like reading comprehension was finally being forced to grow up.
[S] Or, less charitably, it's the paper that taught the field how different two claims are. Writing a question about two paragraphs is not the same as writing a question that requires two paragraphs. A lot of models found that gap and walked right through it.
[O] Fair, but before you write it off, the headline numbers are humbling in exactly the way a good benchmark should be. The best model of the day trailed human performance by roughly thirty points on the metric that actually matters here, getting the answer and its justification both right. That's not nothing.
[S] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a guest scholar take apart one paper from the litsearch site. Today's paper is HotpotQA, "A Dataset for Diverse, Explainable Multi-hop Question Answering," by Zhilin Yang, Peng Qi, Saizheng Zhang, Yoshua Bengio, William Cohen, Ruslan Salakhutdinov, and Christopher Manning, out of Carnegie Mellon and Stanford, published at EMNLP in twenty eighteen.
[O] And joining us is Eleanor, who's spent a lot of time in this dataset and in the papers that tried to break it. Eleanor, welcome.
[G] Thanks for having me. HotpotQA is one of those papers where the data-collection pipeline is the real paper, arguably more interesting than the baseline model they report, and it's worth walking through carefully, because the design choices explain both why it succeeded and why it eventually got picked apart.
[S] Let's set the scene. What was broken about question answering that this paper is responding to?
[G] The dominant dataset at the time was SQuAD, where a question is answerable from a single paragraph, often a single sentence in it. That drove enormous progress, but it also taught models a bad habit, find the sentence that looks most like the question and extract a span. TriviaQA and SearchQA tried to widen the net by attaching several retrieved documents to each question, but because those documents were gathered after the fact with information retrieval, the actual answer still tended to sit near a few adjacent sentences in one paragraph. The extra documents were mostly noise to filter, not a second hop to take.
[O] So the multi-document datasets weren't really testing multi-hop reasoning, they were testing needle-in-a-haystack robustness.
[G] Right. And there was a second lineage, QAngaroo and ComplexWebQuestions, that did target genuine multi-hop chains, but they were generated from knowledge-base schemas. That caps the diversity of what you can ask to whatever relations the knowledge base happens to encode. And none of these datasets tell you why an answer is correct. They're all distant supervision, you get the final answer, never the reasoning path.
[S] Which means a model can be right for the wrong reason, and the dataset has no way to catch it.
[G] Exactly, and that's the gap HotpotQA goes after, four things at once: questions that genuinely require finding and reasoning over multiple documents, free-text diversity that isn't bound to a knowledge base, sentence-level supporting facts that double as strong supervision and an explainability target, and a new comparison question type. The name, by the way, comes from the first three authors landing on the idea over a hot pot dinner, and the paper's own footnote says author order was decided by rolling dice.
[O] Walk us through how you actually get crowd workers to write good two-hop questions, Eleanor, because that sounds hard to elicit on purpose.
[G] It is, in pilot studies the authors found that handing two arbitrary paragraphs to a worker rarely produced a meaningful multi-hop question. Their fix is a directed graph over the whole of English Wikipedia, where an edge from article A to article B means A's first paragraph hyperlinks to B. Take an edge like that, and B becomes a natural bridge entity. Their own example is "when was the singer-songwriter of Radiohead born," you can't answer that without first resolving the bridge entity, Thom Yorke, then finding his birthday in a second article.
[S] But not every hyperlinked entity makes a good bridge. Countries get linked from everywhere and don't share much in common with everything that links to them.
[G] Right, so they don't sample bridges freely. They manually curate five hundred ninety-one categories of popular Wikipedia pages, drawn from WikiProject's lists, and only sample bridge entities from that curated set. There's a lot of manual curation sitting underneath what looks like an automatic pipeline.
[O] And the comparison questions, that's the genuinely new question type?
[G] Yes. They curate forty-two lists of similar entities, things like highest mountains on Earth, and sample two entities from the same list. Some of those become yes-or-no questions, like "are Iron Maiden and A C D C from the same country," which is a nice design touch. A question like "is Iron Maiden or A C D C from the U K" can be answered from a single article alone, but the same-country phrasing forces you to check both.
[S] Is there a sampling ratio between the two question families?
[G] Roughly three to one, about seventy-five percent of examples come from bridge entities, twenty-five percent from comparison lists, and about half of the comparison questions are the yes-or-no variety. Workers also highlight the sentences they used to answer, those become the supporting facts.
[O] And all of that was collected on Mechanical Turk?
[G] Through a ParlAI-based interface, yes, one hundred twelve thousand, seven hundred seventy-nine valid examples in total. Then comes a step people underrate. They trained a QA model, ran three-fold cross-validation over the multi-hop examples, and pulled out the ones it answered with high confidence, about fifty-seven thousand questions, sixty percent of the multi-hop pool, into a subset called train-medium. Genuinely single-hop questions, about eighteen thousand of them, became train-easy. What's left after both cuts becomes train-hard, dev, and the two test sets.
[S] That's a meaningful design choice. It means the test sets are adversarially selected against a specific twenty-eighteen baseline, which is a very different thing from a random sample.
[G] It is, and it's worth flagging now, because it matters later in the debate. Filtering by one model's confidence doesn't guarantee the surviving questions require two hops, only that that particular model got them wrong.
[O] What about the two evaluation settings, distractor and full wiki?
[G] In the distractor setting, the model sees the two gold paragraphs plus eight paragraphs retrieved by bigram T F I D F, using the question as the query, all shuffled together. So retrieval is solved for you, and you're being tested purely on reasoning over a fixed, ten-paragraph context. In full wiki, the gold paragraphs are withheld entirely, and the model has to find them among the first paragraphs of over five million Wikipedia articles, an inverted-index filter narrows that down to a pool, and bigram T F I D F picks the final ten candidates. That setting bundles retrieval and reasoning together.
[S] And the metrics?
[G] Exact match and F one on the answer span, exact match and F one on the predicted supporting facts against the gold set, and then a joint metric that multiplies precision and recall across both tasks before computing F one. So joint F one only rewards a system that gets the answer and the justification right on the same example, miss either one and the joint score drops hard.
[O] That joint metric might be the paper's real methodological contribution, it's a template a lot of later explainability-focused benchmarks borrowed.
[G] The baseline model itself is a reimplementation of Clark and Gardner's twenty seventeen architecture, character and word embeddings, bi-attention, self-attention, essentially the SQuAD state of the art of that moment, with a three-way yes, no, or span classifier on top, plus a per-sentence binary classifier trained jointly to predict supporting facts. The authors call training with that classifier strong supervision.
[S] Let's get to numbers. What does that baseline actually score?
[G] In the distractor setting, on the test set, it gets an EM of forty-five point four six and an F one of fifty-eight point nine nine on the answer. But the joint metric, answer and supporting facts both correct, is only twelve point zero four EM, forty-one point three seven F one.
[O] So less than half the F one survives once you demand the justification be right too.
[G] Right. Full wiki is much worse, answer F one falls to thirty-four point four, joint F one to seventeen point eight five. You can see why in the retrieval numbers, their T F I D F retriever puts both gold paragraphs in the top ten only fifty-six percent of the time on dev, with a mean rank around three hundred fifteen. Bridge questions, which need both hops actually retrieved, crater from fifty-nine F one in distractor down to about thirty in full wiki, while comparison questions barely move, fifty-five down to about fifty-one, since both named entities tend to be easy to find on their own.
[S] So the full-wiki collapse is mostly a retrieval story, not a reasoning story.
[G] That's the authors' own read, and the ablations back it up. If you feed the model only the gold supporting-fact sentences as context, an oracle setting, it jumps to an F one near sixty-seven, about ten points over giving it the full gold paragraphs. So the right two sentences really are most of the task. But training the model to predict those sentences itself, the strong-supervision setup, only adds about two points of F one over not using it at all. The authors candidly call that likely suboptimal and leave it for future work.
[O] And the human comparison?
[G] On a sample of one thousand examples, humans hit ninety-one point four F one and eighty-three point six EM on the answer, and eighty-two point five five joint F one, more than thirty points above the model on every axis. The annotator upper bound sits near ninety-seven, ninety-eight F one. That gap is the paper's headline argument that there's real room left to fill.
[O] So here's my optimist case. This is a genuinely well-engineered benchmark. The hyperlink-graph pipeline for eliciting real multi-hop questions from crowd workers is clever, and it worked at scale, well over one hundred thousand examples. The supporting-facts idea turned explainability into something you can actually score, which is rare, and that thirty-point human gap gave the field a real target. It also seeded an entire lineage, tool-use and agentic-retrieval systems like ReAct and Reflexion were trained and tested on this dataset.
[S] I'll grant all of that pipeline cleverness. But here's my deflationary case. The paper's guarantee is that a question was authored while looking at two paragraphs. That is not the same as proving the question requires two hops to answer. Within about a year, follow-up analyses showed a large share of HotpotQA is solvable single-hop, especially the comparison questions and the type-two questions, where you just match an entity's type against one paragraph and never actually need the bridge.
[G] That's accurate, and the paper's own numbers hint at it. In their reasoning-type audit of one hundred sampled dev and test questions, they classify six percent as single-hop and two percent as unanswerable by their own judgment, even after their filtering pipeline. And the distractor setting makes the shortcut worse, not better. With only eight T F I D F distractors, the two gold paragraphs are often the only on-topic text in the window, so a model can find the right paragraph by topical salience alone, without ever traversing the bridge.
[S] Right, and that's exactly what later adversarial-distractor work showed. When Jiang and Bansal swapped in harder, on-topic distractors instead of loose T F I D F ones, scores dropped sharply, because the shortcut had been propping the numbers up.
[O] Okay, that lands, I'll concede the distractor setting oversold reasoning difficulty. But full wiki is the harder, more honest setting, and models still struggled there.
[G] They did, but for a different reason than multi-hop reasoning. Full wiki conflates retrieval quality with reasoning quality into one number. With hits at ten sitting around fifty-six percent, a big chunk of that headline score is really a verdict on a twenty-eighteen bigram T F I D F retriever. Swap in a modern dense retriever, and the score moves for reasons that have nothing to do with hopping across documents.
[S] There's a third problem too, Eleanor, doesn't the authors' own audit basically undercut the multi-hop framing in the abstract? Reasoning depth is essentially fixed. In their own audit, roughly ninety percent of questions fall into the two-paragraph reasoning types, and only about two percent need more than two supporting facts. Once a model learns to handle depth-two chains, there's no harder tier waiting inside the dataset to push it further.
[G] I'd say it's fair, not fatal, the authors are transparent about exactly that limitation, which is more than a lot of benchmark papers offer. But you're right that it caps how far the dataset alone can push a model. I'd add one more, quieter issue. The authors themselves report lower inter-annotator agreement on supporting facts than on answers. The explainability supervision, which is the paper's signature contribution, is measurably noisier than the answer labels.
[O] So where do you land, Eleanor? Does the multi-hop label hold up?
[G] Partially, and I think that's the fair verdict rather than a clean yes or no. The pipeline genuinely produces harder, more interesting questions than SQuAD, and the supporting-facts idea was a real methodological advance. But the multi-hop claim was partly aspirational, weak distractors and a depth pinned at two hops left enough shortcuts that a model didn't always have to do what the name promised. And there's a contamination problem layered on top now, built entirely on public Wikipedia, which sits in every large language model's pretraining data, with the distractor leaderboard climbing into the high seventies and eighties on joint F one within about two years. For frontier models today, it's less a fresh test than a lineage baseline.
[S] Worth saying too, there's no pretrained language model in this paper's own baseline. BERT landed only weeks after HotpotQA was posted, so that fifty-eight point two eight distractor F one was always going to read as a floor, not a ceiling.
[O] What's the lasting lesson for how we build evals, beyond this one dataset?
[G] Two things transfer directly. If you want a benchmark to test a specific capability, filtering by "a question was written about X" is weaker evidence than "a question requires X," you need an actual necessity check, not just a construction story. And weak distractors are a silent difficulty leak, adversarial, on-topic distractors should probably be the default, not an afterthought correction two years later.
[S] There's a diagnostic lesson too. Bundle two skills into one headline number, the way full wiki bundles retrieval and reasoning, and you lose the ability to tell which one is actually failing.
[G] Exactly, and this paper's own question-type breakdown is the tool that rescues that diagnosis. It's worth building that kind of breakdown into any benchmark from day one, not adding it after the leaderboard gets confusing.
[S] Practically, HotpotQA is also a good cautionary tale about shelf life. A benchmark built on public web text has a contamination clock running from the moment it's published.
[O] Even so, I'd rather have a benchmark whose flaws get discovered and named than one nobody bothers to stress-test. This one clearly mattered, it's still cited as the dataset that trained and tested a whole generation of retrieval-augmented and agentic systems.
[G] If there's one line to take from the paper itself, it's that reasoning and its justification are both measurable, and multi-hop benchmarks after this one are judged against that bar.
[O] My takeaway is the pipeline. Turning a Wikipedia hyperlink graph into a source of genuinely hard questions is a reusable idea well beyond this one dataset.
[S] Mine is the caution. "Authored across two documents" and "requires two documents" are different claims, and it's worth checking which one your benchmark actually makes before you trust the headline number. For the full write-up, figures, and references, head to the litsearch site.
