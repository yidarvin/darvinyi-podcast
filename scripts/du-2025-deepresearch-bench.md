[G] Thanks for having me. This paper is trying to answer a question that's been hanging over the entire deep research agent category since OpenAI and Google both shipped one in early twenty twenty-five: how do you actually know if one of these things is any good?
[S] Start there, Oriane. What is a deep research agent, for anyone who hasn't used one?
[G] It's an agent that takes a research request, autonomously runs many rounds of web search and retrieval, and comes back with a long, cited, analyst-style report instead of a short answer. OpenAI Deep Research, Gemini Deep Research, Perplexity Deep Research, Grok Deeper Search — by the middle of twenty twenty-five the authors call these some of the most widely deployed agents built on language models.
[O] Which is exactly why grading them matters. People are trusting these reports to compress hours of desk research into minutes.
[G] Right, and grading them is genuinely hard for two separate reasons the paper is explicit about. First, the agent's internal reasoning and retrieval are opaque, so the only thing you can actually score is the final report. Second, for a real research question there's no single correct answer to check against — a good report can take many valid shapes and run to thousands of words.
[S] So you can't just build a short-answer test and call it a day.
[G] You can't, and the paper argues existing benchmarks miss in two opposite directions. Browsing and retrieval evals like BrowseComp or GAIA reward short, verifiable factual answers, never the long synthesis a deep research task demands. Long-form generation benchmarks test writing quality but are disconnected from live information retrieval. Nothing tested the actual end-to-end task.
[O] And the few efforts that did try to grade long reports leaned on a judge model with no real structure and no check against what a human would actually say.
[G] Exactly — that's the gap this paper is filling: a benchmark grounded in real demand, plus two evaluation methods the authors go out of their way to validate against human judgment.
[S] Let's get concrete. Where do the hundred tasks actually come from?
[G] The authors start from real usage: about ninety-six thousand raw user queries pulled from a web-search-enabled chatbot. A model called DeepSeek-V3 filters that down to forty-four thousand and nineteen queries that actually fit their definition of a deep research task — multiple rounds of search, synthesis, a real report at the end.
[O] And then they don't just pick a hundred tasks at random.
[G] No. They classify all forty-four thousand queries into twenty-two topic domains, borrowed from a taxonomy called WebOrganizer, and use that distribution to decide how many benchmark tasks each domain deserves. Science and technology, finance, and software development dominate real demand, so they dominate the benchmark too. Domain experts — PhDs or senior practitioners — write and refine candidate tasks, and the team screens everything for quality and difficulty. That's how you land on one hundred tasks, fifty in English and fifty in Chinese.
[S] So the benchmark's shape is empirically derived, not just what seemed like a good idea to the authors.
[G] That's the intent, yes. It's meant to mirror what people actually ask a deep research agent to do, not what's convenient to grade.
[O] Now the part I actually want to dig into — how do you score a report that can run thousands of words with no answer key?
[G] Two frameworks, doing two different jobs. RACE grades the writing itself. FACT grades the citations. Start with RACE — Reference-based Adaptive Criteria-driven Evaluation with Dynamic Weighting. It's built to fix two known failure modes of naive LLM-as-judge scoring.
[S] Which failure modes?
[G] First, a static one-size-fits-all rubric doesn't adapt to wildly different tasks — grading a literature review and grading a quick brief with the same checklist doesn't make sense. Second, when you ask a judge model to score a report in isolation, scores cluster uselessly high, and everything looks like an A-minus. RACE's fix for the first problem: the judge derives its own criteria per task, constrained to four fixed dimensions the authors define up front — Comprehensiveness, Insight and Depth, Instruction-Following, and Readability. For each task, the judge runs multiple trials to settle on how much weight each dimension deserves, then writes a set of specific, weighted criteria inside each one.
[O] So a "give me a market overview" task and a "give me a rigorous technical comparison" task end up graded on different terms, even inside the same four buckets.
[G] Exactly that. And the second problem, scores clustering high, is solved by never scoring a report alone. RACE always scores the target report and a strong reference report side by side, against the same criteria list, then reports the target's score as a fraction of the combined total. A report exactly as good as the reference lands at zero point five — roughly fifty on their zero-to-hundred scale.
[S] Whose reference report is that?
[G] Reports generated by Gemini two point five Pro's own Deep Research product, collected back in April of twenty twenty-five. We'll come back to why that choice matters.
[O] And the judge doing the scoring?
[G] Gemini two point five Pro, for RACE specifically.
[S] Walk me through FACT, because that's the one I trust more on principle — it's checking something falsifiable.
[G] FACT stands for Factual Abundance and Citation Trustworthiness. A judge model — the cheaper Gemini two point five Flash this time — pulls every statement-and-source-URL pair out of a report, collapses duplicates citing the same source for the same fact, then fetches each cited page's text using the Jina Reader API. For every unique pair, the judge reads the fetched page and issues a binary verdict: does it actually support the claim, or not. Two numbers fall out — Citation Accuracy, the fraction of citations that check out, and Average Effective Citations, how many verifiably-supported claims a report packs in.
[O] That's a real verification loop, not just "did you cite something." I like that a lot.
[S] I like it too, in principle. I've got questions about how well it holds up once you look closely — we'll get there.

[O] Retrieves the most versus cites the most reliably — that's a genuinely useful split to know about a tool before you trust its bibliography.
[S] Now the number I actually care about most: does any of this track what a human would say?

[O] Zoom out for a second, Oriane — why should anyone outside this specific leaderboard care?
[G] Because the problems this paper is wrestling with are the general problems of grading any agent that produces long, open-ended, cited output, which is where a lot of agentic tooling is headed. Reference-based relative scoring, and checking claims against fetched sources instead of trusting citations at face value, both generalize well past deep research.
[S] Which is exactly why the judge-circularity issue matters beyond this one paper.
