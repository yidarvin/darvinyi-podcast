[O] Set the scene for us, Cecily. What was actually broken about NLG evaluation before this paper showed up?
[G] Text generation had gotten good enough that outputs were often hard to tell apart from human writing, but the way people scored it hadn't caught up. The workhorse metrics, BLEU, ROUGE, METEOR, all measure overlap with a reference, how many of the same words or phrases show up in the candidate and the reference. The authors cite a survey finding that more than sixty percent of recent NLG papers relied on nothing but ROUGE or BLEU to evaluate their systems.
[S] And overlap breaks down exactly when you want it least, on open-ended, creative generation, where two genuinely good answers can share almost no words.
[G] Right, and it presumes you have a reference at all, which is expensive to collect for every new task. So the field's next move was to skip references entirely and just ask a large language model to score the text directly. GPTScore is the example the authors build against, it treats the model's own generation probability of the candidate text as a quality signal, high probability meaning fluent and good.
[O] So did that work?
[G] Not well enough. When meta-evaluated against human ratings, GPTScore and similar LLM-based evaluators still trailed a much smaller trained model called UniEval, roughly a seven hundred seventy million parameter T5 fine-tuned to answer evaluation questions in a QA format. So you had a reference-free approach that was convenient but not yet good, and nobody had systematically studied why.
[S] So G-Eval's bet is what, that the LLM itself wasn't the problem?
[G] Exactly that. The authors' wager is that the gap was in how you ask the model, not in the model's capability. Give it an explicit task definition, let it write its own evaluation rubric, structure the output as a form, and read a continuous score off the model's own token probabilities instead of taking one integer at face value.
[O] Walk us through it properly, Cecily, because there are three real pieces here.
[G] There are exactly three, and they build on each other. First, a prompt: a natural language task description plus the evaluation criterion. For coherence in summarization, they paste in the DUC quality definition, that a coherent summary should build from sentence to sentence to a coherent body of information, not just be a heap of related facts. Second, an auto chain-of-thought. Feed the model only the task intro and the criteria, nothing else, and ask it to generate its own Evaluation Steps. For coherence you get something like: read the article, identify the main points, check whether the summary covers them in a clear order, then assign a score. The model writes its own rubric before it ever sees a summary to grade.
[S] Why does that matter, versus just handing the model a rubric a human wrote?
[G] Partly practicality, hand-writing detailed steps for every criterion on every task doesn't scale, and partly that the self-generated steps give the model explicit reasoning scaffolding before it commits to a number, the same intuition behind chain-of-thought prompting generally.
[O] And the third piece?
[G] Form-filling scoring. This is the direct contrast with GPTScore. Instead of backing a score out of the probability that the model would generate the target text, G-Eval concatenates the prompt, the auto chain-of-thought, the source article, and the summary, then asks the model to fill in an evaluation form with a score, full stop. Scoring becomes the task itself, not a side effect of generation.
[S] Okay, but just asking a model to output a number one to five is exactly what everyone else already does. What's actually new in the scoring itself?
[G] Two problems the authors noticed and built around. First, naive scoring collapses, one digit, often the middle one like a three on a one to five scale, tends to dominate the distribution, which kills variance and correlation with humans. Second, even when you explicitly ask the model for a decimal, it mostly just emits integers, which creates a lot of ties that erase real quality differences between two summaries. Their fix is probability-weighted scoring, for a predefined set of scores, read the model's probability of emitting each one, and take the weighted sum as the final score, an expectation rather than a single sampled integer.
[O] So instead of "this summary is a three," you get something like two point five nine, which is literally the worked example in their own figure.
[G] Exactly that number, actually, two point five nine is the value in Figure one of the paper.
[S] Here's my first real pushback. How do you even get token probabilities out of GPT-4? OpenAI doesn't expose full log probabilities the way it does on the older completion models.
[G] That's the wrinkle, and it's worth dwelling on. GPT-3.5, specifically the Davinci variant, run at temperature zero, exposes output token probabilities directly, so for G-Eval-3.5 the formula is computed exactly as written. GPT-4 doesn't support that, so for G-Eval-4 the authors sample the model twenty times at a temperature of one and take the empirical frequency of each score as their probability estimate.
[O] Does twenty samples even catch the tail of that distribution?
[G] Not reliably. A score with a true probability around five percent is basically invisible across only twenty draws, you'd expect to see it once if you're lucky. So G-Eval-4 is a coarse estimate standing in for the exact distribution the equation assumes, and it's being compared against a temperature-zero exact readout on the GPT-3.5 side. Two different decoding regimes, worth remembering once we get to the numbers.
[O] Let's get to the numbers, then. What's the actual headline result?
[G] Following the same evaluation protocol as prior work, the authors meta-evaluate on three benchmarks across two tasks. SummEval, for summarization, with four human-rated aspects, coherence, consistency, fluency, relevance, built on CNN slash DailyMail articles. Topical-Chat, knowledge-grounded dialogue, rated on naturalness, coherence, engagingness, and groundedness. And QAGS, specifically about hallucination, factual consistency between a summary and its source, split into a more extractive CNN subset and a more abstractive XSum subset.
[S] Start with SummEval, since that's the one everyone quotes.
[G] G-Eval-4 reaches an average Spearman correlation of point five one four, ahead of the trained state of the art, UniEval, at point four seven four, ahead of GPTScore at point four one seven, and far ahead of plain overlap metrics, ROUGE-1 sits at only point one nine two. The wins are largest on the more semantic aspects, coherence at point five eight two, relevance at point five four seven.
[S] Point five one four versus point four seven four is four hundredths of a Spearman. The abstract says "by a large margin." Is that fair, or is that phrase doing a lot of work for a jump from a seven hundred seventy million parameter model to a vastly larger closed API model?
[G] Fair to push on. On SummEval overall, the margin really is modest, four hundredths. Where it's genuinely large is task-specific, on the CNN subset of QAGS, UniEval's point six six two Spearman sits within a whisker of G-Eval-4's point six eight five. But on the abstractive XSum subset, G-Eval-4 posts point five three seven against UniEval's point four eight eight, and G-Eval-3.5 basically falls apart there at point four zero six.
[O] So the real win is concentrated exactly where the small trained evaluator was weakest, abstractive hallucination detection.
[G] Precisely, and the QAGS average reflects that, point six one one for G-Eval-4 versus point five seven five for UniEval, with the gap almost entirely carried by that one abstractive split.
[S] What about the dialogue benchmark, Topical-Chat?
[G] Nearly a tie between the two backbone sizes. G-Eval-4 averages point five seven five on Pearson and point five eight eight on Spearman, G-Eval-3.5 lands at point five seven four and point five eight five, essentially the same. The authors themselves read this as evidence the benchmark is comparatively easy, model size just doesn't matter much there.
[O] So it's really the hard consistency-checking task where GPT-4's extra capacity is earning its keep, not dialogue quality broadly.
[G] That's the pattern across the whole paper. Model size matters most on consistency and relevance, the semantically hardest dimensions, and matters least on things closer to surface fluency or on an easier benchmark.
[S] What did the two clever bits, the auto chain-of-thought and the probability weighting, actually buy on their own?
[G] Less than you'd guess from how often they get cited. Remove the chain-of-thought step and SummEval's average Spearman drops from point five one four to point five zero zero, fluency takes the hardest hit, point four five five down to point four zero three. Remove probability weighting and it drops to point five zero two. There's a wrinkle worth flagging too, the no-probability version actually scores a higher Kendall-Tau, point four four six versus point four one eight, but that's an artifact, integer scoring produces a lot of ties, and ties don't count as concordant or discordant in that particular statistic, so it looks better than it is. The paper's own table italicizes that row for exactly that reason.
[O] So if the two prompting tricks are each worth roughly a point or a point and a half of Spearman, where's the real signal coming from?
[G] Almost entirely from swapping GPT-3.5 for GPT-4 as the backbone, on the hard aspects specifically. That's exactly the failure mode you'd predict from a judge grading a system that might share its own concept of what good text looks like.

[G] The recipe outlived the specific numbers.
