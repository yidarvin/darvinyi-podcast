[O] Quick puzzle: a language model with six point seven billion parameters, a fraction of the size of GPT-3, ends up beating GPT-3 on exactly the tasks language models are famously bad at. No new architecture, no giant training run.
[S] And the trick is almost embarrassingly simple: the model teaches itself when to press a button. Except "simple" carries a footnote you have to read twice to fully believe. A tool call gets kept only if it lowers the model's own prediction loss. Not if the answer is correct. If the loss goes down.
[O] Which sounds like a technicality until you realize it's the entire method. That's Toolformer, and there's a lot to unpack about what "the model teaches itself" actually means in practice.
[S] Including whether "beats GPT-3" survives past two of the three task families in this paper. I don't think it fully does, and I want to make that case today.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a visiting scholar take apart one paper from the litsearch site. Today's paper is Toolformer, Language Models Can Teach Themselves to Use Tools, by Timo Schick, Jane Dwivedi-Yu, Roberto Dessì, Roberta Raileanu, Maria Lomeli, Luke Zettlemoyer, Nicola Cancedda, and Thomas Scialom at Meta AI Research, posted in February of twenty twenty-three and later published at NeurIPS.
[S] Joining us is Verity, who has spent real time in this paper's appendix, including the parts most people skip. Verity, welcome.
[G] Glad to be here. And since I'm not one of the authors, when I say "the authors show" or "they find," I mean exactly that.
[O] Verity, give us the one-sentence version before we go deep.
[G] A pretrained language model annotates its own training text with candidate tool calls, a calculator here, a search query there, actually executes them, and keeps only the ones that make its own next-word predictions measurably easier. Then it's fine-tuned on that self-curated mix. No human ever labels where a tool call belongs.
[S] Why did this need solving in the first place? Language models already do a lot on their own.
[G] Because the failures are structural, not a scale problem. A model fluent enough to write an essay still can't reliably divide two large numbers, doesn't know what happened after its training cutoff, hallucinates facts, and struggles with low-resource languages. Those aren't bugs that more parameters obviously fix.
[O] So the fix is: let it use a calculator, like a person would.
[G] Right, and by early twenty twenty-three there were two ways people had tried this. One camp, WebGPT and LaMDA-style dialogue systems, trained tool use from heavy human demonstration, people showing the model when and what to search. Expensive, and biased toward what an annotator thinks useful rather than what the model finds useful.
[S] And the other camp?
[G] Few-shot prompting for one specific task at a time, in the style of PAL or ReAct. You already know in advance which tool solves which task, so you hand-write a demonstration for that task. It works, but it doesn't generalize. Nothing in the model itself learns a policy for when a tool is worth reaching for.
[O] So the gap is a model that decides for itself, across arbitrary text, which of several tools to use, when, and with what arguments, without a human ever pointing at the right spot.
[G] That's exactly the target the authors state up front, and it's also exactly the axis Toolformer is built to test.
[S] Walk us through the actual mechanism, because "the model teaches itself" is doing a lot of work in that sentence.
[G] It's a three-step pipeline over a plain-text corpus, a subset of a web-text dataset called CCNet, the kind of data the base model was trained on. Step one is sampling. For each tool, the authors write a short prompt with a handful of demonstrations, as few as five or six examples showing how to call, say, a question-answering API. They feed that prompt plus a passage to the model and ask, at every position, how likely a tool call there would help. Positions above a threshold get kept, and at each one the model samples several candidate calls.
[O] So the model is essentially guessing where a tool call belongs.
[G] Guessing with real probabilities, the same in-context ability that powers few-shot prompting, just repurposed to propose where a call belongs. Step two is execution: every candidate call actually gets run. A calculator call really divides two numbers. A search call really queries a retriever. The result is a real string, not a guess.
[S] And step three is the filter, which is the part I actually want to argue about.
[G] Step three is the heart of the paper. For a candidate call and its result, the authors compute the model's weighted loss over the tokens that come right after, basically, how surprised the model is by what actually comes next, if you give it the tool's answer as context. They compare that to the better of two alternatives: the loss with no tool call at all, and the loss if the model sees the call but not the result. If giving the model the answer drops the loss by more than a threshold, the call survives. Otherwise it's discarded.
[O] So it's not "was the calculator right." It's "did knowing the answer make the following text easier to predict."
[G] Precisely, and that distinction matters more than it sounds. The paper's own limitations section is blunt about how selective this is: processing more than a million documents nets only a few thousand useful calculator examples. Most candidates get thrown away.
[S] What tools did they actually wire up?
[G] Five. A question-answering system called Atlas, itself a retrieval-augmented model fine-tuned on Natural Questions. A Wikipedia search engine using a classic retrieval algorithm called BM25. A calculator, a Python script doing the four basic operations, rounded to two decimal places. A machine translation model called NLLB, six hundred million parameters, covering two hundred languages into English, with the source language auto-detected. And a calendar tool that takes no input and just returns today's date.
[O] And once you've filtered, you just fine-tune on the survivors?
[G] Yes. The augmented dataset is the exact same original text as the unmodified corpus, plus these self-vetted calls spliced in at the positions that earned their place. Fine-tuning on it exposes the model to everything it would have seen anyway, plus a light seasoning of tool calls exactly where they help. At inference, the model decodes normally, and when it emits the special token that signals "I want a tool's answer here," decoding pauses, the real API gets called, the result gets spliced in, and generation continues.
[S] One operational detail caught my eye: they cap it at one API call per input.
[G] Right, and they're explicit about why. Without that cap, the model can get stuck in a loop calling APIs without ever producing real output.
[O] Let's get to the number that opened the episode.
[G] The base model throughout is GPT-J, six point seven billion parameters, compared against plain GPT-J, GPT-J fine-tuned on the same corpus without any tool calls, and "Toolformer disabled," the trained model with tool calls switched off at decoding time, so you can see how much of the gain is from fine-tuning itself versus from actually calling tools.
[S] And the marquee comparison is against much bigger models.
[G] Against OPT at sixty-six billion parameters and GPT-3 at one hundred seventy-five billion, roughly ten and twenty-five times larger. On LAMA, a factual-completion benchmark split into three subsets, SQuAD, Google-RE, and T-REx, Toolformer scores thirty-three point eight, eleven point five, and fifty-three point five. That's an improvement of eleven point seven, five point two, and eighteen point six points over the best same-size baseline, and it clears GPT-3's twenty-six point eight, seven point oh, and thirty-nine point eight. It calls the question-answering tool on ninety-eight point one percent of examples.
[O] That's the headline moment, a model twenty-five times smaller beating GPT-3 outright.
[S] On factual recall, with search disabled for fairness, since LAMA is built from Wikipedia and the model's own search tool would otherwise have an unfair edge. Keep going.
[G] Math tells a similar story. On three word-problem benchmarks, AS-Div, SVAMP, and MAWPS, Toolformer hits forty point four, twenty-nine point four, and forty-four point oh, more than double every same-size baseline, against GPT-3's fourteen, ten, and nineteen point eight. The calculator gets used on ninety-seven point nine percent of examples. But then on question answering, Web Questions, Natural Questions, and TriviaQA, Toolformer reaches twenty-six point three, seventeen point seven, and forty-eight point eight, ahead of same-size baselines but still trailing GPT-3's twenty-nine, twenty-two point six, and sixty-five point nine.
[S] So "beats GPT-3" is true for two of the three task families and false for the third. That's the asterisk I mentioned in the cold open.
[G] That's in the paper. They attribute the QA shortfall to the search tool itself being simple and non-interactive, plus Toolformer's inability to reformulate a query or browse past the first result. The temporal results are genuinely mixed too. On a synthetic dataset the authors built themselves, playfully named DateSet, Toolformer jumps to twenty-seven point three by calling the calendar on fifty-four point eight percent of examples. But on a second temporal benchmark, TempLAMA, the calendar is used on just zero point two percent of examples. The gain there actually comes from the search and question-answering tools, not the calendar the task was ostensibly built to test.
[O] And on the multilingual benchmark?
[G] Mixed to weak. On MLQA, using the translation tool helps versus not using tools at all, but fine-tuning on the web corpus hurts several languages enough that Toolformer doesn't consistently beat plain GPT-J. Critically, the paper checks that none of this costs the model its core ability. Perplexity on two held-out language-modeling sets is essentially identical whether or not the model was trained with tool calls, so it isn't trading fluency for tool skill.
[O] What about the scaling result? I remember that being one of the more interesting figures.
[G] That might be the paper's most conceptually interesting finding. They repeat a stripped-down version of the pipeline on four smaller models from the GPT-2 family, one hundred twenty-four million up to one point six billion parameters, plus the six point seven billion parameter model. Below roughly seven hundred seventy-five million parameters, having tool results available doesn't help at all, performance with and without calls is basically the same. Above that size, a gap opens and keeps widening as the model grows. Using a tool well is itself an emergent capability, not something a model gets for free just by having access to one.
[O] Let me make the strongest optimist case, because I think it's underrated. This is the first paper to show a model discovering, entirely on its own, when and how to use each of five different tools, no per-task prompt engineering, no human pointing at the calculator. And the scaling curve says it should only get better as base models grow, since the ability to exploit a tool result scales with the model. That's the conceptual seed of basically every tool-calling agent that came after it.
[S] I'll grant the historical importance, genuinely. My problem is what the filter is actually optimizing. It keeps a call if it lowers the model's own next-token loss, not if the call is correct. The paper's own qualitative table is damning on this point. They show a Wikipedia search call, on a passage about a song called "Fast Train," that returns text with no genuinely relevant information, and it still passes the filter with a positive score, because whatever it returned happened to make the following words a bit more predictable.
[G] That example is real, and the authors show it specifically to illustrate the limit. But there's a nuance right next to it: they also note that some of this noise can help, because it stops the fine-tuned model from blindly trusting every API result the way it might under a purely correctness-based signal.
[S] That's a fair complication, and it moves me a little. But it doesn't erase the core issue. This is a perplexity-reduction filter wearing a correctness costume, and the "beats GPT-3" framing leans on the correctness reading.
[O] Counter, though: on the tasks where they actually measured it, ninety-seven, ninety-eight percent tool-usage rates on math and LAMA are a real behavioral signal. The model isn't randomly calling tools and getting lucky, it's reliably reaching for the right tool for the right job.
[G] There's a nice piece of evidence for that in the decoding ablation. At the strictest decoding setting, call an API only if it's literally the single most likely next token, the model still calls on around forty percent of the T-REx examples, and shows a real calibration effect. On the examples where it chooses not to call a tool, its own no-tool performance there is already better than the dataset average. It's implicitly recognizing "I don't need help here" before you even loosen the decoding threshold.
[S] That's a genuinely good result, I'll score a point to it. But I still want to flag the baseline framing. GPT-3 and OPT here are the plain, non-instruction-tuned versions, evaluated zero-shot, on a slate of tasks the five tools were specifically chosen to cover. On MLQA, both larger models mostly fail by not even answering in English when instructed to, and the paper's own upper-bound check, giving GPT-3 the question in English, shows it pulling ahead of everything once that handicap is removed.
[G] Also worth being precise about: the question-answering tool, Atlas, was itself fine-tuned on Natural Questions, so the authors disable it specifically for that evaluation to avoid trivializing the benchmark. That's handled well, but it's a reminder that "zero human supervision" applies to where calls get inserted, not to the tools themselves — several were built with real supervision elsewhere.
[O] Fine, I'll take that adjustment. The headline is "self-supervised placement of tool calls," not "self-supervised tools." That's a real distinction and it deserves to survive the episode intact.
[S] Where does this actually go, given the limitations section basically hands us the sequel's outline?
[G] The authors are candid about three gaps. No chaining: a calendar result can't feed a question-answering call, because each tool's calls are sampled and filtered independently, so the fine-tuning data never has one call depending on another's output. That's structurally why TempLAMA doesn't benefit from the calendar tool even though it obviously should. No interactivity: the model can't reformulate a search query or browse past the first result. And it's extremely sample-inefficient, with no accounting for tool-dependent compute cost.
[O] Which reads less like a dead end and more like a to-do list that the next several years of agent research just worked straight through.
[G] That's fair, if slightly generous. It's a twenty twenty-three prototype for a single-call, non-interactive form of tool use, but the filtering idea, keep what helps prediction, discard what doesn't, is the piece that persisted even as chaining and interactivity got added by later work this paper doesn't cover. And if I had to leave you with the paper's own one-line claim, it's this: a language model can supervise its own tool use by keeping only the calls that make its own predictions easier, and that alone made a six point seven billion parameter model competitive with, though not uniformly better than, a model twenty-five times its size.
[O] My takeaway is the emergent scaling curve. Tool use getting better as models grow, with no architecture change, is a trend line worth watching well beyond this paper.
