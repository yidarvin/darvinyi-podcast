[O] Maya, set the scene for us. Why did biomedical question answering need its own dataset in twenty nineteen?
[G] Because the existing options were bad in complementary ways. BioASQ was the largest expert-annotated biomedical QA set at the time, and it still had under three thousand training instances, most of them simple factoid lookups. There were larger automatically-built sets too, emrQA and BioRead among them, but their questions were mostly extractive, the answer sits as a contiguous span you can lift straight out of the passage, with little inference required.
[S] So you had a choice between small and expert, or big and shallow.
[G] Right. And the authors' insight was that PubMed itself already contains the structure they needed. Clinical research articles routinely phrase their own title as a question, report quantitative results in the body, and then commit to a conclusion that answers it.
[O] That's a clever thing to notice, that scientists have basically been hand-writing question-answer pairs for decades without meaning to.
[G] Exactly, and the scale backs it up. The authors found that more than half of roughly seven hundred sixty thousand question-titled PubMed articles can be briefly answered with yes, no, or maybe. Compare that to just one percent of Natural Questions and six percent of HotpotQA. Biomedical writing is unusually well-suited to this format.
[S] But not every one of those seven hundred sixty thousand articles is usable, right? You need the internal structure too.
[G] Correct, you need a structured abstract, one with labeled sections like objective, methods, results, and conclusion, so the conclusion can be cleanly split off. That requirement cuts the pool down to roughly one hundred twenty thousand articles, which is the source for everything that follows.
[O] Walk us through what one instance of PubMedQA actually looks like.
[G] The example in the paper's own Figure 1 is a good one. The question is a real article title, "Do preoperative statins reduce atrial fibrillation after coronary artery bypass grafting?" The context is that paper's structured abstract with the conclusion stripped out, so you get the objective, the methods, and the results, including the actual numbers, postoperative atrial fibrillation was sixteen percent in the statin group versus thirty-three percent in the non-statin group. The long answer is the withheld conclusion sentence. And the yes-no-maybe label, yes in this case, is a human annotator's summary of what that conclusion says.
[S] So the model never sees the conclusion sentence itself at test time, only the evidence that's supposed to justify it.
[G] Right, that's the whole point of the design. Because the question and the eventual answer were both written by the same authors describing the same experiment, the context is guaranteed to actually address the question, which crowd-sourced QA datasets can't promise, since there a worker writes a question for a passage somebody else wrote.
[O] Now, the dataset isn't one uniform pile, it's three subsets with pretty different reliability. Break those down for us.
[G] PQA-Labeled, or PQA-L, is the crown jewel, one thousand instances, expert-annotated by two qualified M.D. candidates. Annotator one gets to see the long answer, the withheld conclusion, so their label is close to free, what the paper calls the reasoning-free setting. Annotator two only sees the question and the context, no conclusion, and has to actually infer the answer, the reasoning-required setting. When they agree, that's the label. When they disagree, they discuss it, and if they still can't agree, the instance is dropped entirely.
[S] And that reasoning-required annotator is the one whose numbers become the human baseline everyone compares against.
[G] Exactly. Five hundred of those thousand instances support ten-fold cross-validation, and the other five hundred form the held-out test set. Then there's PQA-Unlabeled, sixty-one thousand two hundred instances, everything else from the same yes-no-maybe-answerable pool with no manual label at all, meant for semi-supervised bootstrapping. And PQA-Artificial, two hundred eleven thousand three hundred instances, which has no human annotation whatsoever.
[O] How do you generate two hundred thousand labeled questions with zero human labeling?
[G] Pure syntax and negation. The authors take PubMed articles whose titles are declarative statements rather than questions, matching a specific part-of-speech pattern, a noun phrase followed by a verb. They convert the statement into a question by moving or adding a copula or auxiliary verb to the front, "is," "are," "does," "do," and they generate the yes-or-no label purely from whether that verb was negated in the original title.
[S] That sounds like a heuristic that could go wrong in a lot of subtle ways.
[G] It's genuinely crude, and the label distribution shows it, ninety-two point eight percent yes versus seven point two percent no, and no "maybe" category at all, since the rule can't express uncertainty. But it's useful precisely because it's huge, and the authors treat it as pre-training fuel, not as ground truth.
[O] And the baseline model itself, what's actually being trained on all this?
[G] BioBERT, which is BERT further pre-trained on PubMed abstracts and PMC full-text articles. The authors fine-tune it in three phases rather than throwing everything at it at once. Phase One fine-tunes on PQA-Artificial directly. Phase Two is the clever part, they fine-tune a separate copy of BioBERT on PQA-Artificial and then on PQA-Labeled, both under the easy reasoning-free setting, question plus long answer as input, to build a confident pseudo-labeler. That pseudo-labeler labels a class-balanced chunk of PQA-Unlabeled, and a second model gets fine-tuned on those pseudo-labels, this time under the real reasoning-required setting, question plus context, no long answer. The Final Phase takes the Phase-One model, continues it through that pseudo-labeled data, and finishes by fine-tuning on the real PQA-Labeled labels.
[S] So every subset contributes something, even though only the labeled set is actually trustworthy.
[G] That's the design philosophy exactly. On top of that, there's an auxiliary loss, the model also has to predict, from the same classification embedding, which vocabulary words appear in the long answer, as a binary bag-of-words target. The total loss is the classification loss plus a weighted bag-of-words loss. It's a cheap extra signal that doesn't require any new labels, since the long answer is available at training time even though it's hidden at test time.
