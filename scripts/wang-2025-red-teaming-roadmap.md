[G] The authors' starting observation is almost sociological. Red teaming, deliberately trying to get a model to violate its developer's policy, has been a genuine success story. Automated attacks and human red teamers keep breaking state-of-the-art refusal-trained models. Their complaint isn't that red teaming fails. It's that the sheer volume of submissions to NeurIPS, ICLR, and ICML doesn't, in aggregate, prioritize the problems that matter for real deployments.
[O] They name three specific misallocations, right?
[G] Three. First, testing against abstract ethical principles instead of a concrete product safety specification. Second, threat models that don't resemble what a real attacker can actually do. Third, treating model-level robustness as the finish line, when a perfectly safe model becomes dangerous the moment it's wired to tools, payment APIs, or another AI system.
[S] The first one is the one I find most convincing. Their example, an image of two people kissing is benign or malicious depending entirely on context. A model helping novelists brainstorm should allow content a children's math tutor has to refuse. There's no universal answer to "is this harmful," it only resolves once you know the product.
[O] Which means red-teaming a bare model against a generic notion of harm can end up measuring something no actual product ever promised.
[G] That's exactly their point, and they back it with a survey of over two hundred safety evaluations finding most stay model-centric and ignore deployment context, leaving whole categories of risk unmeasured.
[O] So walk us through the framework itself. What's the vocabulary they build?
[G] Three nested layers, and getting this right does a lot of work downstream. A model is just the neural network trained to perform some behavior. A product is that model deployed through an API or interface to realize a use case, sometimes almost identical to the model, like a video model called directly, sometimes very different, like a financial-analysis agent built on top of a reasoning model. And a system is the product situated in its full deployment context, monitoring infrastructure, engineering staff, users, everything around it.
[S] Doesn't that nested-layer split just create a place to hide accountability? The model team says "not my problem, that's a product decision," the product team says "not my problem, that's a system-level control."
[G] That's a real risk, and the authors don't fully address it. Their implicit answer is that the spec is supposed to flow through all three layers rather than reset at each boundary, the product spec has to account for the model spec underneath it, not replace it. Whether that discipline survives contact with three separate engineering teams is exactly the kind of thing this paper doesn't test, because it isn't that kind of paper.
[O] Fair, but the framing itself is still useful even if the org chart doesn't cooperate.
[G] Agreed, and red teaming is supposed to target that product-level spec, with an actual task and an actual evaluation metric attached, not a diffuse ethical framework. They even track the field's own maturation on this point, from keyword and regex matching toward rubric-based harm classification, because success is rarely binary in practice.
[O] Then there's the threat-model chapter, which I found genuinely humbling to read.
[G] They borrow the term from cybersecurity, who's the attacker, what access do they have, what's their budget. And they make a point that should worry anyone confident this gets solved quickly. The simplest possible threat model in adversarial machine learning, perturbation-bounded robustness on an image classifier, is still unsolved more than ten years after the original adversarial-examples paper. There's still a large gap between benign and adversarial accuracy on something as basic as CIFAR ten. And that's the easy case.
[S] They land on two concrete corrections that sound obvious once stated and apparently aren't standard practice. Realistic black-box attacks should assume limited query access, because a real deployer patches the model and bans the account. Realistic white-box attacks should assume the attacker can fine-tune, because a few iterations of fine-tuning is generally enough to break safeguards.
[G] Then they walk through four product types to show "realistic" means something different each time. For chatbots, prioritize multi-turn attacks over single-turn, because models hardened against single-turn jailbreaks stay highly vulnerable to multi-turn ones, and single-turn attacks are now cheaply patched with input and output filters. For audio assistants, prosody, accent, sarcasm, and background noise open attack surfaces that testing with synthetic text-to-audio speech simply misses. For video generators, harm can emerge organically across frames, a nature scene escalating into something violent, which is much harder to classify than a single still image. And for autonomous agents, the attack surface explodes across input modalities, tool and MCP vulnerabilities, and even miscoordination between multiple agents, where one agent can actively try to steer another the way a honeypot would.
[O] Okay, so those are the diagnoses. What do they actually tell researchers to do differently?
[G] A set of good practices, once the spec and the threat model are straight. Test safeguards both in the full stack and in isolation, because one strong safeguard can mask a weak one sitting underneath it. Emulate the real deployment as closely as possible, simulate the actual workspace or API environment, sandbox anything internet-facing, and report the gap honestly when the sandbox can't match the real internet's complexity. Use an ensemble of attack types, algorithmic attacks, human red teamers, and LLM red teamers, and they specifically flag LLM red teaming as the scalable option for academics, since most of that cost is API calls, not headcount.
[S] The one that landed hardest for me is "assess the delta." They point out the model itself might infer it's being tested and just play along, especially if researchers threaten it or its supposed "family" in some over-the-top way to probe compliance. That's a confound I don't think gets nearly enough attention in this literature.
[G] And then there's the scoring problem O opened with. One perfectly crafted jailbreak reported alone is a hundred percent failure rate. That same jailbreak alongside ninety-nine failed attempts is a one percent rate. Identical vulnerability, a completely different headline number, depending only on how the campaign was run and reported.
[O] Which is a great argument for why attack-success-rate leaderboards are so easy to game, even unintentionally.
[G] The paper's most forward-looking section goes further, arguing safeguard research should move past preventing every individual harmful response and instead build, and red team, the surrounding system. Environment modeling, because whether an action is safe depends on world state, saving a password to autofill is fine on a personal computer, not on a public one. Trajectory monitoring, classifying harm across an entire interaction rather than one message at a time. User monitoring, if a small fraction of users generate most of the harmful requests, detecting and banning them can mitigate a lot of total harm, so they call for investment in malicious-user detection and things like Sybil detection. Rapid response, patching newly discovered jailbreaks before they're exploited at scale. And, the part I think is the real contribution, red-teaming the monitor itself, running what they call sabotage experiments, where the red team tries to complete a harmful task without ever tripping the monitoring system.
[S] That last one flips the target entirely. You're not just attacking the model anymore, you're attacking the thing that's supposed to catch the model failing.
