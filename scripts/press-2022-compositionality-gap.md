[S] Scale up a language model five hundred times over, from three hundred fifty million parameters to one hundred seventy five billion, and it gets dramatically better at recalling facts.
[O] But ask it to combine two facts it already knows into one answer, and that improvement almost completely disappears.
[S] Not almost, according to this paper. Completely. The gap between what a model knows and what it can compose sits at roughly forty percent no matter how big you make it.
[O] Which is either the most important negative result in the reasoning literature, or an artifact of one prompting format from 2022, and today we find out which.
[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a guest scholar take apart one paper from the litsearch dot darvinyi dot com map. Today's paper is Measuring and Narrowing the Compositionality Gap in Language Models, by Ofir Press and colleagues, including Muru Zhang, Sewon Min, Ludwig Schmidt, Noah Smith, and Mike Lewis, out of the University of Washington, MosaicML, Meta AI, and the Allen Institute for AI. It was posted in the fall of 2022 and later appeared at EMNLP Findings in 2023, and it's since picked up more than one thousand three hundred citations.
[O] Joining us is Mira, a visiting scholar who's spent a lot of time with this paper and the self-ask line of work it kicked off. Welcome, Mira.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is one of those papers that's narrow in scope, but the finding it reports has aged very well, so I'm glad to dig into it.
[S] Start with the basic setup, Mira. What exactly are they measuring?
[G] The paper asks a narrow question: when a model clearly knows two individual facts, can it combine them into the answer to a question that needs both? Take "who was president when superconductivity was discovered." The model doesn't need new knowledge, it needs the discovery year and who was president that year, and then it needs to put those two things together.
[O] And the reason that's interesting is it separates two things people usually conflate: does the model know the facts, versus can it reason with them.
[G] Exactly, and that's the whole point of the metric they define, the compositionality gap. It's the fraction of two-hop questions the model gets wrong, restricted only to the questions where it already answered both underlying sub-questions correctly when asked separately.
[S] So if the model never knew the sub-facts in the first place, that failure doesn't count against it.
[G] Right, that's deliberate. A model that never knew Frida Kahlo's birthplace was always going to miss a question built on it. The gap only counts the interesting failure: knows both pieces, still can't put them together.
[O] Which by construction should be close to zero if a model is actually doing multi-hop reasoning instead of pattern-matching on training data.
[S] How do you even build a clean test set for this? You need to know the model already possesses both facts, without the test questions themselves being memorized as pairs.
[G] That's the design problem the Compositional Celebrities dataset solves. It's about eight thousand six hundred automatically generated two-hop questions across seventeen templated categories. Each one pairs a celebrity's birth fact, place or year, with a second fact tied to that place or year: capital city, currency, calling code, even who won the Masters golf tournament that year.
[O] So each individual fact is extremely common. Frida Kahlo's birthplace is on Wikipedia. Mexico's calling code is on Wikipedia.
[G] But the specific pairing, "what's the calling code of the birthplace of Frida Kahlo," is not a sentence anyone has ever written. The authors manually spot-checked that these exact phrasings didn't turn up in web search, so the combination itself is very unlikely to be sitting in the training data as a memorized shortcut.
[S] That's the part I want to flag early, because it's the whole experiment's validity. If the pairing itself had leaked into pretraining text somewhere, you're not measuring composition anymore, you're measuring recall of an unusual sentence.
[G] Agreed, and it's one of the more careful contamination checks for a 2022 benchmark paper. There's a related risk later on where they're honest but less protected, but the core construction holds up.
[O] Okay, so that's how you measure the gap. What's the second contribution, the narrowing half of the title?
[G] That's self-ask. It's a prompting method in the spirit of chain of thought, but instead of letting the model reason in one unstructured block, the prompt scaffolds explicit sub-questions. The model outputs "Follow up:", asks itself a sub-question, outputs "Intermediate answer:", answers it, and repeats until it's ready to output "So the final answer is:".
[S] Why does that rigid formatting matter versus just letting the model think out loud, which chain of thought already does?
[G] Two reasons the authors give. First, it disentangles decomposing the question from answering the sub-questions, so the model isn't trying to do both at once in a single stream. Second, because every follow-up question is explicitly marked, the boundary between question and answer becomes mechanically parseable.
[O] And that second point is what unlocks the search engine trick.
[G] Right. Because "Follow up:" is a literal string in the output, you can intercept it: the sub-question gets routed to a real search engine API instead of the model answering it, and the returned snippet is spliced into the prompt before generation continues. Nothing about the model changes, no fine-tuning, no architecture change, not even a different prompt versus plain self-ask.
[S] That's a genuinely elegant piece of engineering, I'll give them that. You get tool use for free out of a formatting convention.
[O] It's also, and I'll say this now because it matters later, exactly the shape of thing today's reinforcement-learning search agents do end to end. Decompose, call a tool, splice the result back in, keep going.
[G] The paper is explicit that this pattern, structured decomposition plus an external call, is the mechanism doing the work. Later systems like Search-R1, R1-Searcher, and DeepResearcher automate the same loop with reinforcement learning instead of a fixed prompt template, but the shape of the loop traces back to here.
[S] Let's get to numbers. What does the gap actually look like across model sizes?
[G] On the base GPT-3 family, from Ada at three hundred fifty million parameters up to Davinci at one hundred seventy five billion, the compositionality gap sits between twenty nine and forty one percent. On the InstructGPT-3 line, the oh-oh-one, oh-oh-two, and oh-oh-three variants, it ranges from thirty two to sixty four percent. In neither family is there a consistent downward trend as size increases.
[O] And single-hop accuracy, meanwhile, keeps climbing with scale like everyone would expect.
[G] It does, and that's exactly the surprising part. The model gets better at knowing things without getting proportionally better at combining them. Overall, Davinci-oh-oh-two answers about forty five point four percent of the two-hop questions correctly. On the easiest category it reaches eighty four point six percent. On the hardest category, birth year paired with that year's literature Nobel Prize winner, it drops to just one point two percent.
[S] But it knows the underlying facts on that hard category?
[G] It answers eighty percent of the sub-questions correctly in isolation. So the model has the facts sitting right there, and still fails to combine them in the vast majority of cases on that category specifically.
[O] There's also a nice finding buried in there about confidence. When they sort sub-question pairs by how confident the model is, measured by perplexity on the correct sub-answer, the most confident bucket composes correctly eighty one point one percent of the time, and a much less confident bucket drops to forty two point six percent.
[S] So knowing a fact "well enough to say it" and knowing it "well enough to reason with it" aren't the same threshold.
[G] That's their reading too, and it's a genuinely useful methodological point beyond this one paper: accuracy alone hides how confidently a fact is stored, and that confidence predicts downstream composability.
[O] Now the narrowing side. How much does self-ask actually help?
[G] On their hand-built dataset, Bamboogle, self-ask reaches fifty seven point six percent versus forty six point four for chain of thought, an eleven point absolute jump. On the two more templated datasets, a Wikipedia-based multi-hop set called Two-Wiki, and one called Musique, the gain over chain of thought is much smaller: thirty versus twenty nine point eight, and thirteen point eight versus twelve point six.
[S] Why would the same method help so much more on one dataset than the others?
[G] The authors' explanation is that Bamboogle's questions are far more varied and dissimilar to the few-shot examples in the prompt. Chain of thought's unstructured reasoning has more room to go off the rails on genuinely novel questions, where self-ask's rigid decomposition holds together better.
[O] And adding the search engine on top of self-ask helps further across the board.
[G] Everywhere, from one point four points up to ten point one points, with the biggest single jump on Two-Wiki, from thirty to forty point one. Worth noting: a plain search engine with no language model at all is nearly useless here, zero percent on Bamboogle, two point two on Two-Wiki. A single query just can't resolve a two-hop compound fact by itself.
[S] So the value isn't "add a search engine," it's specifically the decomposition structure that makes the search engine useful.
[G] That's the paper's claim, yes. Two more comparisons round it out. Against a zero-shot method called "let's think step by step," from Kojima and colleagues, self-ask wins decisively on Compositional Celebrities: seventy nine point six percent versus forty five point seven with the instruct model, fifty four point two versus one point one with the base model. And against Least-to-Most prompting, which also decomposes questions but needs multiple separate forward passes, self-ask matches or slightly beats its accuracy while generating far shorter outputs, five hundred sixty nine tokens versus eight hundred forty four on one dataset, running more than thirty percent faster overall.
[O] There's also a nice practical detail about output cleanliness.
[G] On Bamboogle, forty percent of chain of thought's final answers weren't in short, parseable form, it would write a full sentence instead of a name. That number was seventeen percent for self-ask, and only three percent once the search engine was added.
[O] Okay, I want to make the strongest optimist case. This is a 2022 paper, using nothing but prompting, no fine-tuning, no new architecture, and it already shows you can close a meaningful chunk of a real reasoning failure mode with a formatting trick plus a search API call. That's an enormous amount of leverage for basically zero training cost.
[S] I'll grant the elegance. But "narrowing" is doing a lot of work in that title. Self-ask plus search still only gets sixty percent on Bamboogle, forty point one on Two-Wiki. That's a narrower gap, not a closed one. And the headline scale finding, that the gap itself is stuck around forty percent, is the part I actually care about, and it rests on exactly one model family.
[G] That's fair, and the authors say so themselves in their limitations section, which I'd flag as a real point in the paper's favor rather than a knock against it. Everything on the scale claim is GPT-3 and InstructGPT-3, nothing above one hundred seventy five billion parameters, no open-weights model where you could actually inspect the pretraining corpus instead of inferring contamination indirectly.
[O] But they did try to extend it. They report a compositionality gap of forty two point nine percent for ChatGPT and twenty three percent for GPT-4.
[S] And then immediately undercut their own number, which I respect, but it's still a problem. Since Compositional Celebrities was released publicly before GPT-4 launched, they say the GPT-4 estimate may be invalid if GPT-4 was trained on their own dataset.
[G] Right, that's a genuinely unusual level of honesty for a benchmark paper of that era, disclaiming your own headline extension in a footnote instead of quietly leaving it in the abstract. But it does mean the one data point that would have told us something about a model past one hundred seventy five billion parameters is exactly the one they can't trust.
[O] Where I push back is that the core result doesn't depend on that footnote at all. The flat gap across Ada through Davinci, and across three InstructGPT versions, is the real, clean part of the paper.
[S] Sure, within that family. My second objection is the baseline used to sell self-ask plus search as the win. The search-engine-alone baseline is snippet only, no language model reasoning over retrieved text at all. That's a weak strawman, it doesn't tell you how much of the improvement is decomposition versus just having any retrieval in the loop.
[G] That's a legitimate gap. They do compare against Least-to-Most, a stronger decomposition baseline, and self-ask holds up there on both accuracy and speed. But a retrieval baseline that feeds full passages to the model, rather than a bare snippet, isn't in the table, so the paper can't rule out that better retrieval alone would close more of the gap.
[O] And Bamboogle is a hundred twenty five questions written by the authors themselves. I like it as a stress test, but I wouldn't hang statistical weight on an eleven point gap with no confidence interval reported.
[G] The authors are candid about that trade-off, they built Bamboogle for variety over statistical power, and say so directly. It's reasonable for what the dataset is for, but the sixty percent and fifty seven point six percent numbers should be read as directionally informative, not statistically tight.
[S] So scoring it: the compositionality gap as a diagnostic, and the contamination discipline around building it, that's the paper's strongest, most durable contribution. The specific "gap doesn't shrink with scale" claim is real but narrow, one family, capped size. And "narrowing" is real progress, with a caveat about what the weakest baseline is actually isolating.
[O] I'll take that. The finding that survives every objection we just raised is: this model family, at least, memorizes faster than it learns to compose, and simply reasoning explicitly recovers a meaningful chunk of the difference without touching the weights.
[O] Zoom out for a second, Mira. Why does a 2022 prompting paper still matter now?
[G] Two reasons. First, the diagnostic itself is a template other evaluation work has reused: separate what a model can retrieve from what it can compose, rather than just reporting raw accuracy on the composed question. Second, Bamboogle became a standard small out-of-domain benchmark. Later reinforcement-learning search-agent papers, ZeroSearch among them, report on it right alongside Natural Questions, TriviaQA, and HotpotQA.
[S] It's a good reminder for evaluation practice generally: accuracy on a compound task hides two different failure modes, missing knowledge versus failing to combine known knowledge, and conflating them makes a system look worse, or better, than it is, for the wrong reason.
[O] The self-ask plus search loop is basically the ancestor of the tool-calling agent pattern everywhere now: decompose, call a tool, read the result back in, continue. This paper did it with a fixed prompt; the field spent the years after training that same loop with reinforcement learning.
[G] That lineage is fairly direct. Search-R1, R1-Searcher, DeepResearcher, they all sit downstream of this line of work, because the core insight, that structured decomposition is what makes tool integration clean, still holds once you're training the policy instead of hand-writing the prompt.
[G] If there's one sentence to take from this paper, it's that a model can know two facts perfectly well and still fail to put them together roughly forty percent of the time, and that failure doesn't go away just by making the model bigger.
[S] My one sentence: believe the flat-gap finding for GPT-3-era models specifically, and wait for someone to re-run the scale sweep on a model where you can actually check the training data before calling it a universal law.
[O] Mine is that self-ask is a small piece of engineering with an outsized legacy, a formatting trick from 2022 that quietly previews how every tool-using agent works today.
