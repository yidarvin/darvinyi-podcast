[O] Go on, ruin it for everyone.
[S] That thirty-one percent relative gain on HealthBench is measured against the weakest baseline in the whole table. Compare it to the baseline that also gets to see the gold answer, and the gap nearly disappears.
[O] Except the model still wins that comparison every time it's tried, on both a medical benchmark and a completely different science multiple-choice test it wasn't trained on. That's not a rounding error, that's a signal that generalizes.

[O] Welcome to Litsearch Audio, where an optimist, a skeptic, and a guest scholar take apart one paper from the litsearch site.
[S] Today it's "Rubrics as Rewards: Reinforcement Learning Beyond Verifiable Domains," out of Scale AI, lead author Anisha Gunjal, posted in twenty twenty-five.
[O] And walking us through it is Hannah, who's clearly spent real time in this paper's appendices. Hannah, welcome to the show.
[G] Thanks for having me. This is a fun one, because it takes an idea everybody already trusted for evaluation and asks a genuinely underexplored question: why not use it for training too.

[S] Start with the gap. Why doesn't the recipe that works for math and code just work everywhere?
[G] Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards, RLVR, is the engine behind the recent reasoning jump — DeepSeek-R1, DeepSeekMath, the whole o-series lineage. It works because in math and code the reward is free and exact. Run the test suite, check the boxed answer, return a one or a zero. No reward model, no human raters, nothing subjective to overfit.
[O] And that engine just stalls the second you leave verifiable ground.
[G] Right. Ask a model for the most likely diagnosis given a patient's blood gas numbers, and there's no single string to match against. A good answer has to be judged on accuracy, completeness, safety, whether it avoids a common misdiagnosis — several axes at once, some of them genuinely subjective.
[S] So the standard fix has been a preference-based reward model, trained on pairwise comparisons.
[G] Which the paper is fairly blunt about the problems with. They're expensive to collect at scale, and they're notorious for latching onto superficial artifacts — response length, formatting quirks, whatever idiosyncrasy the human annotators happened to reward.
[O] Meanwhile, evaluation people had quietly half-solved this already.
[G] That's the authors' actual framing, and I think it's the cleanest part of the setup. HealthBench, a benchmark released not long before this paper, grades free-form medical answers against thousands of instance-specific, physician-written rubrics — explicit checklists of what a good answer has to contain. The authors' observation is almost embarrassingly simple in hindsight: if a rubric is good enough to grade a model, why has nobody used it to train one? Rubrics had only ever been used for evaluation. This paper closes that loop.

[S] Walk us through the mechanics. How do you turn a rubric into an actual training signal?
[G] There are two halves, and there's no trained reward model anywhere in the pipeline, which is worth pausing on. First, generate a rubric per prompt. Second, use that rubric directly as the reward inside an on-policy RL loop.
[O] Start with generation.
[G] For every training prompt, a strong LLM — o3-mini for the science set, GPT-4o for the medical set — writes somewhere between seven and twenty self-contained criteria. Crucially, it conditions on the dataset's own golden reference answer as a stand-in for expert supervision. Each criterion gets a category: Essential, Important, Optional, or Pitfall. Pitfalls are phrased as things to avoid, but written positively — "avoids a common misdiagnosis" — so satisfying them still adds to the score rather than subtracting.
[S] And there are design principles behind this, not just "write some checklist items."
[G] Four desiderata, and they're sensible. The rubric should be grounded in expert guidance, it should cover multiple dimensions of quality rather than just one, it should encode that some criteria matter more than others, and — this is the one that makes automation possible — every item has to be self-contained, judgeable in isolation without extra context. A judge model reading one line shouldn't need the rest of the rubric to know what counts as satisfying it.
[O] Okay, so now you've got a checklist. How does that become a scalar number the RL algorithm can actually optimize?
[G] Two competing strategies, and this is really the paper's central experiment. Explicit aggregation asks the judge to score each criterion independently as a zero-or-one check, then takes a weighted, normalized sum. Essential criteria get a weight of one point zero, Important point seven, Optional point three, Pitfall point nine. Implicit aggregation instead hands the judge the whole rubric at once, every criterion and its category label, and asks for one holistic score from one to ten. That delegates the weighting entirely to the judge model instead of hand-tuning it.
[S] Which judge, in both cases?
[G] The cheap one, gpt-4o-mini, in every single training run. That's deliberate. Part of the pitch is that rubric structure should let a cheap judge do a job that would otherwise need an expensive one.
[O] There's a nice theoretical framing tucked into the method section too.
[G] The paper points out that RLVR is just the special case of this whole framework where you have exactly one criterion, weight one, and the zero-one check is exact-match or test execution. Rubric rewards generalize that into multi-dimensional, partly subjective supervision. It's a tidy unification, even if it's more a reframing than a new empirical result.
[S] And the actual training run — what's under the hood?
[G] Small and reproducible by design. Base policy is Qwen two point five, seven billion parameters, trained with GRPO — Group Relative Policy Optimization, the on-policy algorithm from DeepSeekMath — on a single node of eight H100s. Batch size ninety-six, sixteen sampled rollouts per prompt, temperature one. And they release two rubric datasets alongside the paper. RaR-Medicine, about twenty thousand medical reasoning prompts with rubrics from GPT-4o, and RaR-Science, roughly twenty thousand prompts aligned to GPQA-Diamond's categories, with rubrics from o3-mini.
[O] GPQA-Diamond being — remind the listener.
[G] The hardest subset of a graduate-level, multiple-choice science benchmark. It matters here specifically because none of the training happens in multiple-choice format. The model is trained entirely on free-form, rubric-graded prompts, and GPQA is where they check whether any of that transfers to a completely different answer format.

[S] Let's get to the headline numbers.
[G] The best variant, RaR-Implicit, beats the Direct-Likert baseline by up to thirty-one percent relative on HealthBench and seven percent relative on GPQA-Diamond. In absolute terms, HealthBench's overall score moves from twenty-five point five up to thirty-one point two. GPQA-Diamond accuracy moves from thirty-four point eight up to thirty-seven point six.
[O] And that GPQA number is honestly the one I find more interesting than the headline percentage.
[G] Agreed, and here's why it matters. The model was trained purely on free-form science prompts graded against rubrics. It never saw a multiple-choice question with lettered options during training. The fact that the skill transfers to GPQA's format at all tells you the reward isn't just teaching the model to satisfy a checklist syntactically. Something more like general reasoning quality is improving.
[S] Where do the other baselines land on that same table?
[G] Bottom to top: raw Qwen two point five scores seven point seven on HealthBench and thirty-one point seven on GPQA. Instruction-tuning alone gets you to twenty-two point seven and thirty-five. Direct-Likert, an LLM judge just rating the response one to ten with no reference shown, gets twenty-five point five and thirty-four point eight. Reference-Likert, which also shows the judge a gold answer to compare against, reaches twenty-eight point nine and thirty-six point five. Then RaR-Explicit at twenty-nine point seven and thirty-six point nine, and RaR-Implicit on top at thirty-one point two and thirty-seven point six.
[O] There's one number in that table that's almost more informative than the winner.
[G] RaR-Predefined. This is the variant where instead of generating a fresh rubric for each prompt, you use one fixed generic checklist for every prompt — "is it concise," "is it correct," that kind of thing. It collapses to twelve point five on HealthBench. That's worse than the plain instruction-tuned model with no rubric training at all.
[S] So the rubric machinery by itself isn't the ingredient. It's specifically that the rubric is instance-specific.
[G] Exactly the authors' own conclusion. Generic criteria produce a reward signal that's misaligned with what any particular prompt actually needs, and training against a misaligned reward can leave a policy worse off than not training it with rubrics at all.
[O] What does the generic rubric even look like, concretely?
[G] Four fixed lines applied to every response regardless of the prompt. Is it factually correct, does it fully answer the question, is it concise, is it genuinely useful to the user. Reasonable-sounding criteria in the abstract, but none of them can tell a bicarbonate dosing calculation apart from a question about drug interactions. The judge ends up rewarding surface qualities that happen to correlate with quality on average, rather than what that specific prompt actually demanded.
[O] What about the judge-size result — that felt like the most exportable finding in the paper to me.
[G] There are actually two related results, and it's worth keeping them separate. The main one is the alignment study: judges of different sizes score a clinician-approved response against a deliberately degraded one, either with the rubric shown or without it. Rubric guidance improves how often the judge picks the better response at every size tested, and the gains are largest for the smallest judges — it visibly narrows the gap to much larger judges.
[S] And the second one?
[G] A separate appendix experiment trains policies using judges of different sizes as the actual reward source during GRPO, on synthetic medical rubrics, and measures how good the resulting policy is. There, gpt-4o-mini as judge reaches twenty-seven point nine percent with the rubric versus twenty-five point three without it. And the gap is biggest for the smallest judge tested — a seven-billion-parameter Qwen instruct model jumps from twenty-two percent up to twenty-six point seven with the rubric in front of it, nearly catching the larger judges.
[S] So a small, cheap judge with a rubric in hand can substitute for a much more expensive one.
[G] That's the paper's own read, and it might be the single most reusable finding here, independent of everything else we're about to argue over.
[O] There's also the reference-grounding ablation, which I think undersells itself a little in how it's written up.
[G] On an in-domain HealthBench holdout, synthetic rubrics generated with access to a reference answer score thirty-five point nine, essentially matching rubrics written by actual human experts at thirty-four point eight. Take the reference answer away during rubric generation, and quality drops to thirty-two point zero. Expert grounding, whether it comes from a human or from a good reference answer, is doing real work. Rubrics generated with nothing to anchor them fall meaningfully short.

[O] Okay, let's actually argue this. My case first: this is a clean, well-executed idea that closes an obvious gap. Rubrics were sitting right there in eval benchmarks, nobody had turned them into a reward function for on-policy RL, and now someone has, and it works — beats every baseline including one that also gets to see the gold answer, transfers to an out-of-format benchmark it wasn't trained on, and the small-judge alignment result is a free, reusable finding for anyone building LLM-judge pipelines.
[S] I'll take the deflationary case, and I want to start with that headline percentage again. Thirty-one percent relative is measured against Direct-Likert, the weakest reasonable baseline in the table, a judge with no reference answer at all. The fairer comparison is against Reference-Likert, which also gets the gold answer, and there the gap shrinks to twenty-eight point nine versus thirty-one point two on HealthBench, and thirty-six point five versus thirty-seven point six on GPQA. Hannah, the paper plots confidence intervals on GPQA — does RaR actually clear Reference-Likert there, or is it sitting inside the interval?
[G] It's close. RaR's interval and Reference-Likert's overlap on GPQA. So on the multiple-choice side, "beats Reference-Likert" is directionally true but not a clean statistical win. On HealthBench the gap is small but the paper does treat it as a real, if modest, improvement.
[S] And since the rubrics themselves are generated from the reference answer, doesn't that mean a lot of what RaR is buying you is just "have a good reference answer," with the rubric decomposition adding a smaller increment on top?
[G] That's a fair reading, and honestly it's close to how I'd summarize the paper myself. The reference answer is clearly carrying real weight, you can see that in the no-reference ablation dropping several points. The rubric decomposition on top of that reference is a real but more modest gain.
[O] Even a modest, reproducible gain on top of the strongest baseline is still a win, though. And it's a win measured on out-of-distribution training data, which is the harder thing to pull off.
[S] Sure, but here's the part that worries me more than the framing of the headline number. Look at the rubric-design ablation. Dropping the categorical labels entirely, no more Essential, Important, Optional, Pitfall, scores thirty-eight point eight. That's higher than the full rubric's thirty-seven point two. And removing pitfall criteria changes nothing at all, the same thirty-seven point two either way. Hannah, doesn't that mean the entire weighted, categorized rubric machinery the paper spends its first several pages building up barely matters?
[G] The authors say so themselves, quite directly, in that ablation section, and I'd give them credit for not hiding it. The mechanism that's actually doing the work looks like "decompose the reference answer into a checklist and let a judge score it holistically," which is RaR-Implicit, and RaR-Implicit already discards the explicit weights. The categorical weighting and pitfall machinery that gives the method its visual identity in the figures isn't what's earning the gains.
[O] I'll concede that one. That's a real gap between how the method is presented and what the ablations actually show is load-bearing.
[S] One more, and this is the one I'd flag as sharpest. The main headline result trains on the full twenty-thousand-prompt RaR-Medicine set and evaluates on HealthBench, that's clean, genuinely different sources. But every ablation table, the reference-grounding one, the rubric-design one, the rubric-generator-quality one, trains on a three-and-a-half-thousand-prompt slice of HealthBench itself and evaluates on a thousand held-out HealthBench prompts. Same benchmark, same reference answers, same rubric axes, on both sides.
[G] That's accurate. The training judge, gpt-4o-mini, is different from the actual GPT-4 grader HealthBench uses for its official scores, so it isn't fully circular. But the reward signal and the evaluation signal are drawn from the same well in those ablation tables, and that's exactly where the paper's most flattering in-domain numbers come from. I'd treat those ablation conclusions as real but inflated relative to the clean, out-of-distribution main result.
[O] So where does that leave us, does the paper hold up?
[G] As a recipe, yes. Closing the loop between evaluation rubrics and training rewards is a genuinely useful contribution, and the small-judge alignment result stands on its own regardless of any of this. But I'd read the headline number as softer than it's presented, and I'd read the winning mechanism as simpler than the method's own diagram suggests: a good reference answer, decomposed into a checklist, scored holistically. Not the full weighted, categorized rubric apparatus the figures advertise.

[O] Zoom out for a second, who should actually care about this outside people training on HealthBench specifically?
[G] Anyone building a reward signal for a domain without a single right answer. Legal reasoning, customer support quality, anything currently outsourced to an expensive preference-trained reward model is a candidate for this recipe instead.
[S] It also connects directly to something evaluation people already worry about, judge reliability and contamination. If your reward rubrics and your eval rubrics are drawn from the same benchmark, you've built exactly the overlap this paper's own ablation tables have, just with worse consequences, because now you're optimizing against it rather than just measuring against it.
[O] And the paper is upfront that it hasn't stress-tested this over long training runs.
[G] Right, that's explicitly outside its scope. What happens if you train for a lot longer than they did, does the judge start getting gamed the way any imperfect verifier eventually does? That's precisely the question a later paper, "Reward Hacking in Rubric-Based Reinforcement Learning," goes after directly. This paper is best read as the proof that the recipe works at all, not as a stress test of what happens when you lean on it hard.

[G] If there's one line to take from this paper, it's that rubrics can be a reward, not just a ruler, and closing that loop is real and it works, even if the fanciest part of the rubric design, the weights and the pitfalls, turns out not to be where the value comes from.
[O] My takeaway is the optimist's: this is a clean, practical bridge from verifiable RL into the much bigger world of unverifiable domains, and the small-judge alignment result alone is worth adopting today.
[S] Mine's more cautious: read the headline percentage against the harder baseline, not the easy one, and remember the ablations that matter most were run in-house on the same benchmark they're evaluated against.
