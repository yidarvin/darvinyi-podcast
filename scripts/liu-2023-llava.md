[O] Let's set up the gap first. Diego, why couldn't someone just point an existing multimodal model at this problem in early twenty twenty-three?
[G] Because instruction tuning and multimodal training had developed on separate tracks. By that spring the language side had InstructGPT, ChatGPT, Alpaca, Vicuna — fine-tune a base model on machine-generated instructions and it starts following requests. The vision-language side had Flamingo, BLIP-2, OpenFlamingo, KOSMOS-1, trained on image-text pairs with a captioning objective. Not instructions.
[S] What does that distinction actually look like in practice?
[G] The paper shows it directly. Ask BLIP-2 or OpenFlamingo "what is unusual about this image," and they just describe the scene — a man on a car, a yellow cab — instead of answering the question. They were trained to caption, not to follow instructions.
[O] So the fix should just be: collect instruction data for images the way people collected it for text.
[G] Should be. The paper says the blocker was exactly that — hand-collecting a multimodal instruction dataset, pairing images with diverse, richly annotated instructions and responses, is slow and expensive at any scale that matters. Their move is to sidestep human annotation almost entirely.
[S] By using a model that can't even see the images.
[G] Right, that's the part people still find surprising. A language-only GPT-4 gets fed a textual stand-in for the picture — the image's existing captions plus object bounding boxes — and, seeded with a handful of hand-written examples, generates three response types: conversation, multi-turn back-and-forth; detailed description; and complex reasoning, step-by-step answers requiring inference beyond what's stated.
[O] And GPT-4 never once sees a pixel.
[G] Never. It's working entirely from text. The authors ablated ChatGPT against GPT-4 for this generation step and found GPT-4 consistently produces higher-quality data, especially on anything involving spatial reasoning.
[S] How much data does that pipeline actually produce?
[G] One hundred fifty-eight thousand samples total: fifty-eight thousand conversation, twenty-three thousand detailed description, seventy-seven thousand complex reasoning. That's LLaVA-Instruct-158K, and it's the entire supervised signal for teaching a model to have a multimodal conversation.
[O] Now the model side, because I think this part is underrated. Walk us through the architecture.
[G] It's almost aggressively simple, which is the point. A pretrained CLIP vision encoder, the ViT-L-14 variant, produces visual features for the image. A single trainable linear projection matrix, call it W, maps those into the language model's own word-embedding space — a sequence of visual tokens prepended to the instruction tokens. The language model is Vicuna, chosen for the strongest instruction-following behavior among public checkpoints at the time.
[S] One linear layer is the entire bridge between vision and language?
[G] The entire trainable bridge, yes. The vision encoder is frozen throughout. Everything the model learns about connecting "here's what the image contains" to "here's how to talk about it" has to route through that one matrix, at least in the first training stage.
[O] That's the part I find genuinely exciting. It implies the hard problem here was never architecture — it was data.
[S] Or it implies they got lucky that a linear map is sufficient, and a harder benchmark would expose that it isn't. Diego, walk us through training, because I know there are two stages doing different jobs.
[G] Stage one is feature-alignment pretraining. Both the vision encoder and language model stay frozen — only the projection matrix W trains, on about five hundred ninety-five thousand image-text pairs filtered from CC3M, reformatted as "briefly describe this image" instructions, for one epoch. The authors are explicit this isn't teaching the language model anything new — it's training a compatible visual tokenizer.
[S] So after stage one, has the model actually learned to reason about images yet?
[G] Not really — that's the point of separating the stages. Stage two is end-to-end fine-tuning: the vision encoder stays frozen, but now both W and the language model update, for three epochs, on either the full one hundred fifty-eight thousand chatbot dataset or a ScienceQA-reformatted version. That's where it learns to converse. They train a seven-billion and a thirteen-billion parameter variant, on eight A-one-hundred GPUs.
[O] So the story is: cheap alignment step, then the expensive instruction data does the real work.
[G] That's exactly the story, and the paper has an ablation that makes it almost embarrassingly clean.
[S] Let's get the results on the table, then, before anyone gets to editorialize.
[G] Two benchmarks, both built by this team, both graded the same way. GPT-4 gets the image only as ground-truth text — captions and boxes — writes a reference answer, then scores a candidate model's answer against that reference on a one-to-ten scale, reported as a relative score against its own reference.
[O] Start with the ablation, because it's the strongest single result in the paper for me.
[G] LLaVA-Bench COCO: thirty images from COCO-Val-2014, ninety questions across the three response types. The full mix gets eighty-five point one percent. Conversation data alone, seventy-three point eight, down eleven point three. And skip instruction tuning entirely —

[G] Correct — no visual instruction tuning at all, just the aligned-but-not-instructed model. That collapses the score to twenty-one point five percent, a sixty-three point six point drop. That's larger than every architectural and data-mix choice combined, and it's the clearest evidence that instruction tuning specifically is the load-bearing element here, not scale, not architecture.
[O] That's a huge effect size for a paper this early in a new subfield.

BLIP-2 gets thirty-eight point one, plus or minus one — the paper reports that as a plus twenty-nine point gap. OpenFlamingo gets nineteen point one, plus or minus point four, a plus forty-eight point gap. On complex reasoning specifically, LLaVA hits eighty-one point seven, plus or minus one point eight.

[O] Now the one I actually care about for real-world impact: ScienceQA.

[S] And then they bring GPT-4 back into it.
[G] Two ways. [O] A text-only model that can't see the image improves a vision model's accuracy on image questions. That's a genuinely interesting result on its own.
[G] It is, and the paper has an explanation. A meaningful chunk of ScienceQA's image-context questions don't actually require the image to answer correctly. GPT-4 can catch cases where LLaVA got confused by something irrelevant to the real reasoning, purely from text.
